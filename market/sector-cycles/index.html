I.

I. Markets Are Cyclical -- Especially Indian Markets

Let me tell you something most finance influencers will never admit: they have no idea which sector will move next. They chase whatever is running hot and then tell you to buy. By the time you hear about it, the smart money has already rotated out.

India’s stock market is deeply cyclical. Not just in the sense that bull and bear phases alternate – every market does that. What makes India unique is that sectors rotate in a predictable sequence within each cycle. If you understand this sequence, you can position capital months before a rally begins. If you do not, you will always be buying tops and wondering why your portfolio looks nothing like the returns people brag about.

Here is the data I want you to internalize:

  • 1999-2009: Nifty 50 delivered 4x returns
  • 2009-2019: Nifty 50 delivered 2.5x returns
  • 2020-2025: Nifty 50 delivered 2x returns

As our markets grow bigger, the absolute index returns are structurally slowing down. This is not doom and gloom. It means that picking the right sector at the right time has become more important than picking the right stock within a hot sector. A rising tide used to lift all boats. Now it lifts certain boats first, and by the time others start floating, the first ones have already docked and taken profits.

The game in Indian equities is no longer about buying and holding blindly. India is a capital rotation market. I keep saying this because it is the single most important framework for Indian investing. You need to understand which sectors move first, which move last, and why the sequence plays out the way it does.

The Indian market is now a pure trading market. So we TRADE it well – but with an investor’s mindset.

Let me walk you through the entire sector cycle, from the first movers to the last, and show you exactly how to position yourself at each stage.

II.

II. The Sector Rotation Sequence: PSU/Defence to IT to Banks to FMCG

Every bull cycle in India plays out in roughly the same order. The sectors that move first are the ones most directly tied to government spending and policy. The ones that move last are driven by consumer sentiment, which is always the slowest variable to turn around. Here is the sequence:

Stage 1: PSU Banks and Defence/Infrastructure

When a new government spending cycle begins or when the market bottoms out after a crash, the first sectors to rally are the ones that receive direct government capital. PSU banks rally because the government recapitalizes them or policy shifts deposit flows into the banking system. Defence and infrastructure stocks move because capex announcements translate to immediate order books.

We experienced this firsthand. Our community bought PSU banks and AMCs and made 100%+ returns. SBI, for example, we bought at very good valuations – it went up significantly from our entry. Buying SBI now at these levels does not make the same sense. The easy money in PSU banks has already been made.

The point is simple: Stage 1 stocks move because of a clear, traceable catalyst – government money flowing into the system. You do not need to guess whether consumers will spend or whether global IT budgets will increase. The government announces capex, the order books fill up, the stocks move. This is why it is the first stage. It is the most predictable.

Stage 2: IT/Technology

As the recovery matures and global spending cycles align (particularly US monetary easing), IT stocks begin their move. Indian IT is deeply tied to US corporate spending. When US companies increase their technology budgets – whether for consulting, digital transformation, or now data center optimization – Indian IT services firms benefit.

But here is the critical nuance: IT does not rally because India’s economy is doing well. IT rallies because of external demand cycles. This is why IT often moves even when the broader Indian market is consolidating. I changed my view from being bearish IT to neutral IT, and then started buying aggressively. The IT index was down 30% from its peak when we entered. That was the time to buy a turnaround, not chase a growth story.

IT has a role to play in India’s next growth phase: data center optimization, EVs, tech consulting, transition to SaaS for small and mid-size players, and tech integration in manufacturing. The sentiments are terrible. But think about it this way: if AI disrupts IT, who benefits? AI stocks in your portfolio. If AI does not disrupt IT as badly as feared, IT bounces back. Either way, you win if you have positioned correctly across both.

Stage 3: Banks and Financial Services

Private banks and NBFCs typically rally in the middle of the cycle. This happens when interest rate cuts begin, credit growth picks up, and the lending market expands. With ban on betting apps, less FnO participation, and money flowing back into deposits, banks benefit from an improved savings rate relative to GDP.

We were already well-invested in banks before most fund managers started chasing them. Anyone from our community would verify that. The key was entering finance during the early recovery phase and holding through the consolidation.

Now look at what is happening within finance. NBFCs are eating into the market share of traditional banks. Bajaj Finance enjoys a more than 22% CAGR and commands roughly 55% share in consumer durable loans, about 7% share of unsecured personal loans overall, and 23-24% share in unsecured SME loans. But even this dominant player faces disruption from fintech. Jio Financial, Groww, PhonePe – they are all building lending businesses with digital-first, lower-cost customer acquisition strategies. So within Stage 3, there is a sub-rotation happening: from PSU banks to private banks to NBFCs to fintech lenders. Understanding this granularity within a sector is what separates average investors from the ones who consistently generate alpha.

Stage 4: FMCG and Consumption

Consumer-facing sectors are always the last to rally. Why? Because consumer spending is a lagging indicator. People do not start spending freely until they see job stability, wage growth, and general economic confidence – all of which take time to build after a downturn. FMCG, auto, and discretionary consumption stocks only participate meaningfully in the final stages of a bull run.

This is precisely where we are cautious right now. Real wage growth at the national level from Q2 FY20 to Q1 FY25 was essentially flat at about 0.01% per year. Until job creation accelerates and wages start growing meaningfully, the consumption story remains fragile.

I will give you a concrete example of why timing matters in consumption stocks. I exited all positions on Zomato and Swiggy when Swiggy announced it wanted to raise 10,000 crore rupees just one year after its IPO. It also got 2,400 crore from selling its Rapido stake. With Zepto reportedly holding about 1 billion dollars in cash, and competition heating up from BigBasket, Reliance Retail, Amazon, and Flipkart – this is a low-margin business heading into a brutal cash-burn war. Profitability will suffer. I took our community’s profits (21% on Swiggy, 100% on Zomato) and rotated into segments where actual margin expansion is happening. That is what capital rotation looks like in practice.

Every Fund Manager is now running after finance/bank stocks. Guess what: we already have decent positions there – already on good profits.

III.

III. The Three-Segment Framework: How to Divide the Indian Market

Forget the traditional sector classifications that Moneycontrol shows you. When I look at the Indian market for investment purposes, I divide it into three actionable segments:

Segment A: Finance/Banks

This has been the backbone of our India portfolio for a long time. Banks benefit from multiple structural tailwinds:

  • Ban on betting apps and reduced FnO participation pushes savings into the banking system
  • India’s savings rate to GDP has been at a historic low – any turnaround here means massive deposit inflows
  • Rate cuts from RBI directly improve the lending spread

But the landscape within finance is shifting. NBFCs are growing their loan book at 20% CAGR for FY25, while banks are growing at only 12%. Every startup in India is pivoting to lending:

  • Cred: pay bills –> rewards –> BNPL –> personal loans
  • Paytm: wallet –> merchants –> credit for anyone
  • Groww: mutual funds –> stocks –> futures –> loans against security
  • PhonePe: payments –> P2P –> insurance –> lending
  • Razorpay: payment gateway –> business banking –> lending

Private banks are losing market share, and this is being taken by NBFCs and fintech players. The play now is to hold existing bank positions, book profits over time, and gradually rotate into the higher-growth lending segment.

Segment B: Tech/IT

This is a turnaround play, not a growth play. I need you to understand the difference. A turnaround means:

  • You downward average on the asset
  • Stick a 15-20% block on the theme (of your Indian portfolio)
  • And then you stop buying
  • Wait from there for 1-3 years

The sentiments on IT are terrible right now. But one key advantage for good IT firms like TCS: they have a very healthy cash ratio. They can fund the next layer of their development without depending on external credit. That is a massive advantage in a down cycle.

Here is how I am structuring my IT allocation – and I am not overcomplicating it:

  • 50% Nifty IT index
  • 30% Top-3 IT stocks
  • 20% Select picks

Even doing 100% Nifty IT is fine. The point is to have exposure to the turnaround, not to pick the perfect individual stock.

Segment C: Consumption

This is where the game gets deeply interesting, and where most investors get it wrong. To understand consumption, you first need to understand who the consumers actually are. This brings me to the most important framework in this article.

IV.

IV. India's 4-Tier Consumer Framework

Most people talk about “Indian consumption” as if it is one monolithic thing. It is not. India has at least four distinct consumer tiers, and each behaves completely differently in an economic cycle. Understanding this framework is the difference between making money in consumption stocks and getting destroyed by them.

Tier 1: Upper Class – Top 1 Crore Customers

These are people who can afford to buy luxury outright. No loans needed, no EMI calculations. This market never dips in any meaningful way. Whether the economy is booming or struggling, the top 1 crore customers keep buying Mercedes, luxury real estate, premium healthcare, and international brands.

For investors, this means luxury-oriented businesses are recession-resistant in India. The stock prices might correct because of market-wide sentiment, but the actual revenue of these businesses rarely takes a hit.

Tier 2: Middle Class – Top 10 Crore Customers (excluding Tier 1)

This is the aspirational segment. They take loans to buy luxury. They stretch for the premium apartment, finance the SUV, and EMI their way to a lifestyle that looks upper class. When the economy is strong, these consumers drive massive growth. When the economy weakens, they are the first to pull back.

Firms like Tata Motors, mid-range real estate developers, and premium consumer brands are deeply tied to this segment. Unless the job market revives, these firms will continue to struggle. Rate cuts from RBI help here, but they are not sufficient on their own. You need wage growth, and we are not seeing that yet.

Tier 3: Lower Class A – Top 25 Crore Customers (excluding Tier 2)

These consumers cannot afford luxury even with loans. They are price-sensitive on almost everything. Think basic consumer durables, budget smartphones, entry-level two-wheelers. Companies serving this tier compete primarily on price, which means razor-thin margins and constant price wars.

Consider the quick-commerce example. The end customers of Zomato, Swiggy, and Zepto are price hunters in this tier. If the price of Lauki (bottle gourd) goes up by 5 rupees, customers drop off. These platforms have no product moat – Swiggy and Zepto compete for the exact same order. The margin compression in this space is structural, not temporary. Unless these firms research and scale other products, there is no reason why they should go even 3x from current levels. And yet their valuations (Zomato at one point was at 1500 PE) would suggest otherwise. This is the trap that catches investors who do not understand consumer tier dynamics.

Tier 4: Survival Consumption

The remainder of the population. Basic necessities – food, shelter, essential clothing. Companies here benefit from government freebies and tax cuts. Firms like HUL, which sell everyday staples, see revival in this segment first when the government increases subsidies or cuts taxes for lower-income groups.

Tier 5 (Bonus): Government Spending

This is not really a consumer tier, but it functions like one. When the government ramps up capex, PSU firms, defence contractors, and infrastructure companies are the direct beneficiaries. The government has slowed down its capex now, so there is no point picking firms here in the current cycle.

The stock markets rallied primarily due to government Capex spending. This is slowing down. So we are likely to be in an “average” phase.

Here is how this framework maps to investment decisions right now:

  • Tier 1: Keep buying. Hospitals, luxury brands, premium experiences like PVR
  • Tier 2 and 3: Extremely selective. Do not touch auto. Watch for job market revival
  • Tier 4: Can see immediate revival. HUL-type firms benefit from tax cuts and freebies
  • Government spending: Slowed down. No point picking new firms here

The mistake most investors make is treating all consumption the same. They see “consumption theme” mentioned on a brokerage report and buy everything from Zomato to Asian Paints to Tata Motors. Each of these serves a different tier, has a different growth driver, and will react to economic changes on a completely different timeline.

V.

V. The Real Estate Cycle Connection

Here is a theme that most equity investors completely ignore: the real estate cycle has deep implications for how sector rotation plays out, and it is currently setting up for a structural move.

RBI recently changed its ECB (External Commercial Borrowing) norms. This is a big deal. In simple terms, it allows Indian companies – including real estate developers – to raise money from foreign entities. Up until now, sectors like real estate were restricted. Now this is being opened up. Dollar capital can flow into Indian RE.

But it goes deeper:

  1. Norms around REITs (fractional real estate) and taxation on RE have changed
  2. The government is trying to make RE more trading-oriented
  3. When foreigners invest in RE, it scales fast – they care about returns, not sentiment
  4. This means RE is going to become more expensive structurally

The connection to sector rotation is this: as real estate prices rise, it creates a wealth effect. Homeowners feel richer, their net worth on paper increases, and they start spending more freely. This is the mechanism through which the consumption tier eventually catches up to the rest of the market.

But there is an important distinction I keep making: own land and villas, not flats. Flats depreciate. Land appreciates. When foreign capital flows into RE, it is the land value that scales fastest. If you own the right hard assets, you see much faster appreciation.

The investment playbook here is straightforward:

  • When your stock portfolio crosses 2Cr+, withdraw 30% and buy a hard asset (real estate). It gives you safety
  • RE seems to be a very strong structural bet in India over the next decade
  • But when I say RE, I mean land and villas, not flats
  • The manipulation game on RE is just starting

For the equity portfolio, the RE cycle impacts your positions in banking stocks (more home loans), NBFC stocks (housing finance), and eventually consumption stocks (wealth effect spending). Understanding this connection lets you anticipate sector rotation rather than react to it.

There is also an important portfolio management principle embedded here. When your stock portfolio hits 2Cr+, you should withdraw 30% and park it in a hard asset. When it hits 5Cr+, set up a tax base and optimize for 0% capital gains. With the taxes saved, buy a golden visa, residency, travel – enjoy life. This is not about greed. It is about building a system. You are not just a stock picker. You are building an asset base that compounds across multiple classes – equities, real estate, international residency – each reinforcing the other.

Real estate is not separate from your equity strategy. It is the safety net that allows you to take more aggressive bets in the stock market. The person who owns land outright behaves very differently in a market crash than the person whose entire net worth is in equity. The first one buys the dip with leverage. The second one panics and sells. I have seen this pattern play out too many times.

This is my highest conviction India theme right now. Let me explain the depth of this conviction, because hospitals deserve more attention than most investors give them.

India is quickly becoming the cancer capital of the world. Oncology is the fastest-growing sub-segment in hospital revenues. The rate of growth in cancer patients is around 3-3.5% CAGR each year – and these are just official reports. The actual numbers are likely higher given under-diagnosis in rural areas. The consumption of processed food is at an all-time high in India. Even supposedly “safe” food like eggs are contaminated (look up Eggoz if you have not).

Here is the critical structural advantage: unlike many other diseases, cancer treatment is primarily done by organized hospitals. It cannot be handled by secondary and tertiary healthcare facilities. This creates a natural moat. You cannot disrupt Apollo or Max Healthcare with an app or a low-cost clinic. The capital requirements to build oncology capacity are enormous, the regulatory barriers are high, and the doctor talent pool is limited.

I am building 40-50% of my intended hospital positions now and waiting for further corrections to complete the allocation. Names I like: Fortis, Apollo, Max Healthcare. My portfolio construction is based on two questions: am I holding dominant players in sectors unlikely to be disrupted, and are the margins and TAM going to grow? Hospitals answer both with a resounding yes.

  • Sell non-critical products where price wars rage: consider exiting HUL, Asian Paints over time

Index/Trading allocation: 10-15%

  • This cash is for selling put options on stocks you want to own anyway (a strategy I have been teaching the community)

Finance, Healthcare and IT are my top picks in India. I am not experimenting with Manufacturing or AI too much here.

VII.

VII. Why Consumption Is the Only Investable Long-Term Theme

I know the subtitle of this article says “India’s consumption story is the only investable theme.” Let me explain what I mean by that, because it might sound contradictory to everything I just said about being cautious on consumption right now.

Here is the difference between cyclical and structural thinking.

Cyclically – meaning right now, in this particular phase of the market – consumption is the last sector to rally, and many consumption names are still overvalued or facing headwinds. This is why I say sell consumer, do not buy auto, be selective.

But structurally – meaning over the next 10-20 years – consumption is the only truly domestic, India-specific, self-reinforcing theme. Here is why:

  1. IT is an export business. It depends on US corporate spending. When the US sneezes, Indian IT gets pneumonia. You cannot call this a pure India story.

  2. Banking and financial services are infrastructure, not a growth story. Banks will grow, but they grow roughly in line with nominal GDP. The hyper-growth phase where credit penetration was expanding rapidly is maturing.

  3. Government-driven sectors (defence, PSU, infra) are cyclical by nature. They depend entirely on the political capex cycle. When the government slows down spending – as it has now – these sectors stagnate.

  4. Consumption is the only sector that grows because India’s population is young, urbanizing, and aspirational. As 25 Crore people move from Tier 3 to Tier 2 purchasing patterns, and as 10 Crore move from Tier 2 to Tier 1, the compounding effect on domestic consumption is massive.

This is why hospitals are my highest-conviction play. Healthcare consumption is non-discretionary, growing structurally (driven by lifestyle diseases, oncology, medical tourism), and impossible to import from China or outsource to AI. The margins expand as hospitals add capacity, because the demand is not price-elastic at the top end.

The same logic applies to select consumption businesses that serve non-discretionary or aspirational needs that cannot be easily disrupted:

  • DMART: A MOAT-oriented business at a 4-year low. The grocery retail format it dominates is the purest play on India’s consumption growth
  • Premium hospital chains: As discussed above
  • Capital market infrastructure (CAMS, CDSL): As more Indians invest, this is consumption of financial services – structural growth

The consumption stocks you avoid are the ones in commodity businesses where brand loyalty is low and price wars are inevitable: packaged foods, paints, basic consumer durables. These are Tier 3 and Tier 4 businesses competing on cost, and they are going to see margin compression as Jio, Reliance Retail, Amazon, and Flipkart all chase the same customer.

Now, I also want to address something I see a lot of investors getting confused about. People ask: “If consumption is the only long-term theme, why not just buy consumption stocks now and hold for 10 years?” Because valuation at entry determines your returns, not the quality of the theme. A stock or industry can be great, but if you buy at wrong prices, you can get stuck with it for years. I had this experience with HDFC – we eventually made money, but the first few months were painful because we bought too early before the technical breakout.

This is exactly what is happening with many consumption stocks right now. The themes are right. The timing is not. Wait for the technical confirmations. Wait for stocks to trade at or below 200DMA. Wait for the interest rate cycle to translate into actual consumer demand. Then deploy aggressively. That is the approach that has consistently worked for our community.

India is a great market, with a lot of potential. But the focus of the government is not the markets now. It is elsewhere. Hence, India is a trading market.

VIII.

VIII. The Interest Rate Transmission Mechanism

Understanding how interest rates flow through the economy and into sector performance is critical for timing your rotations correctly. Most investors get this wrong because they think of rate cuts as a one-time event. In reality, rate transmission is a multi-quarter, multi-sector process.

Here is how it works in India:

Phase 1: RBI announces rate cuts

The immediate beneficiaries are banks and NBFCs. Their cost of borrowing drops, lending spreads improve, and the market reprices financial stocks upward. This is why finance tends to rally in the middle of the cycle – it coincides with the rate-cutting phase.

But here is the catch: if RBI cuts too fast, we get a delinquency loan problem. More rate cuts mean more borrowing, and if consumers are already leveraged, faster cuts lead to more defaults. This is why the government has been slow in cutting rates – they have been strengthening the financial system even at the cost of growth.

Phase 2: Credit flows to businesses and consumers

As rates drop, the cost of EMIs falls, home loans become cheaper, and small businesses can borrow to expand. This phase takes 3-6 months to play out after the rate cut. It is during this phase that auto, real estate, and consumer durable companies start seeing improved demand.

Housing finance companies like Aavas and Aptus benefit directly here. Lower rates mean more home loan originations, higher ticket sizes, and reduced default risk.

Phase 3: Consumer spending accelerates

This is the final phase, where FMCG and discretionary consumption companies see actual revenue growth. Consumers who have been holding back start spending again. But this only happens if wage growth accompanies the rate cuts. Without jobs, lower rates alone cannot drive sustained consumption.

This is why I said earlier that India’s consumption story is cyclically weak but structurally strong. We are still in Phase 1 to early Phase 2. The full consumption recovery has not even begun. When it does – driven by rate cuts, potential job growth, and government tax relief – the same consumption stocks everyone is bearish on today will be the ones running hardest.

The practical implication: Do not rush into consumption stocks now. Wait for evidence that Phase 2 is translating into real demand (check auto sales data, credit growth numbers, housing starts). Use the time now to build positions in IT (turnaround), hold your finance positions, and accumulate hospitals on every correction.

The India-US Connection

One more thing that ties interest rates to sector rotation: as US monetary policy eases, the rupee stabilizes, FII flows return, and global risk appetite for Indian equities improves. This is the macro tailwind that IT needs for revival. When US companies start increasing their technology budgets again, Indian IT order books fill up, earnings revise upward, and the IT sector finally breaks out of its multi-year consolidation.

This is why I keep saying: Indian IT revival is not an India-dependent event. It is a US-dependent event. And the US monetary cycle is turning. Watch that, not Indian consumer confidence surveys, for your IT entry timing.

Let me also address the IR impact on a specific sector that confuses many investors: NBFCs versus banks in a rate-cutting environment. NBFCs are growing their loan book at roughly 20% for FY25 while banks are growing at only 12%. The leaders – Bajaj Finance, Shriram Finance, Tata Capital, Cholamandalam, Mahindra Finance, Muthoot Finance, HDB Financial Services, and Poonawalla Fincorp – focus on digital lending and quick approvals. They lend more to small businesses, rural markets, and gold-backed loans.

When rates drop, NBFCs actually benefit more than banks in the short term because they can pass on rate cuts faster (they are not burdened by legacy deposit structures) and they can grow their loan books more aggressively into the newly credit-hungry segments. Private banks are losing market share in personal and consumer loans – this shift is structural, not cyclical. So within the finance sector, the rotation is clear: book profits on traditional banks, increase exposure to high-quality NBFCs, and watch fintech lenders like Jio Finance for dip-buying opportunities.

There is one more rate-sensitive segment worth mentioning: gold loan firms. I am not touching them right now. Their previous inventory got revalued at higher rates during the gold rally, which expanded profits artificially. There might be a slowdown in gold buying, plus there is a chance of price stagnation. And the sentiment in the gold loan space is very bullish – which is usually a contrarian signal to stay away.

IX.

IX. Putting It All Together: Your Sector Rotation Playbook

Let me now give you a practical framework you can use to navigate sector cycles going forward. This is not just theory – this is what I have been doing with real money on our community.

Step 1: Identify where we are in the cycle

Look at three things: - Government capex trajectory (rising = early cycle; slowing = mid-to-late cycle) - Interest rate direction (cutting = mid cycle; low rates holding = late cycle) - Consumer confidence and wage growth (improving = late cycle approaching)

Right now, government capex has slowed, RBI is in a rate-cutting cycle, and wage growth is flat. We are firmly in the mid-cycle phase. This means banks and IT should be your focus, with consumption being accumulated selectively on deep corrections.

Step 2: Build your core positions in the leading sectors

Do not wait for confirmation. By the time everyone agrees that IT is recovering, the stocks will already be up 30%. The whole point of understanding sector rotation is to act before the crowd.

I built IT positions when most investors were bearish. I held banks when nobody wanted them. The community made money because we understood the sequence and positioned early.

Step 3: Capital rotation is the game

India is a capital rotation market. When one sector runs up 30%, book 25% of your profits and rotate into the next sector in the sequence that is still beaten down. Here is a practical example:

  • City Union Bank was up 30% in 3 months on our portfolio
  • I rotated some capital out and started positions in beaten-down stocks
  • Took a small position in CRISIL below 200DMA, leaving room to downward average

This is the rhythm: buy beaten down, wait for recovery, book partial profits, rotate into the next beaten-down sector. Repeat.

Step 4: Use index positions for safety and capital deployment

When you cannot find specific stocks at good valuations but want market exposure, use Nifty 50. It is safer, more liquid, and lets you deploy large capital for short-term trades:

  • 10x capital at 15% returns through safe index trades beats 1x capital trying to make 30% on risky individual picks
  • Layer covered calls on your index position for additional premium income
  • When individual stocks correct to your target levels, rotate from index to direct equity

Step 5: Hedge across geographies

This is perhaps the most important lesson I can leave you with. For every 1 unit of Indian equity, you should have at least 2-3 units in US markets. This is not anti-India sentiment. This is math:

  • Nasdaq composite in USD delivers equal performance to India’s small and mid caps over a 10-year period
  • By investing in lower-risk Nasdaq, you get almost equal returns with lower volatility
  • The Sharpe ratio of US tech vastly exceeds Indian small and mid caps
  • Currency depreciation (INR weakening against USD) gives you an additional 3% CAGR tailwind just from exchange rate movements

If you want long-term structural growth: US markets (AI, hyperscalers, tech). If you want India-specific returns: hospitals, IT turnaround, and selective finance. If you want to trade: Nifty 50 with large capital, short durations, and options strategies.

The Bottom Line

Markets are cyclical. Sectors rotate in a predictable sequence. The government spends, PSUs rally. Global conditions improve, IT rallies. Credit expands, banks rally. Consumers feel confident, FMCG rallies. Each phase creates the conditions for the next.

Your job as an investor is not to chase whatever is running hot today. It is to understand the sequence, position yourself in the next sector to rally, and rotate capital systematically as each phase plays out.

I have been doing this for years. The community has verified the results. PSU banks gave us 100%+. Zomato gave us 100%. We exited Swiggy at 21% profits when I saw the q-commerce war coming. We avoided Indian markets for 1.5 years when they were overvalued and put that money into US stocks for 40%+ returns.

None of this was luck. It was understanding cycle, sequence, and timing.

A Note on Position Sizing Across Sectors

One more practical point that ties everything together. Maximum 5% portfolio exposure to each individual stock. This is non-negotiable. I have seen too many investors fall in love with one stock and put 20-30% of their portfolio into it. When it corrects 40% (as many stocks do), they are psychologically and financially destroyed.

The 5% rule gives you room for 20 stocks across multiple sectors. Within those 20 positions, you express your sector views through weighting:

  • If you are bullish on IT, you have 3-4 IT names at 5% each = 15-20% sector exposure
  • If you are cautious on consumption, you have 2 names at 3-4% each = 6-8% sector exposure
  • If you are building hospital positions, you have 3 names at 4-5% each = 12-15% sector exposure

This approach ensures that even if an entire sector goes against you, the damage is contained. And when a sector runs in your favor, the 15-20% weighting generates meaningful returns on the overall portfolio.
