I.

I. The Illusion of 12.5%

Here is a number that every Indian investor knows: the Nifty 50 has delivered 12.5% returns over the last 20 years. It is printed on every mutual fund factsheet, repeated in every financial planning seminar, and embedded in every SIP calculator that promises you a yacht in Ibiza on a 10,000-rupee monthly investment.

It is also, for all practical purposes, a lie.

Not because the number itself is wrong. The Nifty 50 has indeed compounded at roughly 12.5% in nominal INR terms over two decades. The lie is in what that number omits. It omits taxes. It omits currency depreciation. It omits the relentless erosion of purchasing power that makes a rupee today worth meaningfully less than a rupee a year from now. When you strip away the illusions and calculate what you actually earned in real, spendable, globally comparable terms, the picture changes so dramatically that it forces you to rethink everything about how and where you invest.

Let us walk through the arithmetic that most investors never do.

Start with 12.5%. Post long-term capital gains tax at 12.5% (which itself has climbed from 0% before 2018, to 10%, and now to 12.5%), your return drops to approximately 10.5%. Now adjust for INR depreciation against the dollar – the rupee has consistently lost 3-4% per year against the USD over long stretches. Your 10.5% is now 6.5-7% in dollar terms. And the global dollar inflation rate? At least 7-8% per year in real terms when you look at actual costs of goods, services, housing, and education across the world.

The Indian equity market, which everybody celebrates as one of the great wealth-building machines in the world, has barely kept pace with global inflation over the last two decades. Your “wealth” did not grow. Your purchasing power was roughly preserved. That is not the same thing at all.

This is not an abstract point. It is the single most important concept in investing, and everything else – which stocks to buy, which markets to enter, how to size positions, when to trade versus hold – flows directly from understanding it. Your competition is not with other investors. It is not with the index.

Your competition is with inflation.

II.

II. What "Real Returns" Actually Means

The term real returns gets thrown around loosely in finance, but it has a precise and layered meaning that most retail investors never fully unpack. Real returns are what remains after you subtract three distinct forces that eat into your gains: taxes, inflation, and currency depreciation.

Each of these operates independently, and each one compounds against you over time.

Layer 1: Taxes. India’s LTCG tax on equities has followed a clear upward trajectory. Before 2018, long-term capital gains on equities were taxed at 0%[1]. Then it moved to 10%. Then to 12.5%. The direction is obvious, and if you are planning to invest for 20 years, you should be asking yourself where LTCG will be in 2045. There is no reason to believe it will stay at 12.5%. The government’s pattern is unmistakable: cultivate an asset class first, then tax it once adoption is high enough. On a portfolio returning 12.5% nominal, LTCG at 12.5% on the gains takes your effective return down to roughly 10.5%.

Layer 2: Currency depreciation. The Indian rupee has depreciated against the US dollar at a fairly consistent rate of 3-4% per year over long periods. In October 2024, the INR was at 84 to the dollar. By early 2025, it had hit 87.03. That is a 3.5% depreciation in just a few months. This is not a temporary phenomenon – it is structural. India runs persistent current account deficits, has higher inflation than the US, and the RBI can only slow the depreciation, not prevent it. For any investor thinking about wealth in global terms – and you should be, because we live in a connected world where supply chain efficiencies ensure that comparable-quality goods cost roughly the same across nations – currency depreciation is a direct subtraction from your returns.

Layer 3: Inflation. This is the most insidious layer because it is the one governments work hardest to obscure. Official CPI numbers in India might show 5-6% inflation, but the actual cost-of-living increase for the investing class – housing, education, healthcare, travel, dining – runs at 9-12% depending on your segment. Governments worldwide are redesigning their inflation baskets, underreporting inflation numbers, and manipulating the measurement to keep the headline figure low while people experience a cost-of-living crisis that the numbers fail to capture.

When you stack these three layers together, the arithmetic becomes sobering:

  • Start: 12.5% nominal return
  • After tax drag: ~10.5%
  • After currency depreciation (3-4%): ~6.5-7%
  • After real global inflation (7-8%): -1% to +0%

You did not build wealth. You ran on a treadmill.

rreal = (1 + rnominal) ÷ (1 + π) − 1 where π is the realised inflation rate over the same period
Well, actually 🧐 — the approximation people use is wrong on purpose

Most retail explainers shortcut this to rreal ≈ rnominal − π. That subtraction is only a first-order Taylor approximation of the Fisher equation above. It’s fine when both rates are small, but breaks down precisely when it matters most — in high-inflation regimes.

Example: at 12.5% nominal and 10% inflation, the approximation says 2.5% real. The exact Fisher answer is (1.125 ÷ 1.10) − 1 = 2.27%. A 10% gap in the result. Stack that across two decades and you’ve lied to yourself about the terminal wealth.

III.

III. The Starbucks Test: Why Purchasing Power Is the Only Benchmark

Abstract percentage returns are hard to feel. So consider a concrete example that makes the concept visceral.

Walk into a Starbucks in the UAE and order an espresso. You will pay about 16 AED, which converts to roughly 376 INR. Walk into a Starbucks in India and order the same espresso – you will pay about 250 INR. But adjust for the 30% effective tax differential between the two jurisdictions, and the cost comes out to roughly the same.

This is not a coincidence. In a globally connected world, the prices of comparable-quality goods converge across nations. The supply chain efficiencies, the arbitrage by multinational companies, the simple reality that raw materials trade on global markets in dollars – all of this means that your “cheap rupee” buys you less and less of the same quality of life over time.

Your wealth is not the number in your demat account. Your wealth is what that number can buy you – how many years of retirement, how many international trips, what quality of healthcare, what calibre of education for your children.

Consider a more extreme illustration. You might be worth 100 crores in India today. If your actual inflation rate is 10-12% (which it easily is in the segment of people who invest and consume quality goods), and your ability to generate returns is 5% (the after-tax return on an FD), you are losing 5-7% of your purchasing power every year. At 7% annual erosion, your 100 crores of purchasing power gets cut in half in 10 years. The number in your bank account might still say 100 crores, or even 150 crores, but what you can buy with it has been gutted.

This is not scaremongering. It is arithmetic.

The practical question becomes: what is the real hurdle rate? Not the nominal return you need, but the actual post-tax, post-inflation, currency-adjusted return you need to genuinely grow your purchasing power?

The answer is that you need to beat 7-8% dollar inflation in the world, post taxes. That is your benchmark. Not the Nifty 50. Not your neighbour’s portfolio. Not your mutual fund’s 3-star rating. The question is starkly simple: what is your strategy to beat 7-8% real global inflation after all costs?

IV.

IV. The High-Inflation World: Why the Problem Is Getting Worse

Before we look at how Indian equity returns have decelerated, it is critical to understand that the inflation side of the equation is accelerating. We are moving into a structurally high-inflation world, and the forces driving it are not going away.

Professor Damodaran of NYU Stern made an observation that cuts to the heart of this: interest rates in the economy go up when expected inflation goes up. If you are putting your money in FDs at 5-6%, and the expected inflation is higher than that, you should not be happy with 5-6%. You should demand a higher return. But in practice, the central banks are pursuing a conflicting mandate – they want growth, which means keeping interest rates as low as possible, while inflation keeps pulling rates higher. Something does not add up.

Here is what is happening under the hood:

  • The Fed keeps interest rates low to increase money supply and velocity in the economy.
  • Once that money supply (M1) is increased, it translates into demand for higher yields, because investors expect inflation to rise.
  • This creates a massive breakdown in the bond market.
  • The traditional inverse relationship between equities and bonds – where one acted as a hedge against the other – is now broken. Both can fall simultaneously.

The old portfolio construction playbook of 60% equities and 40% bonds no longer provides the safety it once did[2].

The practical implications hit you directly. Governments worldwide are pursuing “growth” through easy money, but this growth is not real – it is liquidity-driven. Assets go up 10-15% per year in nominal terms, but inflation in countries like India runs at 9-10% for the investing class. The real growth of wealth is 2-3% for people who invest, and outright negative for those who do not. People who put their money in PF, PPF, and EPF – instruments that were designed as long-term savings vehicles – will suffer badly due to super-high inflation and long lock-in periods that prevent them from redeploying capital.

The cost-of-living crisis is already here. People are witnessing it in every area of their lives – housing, food, services, transportation. But the official inflation numbers do not capture it because governments are redesigning their inflation baskets and underreporting the true figures. The gap between measured inflation and experienced inflation is itself a form of hidden taxation.

In simple terms: the inflation you need to beat is not the 5-6% that the CPI says. It is the 9-12% that you actually experience. And that difference – the 3-6% gap between official and real inflation – is silently destroying the purchasing power of every investor who measures performance in nominal terms.

In this high-inflation world, the 30-year Treasury yield tells its own story. When it spikes, it means investors are demanding higher returns for holding US debt. A sudden spike typically means a counterparty might be selling – for example, China selling US bonds to teach the US a lesson about who the big player really is. To maintain demand, higher yields must be offered. This needs to be read alongside the DXY (dollar index), which indicates the strength of USD relative to other global currencies. DXY getting weaker means USD is getting cheaper, and long-term investors may be moving out of bonds entirely.

The implications cascade through everything:

  • International investors might buy more US-denominated assets as USD gets cheaper, making those assets more lucrative.
  • The demand for treasuries might be falling in the long term. People do not wish to hold bonds for now.
  • The ERP (equity risk premium) goes higher as bond yields rise, making the stock market a higher-risk, higher-reward game.

In simple terms: US markets that used to deliver 10% CAGR with 50 units of volatility are now likely to deliver 10.5% CAGR with 80 units of volatility. The return is marginally higher because inflation itself is higher, but the ride is significantly rougher. And the old hedge – bonds going up when stocks go down – no longer works. Both can fall simultaneously, leaving you with nowhere to hide except hard assets.

This is the macro backdrop every investor faces. The question is no longer whether inflation will erode your returns. It will. The question is how fast, and what you are doing about it.

V.

V. The Nifty Deceleration: Why History Will Not Repeat

The problem becomes even more acute when you look at the structural trajectory of the Indian market. The popular narrative – invest in the Nifty for 20 years, you will be rich – relies on an implicit assumption that the next 20 years will look like the last 20. The data suggests otherwise.

Break the Nifty 50 into phases:

  • 1999 to 2007: The Nifty went from 1,000 to approximately 5,185. A 5x return in roughly 9 years. This was the golden period – India was opening up, PE expansion was enormous, fresh capital was pouring in from FIIs discovering India for the first time.

  • 2010 to 2019: After recovery from the 2008 crash, the Nifty went from roughly 6,000 to 12,500. A 2x return. On a CAGR basis, this is 8-8.5% pre-tax. In dollar-adjusted terms, Indian markets gave approximately 2% cumulative appreciation across this entire decade. That is not 2% per year. That is 2% total.

  • October 2021 to February 2025: The Nifty has given 19% nominal returns. In that same period, INR has eroded by 18%. So the effective net gain over four years is 1%. That is a 0.23% CAGR in dollar terms.

The pattern is unmistakable: each successive phase has delivered lower real returns than the previous one. The market is maturing. The easy PE expansion from a low base is done. The demographic dividend narrative is priced in. The structural advantages that made Indian equities a screaming buy in 2003 no longer exist in the same magnitude.

Mutual fund houses mix the data from all these phases together and tell you to invest for 20 years and expect magical compounding. This is what historians call a historian’s fallacy – the assumption that because something happened in the past under a specific set of conditions, it will happen again under different conditions. Almost 99% of investors in India are making this mistake.

Now add the tax trajectory on top of decelerating returns. LTCG was 0% before 2018, then 10%, now 12.5%. In the last 5 years NIFTY has gone up by 125%, but usually this should be around 100%, so therefore the investments you make now are unlikely to generate more than 11% CAGR on a historic basis. The government cultivates an asset class first, promotes it through “SIP karo” advertisements, gets broad adoption, and then starts extracting its pound of flesh. Where do you see LTCG in 2030? In 2035?

If returns are decelerating and taxes are escalating, the real return for the next 20 years could be significantly worse than the last 20. And the small/mid cap investors who chased growth? They have witnessed 40-50% corrections that have wiped years of hard work. In real terms, adjusted for currency and inflation, these investors have experienced genuine, deep wealth destruction.

Here is another way to see the problem. People look at “value” stocks like HUL and think they are being conservative. But even value stocks in India are unlikely to generate more than 15% CAGR. For people taking market risk, these are low returns. Growth stocks like CDSL, Angel One, Motilal Oswal, Jio Finance, and IDFC First Bank – these have fallen 2-2.5x compared to the broader market during corrections. They rise fast and fall fast. India has a structural issue with its economy right now, so people are betting less on growth and more on value. But if funds keep buying just value stocks, you will not see more than 15% CAGR. And 15% nominal in INR, after taxes and currency adjustment, barely clears the real-returns hurdle.

The point is not that India is uninvestable. The point is that the easy money era in Indian equities is over. Time in the market is not necessarily better than timing the market – and the data backs this up. If you as an investor do not capture the asymmetric gain phases – the 20% dips, the bear market bottoms – you will not make much money. Brave people make money in the market. Think independently. Look at the data. And do not miss great buying opportunities.

VI.

VI. The FD Trap, the UAE Test, and What Actually Beats Inflation

If the equity market’s real returns are barely positive, what about “safe” assets like fixed deposits? Here the picture is even worse.

Post-tax FD rates in India are approximately 5% for most investors. If inflation in your segment is 11-12% (which it realistically is for middle-class urban Indians when you account for housing, education, healthcare, and lifestyle inflation), you are not earning a safe 5%. You are losing 6-7% of your purchasing power every single year. Your FD is not a savings instrument. It is a wealth destruction machine operating in slow motion.

Now consider the comparison with a genuinely zero-tax jurisdiction. In the UAE, you can get an FD at 4.75% in AED terms. Since AED is pegged to USD, there is no currency depreciation. Adjust for the fact that the INR depreciates 4-5% against the USD annually – that AED-denominated FD effectively yields 9-10% in INR terms. And with 0% capital gains tax in the UAE, this is a genuinely risk-free return of 10.5-11%.

So a completely risk-free instrument in the UAE delivers a higher effective return in INR terms than what most Indian equity investors earn after taking significant market risk. Unless the Indian market generates at least 15-16% nominal returns for sophisticated investors – FIIs, HNIs, NRIs who have the option to deploy capital elsewhere – there is no rational reason for them to accept Indian equity market risk.

This is not a theoretical comparison. It explains exactly why FIIs have been leaving Indian markets in record numbers. They invest in foreign currency, convert to INR, generate returns in the Indian market, and then convert back to their home currency. When the INR is constantly falling, their returns get eaten alive by currency depreciation. The move to increase LTCG from 10% to 12.5% made this worse. When 100 goes to 150 and you pay 12.5% on the 50 gain, it is somewhat tolerable. But when 100 goes to 110, and you pay 12.5% on the 10 gain, the total post-tax, post-currency return looks absurd for the risk taken.

India’s Provident Fund tells the same story. PF rates in 2001 were 8.5-9%. Now they sit at 8.1% despite record inflation. In real terms, these are not assets anymore. You are actively losing wealth by investing in them.

Now look at what actually generates real returns globally. Consider the QQQ (Nasdaq 100 ETF):

  • Last 5 years CAGR: 12.95% (in USD)
  • Last 10 years CAGR: 19%
  • Last 15 years CAGR: 19.5%
  • Last 30 years CAGR: 13.97%

These returns are in USD. There is no currency depreciation to subtract. If you had invested 100,000 USD equally weighted across Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, and Netflix ten years ago, that portfolio would have grown to approximately 561,000 USD. That is a CAGR of 19% in dollar terms, or roughly 23-24% in INR terms. No currency headwind – you actually benefit from INR depreciation on the translation back.

Meta alone tells a compelling story. Here is the EPS CAGR:

  • Last 3-year EPS CAGR: 20.7% per year
  • Last 5-year EPS CAGR: 30.6% per year
  • Last 10-year EPS CAGR: 36.2% per year

In India, we keep looking for 20% CAGR opportunities in INR terms. But when a monopoly stock gives 25% CAGR in INR terms, we are too scared to invest.

The structural reason for this gap is simple. Real returns come from whatever adds to real GDP growth. Tech firms drive GDP forward – that is not speculation, it is measurable. The hyperscaler capex spending is funded from free cash flows, not speculative capital raises. Compare this with Indian manufacturing: a firm borrows at 8-9%, GDP grows at 6%, the firm generates 9-10% CAGR. What is the real growth? Close to nothing. The cost of capital is almost as high as the nominal return.

And here is the uncomfortable part. The US has monopoly tech platforms – Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Apple, NVIDIA – that generate operating margins of 28-61%, grow earnings at 20-40% CAGR over long periods, and trade in a hard currency. India has no equivalent growth assets at that scale. India is not meaningfully participating in AI. India does not have firms generating those kinds of margins or growth rates. The US creates intellectual property that scales at near-zero marginal cost. India creates goods and services that require linear inputs of capital and labour. The returns follow accordingly.

This does not mean Indian equities are useless. It means they serve a different purpose in a portfolio. India is for trading and capital rotation. The US is for long-term compounding. Treating Indian equities as a buy-and-hold, 20-year SIP machine is a strategy that has already stopped working in real terms.

VII.

VII. Real Returns in Practice: How the Math Changes Every Decision

Understanding real returns is not just an intellectual exercise. It fundamentally changes how you make every investment decision. Let me walk you through several practical applications.

Why 1% on large capital is not small. Many investors scoff at making 1-2% returns on a trade. But consider this: if you buy a large block of the Nifty 50 around Diwali and it goes up 1-1.5% during Muhurat Trading in three days, that is an annualized return of over 200%. I put approximately 8 crores into a Nifty 50 trade, made 2.24% in a month – that generated roughly 16 lakhs in absolute terms on a highly safe trade. The annualized CAGR on that capital deployment was 25%+. That 16 lakhs of profit then gets rotated into riskier, higher-return assets like BTC. This is how real returns compound: not through headline-grabbing percentage gains, but through deploying large capital at safe, modest returns and then rotating the profits into asymmetric opportunities.

Why the SGB lesson matters. The government issued Sovereign Gold Bonds, locking people into long-term debt instruments. When major SGB liabilities started maturing from end of 2023, the government reduced import duty from 15% to 6%, artificially increasing gold supply and crashing prices. This killed returns by at least 9% for SGB buyers at precisely the worst time – maturity. If you had physical gold, you had the option of booking profits earlier. The lesson? Long lock-in periods are a real-returns killer, not just because of inflation erosion, but because you lose the optionality to act when the government changes the rules on you.

Why commission drag is a real-returns problem. Mutual fund houses have started increasing TER (total expense ratios) by as much as 10%. Here is the math on what a 1% differential in commissions does over 40 years:

  • 25K SIP at 12.5% average returns = Total corpus of 28.2 Crores
  • Same SIP at 11.5% (after 1% commissions) = Total corpus of 21.26 Crores
  • Portfolio difference: 32.6%

A 1% annual fee difference destroys almost a third of your terminal wealth. When your real returns are already thin – maybe 2-3% after taxes, inflation, and currency – a 1% commission drag wipes out a third to half of your actual wealth creation. This is why I keep saying: avoid large-cap active mutual funds. Statistically 85%+ of them do not beat even Nifty 50, and they charge the highest commissions for the privilege of underperformance.

Why tranche buying exists. If your real return target requires a specific entry price, going all-in at one price level is a gamble. Start with 33-40% of your intended position, add at the Bollinger Band mean, add more at the next technical support. Every rupee of better entry price flows directly to your real return. When buying a stock at current levels, the approach should be: 4-5% of portfolio initially, increase to 7-7.5% if it falls to key support, max out at 10% only at deeply discounted levels. This might take months. The patience is the strategy.

Why you must separate trading markets from compounding markets. India is a capital rotation market. It is not a buy-and-hold market. The data proves this: the Nifty’s real returns over the last decade have been near zero for a buy-and-hold investor. But an active trader who bought the October 2024 dip, made 3-4% on a swing trade, exited at resistance, rotated into the next opportunity – that trader extracted real returns from a market that delivered no real returns to passive holders. India rewards activity, precision, and rotation. The US rewards patience and compounding. Confusing the two is one of the most expensive mistakes an Indian investor can make.

VIII.

VIII. Building a Real-Returns Portfolio: The Practical Framework

Once you accept the real-returns framework, portfolio construction changes fundamentally. Here is how to think about it in practice.

Start with the hurdle rate. Your minimum acceptable real return is the rate at which global purchasing power inflates – roughly 7-8% in dollar terms. Anything below this and you are losing ground. Your target should be meaningfully above this – 15% real CAGR is a reasonable ambition that, compounded over a decade or two, genuinely builds wealth.

Allocate by function, not by geography alone. Your portfolio should have four functional layers:

1. Long-term compounders (40-60% of portfolio). These are your US tech monopolies – Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, Netflix. They generate 20-40% EPS CAGR over long periods, trade in a hard currency, and benefit from AI. If you had invested 100K USD equal weight across these 4 stocks, the value now would be 561K USD. That is a CAGR of 19% in USD terms, or close to 23-24% in INR terms. Meta at $300K invested, Amazon showing 33%+ EPS growth, Netflix stabilizing subscriber growth, Microsoft’s Azure margins expanding – these are not speculative. These are monopoly businesses with structural moats.

2. Capital rotation trades (20-30% of portfolio). These are your Indian equity positions. India is a trading market, not a compounding market. Pick industries and stocks that grow faster than Nifty 50 – target at least 15% CAGR. Buy cash-rich firms with 15% ROIC, 15% cash flow growth, 15% expansion-oriented growth. Stocks like HDFC Bank, Kotak Mahindra, Angel One, CDSL, CRISIL. The Nifty 50 swing trade – buying a large block during panic, making 2-3% in a month, rotating profits into higher-beta assets – is the template.

3. Hard assets for capital protection (15-25% of portfolio). Real estate in hard-currency jurisdictions, physical gold (not ETFs – they have massive tracking errors, commissions, and expense ratios). One aggressive move has been to build RE to roughly 30% of total portfolio since 2022, now up 40-50%. In a world where fiat currencies are being debased, hard assets provide the floor that financial assets cannot. Bitcoin is evolving into a strategic reserve asset – potentially 10-20% allocation over time as the transition from USD-backed to blockchain-backed reserves accelerates.

4. Asymmetric bets (5-10% of portfolio). Small positions in high-conviction opportunities. BTC as a liquidity-cycle play. Turnaround stocks like private banks, broking firms, or pharma names like Dr Reddy’s where valuations have cooled off but numbers are good – expecting 15% CAGR from these in an average market. Size these so a total loss does not damage your portfolio, but a hit delivers outsized real returns.

Measure everything in real terms. After every quarter, calculate your portfolio’s return net of taxes paid or accrued, adjust for INR depreciation against the dollar, and subtract your estimate of real inflation. If that number is positive, you are actually building wealth. If it is negative or near zero, you need to restructure. The nominal number on your brokerage app is entertainment, not information.

Optimise the tax layer aggressively. When you hold US stocks long-term, you do not pay capital gains tax until you book profits. If you do not sell for 15 years, who knows where you will be a tax resident then? 15 years ago, I was working at a non-profit teaching English to kids. And hated money. But the big bad world made me a capitalist. Point being: if you do not diversify, you will have zero options, even later in life. Buying high-quality US firms that you will not sell for 15 years gives you tax optionality that no Indian FD, PF, or mutual fund can match.

Accept the volatility. In the current regime, equity markets come with higher volatility for modestly higher nominal returns. The equity risk premium is expanding as bond yields rise and the traditional inverse relationship between stocks and bonds breaks down. You need to be psychologically prepared for 10-20% drawdowns on individual positions. If you cannot handle that, you need to accept that your real returns will be lower – possibly negative.

IX.

IX. The Only Question That Matters

Every sophisticated investor, every fund manager who actually compounds wealth over decades, every large-ticket player who has options for where to deploy capital – they all think in real terms. The nominal return is the number you tell yourself. The real return is the number that determines whether your children will have the same quality of life that you had, or worse.

The shift from nominal to real thinking changes everything:

  • It explains why you must diversify globally – because Indian equities alone cannot reliably beat real global inflation. Just simply buy US Index, Indian Index, China Index. You do not even need a 4th asset.

  • It explains why US tech monopolies are not a luxury allocation but a necessity – because they are among the few assets on earth that consistently generate real returns above 10%.

  • It explains why India is a trading market, not a holding market – because the real returns from buy-and-hold Indian equities have been near zero for a decade.

  • It explains why tax optimisation is not aggressive planning but defensive survival – because in a thin real-return environment, a 2% tax drag is the difference between building wealth and treading water.

  • It explains why hard assets matter – because when fiat currencies are being debased, physical assets hold their value. The transition to BTC as a strategic reserve asset, the persistent strength of gold, the reliability of cash-flow-generating real estate – all of these make sense through the real-returns lens.

The mutual fund industry, the financial advisory complex, the SIP marketing machine – none of them want you to think in real terms. Because when you do, the product they are selling looks a lot less attractive. A 12.5% nominal return sounds wonderful. A 0-1% real return for the risk you are taking sounds like a bad deal. And it is.

The investor who understands real returns asks one question, relentlessly, about every investment they make: after taxes, after inflation, after currency adjustment, does this make me richer in real terms? If the answer is no, they pass. If the answer is yes, they size the position according to their conviction and hold with discipline.

There are not enough growth options in the world that can generate more than 10% real growth in dollar terms. That is the hard truth. Finding them, concentrating in them, and protecting the returns through smart tax and currency management – that is the entire game. Everything else is noise.

Here is what I will be doing: going 20% BTC at some stage (currently around 7-8%), buying at least 30% real estate across India, Dubai, and Thailand, investing in US equities and Indian equities, and treating bond investing in India as pointless due to after-tax non-sense returns. People who are doing PF, PPF, EPF will suffer badly. Doing bond investing especially in India is pointless. There is no better time to be asset class diversified. People who do not see this will learn a very harsh lesson in the next few years.

The transition is already underway. Banking is likely to move towards USD stablecoins built on blockchain. BTC becomes the next gold, because it allows easy transmission between stablecoins and a fixed-supply reserve, and it takes away the power from the US to deflate USD at the pace they are doing now. The US is already building a strategic BTC reserve. This is not fringe thinking anymore – this is government policy. Understanding this macro shift is essential to understanding where real returns will come from in the next decade.

Your competition is not with other investors. It is not with the market. It is with inflation. And inflation is undefeated against anyone who does not understand this.

All you need to do is target a 15% CAGR by going to assets like equity, BTC, or whatever you like in a sensible way. Control your cost of living – fix your housing, have the option to migrate to different low-cost-of-living cities or economies. If possible, optimise for taxes. The rest will follow.

I am not writing all this to scare you. I am writing this so that you see the writing on the wall. And start making financially savvy moves. These are not the things people even have the guts to talk about. And I am not trying to push you in any direction. You do whatever you feel is right. My job is to present fact-driven analysis, which most people will not.

To see all this, you need to look at things independently, study finance like a nerd, and act before others do. If you study complicated things, you get a knowledge alpha. And in a world where real returns are thin, that knowledge alpha is the only edge that truly compounds.

  2. The classic 60/40 relied on equity-bond correlation being reliably negative.
