I.

Why is 12.5% nominal a lie you tell yourself?

Every Indian investor knows the number: the Nifty 50 has delivered roughly 12.5% nominal returns over the last 20 years. It is printed on every mutual fund factsheet, repeated in every financial-planning seminar, and embedded in every SIP calculator that promises a yacht in Ibiza on a ₹10,000 monthly investment.

It is also, for all practical purposes, a lie.

Not because the number itself is wrong — the index did compound at about 12.5% in nominal INR. The lie is in what that number omits. It omits taxes. It omits currency depreciation. It omits the relentless erosion of purchasing power that makes a rupee today worth meaningfully less than a rupee a year from now. Strip away the illusions, and the picture changes so dramatically that it forces you to rethink everything about how and where you invest.

Walk through the arithmetic most investors never do. Start with 12.5%. Pay long-term capital gains tax of 12.5% — itself climbed from 0% before 2018[1] — and your return drops to roughly 10.5%. Now adjust for INR depreciation against the dollar (the rupee has consistently lost 3–4% per year for long stretches): your 10.5% becomes about 6.5–7% in dollar terms. Net it against real global dollar inflation in actual costs of goods, services, housing, and education — 7–8% per year on a lived basis — and what remains is somewhere between −1% and 0% real.

The same arithmetic, written out in a single line:

0.125 → ×(1−0.125) = 0.109 → ÷(1+0.04) = 0.0668 → ÷(1+0.075) = −0.008 12.5% nominal → 10.9% post-tax → 6.7% USD → −0.8% real

One arrow per layer. Each layer is a multiply or divide; none of them are subtractions. By the time the chain runs to completion, the number you started with has been transformed beyond recognition. The investor who reads "12.5%" off the brochure and the investor who runs the chain are looking at the same starting input and finishing with completely different answers.

The Indian equity market — celebrated as one of the great wealth-building machines — has barely kept pace with global inflation over the last two decades. Your "wealth" did not grow. Your purchasing power was approximately preserved. That is not the same thing at all.

This is not an abstract point. It is the single most important concept in investing, and every downstream decision — which stocks to buy, which markets to enter, how to size positions, when to trade versus hold — flows directly from understanding it. Your competition is not other investors. It is not the index.

Your competition is with inflation.

The framing matters because it determines what counts as a win. A portfolio that grew 11% in a year where inflation ran at 9% and the rupee dropped 4% has produced a real return that is meaningfully negative once you net out the layers. The headline 11% feels like a small win. The underlying reality is a small loss. The investor who books the win and posts the screenshot has been quietly transferring purchasing power for an entire year to the three counterparties that take their cut without asking.

The rest of this article is the math, the visuals, and the practical framework for thinking about this layer-by-layer. Three interactive widgets, each one designed to push back against a specific kind of magical thinking the financial-services industry has built into its sales narrative. By the end you will not be looking at a return number the same way again — and if you are doing your job, neither will the people you talk to about money.

II.

What does the Fisher equation actually say after taxes and currency?

The term real returns gets thrown around loosely. It has a precise, layered meaning that most retail investors never fully unpack. Real returns are what remains after you subtract three distinct forces that eat into your gains: taxes, inflation, and currency depreciation. Each operates independently. Each compounds against you.

Layer 1: taxes. India's LTCG on equities has followed a clear upward trajectory: 0% before 2018, then 10%, now 12.5%. There is no reason to believe it stops here. Governments cultivate an asset class first, then tax it once adoption is high enough. On a 12.5% gross return, a 12.5% LTCG haircut drops you to about 10.5% post-tax.

Layer 2: currency depreciation. The INR has slipped against the USD at roughly 3–4% per year over long periods. In October 2024 the rupee sat at 84/USD; by early 2025 it had touched 87 — a 3.5% depreciation in a handful of months. India runs persistent current-account deficits, has higher inflation than the US, and the RBI can only slow the drift, not stop it. For anyone thinking about wealth in global terms — and you should, because comparable-quality goods cost roughly the same across nations — currency depreciation is a direct subtraction from your return.

Layer 3: inflation. The most insidious layer, because governments work hardest to obscure it. Official CPI might show 5–6%; the actual cost-of-living increase for the investing class — housing, education, healthcare, travel, dining — runs at 9–12%. Inflation baskets are redesigned, components are reweighted, the headline figure is kept low while people experience a cost-of-living crisis the numbers do not capture.

The mechanics of CPI manipulation are mostly mundane and entirely legal:

  • Hedonic adjustments. A new iPhone is treated as "better" than the previous model, so the headline price is statistically discounted to reflect "quality improvement." The consumer pays the full sticker price; the statistician records only a fraction of the increase.
  • Substitution effects. When beef prices rise, the basket assumes you switch to chicken. The headline inflation number reflects the cheaper basket, not the basket you actually want.
  • Owner-equivalent rent. Instead of tracking home prices (which have run at 8–15% in major Indian metros), CPI tracks a model-derived "imputed rent" based on rental yields. Rentals in India yield 2–3%, badly under-tracking the actual asset-price inflation that the home buyer experiences.
  • Basket reweighting. Roughly every five to seven years, the weights are revised — usually in directions that lower the published rate. Education, healthcare, and housing — the categories that genuinely run hot — tend to get under-weighted.

The combined effect is that official CPI consistently runs two to four percentage points below the inflation an upper-middle-class household actually experiences. Over twenty years, that gap is the difference between a portfolio that doubled in real terms and one that quietly halved.

Stack them and the arithmetic is sobering:

  • Start: 12.5% nominal return
  • After LTCG: ~10.5%
  • After INR depreciation (3–4%): ~6.5–7%
  • After real global inflation (7–8%): −1% to +0%

You did not build wealth. You ran on a treadmill.

The precise relationship between these layers is the Fisher equation — not the shortcut subtraction that most retail explainers use, but the exact compounded form:

rreal = (1 + rnominal) ÷ (1 + π) − 1 where π is the realised inflation rate over the same period
Well, actually 🧐 — the approximation people use is wrong on purpose

Most retail explainers shortcut this to rreal ≈ rnominal − π. That subtraction is only a first-order Taylor approximation of the Fisher equation above. It's fine when both rates are small, but breaks down precisely when it matters most — in high-inflation regimes.

Example: at 12.5% nominal and 10% inflation, the approximation says 2.5% real. The exact Fisher answer is (1.125 ÷ 1.10) − 1 = 2.27%. A 10% gap in the result. Stack that across two decades and you've lied to yourself about the terminal wealth.

Well, actually 🧐 — currency depreciation compounds, not adds

People assume INR-to-USD adjustment is a subtraction. It isn't — it's another divide, exactly like inflation. If equities return rINR and the rupee depreciates at d per year, your USD-equivalent return is (1 + rINR) ÷ (1 + d) − 1, not rINR − d.

At 12.5% INR returns and 4% rupee depreciation, the shortcut gives 8.5% USD; the exact answer is 1.125 ÷ 1.04 − 1 = 8.17%. Small per year. Half a percent of terminal wealth lost per decade if you compound the wrong way.

Once you stop using the approximation and start chaining the three divides — tax, depreciation, inflation — the picture is much darker than the marketing slides ever admit.

The three layers are not independent in the messy real world either. Higher inflation in India tends to widen the rate differential against the US, which accelerates the rupee's depreciation, which forces the RBI to raise rates, which depresses equity valuations, which compresses your gross return. Each input reinforces the others in a quiet feedback loop. The investor who models the layers independently and adds them together is being kind to themselves. The investor who models the joint distribution — where bad inflation surprises arrive bundled with currency shocks and rate hikes — is being honest.

This is also why the Fisher equation is so often taught and so rarely applied. It is easy to write down. It is easy to forget. The investor who reaches for a calculator the moment they hear "8% return" and asks against what inflation, against what tax, against what currency drift is doing the actual job of an investor. Everyone else is reading a number off a screen.

Well, actually 🧐 — the post-tax line is a simplification

Widget 1 applies the tax rate as a continuous drag on the annual return: r × (1 − tax). In reality, capital-gains tax is paid only when you realise the gain, not annually. A buy-and-hold position with deferred LTCG compounds slightly faster than the widget shows.

The simplification is conservative for buy-and-hold and accurate for active rotation strategies where positions turn over frequently. For most retail investors with a mix of holding and rotation, the widget's continuous-tax assumption is within 0.3–0.5% of the realistic answer over 30 years — small compared to the inflation and currency effects it visualises.

Pay close attention to how non-linearly small numbers compound here. Move inflation from 7% to 8% — a single percentage point — and the terminal real wealth at year 30 falls by roughly a quarter. Add another point of tax drag and the runway extends by years. This is why people who say "inflation is only 6%, not the end of the world" do not understand inflation. The end of the world is what happens when you stretch a one-point gap across thirty years.

The red shaded band between the nominal line and the real line is the gap you do not get to spend. It is the wealth that exists on the brokerage app and does not exist anywhere else. The bigger that band, the more your portfolio has been transferred from you to the three layers above. Tax, currency, inflation. Three counterparties you did not choose, all collecting from the same pot.

IV.

Why is purchasing-power parity the only honest benchmark?

Abstract percentage returns are hard to feel. The Starbucks test makes them concrete.

Walk into a Starbucks in the UAE and order an espresso. You will pay about 16 AED, which converts to roughly ₹376. Walk into a Starbucks in India and order the same espresso — you will pay about ₹250. Adjust for the roughly 30% effective tax differential between jurisdictions and the post-tax cost comes out within a few percent of each other. The drink is the same. The bean is the same. The barista's wage differs, the rent differs, but the global-arbitrage layer pulls the all-in price toward a single line.

This is not a coincidence. In a globally connected world, the prices of comparable-quality goods converge across nations. Supply-chain efficiencies, multinational arbitrage, raw materials trading in dollars on global markets — all of this means a "cheap rupee" buys less and less of the same quality of life over time. The exchange rate is not just a number; it is the discount your savings take when measured in goods anyone in the world can buy.

Your wealth is not the number in your demat account. Your wealth is what that number can buy — how many years of retirement, how many international trips, what quality of healthcare, what calibre of education for your children.

Consider a more extreme illustration. You might be worth ₹100 crores in India today. If actual experienced inflation is 10–12% (which it easily is in the segment of people who invest and consume quality goods), and your ability to generate post-tax returns is 5% (an FD), you are losing 5–7% of purchasing power every year. At 7% annual erosion, your ₹100 crores of purchasing power gets cut in half in ten years. The number in your bank account might still say ₹100 crores, or even ₹150 crores. What you can buy with it has been gutted.

This is not scaremongering. It is arithmetic.

The practical question becomes: what is the real hurdle rate? Not the nominal return you need, but the actual post-tax, post-inflation, currency-adjusted return required to genuinely grow purchasing power?

The answer is that you need to beat 7–8% real global dollar inflation, post taxes. That is your benchmark. Not the Nifty 50. Not your neighbour's portfolio. Not your mutual fund's 3-star rating. The question is starkly simple: what is your strategy to beat 7–8% real global inflation after all costs? If you cannot answer that, the rest is theatre.

The Big Mac index, the iPhone index, the Toyota Camry index — pick whichever consumer good you actually buy. Every one of them tells the same story: across reasonably-developed economies, the all-in cost (taxed, shipped, supported, financed) converges within a band of 10–20%. The wedge between countries is mostly explained by tax regime, labour cost in the local service layer, and shipping friction. Once you account for those three, the price of comparable quality is roughly the same in Bombay, Bangkok, and Berlin. The investor who treats ₹1 in Mumbai as functionally identical to 0.012 USD in Dallas is in for a surprise the first time they need a heart-bypass surgery or a graduate degree.

This is the deepest reason indexing your wealth to a single currency is a trap. The currency is a unit of measure, not a unit of value. The value is the basket of things the unit can buy. When the basket and the unit float apart — which is what currency depreciation is — you lose value silently, even as the unit count rises.

A few concrete cross-jurisdiction price anchors, post-tax, that illustrate the parity:

  • A new iPhone Pro: roughly ₹1.4 L in India, $1,000+sales-tax in the US (~₹90K at 90/USD), 4,500 AED in the UAE (~₹1.05 L). Within 30% even before adjusting for income-tax differential.
  • A Toyota Camry equivalent: ₹48–50 L in India, $30–35K in the US, similar in the UAE. The Indian price is meaningfully higher due to import duty.
  • An MBA from a top-30 US business school: $150K all-in over two years — about ₹1.35 Cr. The same education, attempted at the equivalent Indian institution, is dramatically cheaper but also gives access to a smaller career-outcome distribution.
  • A heart bypass: $100K in the US, $15–20K in India for comparable-quality private hospital. This is the rare category where India is genuinely cheaper.

The takeaway: India is cheaper for labour-intensive services (healthcare, domestic help, education at the lower end), neutral for globally-traded goods, and dramatically more expensive on a tax-equivalent basis for high-end luxury and imports. The blend of inflation any individual experiences depends entirely on which side of that mix their consumption sits on.

V.

Why won't the next twenty years of Nifty look like the last twenty?

The popular narrative — invest in the Nifty for 20 years, you'll be rich — relies on an implicit assumption that the next 20 years will look like the last 20. The data suggests otherwise.

Break the Nifty 50 into phases:

  • 1999–2007: from 1,000 to roughly 5,185. A 5× return in nine years. The golden period — India opening up, PE expansion, fresh FII capital discovering the market.
  • 2010–2019: from roughly 6,000 to 12,500. A 2× return; about 8–8.5% pre-tax CAGR. In dollar-adjusted terms, Indian markets gave approximately 2% cumulative appreciation across the entire decade.
  • Oct 2021 – Feb 2025: Nifty up 19% nominal. INR down 18% in the same window. Effective net dollar gain across four years: 1%. That is a 0.23% CAGR.

The pattern is unmistakable: each successive phase has delivered lower real returns than the previous one. The market is maturing. The easy PE expansion from a low base is done. The demographic-dividend narrative is priced in. The structural advantages that made Indian equities a screaming buy in 2003 no longer exist in the same magnitude.

Mutual fund houses mix data from all these phases together and tell you to invest for 20 years and expect magical compounding. This is what historians call a historian's fallacy — assuming that because something happened under a specific set of conditions, it will happen again under different conditions. Almost every retail investor in India is making this mistake.

Three forces drove the 1999–2007 boom that no longer apply at remotely the same magnitude:

  • Foreign-capital discovery. India was effectively closed to global equity flows until liberalisation. Once the gates opened, foreign institutional buying drove a one-time rerating that simply cannot repeat. The marginal foreign buyer of Indian equity in 2025 already owns plenty.
  • PE expansion off a low base. The Nifty was trading at single-digit forward multiples in 2002. Multiples doubled and tripled. Today's starting multiple is 22–24×, leaving no obvious room for that mechanic again.
  • Demographic-dividend optimism. The narrative that a young workforce would drive sustained 8–10% real GDP growth got priced in fully by 2019. Actual delivered real GDP has tracked closer to 6%, and inflation has eaten the rest.

None of these three forces is coming back. If you are extrapolating from 1999–2007 you are extrapolating from a once-in-a-generation regime change that has already happened. The next twenty years cannot, by construction, contain another opening of the same gates.

Now layer on the tax trajectory. LTCG was 0% before 2018, then 10%, now 12.5%. In the last five years Nifty went up 125%; usually that range is closer to 100%, so the investments you make now are unlikely to generate more than 11% CAGR on a historic-base-rate basis. The government cultivates an asset class first, gets broad adoption, then starts extracting. Where will LTCG be in 2030? In 2035?

Add in the fact that the broader macro plumbing has changed. The classic 60/40 portfolio relied on equity-bond correlation being reliably negative — bonds going up when stocks fell. From 2022 onward that correlation flipped positive in most of the developed world[2]. The old hedge no longer hedges. The same regime change is happening in Indian credit: bonds and equities now sell off together in stress windows, and the diversification you thought you had does not exist.

If returns are decelerating, taxes are escalating, and the diversifying assets have stopped diversifying, the real return for the next 20 years could be significantly worse than the last 20. The small/mid-cap investors who chased growth? They have witnessed 40–50% corrections that have wiped years of work. In real terms, adjusted for currency and inflation, these investors have experienced genuine, deep wealth destruction — even when their nominal P&L looks vaguely positive.

Well, actually 🧐 — the small-cap "premium" is largely an illusion in real terms

The historical academic argument for owning small caps is the "size factor" — the long-run finding that smaller companies have outperformed larger ones on average, by roughly 2–3% per year in US data. Multiple papers since 2010 have questioned whether this premium still exists after fees, transaction costs, and survivorship bias.

In Indian small-cap data, the surface-level outperformance versus Nifty is real, but it is achieved through volatility that materially erodes the geometric mean. A series that returns +60% then −40% has the same arithmetic mean as one that returns +20% each year, but the terminal wealth is dramatically lower. Indian small-caps deliver that asymmetry: cheerful arithmetic means, brutal geometric means. The real-returns investor wants the second number, not the first.

The point is not that India is uninvestable. The point is that the easy-money era in Indian equities is over. Time in the market is not necessarily better than timing the market — the data backs this up. India rewards activity and rotation. Treating it as a buy-and-hold compounding machine is a strategy that has already stopped working.

One way to see why the deceleration is structural rather than cyclical: the Nifty's P/E expansion has done most of the heavy lifting in every previous phase. From 1999 to 2007, multiples expanded from roughly 10× to 25×. That is a 2.5× tailwind on top of whatever the earnings did. From 2010 to 2019, multiples drifted from 22× to 27× — a much smaller boost. Today, the Nifty trades around 22–24× earnings, which is roughly where developed markets sit. There is no rerating runway left. Future returns must come from earnings growth alone, which is far harder to engineer than multiple expansion.

Compare that with the US, where the same mechanic still has room. The S&P 500 trades around 22× trailing, but the Nasdaq 100 has the additional kicker of monopoly tech businesses with 25–35% earnings growth coming through. Even at static multiples, those earnings double a portfolio every three to four years. India's headline earnings growth has been in the 8–12% range for a decade now — a number that takes eight to ten years just to double, before you net out any of the three layers above.

VI.

Why is your FD a slow wealth-destruction machine?

If equity real returns are barely positive, what about "safe" assets like fixed deposits? The picture is worse.

Post-tax FD rates in India are approximately 5% for most investors. If experienced inflation is 11–12% (realistic for middle-class urban India once you include housing, education, healthcare, and lifestyle), you are not earning a safe 5%. You are losing 6–7% of purchasing power every year. The FD is not a savings instrument. It is a wealth-destruction machine operating in slow motion.

The FD's marketing has been so effective that most Indian families still think of it as the "safe" anchor in a portfolio. Decompose what that "safety" actually means:

  • Nominal safety: the rupee number cannot go down. True. Largely meaningless.
  • Real safety: the purchasing power of the deposit. Falling at 6–7% per year for a typical urban investor.
  • Optionality safety: can you redeploy when an opportunity comes? Mostly no — FD lock-ins or early-withdrawal penalties bite hard exactly when you want to act on a market dislocation.
  • Counterparty safety: the bank cannot fail. Mostly true, but DICGC insurance caps at ₹5 lakh per depositor per bank — a number that has not been revised in line with inflation either.

The only thing the FD is safe from is the headline-number drop. It is unsafe in every other dimension that matters to a real-returns investor.

Now look at the genuinely zero-tax jurisdictions. In the UAE you can get an FD at 4.75% in AED, and AED is pegged to USD — no currency depreciation. Adjust for the fact that the INR depreciates 4–5% against the USD annually, and that AED-denominated FD effectively yields 9–10% in INR terms. A completely risk-free instrument in the UAE delivers a higher effective return in INR terms than what most Indian equity investors earn after taking significant market risk.

Unless the Indian market generates at least 15–16% nominal returns for sophisticated investors — FIIs, HNIs, NRIs who can deploy capital elsewhere — there is no rational reason for them to accept Indian equity market risk. This is not theory. It is exactly why FIIs have been leaving Indian markets in record numbers since LTCG rose from 10% to 12.5%. When 100 goes to 150 and you pay 12.5% on the 50 gain, it is tolerable. When 100 goes to 110, and you pay 12.5% on the 10 gain plus eat 18% currency depreciation, the post-tax, post-currency return looks absurd for the risk taken.

Well, actually 🧐 — the AED peg isn't free

The UAE pegs AED to USD at 3.6725. That peg is maintained by the central bank holding large USD reserves and standing ready to defend the rate. The country can afford this only because oil revenues throw off persistent USD inflows. Pegs that look solid for decades have broken when the underlying current-account surplus disappears — Argentina 2001, Switzerland 2015. The AED peg is not a law of nature; it is a policy choice underwritten by oil.

The practical implication is mild — the UAE has effectively unlimited reserves and the peg has held since 1997 — but it is the only honest reason to size positions sensibly rather than treating AED as literally USD.

India's Provident Fund tells the same story in slow motion. PF rates were 8.5–9% in 2001. Today they sit at 8.1% despite record inflation. In real terms, these are not assets anymore. You are actively losing wealth by holding them. The instruments designed as long-term savings have become long-term lock-ins on instruments that no longer beat inflation.

Hold that metric across instruments and rank them. Most retail investors have never produced this ranking. They have a vague sense that equities beat FDs and that NRIs do better, but no concrete number.

Two more things the widget makes visible that the brochure never does:

  • Below-principal bars are flagged with a "↓ destroyed" marker. If the bar is shorter than the dashed principal line, the instrument has not preserved purchasing power. This is what wealth destruction looks like on a chart: the asset still exists in nominal terms, but in real terms it is smaller than it started.
  • The relative ranking is stable across realistic inflation assumptions. Slide inflation from 5% to 12%. The order of the bars rarely changes. Indian FD stays last; US tech stays first. What changes is the magnitude of the gap and whether the middle two cross the principal line. The hierarchy is structural, not parameter-dependent.

The relative ranking of common Indian-resident options, sorted by 20-year real outcome at realistic inflation:

  • Worst: savings-account balance, EPF, PPF, traditional endowment insurance — all running well below experienced inflation, all locked in or behaviourally sticky.
  • Bad: bank FDs, NSC, Kisan Vikas Patra — the headline rate barely matches official CPI, let alone experienced inflation.
  • Marginal: Indian large-cap mutual funds, Nifty 50 index funds — real returns hovering near zero after the four-layer stack.
  • Decent: selectively-rotated Indian mid-caps, gold, Indian REITs with hard-asset cash flow — real returns positive but volatile, requires active management.
  • Best: US equity (via direct overseas or LRS-routed broker), US tech ETFs, Bitcoin, real estate in hard-currency jurisdictions, UAE/Singapore deposits — the only categories with consistent positive real returns at scale.

Now look at what actually generates real returns globally. The QQQ (Nasdaq 100 ETF) has delivered roughly 13% CAGR over the last five years in USD, 19% over ten, 19.5% over fifteen, and 14% over thirty — in USD, with no currency depreciation to subtract. A $100,000 investment ten years ago split equally across Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, and Netflix would now be approximately $561,000[3]. That is roughly 19% CAGR in dollars and 23–24% in INR terms once you add the depreciation tailwind.

Eleven percent real, post-tax, post-inflation, post-currency. Even after the four-layer haircut, a US-tech-ETF exposure clears the 7–8% real-inflation hurdle by a clear margin. Almost no Indian-equity instrument can match that math.

The structural reason for the QQQ-Nifty gap is simple. Real returns come from whatever adds to real GDP growth in the underlying economy. Tech firms drive GDP forward — that is not speculation, it is measurable. The hyperscaler capex spending is funded from free cash flow, not speculative capital raises. Compare with Indian manufacturing: a firm borrows at 8–9%, GDP grows at 6%, the firm generates 9–10% nominal CAGR. The real growth is close to zero because the cost of capital is almost as high as the nominal return. India creates goods and services that require linear inputs of capital and labour. The US creates intellectual property that scales at near-zero marginal cost. The returns follow accordingly.

This does not mean Indian equities are useless. It means they serve a different purpose in a portfolio. India is for trading and capital rotation — the next section gets concrete about what that looks like in practice. The US is for long-term compounding. Treating Indian equities as a buy-and-hold 20-year SIP machine is a strategy that has already stopped working in real terms, regardless of what the brochures say.

VII.

How does this math change every portfolio decision?

Understanding real returns is not just an intellectual exercise. It changes every decision you make.

Why 1% on large capital is not small. Many investors scoff at 1–2% returns on a trade. Yet a 2% move on a Nifty 50 block held for three days around Muhurat trading annualises to over 200%. Eight crores into a Nifty 50 swing, 2.24% in a month, ₹16 lakh absolute on a near-risk-free trade. Annualised CAGR on the capital: 25%+. That ₹16 lakh then rotates into riskier, higher-return assets. This is how real returns compound — not through headline-grabbing percentage gains, but through deploying large capital at safe, modest returns and rotating the profits into asymmetric opportunities.

The mathematical reason this works is that annualised return scales by (1 + rperiod)365/days − 1. A 2% gain held three days is not "2%." It is roughly (1.02)121.7 − 1 ≈ 1057% annualised — though no one actually compounds that fast in practice because of the cost of holding capital between opportunities. The honest version is: a 2% gain in three days at risk-controlled exposure, repeated four to six times a year, beats most "good" stock picks because the holding period is short and the capital recycles into the next high-conviction setup.

Why commission drag is a real-returns problem. Mutual fund houses have started increasing TER by as much as 10%. Run the math on what a 1% differential does over 40 years:

A 1% annual fee difference destroys almost a third of your terminal wealth. When real returns are thin — maybe 2–3% after taxes, inflation, and currency — a 1% commission drag wipes out a third to half of actual wealth creation. Statistically, 85%+ of large-cap active mutual funds do not beat the Nifty 50 — and they charge the highest commissions for the privilege of underperformance.

The hierarchy of fund-fee efficiency for an Indian retail investor, cheapest to most expensive:

  • Direct-equity (broker brokerage only): roughly 0.03–0.10% all-in if you use a discount broker. The cheapest path, requires the most attention.
  • Index funds and ETFs (direct plan): 0.10–0.30% expense ratio. Cheap, broadly diversified, no manager risk.
  • Index funds (regular plan via distributor): 0.40–0.70%. The same product, with the distributor commission added back. Avoid.
  • Active large-cap funds (direct): 1.0–1.5%. Hard to justify given the 85% underperformance rate.
  • Active mid/small-cap (regular): 2.0–2.5%. The most expensive category, with the most adverse selection.
  • Unit-linked insurance plans (ULIPs): total cost easily exceeds 3.5% in early years. Marketed as "insurance plus investment." Mathematically dominated by buying term insurance and an index fund separately.

Why tranche buying exists. If your real-return target requires a specific entry price, going all-in at one level is a gamble. Start with 33–40% of intended position, add at the Bollinger Band mean, add more at the next technical support. Every rupee of better entry price flows directly to real return. When buying a stock at current levels, the approach should be: 4–5% of portfolio initially, increase to 7–7.5% if it falls to key support, max out at 10% only at deeply discounted levels. This might take months. The patience is the strategy.

Why you must separate trading markets from compounding markets. India is a capital-rotation market. The Nifty's real returns over the last decade have been near zero for a buy-and-hold investor. But an active trader who bought the October 2024 dip, made 3–4% on a swing, exited at resistance, rotated into the next opportunity — that trader extracted real returns from a market that delivered none to passive holders. India rewards activity, precision, and rotation. The US rewards patience and compounding. Confusing the two is one of the most expensive mistakes an Indian investor can make.

Why long lock-ins are lethal. When the government issued Sovereign Gold Bonds, then reduced import duty from 15% to 6% as major maturities approached in late 2023, the artificial supply increase crashed prices precisely at maturity. SGB buyers lost at least 9% of return at the worst possible moment. Physical gold holders had optionality — they could book earlier or hold. The lesson generalises: long lock-ins are a real-return killer not just because of inflation erosion, but because you lose the optionality to act when the rules change on you.

The hierarchy of lock-in cost, in real-returns terms:

  • Lowest cost (highest optionality): direct equity in a daily-liquid market — you can rotate any morning the market is open.
  • Low cost: ETFs, index funds, gold ETFs — trading day liquidity, modest transaction friction.
  • Medium cost: tax-saving instruments (ELSS) with 3-year lock-ins, debt funds with exit-load windows. Tolerable if the post-tax rate beats the alternative.
  • High cost: SGBs, PPF, EPF, NPS — multi-year to multi-decade locks on instruments whose rules can be changed mid-flight.
  • Highest cost: insurance-linked investments, traditional endowment policies, real estate that takes 6–18 months to exit. You can pay this cost only if the underlying instrument has dramatically better economics — which most do not.

Why concentration beats diversification past a point. Modern portfolio theory teaches diversification reduces risk — and at the index level, it does. But once you hold 30+ names, additional diversification reduces idiosyncratic risk by less than 1% per added position while diluting the return concentration that creates real alpha. The investors who genuinely compound at 15–20% real do not own 200 stocks. They own 8 to 15 names with thesis-level conviction. The "diversify across 100 stocks" advice is risk-management theatre for portfolios that were never going to beat inflation in the first place. The relevant question is not "how many stocks" but "how many businesses do I actually understand well enough to size sensibly?"

Well, actually 🧐 — diversification has a measurable saturation curve

The textbook number for portfolio risk reduction is from Evans & Archer (1968): single-stock idiosyncratic risk is reduced by roughly 80% with about 15–20 stocks, 90% with 30, and the marginal benefit becomes negligible past 50. The benefit is the variance of returns; it is not the same as the total expected return.

What concentration buys you is the ability to size winners hard enough that they actually move the portfolio. A 0.5% position in a 10× bagger adds 4.5% to the portfolio — nice, but unlikely to change your life. A 5% position in the same name adds 45% — transformative. Same stock pick, totally different portfolio outcome, driven entirely by sizing.

Why dividends are a tax-inefficiency, not a feature. A high-dividend portfolio looks attractive because the cash flow is visible. But dividends are taxed annually at marginal rates, forcing a continuous tax leak from the compounding machine. The same total return delivered through buyback-driven capital appreciation defers tax until you choose to sell — potentially in a different jurisdiction, a different decade, or never. For real-return purposes, the dividend payer is almost always strictly worse than the buyback equivalent at the same gross yield. Indian dividend yields, taxed as income, are particularly painful.

VIII.

What does a real-returns portfolio actually look like?

Once you accept the real-returns framework, portfolio construction changes fundamentally.

Start with the hurdle rate. Your minimum acceptable real return is the rate at which global purchasing power inflates — roughly 7–8% in dollar terms. Anything below that and you are losing ground. Target meaningfully above — 15% real CAGR — and compounded over a decade or two, that genuinely builds wealth.

Look at how a balanced real-returns portfolio actually splits, at a representative ₹5 crore base:

~₹2.5 Cr

~₹1.25 Cr
India rotation book (25%)
~₹1 Cr

~₹25 L
Asymmetric bets (5%)

Allocate by function, not by geography. Four functional layers:

1. Long-term compounders (40–60% of portfolio). US tech monopolies — Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, Netflix, NVIDIA, Google. 20–40% EPS CAGR over long periods, hard-currency denomination, structural moats. Operating margins of 28–61%, intellectual property that scales at near-zero marginal cost.

2. Capital-rotation trades (20–30% of portfolio). Indian equity positions, but as a trading book, not a holding book. Pick industries and stocks that grow faster than Nifty 50 — target at least 15% CAGR. Cash-rich firms with 15% ROIC, 15% cash-flow growth, 15% expansion-driven growth. HDFC Bank, Kotak Mahindra, Angel One, CDSL, CRISIL. The Nifty 50 swing trade — large block during panic, 2–3% in a month, rotate profits into higher-beta assets — is the template.

3. Hard assets for capital protection (15–25% of portfolio). Real estate in hard-currency jurisdictions, physical gold (not ETFs — they have tracking errors, commissions, expense ratios). In a world where fiat currencies are being debased, hard assets provide the floor that financial assets cannot. Bitcoin is evolving into a strategic reserve asset — potentially 10–20% allocation over time as the transition from USD-backed to blockchain-backed reserves accelerates.

4. Asymmetric bets (5–10% of portfolio). Small positions in high-conviction opportunities. BTC as a liquidity-cycle play. Turnaround stocks where valuations have cooled but numbers are good. Sized so a total loss does not damage the portfolio, but a hit delivers outsized real returns.

Notice what this framework is doing. The first two layers are equity, but split by role: one for compounding through patient holding, one for harvesting volatility through active rotation. The third layer is structurally uncorrelated to either — hard assets do not care what the Nifty does in any given week. The fourth layer is a controlled appetite for tail outcomes. Each layer beats a different real-return enemy: compounders beat inflation through pricing power, rotation trades beat the currency drift through hard-currency exposure, hard assets beat debasement through scarcity, and asymmetric bets beat boredom (the silent killer of real returns over a long career).

The single most common mistake is to fund layer 3 (hard assets) by selling layer 1 (compounders) every time the equity market wobbles. The hard-asset layer is supposed to be funded from savings, not from rotating out of the compounders. If you find yourself liquidating Microsoft to buy gold every six months, you are not running this framework — you are running a market-timing strategy in disguise, and the data is unanimous on how that ends.

Measure everything in real terms. After every quarter, calculate your portfolio's return net of taxes paid or accrued, adjust for INR depreciation against the dollar, subtract your estimate of real inflation. If that number is positive, you are actually building wealth. If it is negative or near zero, restructure. The nominal number on your brokerage app is entertainment, not information.

Optimise the tax layer aggressively. When you hold US stocks long-term, you do not pay capital gains tax until you book profits. Hold for 15 years and your tax residency may be different by then. That optionality is real. Buying high-quality US firms you will not sell for 15 years gives you tax flexibility that no Indian FD, PF, or mutual fund can match.

Accept the volatility. In the current regime, equity markets come with higher volatility for modestly higher nominal returns. The equity risk premium is expanding as bond yields rise and the traditional inverse stock-bond relationship breaks down. You need to be psychologically prepared for 10–20% drawdowns on individual positions. If you cannot, accept that your real returns will be lower — possibly negative.

A useful mental rule for sizing volatility tolerance: if a 30% drawdown on your largest position would force you to sell, the position is too big. Either trim it until that hypothetical drawdown is survivable, or pre-commit a hedge (e.g. a put-spread on the index, see the options-as-insurance explainer) that bounds the worst case. Capacity to sit through volatility is not a personality trait; it is a function of position size relative to total assets. Most "I panic in drawdowns" stories are really "I had positions too big to sit through drawdowns" stories.

Separate the asset side from the liability side. Most retail investing advice obsesses over what to buy. The other half of the equation — what you owe, what you spend, how mobile your cost base is — matters at least as much for real returns. A ₹5 lakh annual housing cost difference compounds the same way an extra two percentage points of return does. The investor who controls their cost of living and has the geographic flexibility to move when a jurisdiction turns hostile is the one who keeps real wealth. The investor who locks themselves into a 30-year home loan in a Tier-1 metro has effectively shorted their own real returns for three decades.

Notice that none of these moves require beating the market. They require thinking clearly about three things: where in the world your money sits, how it is taxed when it grows, and what it can buy when you spend it. None of those are stock picks. All of them dominate stock picks in terms of impact on terminal real wealth.

A quick frame for what "dominate" means here, on a representative 20-year horizon:

  • Stock-picking alpha: the best honest active managers in the world deliver 1–3% over their benchmark, before fees, over very long periods. After fees and taxes the persistent edge for retail is closer to zero.
  • Currency exposure: shifting from 100% INR to 60% USD over 20 years adds roughly 3–4% per year, persistently, with no skill required. Just the structural currency drift in your favour.
  • Tax-jurisdiction shift: moving from a 12.5% LTCG regime to a 0% one adds roughly 1.5–2% per year on top, again with no skill required — just paperwork.
  • Inflation regime: consuming in a 3%-inflation basket versus a 9%-inflation basket is worth roughly 5–6% per year of preserved purchasing power, again no skill.

The skill-required gains are 1–3%. The structural gains are 9–12%. The math is uncomfortably one-sided. Most retail investors spend almost all their attention on the skill-required margin and almost none on the structural margin — and the financial-services industry is happy to keep the conversation focused on the wrong axis.

IX.

The only question worth asking before any investment

Every sophisticated investor — every fund manager who actually compounds wealth over decades, every large-ticket player with options on where to deploy — thinks in real terms. The nominal return is the number you tell yourself. The real return is the number that determines whether your children will have the same quality of life you had, or worse.

The shift from nominal to real thinking changes everything:

  • It explains why you must diversify globally — because Indian equities alone cannot reliably beat real global inflation. Just buy a US index, an Indian index, and a China index. You do not even need a fourth asset class.
  • It explains why US tech monopolies are not a luxury allocation but a necessity — they are among the few assets that consistently generate real returns above 10%.
  • It explains why India is a trading market, not a holding market — the real returns from buy-and-hold Indian equities have been near zero for a decade.
  • It explains why tax optimisation is defensive survival, not aggressive planning — in a thin real-return environment, a 2% tax drag is the difference between building wealth and treading water.
  • It explains why hard assets matter — when fiat currencies debase, physical assets hold value. The transition to BTC as a strategic reserve, the persistent strength of gold, the reliability of cash-flow-generating real estate — all make sense through the real-returns lens.
  • It explains why commission drag is fatal, not academic — a 1% TER difference is roughly a third of terminal wealth over four decades. The actively-managed-large-cap mutual fund is not just a product; it is a tax on inattention.
  • It explains why dividends are inefficient versus buybacks — mandatory annual realisations interrupt compounding. The same gross return delivered through buybacks gives you control over when to pay tax.
  • It explains why currency mobility matters more than asset selection — the difference between holding INR and holding USD over 30 years is roughly equivalent to picking the right stock for thirty years straight.

The mutual-fund industry, the financial-advisory complex, the SIP-marketing machine — none of them want you to think in real terms. Because when you do, the product they are selling looks much less attractive. A 12.5% nominal return sounds wonderful. A 0–1% real return for the risk you are taking sounds like a bad deal. And it is.

The one question

After taxes, after inflation, after currency adjustment, does this investment make me richer in real terms? If the answer is no, pass. If yes, size according to conviction and hold with discipline. Everything else is noise.

There are not enough growth options in the world that can generate more than 10% real growth in dollar terms. That is the hard truth. Finding them, concentrating in them, and protecting the returns through smart tax and currency management — that is the entire game.

Your competition is not other investors. It is not the market. It is with inflation. Inflation is undefeated against anyone who does not understand it.

The deepest implication of all this is that the conventional advice — "start a SIP early, hold for the long term, do not check the market, do not panic in downturns" — is not wrong; it is simply incomplete. Each of those instructions assumes the layers above are fixed and benign. They are neither. The early SIP is a perfectly reasonable instruction conditional on the SIP being denominated in something that beats global inflation. Most Indian SIPs are not. Holding for the long term is a perfectly reasonable instruction conditional on the holding being in instruments that compound in hard currency. Indian large-caps for most retail investors are not. The advice was never wrong; it was just being given as if the layers did not exist, by people who profit from you not asking about the layers.

The cleanest mental upgrade is to stop thinking of returns as a single number and start thinking of them as a stack. Gross return minus tax stack minus currency stack minus inflation stack equals real return. Every investment decision is then a question about how each stack will move over your holding period, and how much of the gross you will get to keep. Most of investing is gross-return picking. Most of wealth creation is keeping the stack from eating you alive.

Target a 15% real CAGR by going to assets that can plausibly deliver it — equity in hard currency, hard assets, asymmetric bets — in a sensible weight. Control your cost of living — fix your housing, have the option to migrate to lower-cost-of-living cities or economies. If possible, optimise for taxes. The rest will follow.

The point of this article is not to scare. It is to make the writing on the wall legible, so you can act before the people around you do. Study finance like a nerd. Look at things independently. In a world where real returns are thin, knowledge alpha is the only edge that truly compounds.

The sequence of moves for someone starting from a typical Indian-resident portfolio:

  1. Audit the current portfolio in real terms. Run every holding through Widget 1's logic. Note which positions have a positive real CAGR over the realistic next-5-year inflation assumption. Most won't.
  2. Open the foreign-brokerage path. LRS allows $250,000 per individual per year. A US brokerage account in your name, funded incrementally, gets you direct exposure to the compounders.
  3. Reduce FD / PF / endowment exposure gradually. Not all at once — tax events matter — but at every natural redeployment point, move the freed capital toward real-return-positive instruments.
  4. Build the hard-asset layer over 24–36 months. Physical gold, BTC bought systematically, real estate in a hard-currency jurisdiction if scale allows. Hard assets are the ballast that lets you ride out equity drawdowns without selling at the bottom.
  5. Plan the residency optionality. Not necessarily move — just understand the legal and tax mechanics of moving, so the option exists if and when it makes sense.

One last note for the parents reading. Your children's purchasing power in 2050 is being set right now, by the layered decisions you make about where to hold currency, where to source equity, and how to fund the costs you cannot avoid. By 2050 the goods that signal "middle-class life" — quality education, decent healthcare, international travel, a home large enough for a family — will cost what they cost on a global market. Your job between now and then is not to maximise the nominal balance in your demat account. It is to maximise the share of that global market basket your family can afford. Those are two completely different optimisation problems, and only one of them is what every SIP advert is selling you on.

Consider what a quality MS degree at a US university costs today: about $90,000–120,000 all-in. At 4% annual cost inflation in dollar terms, that becomes $180,000–240,000 by 2045. At 4% INR depreciation, the rupee cost roughly triples to ₹3–4 crores for a single degree. The Indian SIP-only portfolio that has been compounding at 12.5% nominal will have approximately tripled in INR terms over the same window — but every rupee of that gain has to be net of taxes, and every rupee buys roughly the same real basket as it did at the start. The parent who funded the degree through an INR-only path is running uphill against three layers all working in concert. The parent who held US tech directly is mostly riding a tailwind.

The same math applies, even more starkly, to retirement. A retirement that aims for ₹1 lakh per month in today's purchasing power across a 25-year horizon needs to be funded by a corpus that grows in real terms, not nominal. The Nifty-only SIP plan that "works on paper" works only if the inflation assumption is implausibly mild. Any realistic inflation assumption forces the corpus 30–50% higher than the headline planner suggests, and that gap has to come from somewhere — usually from working longer or living poorer.

Read it again. Run the widgets at numbers that match your own situation. Notice where the real-CAGR turns negative on the first widget, where the FD bar collapses below principal on the second, where the PPP gap on the third refuses to close. That is the writing on the wall. Make the move.

If you remember nothing else from this article, remember these five operational consequences:

  1. The brokerage app's headline return is entertainment, not information. The number that matters is real, post-tax, currency-adjusted. Track that number, or you are tracking a number that doesn't determine your actual wealth.
  2. Indian large-cap buy-and-hold has been a near-zero-real-return strategy for a decade. Treat India as a trading book. Treat US tech as a holding book. The two require completely different operational tempos and skill sets.
  3. FDs and PF are wealth-destruction instruments at current inflation rates. Move the cash you do not need imminently into instruments that have any chance of beating real inflation. Yes, that means equity. Yes, that means volatility.
  4. Optimise the tax jurisdiction, not just the asset selection. Two percentage points of tax savings per year, compounded for thirty years, doubles your terminal wealth on its own. This is real money. Treat it like real money.
  5. Diversify across currencies, not just across stocks. A 60% INR / 30% USD / 10% hard-asset split is significantly more robust to the next decade's most likely real-return regime than a 100% INR allocation across forty stocks.

None of this is glamorous. None of it makes for a viral tweet. Every step is incrementally a little uncomfortable — opening the foreign brokerage account, navigating LRS limits, paying for a tax consultation, holding a position through a 20% drawdown. The discomfort is the moat. If it were easy, it would already be priced in. The fact that most retail investors will not do these five things is precisely why the few who do compound at materially higher real rates over a generation.
