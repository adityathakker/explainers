I.

I. The Biggest Mistake Investors Make Has Nothing to Do With Stock Picking

Let me tell you something that will change how you think about investing forever. The difference between an investor who builds lasting wealth and one who blows up their portfolio has almost nothing to do with which stocks they pick. It has everything to do with how much money they put into each position.

I have been investing for a long time now. And if I had to distill everything I have learned into one sentence, it would be this: position sizing is the game. Stock picking is the sideshow.

Most retail investors spend 95% of their time researching which stock to buy. They read annual reports, watch YouTube videos, follow social media tips, and agonize over whether Company A is better than Company B. Then they take all of that careful research and blow it by putting 25% of their portfolio into a single stock because they are “really confident” about it. That is not investing. That is gambling with extra steps.

Here is the reality. My hit rate as an investor is about 60%. That means roughly 4 out of every 10 stocks I pick turn out to be wrong. And that is better than most professional fund managers. The reason I still make money consistently is not because I pick winners more often than other people. It is because when I am wrong, the damage is contained. And when I am right, I have enough capital spread across enough winners to benefit meaningfully.

Your portfolio should win, not individual stocks. As long as you are not more than 5% invested in any stock in total, you will be fine.

That quote is the single most important rule I follow. And it is the rule that most investors violate, usually because they confuse conviction with recklessness.

Think about what happens when you put 20% of your portfolio into one stock and it drops 50%. Your entire portfolio is down 10% from that one position alone. Now you are in a hole, you are emotional, you are making panic decisions. But if that same stock was only 3% of your portfolio and it drops 50%, you have lost 1.5% overall. You barely feel it. You can think clearly. You can decide rationally whether to hold, average down, or exit. That psychological freedom is worth more than any stock tip anyone will ever give you.

Position sizing is not about limiting your upside. It is about ensuring your survival. The investors who compound wealth over decades are not the ones who hit home runs. They are the ones who never blow up. They are the ones who are still in the game after 10, 20, 30 years, letting compounding do its work.

In this article, I am going to walk you through the exact framework I use to size positions, buy in tranches, allocate across asset classes, and manage risk. These are not theoretical concepts. These are the actual rules I follow with my own money. And they are, without question, the most important tools in my investing toolkit.

II.

II. The 5% Rule: Your Non-Negotiable Ceiling

If there is one rule you take away from everything I write, let it be this: never invest more than 5% of your total portfolio in any single stock.

This is not a guideline. This is not a suggestion. This is a hard ceiling that I never violate, and neither should you.

Let me explain why this number works. If you have 20 stocks in your portfolio, each at 5%, and one of them goes to zero – absolute zero, the company vanishes – your portfolio is down 5%. That is a bad day, but it is not a catastrophe. You recover from 5% in a good quarter. But if you had 20% in that stock, you have lost a fifth of everything you have. That can take years to recover from, and psychologically, most people never do recover. They become scared, they become conservative at exactly the wrong time, and they permanently impair their wealth-building ability.

Now, I often go even lower than 5%. For riskier stocks – small caps, turnaround stories, companies with uncertain futures – I cap my exposure at 2-2.5% of my portfolio. Look at how I approach stocks like Bandhan Bank or Natco Pharma or Armaan Financials. These are stocks where I see genuine potential – sometimes 3X returns in 3-5 years. But they also carry real downside risk of 40-50%.

When I talk about Bandhan Bank, I tell the community: if you are not invested, consider 2.5% of your portfolio. Why 2.5% and not 5%? Because this is a microfinance institution turned bank. It does riskier lending. There is systemic risk inherent in the business model. The stock can either go 3X in 3 years or have a 40-50% downside. At 2.5% allocation, even a 50% crash only costs you 1.25% of your total portfolio. That is manageable. That lets you sleep at night.

Same story with Armaan Financials. The fintech sector is going through pain due to RBI regulatory tightening. The stock is under pressure. But the PE is reasonable and the numbers are good. My maximum exposure? 2.5% of my portfolio. I am putting more money into it – another 25% downward averaging – but even after that, I am still within my 2.5% cap. And I still have one block of money remaining for future averaging if needed.

The 5% rule also governs when you should NOT average down. This is critical. Many investors treat downward averaging as a way to “fix” a losing position. They keep throwing money at a falling stock, hoping to lower their average cost. But if the stock already exceeds your 5% allocation limit, you do not downward average. You wait. Period. Adding more money to a position that already takes up too much of your portfolio is not averaging down – it is doubling down on a mistake.

Don’t make stocks an ego issue. Your portfolio should win, not individual stocks. As long as you are not more than 5% invested in any stock in total, you will be fine. You don’t downward average if the stock already exceeds your 5% limit. You wait. Good things turn around.

Here is a practical framework for position sizing by risk category:

  • Large caps and index ETFs (NIFTY 50, Bank ETFs): Up to 5% per individual holding, and these can constitute 60-80% of your equity portfolio collectively
  • Mid caps with proven track records: 3-5% per position
  • Small caps and turnaround stories: 2-2.5% per position maximum
  • Speculative plays (pre-IPO, unlisted, very early stage): 1-2% per position maximum
  • Things like meme coins, highly speculative instruments: Zero. Just zero.

The beauty of this system is that it takes ego out of the equation. You do not need to be right about every stock. You just need to be right about enough of them, and wrong about the rest in a way that does not cripple you. That is how real wealth gets built.

III.

III. Tranche Buying: Why You Never Go All-In at Once

The second pillar of intelligent position sizing is tranche buying – the practice of building into positions gradually rather than deploying all your capital at once.

Here is the simplest version of this rule: always buy in 3 tranches. Whether you are entering a new position or averaging into an existing one, you divide your intended allocation into three parts and deploy them at different times.

Why three? Because no one – and I mean no one – can consistently call the bottom of a stock or a market. Not me, not Warren Buffett, not any algorithm. When I say a stock looks good at current levels, I am saying the risk-reward makes sense for a purchase. I am not saying it cannot fall another 15-20% from here. It absolutely can. And if it does, I want to have dry powder remaining to buy more at even better prices.

Let me give you a concrete example. When I talk about building positions in hospital stocks, the strategy is simple: do one bulk buy now and then start a SIP in 4-5 top names. That is essentially tranche buying. You get some exposure immediately so you do not miss the upside if the stock moves quickly, but you also preserve capital for future purchases at potentially better prices.

Another example: Supriya Lifescience. When I see a stock giving a breakout and members sitting on healthy profits, what do I tell new investors? Do not invest more than 33% as your first tranche. Invest 33 rupees, keep 66 rupees for later. That is the one-third rule. You are building a position, not making a bet.

The same logic applies to downward averaging. When Natco Pharma dropped 28%, I did not dump all my remaining allocated capital into it at once. I averaged down to bring my loss to 14%, but I deliberately left one more round of money for downward averaging later. Why? Because the stock could fall further. And if it does, I want to be able to buy more at even lower prices, further reducing my average cost.

Tranche buying applies at every scale. When I booked 20% profits on NIFTY 50, I did not immediately redeploy all of that capital. I took 60% of the amount and put it into NIFTY Private Bank ETF as a bulk buy, reserving 40% for subsequent deployment. Even at the index level, I am buying in tranches.

Here is how I think about the three tranches practically:

Tranche 1 (33% of intended position): This is your initial entry. You have identified a stock or asset with good risk-reward at current levels. You buy your first third. If the stock immediately runs up, you are in the game. You have exposure. You did not miss the move entirely.

Tranche 2 (33% of intended position): This happens when one of two things occurs. Either the stock pulls back to a better support level (making your average cheaper), or the stock consolidates and confirms its direction (giving you more confidence). Either way, you are deploying more capital with additional information that you did not have when you made your first purchase.

Tranche 3 (33% of intended position): This is your final deployment. By this point, you either have a clear winner that you are adding to on strength, or you have a stock that has fallen enough to offer deep value. In the first case, you are upward averaging into a confirmed trend. In the second case, you are completing your downward averaging at a significantly lower cost basis.

Buy things slowly. Upward average slowly. Buy quality firms, where you can handle a 20% downturn.

The tranche approach is not just about getting a better average price, though that is a significant benefit. It is fundamentally about managing your psychology. When you go all-in on a stock and it drops 15% the next week, you feel trapped. You have no more capital to deploy, and every further drop feels like a wound you cannot treat. But when you have bought only one-third and the stock drops 15%, you feel the opposite – you feel opportunity. You have capital ready. The falling price is not a threat; it is a chance to improve your position. That psychological difference is enormous, and it is why tranche buying, more than almost any other technique, separates professional investors from amateurs.

Let me also address a common question I get: what about stocks where you are afraid of missing the run-up? Stocks like Jio Finance or Zomato that are moving fast. The answer is still the same. Absolutely do not bulk buy these now if you are an investor. As investors we buy in tranches. We buy at consolidation points. These stocks are still falling, so wait for consolidation. You can run SIPs for the next few months if you want exposure, but bulk buy now? No. The tranche discipline holds regardless of how exciting the stock looks.

IV.

IV. Asset Allocation: The Decision That Matters More Than Any Stock Pick

Here is something I have been saying with increasing urgency: the investment landscape right now is less about individual stocks and more about asset class allocation. Allocate your capital poorly, and you will see bad results regardless of which stocks you pick within each category.

We worry too much about picking great individual stocks. But we forget the fact that if for some reason the entire emerging markets go down by 40%, then every stock you own within those markets goes down too. Your brilliant stock pick inside a collapsing market is still a losing position.

This is why I spend as much time thinking about how much goes into Indian equities versus US equities versus gold versus Bitcoin versus real estate as I do thinking about individual stocks. In fact, I probably spend more time on it. The macro allocation decision is the biggest driver of your portfolio returns over any 5-10 year period.

Let me share what my allocation looks like, because I think frameworks are more useful than abstractions:

My overall portfolio allocation: - 60% Stocks - 30% Real Estate - 10% BTC/Gold

Within the stocks allocation: - 65% across US and China equities - 35% India equities - Within India: approximately 80% large caps, 20% mid and small caps

Now, your allocation will be different from mine. It depends on your age, your income, your risk tolerance, your geographic location, and your investment horizon. But the principle is the same: you need to deliberately decide how much goes where before you start picking individual stocks.

If I had to give you a simple starting plan for someone who does not want to overthink it:

  • 60% Indian equities (predominantly through NIFTY 50 ETF or top mutual funds)
  • 20-30% US equities (NASDAQ, S&P 500 exposure)
  • 10% Gold/BTC (your neutral, non-sovereign currency hedge)

The reason US equities deserve a significant allocation is straightforward. The AI race is happening primarily in the US. US growth stocks are exceptional long-term bets. It might look expensive right now. But there is a good case to be made that this is the trade of the decade. If you miss it, you might pay a heavy price. US growth stocks are a buy-on-dips story.

The reason Gold and BTC deserve an allocation is because the world is becoming more uncertain. Trade wars are increasing regionalism. There will be multiple power centers. You need exposure to neutral currencies that are not tied to any single government’s monetary policy. How much neutral currencies to buy – gold, BTC, and in which format – is a question every serious investor needs to answer.

And the reason I keep a significant portion outside India is simple: home bias is the enemy of diversification. We all like investing in our own country. It feels familiar, comfortable, patriotic even. But it violates the principles of diversification. No one can predict that on a long-term basis, Indian markets will or will not generate alpha. I am not writing this to scare you. I am writing this to tell you that diversification works at a global level.

If India does well and the rest does poorly, your Indian holdings carry the portfolio. If India underperforms and the US or China or BTC does well, those holdings balance things out. That is the whole point. You are not betting on one outcome. You are building a portfolio that makes reasonable money across multiple outcomes.

Anyone who tells you that market X is the only great one and the rest are duds – they are idiots, or they are trying to mislead you.

V.

V. Cash Is a Position: The Most Underrated Risk Management Tool

Most investors think of cash as the absence of a position. As money that is not working for you. As dead weight in the portfolio.

They are wrong. Cash is a position. And it might be the most important one you hold.

When the market is at an all-time high, it is critical to lower your risk by keeping cash on the side. I routinely keep 20% or more in cash during uncertain or elevated markets. This is not laziness or indecision. This is a deliberate, active risk management strategy.

Here is why. Markets correct by 10% or more multiple times in every multi-year cycle. In the last 2.5 years alone, the Indian market has corrected by 10% or more at least 4 times. These corrections are your bulk buy opportunities. But you can only take advantage of them if you have cash ready to deploy.

Think about the math. If you are 100% invested when the market drops 15%, you have lost 15% and you have no way to improve your situation except waiting. But if you are 80% invested with 20% in cash, you have lost 12% on your invested portion (net 9.6% on total portfolio), and you now have that 20% cash ready to deploy at prices that are 15% cheaper. When the market recovers – and it always does eventually from these levels – your cost basis is significantly lower than someone who was fully invested the whole time.

Historically, whenever the market has gone 15% from its peak, people who bought at those levels have made around 20% CAGR over the next 3-year period. But you cannot capture that 20% CAGR if you have no cash to deploy at the moment of opportunity.

The question then becomes: when do you deploy the cash? Here is my approach:

Step 1: Identify the broad support levels on the index (NIFTY 50, for example). These are well-established technical levels where buying interest historically comes in.

Step 2: As the market approaches these levels, begin deploying cash in tranches (there is that word again). Not all at once. In stages.

Step 3: Buy the safest assets first. This depends on market type. In a risky, trending-down market with poor global outlook, you buy the safest assets possible – NIFTY 50 ETFs, bank ETFs, large-cap stalwarts where the principal risk is close to zero. HDFC, Kotak, ICICI are likely to remain the top 3-4 private banks in India for the next 5 years. The principal loss on such companies is minimal.

Step 4: Only when the trend reverses and you have clarity do you pick more individual stocks and add more risk.

This approach means you will never buy the exact bottom. You will always feel like you could have deployed more cash earlier or later. That is fine. The goal is not perfection. The goal is systematic deployment that keeps you in the game and captures the bulk of the recovery.

This is NOT the market where you bulk buy and lose money. Buy things slowly. Upward average slowly. Buy quality firms, where you can handle a 20% downturn.

Cash also serves another function: it prevents panic. One of the primary goals of intelligent investing is to avoid panic. If I can just help you do this one thing – avoid panic – you eventually would make very good money. Having cash on the side is the single best anti-panic device. When the market is falling and your invested positions are red, knowing that you have dry powder to deploy removes the emotional urgency to sell. You are not a victim of the downturn; you are a buyer waiting for better prices.

Let me also talk about how the professionals think about cash deployment versus the amateurs. The amateur investor sees a 15% correction and freezes. “What if it falls more?” they think, and they hold their cash, waiting for the perfect bottom. The professional investor sees a 15% correction and starts buying systematically – not everything at once, but in calculated tranches at predetermined support levels. The professional knows the bottom is unknowable, so they do not try to call it. They simply execute their plan.

When I was most recently deploying cash, the market was about 14-15% from its top. I did my bulk buy at that level. Was that the absolute bottom? No idea. But the historical data shows that buying at 15% from peak, in safe assets, with a 3-year horizon, generates excellent returns. That is a good enough reason to act.

VI.

VI. Upward and Downward Averaging: The Science of Adding to Positions

Once you have a position established in tranches, the question becomes: when and how do you add to it? This is where the concepts of upward averaging and downward averaging come in, and most investors get both of them spectacularly wrong.

Let me start with the most common mistake. Many people think that if you are at a 15% loss on a stock, you should buy more to “cover” that loss. This is what I call ego-driven averaging, and it is not a sensible way to manage positions. You are not averaging down to save face. You are not averaging down because your ego cannot accept a loss. You are averaging down only when the fundamental thesis still holds and the stock has moved to a price where the risk-reward is even better than when you first bought.

Here is my framework for deciding whether to average down:

Check 1: Does the stock still deserve its allocation? If your original thesis has broken down – the company’s fundamentals have deteriorated, management has changed direction, the industry dynamics have shifted – then averaging down is just throwing good money after bad. Cut the position and move on.

Check 2: Is the stock within your position size limits? If the stock already exceeds your 5% allocation limit (or 2.5% for riskier names), you do not average down. Full stop. The position is already too large for your risk parameters. You wait.

Check 3: Do you have remaining tranches to deploy? If you have already deployed all three of your planned tranches, you have no more capital allocated for this position. The averaging is done. What you have is what you have. I always leave at least one block of money remaining for potential future averaging.

When I average down on Natco Pharma, here is exactly how I think about it. The stock currently trades at a PE of 8-9, which is very low. The firm was growing fast until recently – healthy year-on-year revenues, expanding EPS. This quarter spooked investors because of a major revenue shrink, likely due to tariff war impacts on their 90% international business. My total loss on the stock was around 28%. I averaged down to bring this to 14%. But critically, I will not invest more than 2-2.5% of my entire capital in the stock even after averaging. And I left one more round of money for downward averaging later, in case the stock falls further.

That is disciplined downward averaging. The thesis still holds (good firm in a temporary headwind). The position is within size limits (2-2.5% maximum). And I have reserved capital for one more tranche if needed.

Upward averaging is equally important and equally misunderstood. When a stock in your portfolio is working and trending higher, many investors are reluctant to add more. They think they have “missed it” or that buying higher is illogical. But upward averaging is one of the most powerful tools in investing.

Here is how I approach it. At every stage, I check two things: (a) how much money do I have to invest, and (b) where can I invest it best? If a stock I already own is trending up, has solid fundamentals, is not in an overvalued zone, and represents the best risk-reward available to me, I will keep adding. The fact that my cost basis goes up is irrelevant if the stock has further room to run.

When I upward averaged stocks in my portfolio, many positions were already up 20% or more. Some people thought that was weird. But here is the logic: I am confident that some of these firms will make 3X returns in 5 years, some will hardly move, and along the way we will see a lot of volatility. As long as these firms are not in an overvalued zone, it is fine for me to keep adding. The compounding happens over years, not days.

I pick small-cap, positive cash flow firms in expanding industries because in a market where generating 15% CAGR might be tough, these are the companies that help me meet that requirement. And when I find such firms, I want to build meaningful positions in them – gradually, through upward averaging as they confirm my thesis.

The key insight is this: your average cost is irrelevant except as a risk management tool. What matters is the future expected return from the current price. If a stock at 100 has a 50% probability of going to 200 and a 20% probability of going to 50, that is a good bet regardless of whether your average cost is 60 or 90. The average cost only matters insofar as it determines your downside risk and your position size relative to your portfolio.

I am also okay with being down 20% on some positions. When I upward average stocks that are in momentum, I am explicitly accepting the possibility of a 20% fall on each of them. The reason I am okay with that is because we are already at least 20% up on each of these stocks to begin with. If you have a 20% profit cushion on a stock and it falls 20%, you are roughly at breakeven. You have lost nothing. That cushion, built by entering early and through smart tranche buying, gives you the room to be more aggressive on the upside.

And just to bring this full circle – look at what happened with SBI Cards. People were feeling dejected about that stock. Then it went up 17% in a single month. Compared to the broader market, that is effectively 25% outperformance. The investors who had proper position sizing could hold through the drawdown and benefit from the recovery. The ones who were oversized and panicked out missed the move entirely.

VII.

VII. Diversification Beyond Stocks: The Multi-Layer Defense System

Position sizing within your stock portfolio is critical, but it is only one layer of the defense system. True risk management requires diversification across multiple dimensions – asset classes, geographies, currencies, and even brokerages.

Let me break this down layer by layer.

Layer 1: Asset Class Diversification

Your portfolio should not be 100% equities. Even the most aggressive growth investors need non-equity exposure because different asset classes respond differently to different economic environments. When equities crash in a tariff war, gold might surge. When Indian markets stagnate, US tech stocks might be ripping. When both equities and bonds struggle in a high-inflation environment, real estate and Bitcoin might provide the hedge you need.

My allocation of 60% stocks, 30% real estate, and 10% BTC/Gold is designed exactly for this. The stocks provide growth. The real estate provides inflation protection and cash flow. The BTC/Gold provides insurance against currency devaluation and geopolitical uncertainty.

Now let me be clear – I am not buying gold and silver at every price level. When they have run up too much, the risk-reward does not make sense. As interest rate policy eases, there is a chance for correction. So even within your alternative asset allocation, you need to be price-sensitive and apply the same tranche buying discipline.

Bitcoin, on the other hand, is a strong mid-term asset. I recently added more BTC at around 111K. But – and this is where position sizing comes in again – BTC can undergo a serious drawdown. So I keep a 50% downward average room. That means when I buy BTC, I deploy only half my intended allocation, keeping the other half for a potential 40-50% dip. That is tranche buying applied to crypto.

Layer 2: Geographic Diversification

This is becoming more important by the day. The trade war is increasing regionalism in the world. There will be multiple power centers. As investors, you need to think about how much you allocate to Indian equities versus US equities versus emerging markets like China or the UAE.

India and the US are mostly positively correlated – India is aligned to the US, so if US equities go down, so would India’s. This means holding both does not give you as much diversification as you might think. You need to think about truly uncorrelated or differently correlated exposures.

I have pivoted around 5-10% of my portfolio to China. The Chinese market is very cyclical and non-transparent, which is why I am taking only a limited bet. But the risk-reward on specific opportunities there – after China announced its version of QE and stocks like Alibaba surged 4% in a single day – justifies a position. Notice the sizing: 5-10%. Not 20%. Not 30%. A measured bet that I can afford to be completely wrong about.

New emerging markets like the UAE stock market are also worth considering. They have diversified their economy beyond oil. It is more balanced now. These are the kinds of asymmetric opportunities that geographic diversification opens up – but only if you approach them with proper position sizing discipline.

Layer 3: Currency Diversification

When the rupee depreciates at 3-4% per year against the dollar, holding 100% of your assets in INR-denominated instruments is a slow leak in your portfolio. You need exposure to USD-denominated assets (US stocks and ETFs), to hard assets (gold), and to non-sovereign digital currencies (Bitcoin). This is not speculation. It is hedging against the very real, very consistent depreciation of your home currency.

Layer 4: Platform Diversification

This one surprises people, but I take it seriously. In investing, you need to be diversified even at the brokerage level. I personally use 5-6 brokers. I trust none of them. But I trust diversification.

There have been glitches on trading apps, allegations of brokers not even allocating mutual fund units that were purchased, problems with order execution on volatile days. People have made a hue and cry on social media, but no action has been taken. And honestly, I do not think anything is going to happen. So the pragmatic response is not to complain louder – it is to spread your assets across multiple platforms so that no single point of failure can lock you out of your own money.

Layer 5: Time Diversification

This is really what tranche buying achieves at a macro level. By spreading your purchases across time, you smooth out the volatility of any single entry point. You are not betting that today is the best day to invest. You are betting that over the next several months of systematic deployment, you will capture a reasonable average entry price.

When these five layers work together – asset class, geography, currency, platform, and time diversification – you build a portfolio that is genuinely resilient. Not a portfolio that does well when everything goes right. A portfolio that survives and recovers when things go wrong. And in investing, things will go wrong. Repeatedly. The only question is whether your portfolio construction can handle it.

VIII.

VIII. The Psychology of Position Sizing: Why Discipline Beats Intelligence

I need to be honest with you about something. Everything I have written so far is intellectually simple. The 5% rule, tranche buying, asset allocation frameworks – none of this is rocket science. A reasonably intelligent 20-year-old could understand all of it in an afternoon.

The hard part is not understanding it. The hard part is doing it. Consistently. For years. Especially when every emotional impulse in your body is screaming at you to do the opposite.

Here is what happens in practice. You find a stock you love. You have done the research, the numbers are beautiful, the story is compelling. Your rational allocation plan says: put 3% of your portfolio into it, in three tranches. But your emotions say: this is a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity, put 15% in right now, you do not want to miss this.

Or the opposite scenario. A stock you own has been falling for weeks. Your position sizing discipline says: you are at your 5% limit, do not add more. But your emotions say: if you just average down one more time, you can bring your cost basis to a level where you will break even on the next bounce.

In both cases, the emotional response feels like the smart move in the moment. And in both cases, following it will eventually destroy you. Maybe not this time. Maybe you get lucky and the 15% bet works out. But over a career, over hundreds of decisions, breaking your position sizing rules will eventually catch you in a catastrophic loss that wipes out years of gains.

I cannot overstate this: investing is a risk management game. And buying good things cheap is the easiest risk management strategy. The returns are a byproduct of managing risk well. Not the other way around.

You can only do all this if you stop tracking your portfolio on a daily basis. When there is a war going on, even if you are the best warrior, things on the battlefield always look ugly.

The daily tracking habit is one of the worst things retail investors do to themselves. When you check your portfolio every day, every red number triggers an emotional response. Every green number triggers a different emotional response. Neither of these responses is useful for making good long-term decisions. They just create noise that makes it harder to stick to your system.

Here is what disciplined position sizing looks like in practice. When I tell the community about a stock I am buying, I always mention the position size. Always. Not because the community asked, but because the position size IS the recommendation. Telling someone to buy Armaan Financials without telling them to limit it to 2.5% of their portfolio is irresponsible. Telling someone that hospital stocks look good without telling them to invest 33% now and keep 66% for later is incomplete advice. Telling someone to buy OYO without saying “treat it like a small-cap stock, invest 3-5% capital max, and don’t be greedy” is setting them up for a painful lesson.

The stock pick without the position size is half a sentence. It is like telling someone to drive to Delhi without mentioning which highway to take. You might get there, but you might also end up somewhere very different.

My hit rate is about 60%. That means 40% of my picks are wrong. If I had concentrated positions in those 40% losers, I would be in serious trouble. But because every position is sized appropriately for its risk level, the 60% winners more than compensate for the 40% losers. The math works not because I am a genius stock picker – I am not – but because the position sizing ensures that my mistakes are small and my successes are large relative to the risk taken.

This is also why I tell people to buy things they can afford to hold even if the stock goes 40% down. Because good things come back up. But they only come back up for the investor who was properly sized and can afford to wait. The investor who had too much in the position and panicked out at the bottom does not get the recovery.

Let me address something else that undermines psychology: the comparison trap. People listen to podcasts, follow social media accounts, and see others posting about 100% gains on a single stock. What they do not see is the position size. That person who made 100% on a stock might have had 1% of their portfolio in it – a 1% portfolio impact. Meanwhile, their other concentrated bets might have lost 30%, dragging their overall portfolio down. You never see the full picture. So stop comparing your returns to individual stock stories and start thinking about your portfolio as a system.

I tell things honestly. I put my real money behind my opinions. My thesis can be wrong – and it is wrong 40% of the time. Take my viewpoint, absorb other viewpoints, apply what you like, ignore what you do not. But whatever viewpoints you adopt, size them properly. That is the non-negotiable.

IX.

IX. Putting It All Together: Your Position Sizing Action Plan

Let me synthesize everything into a concrete, actionable framework that you can start using immediately.

Rule 1: The 5% Hard Ceiling

No single stock ever exceeds 5% of your total portfolio. For small caps and turnaround stories, the ceiling is 2-2.5%. For speculative plays like unlisted stocks or pre-IPO opportunities, the ceiling is 1-2%. There are no exceptions. Not for your highest-conviction idea. Not for the “sure thing.” Not ever. This rule alone, if followed rigorously, would save most retail investors from the worst outcomes they experience.

Rule 2: The 3-Tranche Entry

Every new position is built in three tranches of roughly equal size. Tranche 1 goes in when you identify the opportunity. Tranche 2 goes in either on a pullback (better price) or on confirmation (more confidence). Tranche 3 goes in to complete the position when you have maximum information. If the stock runs away before you deploy all three tranches, that is fine – you captured some of the move. If the stock falls, that is also fine – your remaining tranches go in at better prices.

Rule 3: The Asset Allocation Foundation

Before picking any individual stock, decide your macro allocation: - How much in equities versus real estate versus gold/BTC? - Within equities, how much in India versus US versus other markets? - Within Indian equities, how much in large caps versus mid/small caps? - How much in cash, waiting for the next correction?

This framework prevents the common trap of accidentally ending up 100% invested in Indian small caps because you kept finding “great opportunities” one stock at a time.

Rule 4: The Cash Reserve

Always maintain a cash reserve of at least 10-20% of your portfolio during normal markets, and up to 30% during elevated or uncertain markets. This cash is not dead money. It is ammunition. It is what allows you to buy aggressively when the market gives you a 10-15% correction – those bulk buy opportunities that come multiple times in every cycle. Remember: in the last 2.5 years, the Indian market has corrected by 10% or more at least 4 times. Each one of those was an opportunity that only cash-holding investors could exploit.

Rule 5: The Averaging Discipline

Before averaging down on any position, ask three questions: (1) Does the fundamental thesis still hold? (2) Is the position within its allocation limit? (3) Do I have remaining tranches for this stock? If any answer is no, you do not average down. If all answers are yes, you deploy one more tranche, and you leave at least one final tranche in reserve.

For upward averaging, the questions are different: (1) Is the stock in a confirmed uptrend? (2) Is it still below fair value or in an overvalued zone? (3) Is it still the best use of this capital compared to other opportunities? If all answers are yes, add to the position. Remember: loss covering or profit covering is not a sensible way to downward or upward average. The fundamentals must drive the decision, not your P&L.

Rule 6: The Diversification Audit

Periodically review your portfolio across all five layers of diversification: asset class, geography, currency, platform, and time. If you find concentration in any layer – too much in Indian equities, too much on one brokerage, too much deployed in a single week – rebalance. This audit is as important as your stock research. Maybe more important.

Rule 7: The Anti-Panic Protocol

When markets are falling and the news is terrifying, your position sizing system is your anchor. You do not panic-sell because no single position is large enough to destroy your portfolio. You do not fear-buy because you have a systematic tranche plan. You do not overreact because you have cash reserves ready for deployment at predetermined support levels. The system runs the show, not your emotions.

Here is the honest truth about investing that most people do not want to hear. The returns do not come from being smarter than everyone else. They come from being more disciplined. They come from doing the boring, systematic, unglamorous work of sizing positions correctly, buying in tranches, maintaining cash reserves, and diversifying across multiple dimensions.

Your job as an investor is to protect and grow your capital. So be ruthless – and sector, geography, and asset class agnostic. The way you do that is not by finding the one perfect stock. It is by building a system where no single mistake can hurt you badly, and where the collective weight of many decent decisions – properly sized, properly diversified, properly timed – compounds into genuine wealth over decades.

Relax. Look for the best opportunities. Invest where you can make good returns. But never, ever forget that how much you invest matters more than what you invest in.

That is the art of position sizing. And it is, without question, the most important skill you will ever develop as an investor.
