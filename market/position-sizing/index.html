The Art of Position Sizing

Edge tells you whether to bet. Sizing tells you whether you'll still be there to bet again.

I.

Why does picking the right stock barely matter?

The difference between an investor who builds lasting wealth and one who blows up has almost nothing to do with which stocks they picked. It has everything to do with how much money they put into each one. Most retail investors spend 95% of their time on the picking and 5% on the sizing, then wonder why their returns don't match the names in their watchlist.

My own hit rate is about 60%. Four out of every ten ideas I commit to turn out wrong — and that is actually better than most professional fund managers. The reason the portfolio still compounds is not because I pick winners more often. It is because when I am wrong the damage is contained, and when I am right enough capital is spread across enough winners for the math to work.

Think about what happens when you put 20% of a portfolio into one name and it drops 50%. The whole portfolio is down 10% from that single position. You are emotional. You are making panic decisions. But put 3% into the same stock and a 50% drop costs you 1.5% overall. You barely feel it. You can think. You can decide rationally whether to hold, average, or exit. That psychological freedom is worth more than any stock tip anyone will ever give you.

Your portfolio should win, not individual stocks. As long as you are not more than 5% invested in any stock in total, you will be fine.

Position sizing is not about limiting upside. It is about ensuring survival. The investors who compound wealth across decades are not the ones who hit home runs — they are the ones who never blow up. They are still in the game after ten, twenty, thirty years, letting compounding do the work the picking never could.

This article is the framework I actually use, with the math worked out so you can feel it. The questions stack: when you have an edge, how big a bet is optimal? What happens if you go bigger? Why is "more conviction = more sizing" the move that breaks careers? By the end the answers will be in your fingertips, not just your head.

II.

How much should you bet when you have an edge?

Start with the cleanest possible version of the question. You face a repeated bet. With probability p you win b rupees for every rupee staked; with probability q = 1 − p you lose the rupee. How much of your bankroll should you risk on each turn?

Edward Thorp brought this problem from Bell Labs (where John Kelly first solved it for noisy telephone channels[1]) to the blackjack tables of Las Vegas and then to a Wall Street fund that compounded at over 19% a year for twenty-eight years. The answer he used is one of the most important formulas in finance, and it fits on a postcard:

f* = (b · p − q) ÷ b f* = optimal fraction of bankroll · p = win probability · b = payoff ratio · q = 1 − p

Three things to notice before you touch the simulator. First, if b · p < q you have no edge — the formula goes negative, which means the correct bet size is zero (or short, if you can). Second, the formula does not care how excited you feel; it only cares about expected value per dollar. Third, the optimal fraction can be surprisingly small. A 55% coin paying even money — a genuinely huge edge in trading terms — tells you to risk only 10% of bankroll per bet.

Well, actually 🧐 — most pros bet half-Kelly, not full

The Kelly fraction is the bet that maximises the expected log-growth of the bankroll. It does not minimise drawdowns. At full Kelly, you should expect a 50% drawdown roughly half the time you compound. That is bearable on paper and unbearable in practice, because you cannot run an expected-value spreadsheet during the drawdown — you are too busy questioning your edge.

Almost every professional gambler and quant fund runs fractional Kelly, usually 0.25× to 0.5× full Kelly. You give up some long-run growth in exchange for shallower drawdowns and a tighter distribution of outcomes. The Kelly cell above also shows the half-Kelly figure for exactly this reason.

The reason this formula matters for stocks is not that picking equities is a coin flip — it is that almost every active decision has an implied p and b. When I tell the community Bandhan Bank could give 3× in three years or drop 40–50%, that is a payoff structure with a clean Kelly answer. Reasonable inputs — say p = 0.55 and b = 4 (a 200% gain weighed against a 50% loss) — spit out Kelly around 44%. Cut that to a quarter-Kelly to stay sane, and you land at ≈11% — which is still far above the 2.5% cap I actually use. The cap exists because my edge estimate could be wrong, not because the math is.

III.

Why does the 5% ceiling work for almost everyone?

If Kelly is the theoretical answer, the 5% rule is the practical one. No single stock ever exceeds 5% of total portfolio. Twenty positions at 5% each, one goes to absolute zero, the company vanishes — portfolio down 5%. That is a bad day. It is not a catastrophe. You recover in a good quarter.

At 20% in the same stock you have lost a fifth of everything. That takes years to recover from, and psychologically most people never do. They become risk-averse at exactly the wrong moment, permanently impairing their wealth-building ability. The 5% cap exists because it makes the worst-case outcome — the one your spreadsheet says is impossible right up until it happens — survivable.

For riskier names I cap lower. The actual ladder I use:

Speculative (pre-IPO, very early)1–2%−1% to −2%
Meme coins, lottery tickets0%n/a

This is where the picking-versus-sizing tension lives. When I look at Bandhan Bank — a microfinance institution turned bank, doing riskier lending, real systemic risk in the business model — the realistic outcomes are 3× in three years or a 40–50% drawdown. Genuine asymmetry. But at 2.5% allocation, even a 50% crash costs the portfolio 1.25%. That lets you sleep at night, which lets you stay invested, which lets the 3× case actually happen. The cap is not the enemy of conviction; it is the price of being around long enough for conviction to pay.

The 5% rule also governs when you should not average down. Many investors treat averaging as a way to "fix" a losing position — keep throwing money at a falling stock to lower the average cost. But if the position already exceeds your 5% allocation limit, you do not average down. You wait. Period. Adding money to a position that already takes up too much of your portfolio is not averaging down. It is doubling down on a mistake.

Don't make stocks an ego issue. Your portfolio should win, not individual stocks. You don't downward average if the stock already exceeds your 5% limit. You wait. Good things turn around.

IV.

What happens when you bet too big — even with an edge?

"I have an edge, so the bigger I bet the more I'll make" is the single most expensive sentence in retail trading. It is wrong in a way the spreadsheet hides until your account is empty.

The simulator below runs a bettor with a genuine, persistent edge through 1,000 parallel universes. Same edge, same number of bets, same probability of winning each one — only the bet size changes. The output is not the average final bankroll. It is something more useful: the fraction of universes in which the bettor is ruined (defined as bankroll dropping below 20% of starting capital, which in practice ends most trading careers[2]).

Set bet size to 5% with a 5% edge — ruin is rare, the median compounds nicely. Now crank bet size to 40% with the same edge and hit Simulate. Half the universes are wiped out. The edge didn't change. Only the sizing did. Notice the mean stays high while the median collapses — that is the volatility tax in action.

Well, actually 🧐 — why the mean and median diverge so violently

Bankroll multiplies, it doesn't add. Win 50%, lose 50%, you end at 1.5 × 0.5 = 0.75 — a 25% loss, not breakeven. That asymmetry is called the volatility tax, and it scales with the square of bet size. Double your sizing, the volatility drag quadruples.

The mean of a log-normal distribution is pulled up by the small number of universes where the bettor got lucky over and over and ended at 100×. The median — the typical outcome — is what you'll actually experience. When the gap between mean and median is enormous, you are betting too big. Kelly is precisely the size that maximises the median, not the mean.

The lesson is uncomfortable for anyone who confuses bigger bets with bigger conviction. Every investor who has blown up on options, leverage, or concentrated positions did so with what they believed was an edge. They were sometimes even right about the edge. The instrument that destroyed them was the position size, applied to an edge that was real but smaller than they sized for.

V.

Why do you never go all-in at one price?

Even after the Kelly math says 11% and the cap pulls it down to 2.5%, there is one more step before money moves: you split that 2.5% into three tranches and deploy them over time. Whether entering a new position or averaging into an existing one, you divide the intended allocation into three roughly equal parts.

Why three? Because nobody — not me, not Buffett, not any algorithm — can consistently call the bottom of a stock or a market. Saying "this looks good at current levels" is a statement about risk-reward, not about whether it can fall another 15–20% from here. It absolutely can. If it does, I want dry powder to buy more at better prices.

A concrete example. When I see a stock giving a breakout and members already sitting on healthy profits, what do I tell new investors? Do not invest more than 33% as your first tranche. Invest 33 rupees, keep 66 for later. That is the one-third rule. You are building a position, not making a bet.

The same logic governs downward averaging. When Natco Pharma dropped 28%, I did not dump remaining allocated capital at once. I averaged down to bring the loss to 14%, then deliberately left one more round of money in reserve. If the stock falls further, that final tranche goes in at even lower prices. If it recovers from here, that money funds the next idea.

Here is how the three tranches sit in time:

  • Tranche 1 (33%): Initial entry. Risk-reward looks good at current levels. If the stock immediately runs up, you have exposure — you didn't miss the move entirely.
  • Tranche 2 (33%): Deploys on either a pullback to a better support (cheaper average) or a consolidation that confirms direction (more confidence). Either way, more capital with information you didn't have on day one.
  • Tranche 3 (33%): Final deployment. Either you are upward averaging into a confirmed trend, or completing a downward average at a meaningfully lower cost basis.

Buy things slowly. Upward average slowly. Buy quality firms, where you can handle a 20% downturn.

The tranche approach is not really about getting a better average price — that is the bonus. It is fundamentally about managing your psychology. Go all-in and watch the stock drop 15% the next week and you feel trapped. No more capital. Every further drop is a wound you cannot treat. Buy one-third and the same 15% drop feels like opportunity — capital is ready, the falling price is a chance to improve the position. That difference in emotional state, more than any technical edge, is what separates investors who survive cycles from those who don't.

This also applies to the supposed "stocks you might miss." Names moving fast in the news, weekly breakouts on social media. Same answer: do not bulk buy. We buy at consolidation points. We tranche. If the stock runs without us, fine — one missed move is the price of not blowing up on a chase.

VI.

Why does the optimal sizing curve turn down, not flatline?

The intuition most investors carry is that returns scale roughly linearly with sizing — risk more, make more, with diminishing returns past some point. That is wrong in a specific, dangerous way. Plot your long-run growth rate against bet size and the curve does not flatten past the optimum. It turns over and goes negative. Bet bigger than Kelly and you make less money, not more. Bet much bigger than Kelly and you compound your way to zero with mathematical certainty, even with a real edge.

The mathematics for the simple repeated bet is exact. The expected log-growth per bet is:

G(f) = p · ln(1 + b·f) + q · ln(1 − f) G(f) = expected log-growth per bet at fraction f · maximised at f* = (bp − q)/b

Take the derivative, set to zero, you get Kelly. But the shape of G(f) — the curve, not the peak — is the part that changes how you think. Drag the sliders below and stare at the second crossing point: the bet size where growth returns to zero. Everything to the right of that crossing is bankroll death, slowly at first and then all at once.

Well, actually 🧐 — the cliff is steeper than the climb

If you look closely at the curve, the slope down-from-Kelly is steeper than the slope up-to-Kelly. The penalty for over-sizing is larger than the reward for hitting the peak exactly. This is why fractional Kelly — deliberately sizing below the optimum — is so popular: it sits on the gentle uphill side, where being a bit off is barely a cost. Sitting at exactly Kelly is a knife edge. Sitting past Kelly is a slide.

The practical translation: when in doubt about your edge, halve the size. The penalty for being too conservative is small. The penalty for being too aggressive is unbounded.

This is why every section so far has been pulling you toward smaller bets than your gut says. The 5% cap, the 33% tranches, the 2.5% on riskier names — they are all empirical safeguards against landing on the wrong side of this curve. The cliff is invisible during the climb because you make money on the way up. It only becomes visible when you've already gone over it, and by then there is no money left to size.

VII.

Why is cash a position, not the absence of one?

Most investors think of cash as the absence of a position — money not working, dead weight in the portfolio. They are wrong. Cash is a position. And it might be the most important one you hold.

The Indian market has corrected by 10% or more at least four times in the last 2.5 years. These corrections are bulk-buy opportunities, but you can only exploit them if you have cash ready to deploy. If you are 100% invested when the market drops 15%, you are down 15% with no way to improve the situation except waiting. If you are 80% invested with 20% in cash, you are down only about 12% on the invested portion (≈9.6% on total) and you have that 20% cash ready to deploy at prices 15% cheaper. When the market recovers — which it always does eventually from those levels — your cost basis is materially lower than the investor who was fully invested through the drop.

Historically, whenever the market has been 15% from its peak, buyers at those levels have made roughly 20% CAGR over the next three-year period[3]. You cannot capture that CAGR if you have nothing to deploy at the moment of opportunity. The cash isn't earning a yield; it is earning optionality on a future re-rating, which is worth far more.

How much cash, and when to deploy:

  • Normal markets: 10–20% cash reserve.
  • Elevated or uncertain markets (current top-of-cycle conditions): 20–30%.
  • Deployment trigger: Identify the broad support levels on the index. As the market approaches them, deploy in tranches — not all at once, in stages.
  • Order of buying: Safest assets first — NIFTY 50 ETFs, bank ETFs, large-cap stalwarts where principal risk is near zero. Only when the trend reverses and there is clarity do you add individual-name risk.

This is NOT the market where you bulk buy and lose money. Buy things slowly. Upward average slowly. Buy quality firms, where you can handle a 20% downturn.

Cash also prevents panic, which is its under-appreciated second job. One of the primary goals of intelligent investing is to avoid panic — if I can help you do that one thing, you eventually make very good money. Cash on the side is the single best anti-panic device. When the market is falling and invested positions are red, knowing you have dry powder removes the emotional urgency to sell. You are not a victim of the downturn. You are a buyer waiting for better prices. That reframing alone changes everything.

The amateur sees a 15% correction and freezes — "what if it falls more?" — holding cash waiting for the perfect bottom. The professional sees the same correction and starts buying systematically at predetermined support levels. They know the bottom is unknowable, so they do not try to call it. They execute the plan. The plan is what you build during the calm, so you can run it during the storm.

VIII.

Why does discipline beat intelligence over a career?

Everything written so far is intellectually simple. The Kelly formula, the 5% rule, tranche buying, cash reserves — none of this is rocket science. A reasonably bright twenty-year-old could understand all of it in an afternoon. The hard part is not the understanding. The hard part is doing it. Consistently. For years. Especially when every emotional impulse in your body is screaming at you to do the opposite.

Here is what happens in practice. You find a stock you love. Research done, numbers beautiful, story compelling. Your rational allocation plan says: 3% in three tranches. Your emotions say: this is a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity, put 15% in right now. Or the opposite: a stock you own has been falling for weeks. Discipline says you are at the 5% limit, don't add. Emotions say if you just average down one more time you can break even on the next bounce.

In both cases the emotional response feels like the smart move in the moment. In both cases, following it will eventually destroy you. Maybe not this time. Maybe you get lucky and the 15% bet works out. But over a career, over hundreds of decisions, breaking your position-sizing rules will catch you in a catastrophic loss that wipes out years of gains. The Kelly cliff doesn't care that you felt confident about this one.

I cannot overstate this: investing is a risk-management game. Buying good things cheap is the easiest risk-management strategy there is. The returns are a byproduct of managing risk well, not the other way around.

You can only do all this if you stop tracking your portfolio on a daily basis. When there is a war going on, even if you are the best warrior, things on the battlefield always look ugly.

The daily tracking habit is one of the worst things retail investors do to themselves. Every red number triggers an emotional response. Every green number triggers a different emotional response. Neither is useful for long-term decisions; they just create noise that makes it harder to stick to the system. The portfolio doesn't need you to watch it. The system needs you to follow it.

Two more comparison traps worth naming. First: people listen to podcasts, follow social media, see others posting 100% gains on a single stock. What they do not see is the position size. The 100% might be on 1% of portfolio — a 1% impact — while the same poster's concentrated bets are quietly down 30%, dragging the real portfolio. You never see the full picture. Stop comparing your returns to individual stock stories and start treating your portfolio as a system. Second: the stock pick without the position size is half a sentence. Telling someone to "buy Bandhan Bank" without "and cap it at 2.5%" is like telling them to drive to Delhi without mentioning the highway. They might get there. They might end up somewhere very different.

My hit rate is 60%. Forty percent of the picks are wrong. If those losers were concentrated positions, the portfolio would be in serious trouble. But because every position is sized appropriately for its risk, the 60% winners more than compensate. The math works not because I'm a genius stock picker — I'm not — but because the position sizing ensures mistakes are small and successes are large relative to the risk taken. That asymmetry, applied over thousands of decisions, is the entire game.

IX.

The seven-rule playbook and your next move

Stack everything together into a concrete framework that runs without you having to re-derive it during a drawdown.

Rule 1. The 5% hard ceiling. No single stock ever exceeds 5% of total portfolio. Small caps and turnaround names cap at 2–2.5%. Speculative pre-IPO at 1–2%. No exceptions, not for the highest-conviction idea, not ever. This rule alone, followed rigorously, prevents most of the worst outcomes retail investors actually experience.

Rule 2. The three-tranche entry. Every new position built in three roughly equal tranches. Tranche 1 on identification, tranche 2 on pullback or confirmation, tranche 3 on maximum information. If the stock runs away before all three deploy, that is fine — you captured some of the move. If it falls, remaining tranches go in at better prices.

Rule 3. Size from Kelly, then halve. If you have a defensible estimate of p and b, compute Kelly. Then bet quarter to half of that — the cliff is steeper than the climb. The half-Kelly bettor gives up ~25% of long-run growth in exchange for radically tighter drawdowns. That trade is almost always worth it.

Rule 4. The asset-allocation foundation. Before picking any stock, decide the macro allocation: equities vs real estate vs gold/BTC, India vs US vs other markets, large vs mid/small. Reasonable starting weights for a long-horizon investor:

Rule 5. The cash reserve. 10–20% cash in normal markets, 20–30% in elevated or uncertain ones. The cash is not dead money. It is ammunition for the next correction, of which there will be several over any multi-year cycle.

Rule 6. The averaging discipline. Before adding to any position, three checks: does the thesis still hold? Is the position within its cap? Do I have remaining tranches? If any answer is no, you do not add. For upward averaging, replace "thesis holds" with "still in a confirmed uptrend and not in overvalued territory." Cost-basis psychology does not enter either decision — only forward expected return does.

Rule 7. The anti-panic protocol. When markets are falling and news is terrifying, the position-sizing system is the anchor. You don't panic-sell, because no single position is large enough to destroy the portfolio. You don't fear-buy, because the tranche plan paces deployment. You don't overreact, because cash reserves are ready for predetermined support levels. The system runs the show, not the emotions.

If you remember one thing

How much you invest matters more than what you invest in. Edge alone doesn't compound. Edge plus survival does. The Kelly cliff, the 5% ceiling, and the three-tranche rule are all the same idea in three costumes: bet small enough that being wrong is recoverable, and you will be there for the small number of right answers that pay for the whole career.

So — given all of the above — what should you actually do?

If you are starting from scratch: don't trade. Spend a month writing down the position size you would assign to every name you currently own or would consider. Then compare against the table in Section III. Most readers find at least one position over its cap. Fix that first, before adding anything new.

If you already invest but ad hoc: commit one rule from this article and run it for a quarter. The 5% cap is the highest-leverage one. Tranche buying is the second. You don't need to adopt all seven on day one. You need one to become a habit before the next layers on.

If you already size deliberately: the marginal improvement is Kelly-calibration on your highest-conviction positions and the cash discipline in elevated markets. Most disciplined investors I know carry too little cash at tops and too much at bottoms — the exact opposite of optimal. The fix is mechanical, not analytical: pre-commit deployment to support levels and trigger automatically.

The mental shift is the actual product of this article: position sizing is not a defensive afterthought. It is the strategy. The stock pick is the tactic. Every serious portfolio in the world — hedge fund, family office, endowment, sovereign wealth — runs the sizing math first and the picking second. The retail story that sizing is "boring risk-management" while picking is "the real skill" is a marketing narrative from people who profit when you trade more, not when you compound more.

Build better systems. The most powerful move in investing is not finding the next multibagger. It is being properly sized for the multibaggers you already own, and being properly sized for the losers you are about to discover you also own.

That is the art of position sizing. And it is, without question, the most important skill you will ever develop as an investor.
