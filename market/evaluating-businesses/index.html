I.

What actually separates an investor from a gambler?

Most people who call themselves investors are doing something much closer to gambling. They buy on tips, on headlines, on the WhatsApp group's enthusiasm. No framework, no checklist, no way to decide whether a business is worth owning at any price, let alone the price being asked. Then they wonder why a decade goes by and the portfolio has done nothing.

The difference is not intelligence or access to information. The professionals do not have a secret data feed. What they have is a small set of mechanical filters they apply to every business before any rupee moves. Filters that, when violated, mean the position simply does not get taken — no matter how good the story sounds.

This article is those filters. The core is two numbers and a six-line checklist. Return on invested capital. Operating margin trend. Six binary questions about the business. Apply them consistently, and the disasters — the Lenskart at 200 PE, the Adani at 4% ROIC, the Tesla on narrative — filter themselves out before they touch your portfolio.

The three simulators in this piece exist because the math here is genuinely counter-intuitive. A 25%-ROIC business compounding at 5% top-line growth crushes a 8%-ROIC business compounding at 25% top-line growth, over a long enough horizon. That sentence is not a typo. Widget two shows you why.

Your competition is not other investors. It is inflation. The goal is not stock tips; it is the framework that produces stock decisions.

II.

What does ROIC really tell you?

If you were allowed one number to evaluate a business, it would be Return on Invested Capital. ROIC tells you how many cents of after-tax operating profit the business generates for every dollar it has put to work in the operation — factories, software, working capital, acquisitions, every form of deployed capital. It is the answer to the only question that matters: does this business multiply the capital it absorbs, or dissolve it?

ROIC = NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital
NOPAT = Operating Income × (1 − tax rate) Invested Capital = total debt + total equity − cash & equivalents

Then compare ROIC to the company's Weighted Average Cost of Capital — what it costs to fund that invested capital from debt and equity holders combined. For most large-caps, WACC sits in the 8–12% band. The single rule that explains more outperformance and underperformance than any other:

  • ROIC > WACC by a wide margin: every rupee reinvested makes the business more valuable. The compounding engine is running.
  • ROIC ≈ WACC: the business is treading water. Growth produces no shareholder value, just bigger numbers.
  • ROIC < WACC: every rupee reinvested makes shareholders poorer. The business is grinding capital into dust.

Take Adani Enterprise. Reported ROIC has hovered around 4–6% for years while WACC sits around 11–12%. The math says the more the group grows, the more wealth disappears — which is exactly what shareholders watched happen over the last four years: roughly −22% returns despite headline revenue expansion[1]. Now compare to Meta. ROIC compresses in heavy-capex years (the Metaverse build, the current AI infrastructure cycle) but the long-run average sits well into the high-20s — double the cost of capital. Same revenue-growth headline, opposite shareholder outcome.

Well, actually 🧐 — how to compute invested capital cleanly

The textbook definition of invested capital is "all capital subject to a return requirement." In practice, the cleanest computation is: total debt + book equity − cash and marketable securities. The cash subtraction matters — a company sitting on $80B of cash (looking at you, Apple) shouldn't be charged a return-on-equity for capital that's literally earning Treasury yield in a money-market fund.

For comparing across companies, the more robust variant is ROIIC — return on incremental invested capital. Take the change in NOPAT over five years, divide by the change in invested capital. This strips out legacy moats and tells you what new capital is actually earning today. Many great old businesses have wonderful ROIC and terrible ROIIC — growth opportunities are gone and they're forced to reinvest at much lower rates.

The Adani example is sometimes told as a politics story. It isn't. It's an arithmetic story. A business cannot, by physics, compound shareholder wealth while consistently earning less on its capital than that capital costs. The connections may win contracts; they cannot manufacture returns on those contracts that exceed WACC. Eventually, the balance sheet stretches, the borrowing gets weirder, and the stock reflects the underlying value destruction. Always.

III.

Why does growth without ROIC destroy value?

Most retail investors are obsessed with one number: revenue growth. "It's growing 40% a year!" "TAM is $1 trillion!" "This is the next Amazon!" Almost none of them ask the follow-up that decides whether the growth is worth anything: at what return on capital?

Here is the mental model. Imagine two companies, both growing revenue at 20% per year. Company A reinvests every dollar of incremental capital at a 25% ROIC. Company B reinvests at 6%. Both look identical on the revenue line. Over a decade, A multiplies shareholder equity roughly 6×; B barely keeps up with inflation. Same growth rate, different worlds, because growth is just the multiplier; ROIC is the rate.

Four quadrants, four very different investment outcomes:

  • Top-right — compounders (high ROIC, high growth). These are the businesses that turn a small position today into a meaningful one in a decade. Visa, Microsoft, ASML, Mastercard. Rare, expensive, worth paying up for — within reason.
  • Top-left — cash cows (high ROIC, low growth). The runway is shorter but every reinvested dollar still earns well above cost of capital. Coca-Cola, ITC, large utilities. Excellent dividend machines.
  • Bottom-right — growth at any price (low ROIC, high growth). Tempting; lethal. The growth absorbs huge capital at returns below WACC, so revenue compounds but per-share value does not. Tesla, Uber for most of its history, most consumer internet land-grabs.
  • Bottom-left — value traps (low ROIC, low growth). The stock screen will show them as "cheap." They are cheap because they deserve to be. Many Indian PSUs and most legacy telco.

The matrix collapses to one rule of thumb: only the top half of the chart is investable as a buy-and-hold. The bottom-right may work as a trading vehicle on narrative; the bottom-left may work as a deep-value bet if you have a catalyst; neither belongs in a long-term portfolio.

Well, actually 🧐 — the "growth destroys value" math

The formal version of this is in any corporate finance textbook. A business's intrinsic value can be approximated as: V = NOPAT1 × (1 − g/ROIC) ÷ (WACC − g). When ROIC equals WACC, the value reduces to NOPAT/WACC — growth literally adds nothing. When ROIC is below WACC, the (1 − g/ROIC) term flips negative, so more growth makes the company less valuable.

This is the math behind why the market often refuses to give a high PE to a fast-growing but capital-hungry business. The market is correct; the equation says the growth is destroying value. Tesla's PE has compressed every year that capex has scaled faster than incremental margins. The matrix is just what the formula looks like with the y-axis flipped to ROIC.

IV.

How does a 25% ROIC business beat a 25% growth business?

Section III made the claim. This section proves it with the calculator that decides it. The right framework for what a stock is worth over decades is not its growth rate — it is the rate at which its per-share value compounds. And per-share value compounds at roughly:

per-share growth ≈ ROIC × reinvestment rate + dividend yield reinvestment rate = fraction of NOPAT plowed back into the business

Read that formula one more time, because it is the entire game. A business with 25% ROIC that reinvests 60% of profits compounds value at 25% × 60% = 15% per year, plus whatever it pays out as dividend. A business with 8% ROIC that reinvests 100% of profits compounds at only 8%. Higher growth, slower compounding — because the reinvestment is at a much worse rate.

The interesting comparison is between B (mid-cap with balanced ROIC and growth) and C (the growth-at-any-price story). Both look glamorous on top-line. B compounds at 15%; C tops out at its 8% ROIC because that is the actual rate at which new capital is earning. The headline 25% revenue growth is being purchased at a cost-of-capital-destroying return. After twenty years, B has multiplied capital roughly 16×; C, only about 4.6×[2].

And the quiet winner over a long enough horizon is often Business A, the "boring" cash cow that pays a small dividend and reinvests a bit at very high returns. Crank the years slider up to 30 and watch it overtake. The lesson is not that growth doesn't matter — it does. The lesson is that the quality of growth, the ROIC at which each marginal rupee is reinvested, matters dramatically more than the headline growth rate.

The compounding rule

Over a long enough horizon, the stock with the highest per-share value growth — not the highest revenue growth — wins by a margin that looks like a different asset class. The fastest path to that growth is ROIC, not topline. Hunt for ROIC. Pay for ROIC. Hold ROIC.

V.

Which metric predicts the next five years of stock price?

If ROIC tells you whether a business creates value at all, operating margin tells you which direction it is heading. And the direction of operating margins is, empirically, one of the cleanest forward-looking signals in equity investing.

The reason is mechanical. Earnings = revenue × margin. When margin expands, earnings grow faster than revenue — you get a double compounding effect. When margin compresses, even strong revenue growth can produce flat or falling earnings. And because PE multiples tend to follow earnings trajectory, margin expansion drives both numerator (E) and denominator (P) of returns.

Notice Tesla. Headline revenue grew. Margins compressed. The stock went sideways for years even with continued top-line expansion. The market knows the formula: revenue without margin is not earnings, and stock prices respond to earnings.

The five questions to ask when you see a margin trend:

  1. What is driving it? Pricing power and operating leverage are durable. Cost-cutting has a floor.
  2. How does it compare to industry peers? A 12% margin in a 4% industry is a moat in disguise.
  3. Where is the high-margin segment growing? Amazon's overall margin keeps expanding because AWS (a 35%+ margin business) grows faster than retail (a 5% margin business). The mix shift does the work.
  4. Is capex compressing margins for the right reason? Meta's current margin dip funds infrastructure that, if it pays off, expands the margin base materially in 2027–2028.
  5. Is competition forcing the trend? Tesla's margin compression came from BYD pricing pressure — structural and durable. NVIDIA's might come from Trainium/Inferentia and that is the genuine risk in the thesis.
Well, actually 🧐 — margins versus contribution margins

For platform and software businesses, the headline operating margin can hide the real story. A SaaS company spending heavily on sales-and-marketing to acquire customers will show compressed near-term margins even if the underlying unit economics — the contribution margin per customer cohort — are world-class.

The trick is to look at gross margin (a cleaner unit-economic signal) alongside the long-run customer lifetime value vs CAC ratio. A 80%+ gross margin with LTV/CAC of 3×+ and shrinking sales-spend-as-percent-of-revenue is the signature of a healthy software business even if operating margin looks ugly today.

VI.

What does a real competitive moat look like?

ROIC and margins are results. The thing that produces them, year after year, is competitive position — the famous "moat." Buffett popularised the metaphor; most investors use it like a vibe rather than a framework. The cleaner version is to break the moat into three explicit dimensions and score each one separately.

Research moat. Can the company attract the world's best talent and outspend competitors on R&D? Microsoft, NVIDIA, ASML, and Google score nine or ten here. Their R&D budgets are larger than most public companies' entire revenue. A decade of cumulative research is genuinely hard to replicate with a single year of spending.

Distribution moat. Can the company put a product in front of nearly anyone, faster than anyone else? Meta is the textbook case — roughly 40% of the planet uses one of its products daily. Once distribution is built, every incremental product launch has a starting audience in the billions. In India, the Zomato/Swiggy duopoly in food delivery is the same shape at smaller scale: rider fleets and restaurant relationships that a new entrant cannot buy at any reasonable price.

Product moat. Does the company control something competitors literally cannot replicate? ASML's EUV lithography is the canonical example — physically required for advanced chip manufacture, with no substitute. TSMC's 3nm fabrication. Visa's processing network. These are not businesses where "competition will catch up." Competition cannot catch up without rebuilding multi-decade engineering work.

Great companies score highly on at least two of the three. Eternal (Zomato's parent) shows what happens when one is missing: distribution 9, research 8, product 4 (because the food itself is commodity FMCG that customers will switch on for a five-rupee price difference). At 1500 PE, the market is paying compounder valuations for a two-moat company that scores low on the moat that matters most for retention. That math does not work.

Meta scores high on all three, which is why it remains a genuine compounder. Distribution is the daily-active platform. Research is the $50B+ annual R&D budget. Product moat is being rebuilt right now — the AI infrastructure layer (Meta Compute) is the bid to own the product moat for the next decade. If it works, the stock re-rates dramatically; if it doesn't, the business still has two intact moats. That is what asymmetric upside looks like.

VII.

The six-point investability checklist

ROIC and moats answer the "is this a quality business" question. The six-point checklist answers the operational follow-up: is this quality business investable right now, at this price, in this regime?

The six points, in order of how often they kill an investment thesis:

  1. High ROIC. The single binary filter. Above 15% on a five-year average — pass. Between 8 and 15 — conditional, depends on whether it's improving. Below 8 — not investable as a compounder.
  2. Low net debt. Net debt to EBITDA below 2× means the business is not financially fragile. Above 4× means a single bad quarter can trigger a covenant breach.
  3. Wide moat. Score at least 7/10 on at least two of the three moats from Section VI.
  4. Capable management. Do they buy back stock when cheap and issue when expensive? Are capital allocation decisions defensible in hindsight? Is there a track record of compounding book value per share?
  5. Secular tailwind. Is the industry growing 8%+ structurally, or is it a mature/declining vertical where you need to outcompete the cycle?
  6. Fair price. Forward PE below the industry median, or EV/EBITDA below the company's own five-year average. Even a great business is a bad investment at the wrong entry.

The discipline of forcing a binary pass/fail on six independent questions is what stops you buying because "the story is good." Stories are how people lose money. Checklists are how people compound.

VIII.

Why are PE and EV/EBITDA never interchangeable?

Even the best business in the world is a bad investment at the wrong price. Item six of the checklist matters. But "valuation" is not one number; the metric you use should match the kind of business you're valuing.

PE (price ÷ earnings per share) works for asset-light businesses where most of the value is in the income statement — tech, financial services, consumer brands. It implicitly assumes the capital structure (debt vs equity) is roughly normal for the sector.

EV/EBITDA (enterprise value ÷ EBITDA) works for capital-intensive businesses where debt is part of the operating model and depreciation distorts net income — hospitals, real estate, infrastructure, bottling, utilities. Enterprise Value adds back debt and subtracts cash, giving you the true price to acquire the entire business; EBITDA strips out the non-cash and capital-structure noise.

Use the wrong one and you systematically misprice. A hospital chain on PE looks expensive because depreciation eats into net income; on EV/EBITDA the same business looks reasonable. A SaaS company on EV/EBITDA looks gorgeous because there's no debt; on PE it might look fully priced because option-based stock comp is sneaking into the cost line.

The Lenskart case study is the cautionary tale. Reported PE around 200. Operating margin 1.7%. The founder allegedly took a 200-crore personal loan to buy stock at an 8,500-crore valuation; three months later the IPO valuation was 70,000 crore[3]. No proprietary technology, no global expansion path, no margin profile that supports a tech multiple. The PE is not a typo; it is a sentiment number waiting to compress. When growth slows even slightly, the multiple has nowhere to go but down — and that is where the worst kind of capital destruction happens, because investors keep averaging down on what they think is a quality name.

The valuation discipline

A high PE is fine if growth and ROIC justify it. A high PE on a business that has slowing growth or low ROIC is a trap that takes years to unwind. The market eventually re-rates. It always does.

Well, actually 🧐 — the currency tailwind nobody tells you about

When you compare an Indian stock to a US stock, you are not comparing apples to apples. The INR has depreciated against the USD at roughly 3–4% CAGR for the last two decades. If you buy a US stock today at $100 and it does nothing for three years, you still earn the currency move — about 10% over that window for an Indian investor.

So a US business compounding earnings at 15% per year is, for an Indian holder, effectively compounding at 18–19% before tax. That is the unspoken tailwind on the global portfolio — and the unspoken headwind on the home-only one. Most mutual-fund marketing material will not mention this because it would weaken the case for Indian SIPs.

IX.

Putting it together: a complete evaluation system

You now have every tool you need. The full evaluation workflow takes about 30 minutes for any new stock, and the steps are sequential — if a step fails, stop and move on; do not rationalise.

Step 1 — ROIC vs WACC (2 minutes). If ROIC is consistently below WACC, stop. The Adani check. This alone filters out a quarter of the market.

Step 2 — Three moats (5 minutes). Score research, distribution, product. Need 7+/10 on at least two. Eternal at 200 PE fails here.

Step 3 — Six-point checklist (10 minutes). Run Widget 3 honestly. 6/6 is rare and earns aggressive sizing. 5/6 is the sweet spot. 4/6 is a small position. Below 4 is a pass. I run this checklist on every position before sizing it — not as a ritual, but because skipping it is how I have personally bought value traps.

Step 4 — Numbers in three timeframes (10 minutes). Operating margin trajectory over five years. EPS CAGR over three, five, and ten years. Revenue quality — what share comes from the highest-margin segments? FCF growing or being eaten by capex?

Step 5 — Match valuation to business model (5 minutes). PE for asset-light, EV/EBITDA for capital-heavy, EV/EBIT for businesses with weird depreciation. Compare to peer median and to the company's own five-year average. If both are stretched and growth is slowing, wait.

Step 6 — Size to conviction.

Step 7 — Match holding period to thesis. Structural compounders — five years minimum, do not look at the price tomorrow. Cyclical recoveries — two to three years across the cycle. Special situations — one to two years with strict position limits and predefined exit triggers.

That is the whole system. Two numbers (ROIC, operating margin), one matrix (Widget 1), one formula (per-share value compounding = ROIC × reinvestment rate + yield), and one checklist (Widget 3). Apply it consistently and the worst-case outcomes get clipped almost automatically. Every Adani-style disaster fails Step 1. Every Lenskart-style trap fails Step 5. Every value-trap PSU fails Step 3. The system will not catch every winner; no system does. But it almost never gets you killed, which is the actual job.

Your competition is not other investors. It is inflation. The businesses that compound per-share value at 15–20% per year — the ones with high ROIC, durable moats, expanding margins, and a price you didn't overpay for — those are how you win. Everything else is noise dressed up as analysis.
