I.

Why doesn't buy-and-hold work in India?

Say "India is a trading market, not an investing market" and the entire SIP-distribution complex will tell you that you're wrong. They have a financial reason to tell you that. You have a portfolio reason to ignore them.

Here is the uncomfortable arithmetic. Over the eighteen months ending mid-2025, broad-market Indian investors made roughly zero percent in nominal terms. Adjusted for the rupee's drift against the dollar, slightly negative. The Mutual Fund Sahi Hai crowd built an industry on top of a US-flavoured narrative — buy quality, hold forever, let compounding work its magic — and then quietly raised expense ratios by 10% while no one was looking[1].

The reason that narrative works in the United States and doesn't work here is structural. The S&P 500 is, in practice, a portfolio of seven mega-cap monopolies — Apple, Microsoft, Google, Amazon, NVIDIA, Meta, Tesla — riding global technology adoption denominated in the world's reserve currency. Hold the index and you ride the monopoly. India has no equivalent stack at that scale. What India has is a wide, cyclical, sentiment-driven market where individual sectors take turns leading and turns dying.

Five concrete reasons buy-and-hold fails here:

  1. Sectors are violently cyclical. The real return ceiling for the index is roughly inflation plus two-to-three percent — call it 12-13% pre-tax. That figure holds, but only if you're in the right sector at the right time. Bought IT, hospitals, or PSUs at the top and you waited years for nothing.
  2. Valuations rarely stay cheap. When a sector is hot, it gets bid to absurdity. When it turns, it can stay dead for two or three years. Capital trapped in a sideways stock is capital not compounding.
  3. The taxman keeps moving the goalposts. LTCG was 0% before 2018, then 10%, then 12.5%. Every step up erodes the compounding edge that long holds are supposed to give you[2].
  4. The rupee bleeds your real return. A 12% INR CAGR with 4-5% annual depreciation and rising LTCG nets out to mediocrity once you measure in purchasing power.
  5. SIP flows have changed market structure. Domestic institutional buyers are now the counter-flow to FII selling. That puts a floor under prices but also prevents the deep, sustained corrections that would make passive entries cheap. You get a long, sideways grind — paradise for rotators, purgatory for holders.
Well, actually 🧐 — the US comparison is overstated

It is fashionable in Indian finance circles to claim "US buy-and-hold works because of the Mag 7." That's partially true and partially propaganda. From 2000 to 2013, the S&P 500 produced essentially zero real return — a thirteen-year sideways grind featuring two 50% drawdowns. Buy-and-hold investors who started in 1999 watched their portfolios go nowhere for over a decade.

The 2013-2024 mega-cap-tech super-cycle is the exception, not the rule. Even American investors had to live through long flat periods where active rotation would have crushed passive holding. The lesson generalises: no market trends forever. The American experience for the last decade simply rhymes with the Indian experience of the next decade. India is just earlier in the cycle of figuring out what to do about it.

The point

India does not reward the investor who sits still. It rewards the investor who reads the regime, moves capital to where the risk-reward is best right now, and books profits at resistance. This isn't speculation. It's a different operating system for a different market.

Consider HDFC Bank. Impeccable fundamentals. Record results. Growing deposits, expanding loan book, clean asset quality. And for long stretches, dead money. The stock eventually catches up to the economy. But in the interim, your capital is hostage. The alternative is to take control of your own allocation — and that starts with seeing what rotation actually looks like.

II.

What does capital rotation actually look like?

Capital rotation is the simplest investing strategy ever described. It is also one of the hardest to execute, because it requires you to sell things you're emotionally bonded to.

The strategy in one line: buy what is cheap. Hold until it becomes fairly valued. Sell, and move the proceeds to the next cheap thing. Repeat.

That's it. The art lies entirely in three places: identifying which sector is in which phase, sizing the position so you can survive being early, and having the discipline to sell when the thesis has played out instead of falling in love with the stock.

A concrete cycle, end to end. PNB (Punjab National Bank) was deeply hated in 2023 — fraud headlines, asset-quality fears, a PE around 18 on a depressed earnings base. Sentiment was awful, which is exactly when the math gets interesting. Build a position 10-20% of intended size. Add as the chart confirms. Hold through the slow recovery. Take it up roughly 100% over the cycle. Sell most of it. Move the proceeds to SBI and Bank of Baroda when PSU sentiment broadened out. SBI ran another 40%. By then PSU banks were no longer cheap — but private banks were unloved on a relative basis. Rotate again, this time into the Private Sector Bank Index. The capital never stopped working.

A second example, this time from the technology side. Nifty IT had corrected 35% from peak by mid-2025 — a brutal selloff driven by US client budget freezes, GenAI overhang, and the visa narrative. I was structurally bearish on long-term IT margins, but at a 35% discount, the math for a swing trade was compelling: limited downside (the sector was already cheap), 15-20% upside to the next resistance, a 1:3 risk-reward. Bought the IT Index aggressively. Within four months the sector caught a sentiment shift on GenAI revenue commentary and was up 25%. Exited at resistance. Rotated the proceeds into healthcare, which had just begun Phase 1 after a long sideways grind. The trade was never about loving IT. It was about exploiting a temporary mispricing while keeping the structural view intact.

And a third, smaller but instructive. The capital-market plays — CDSL, CAMS, Angel One — went on a tear in 2023 as F&O participation exploded. By early 2024 they were on every retail watchlist. Trim 60% of position. Rotate into FMCG names trading at their 200DMA after a long defensive underperformance. The capital-market stocks corrected 30% over the next two quarters. The FMCG positions delivered 15% on the rotation. No predictions were required. Only attention to where the crowd was, and the discipline to be elsewhere.

Every rotation follows the same five-step pattern:

  1. Identify a sector in negative sentiment. Charts ugly. Media bearish. Nobody on social media touting it. That's your entry signal — not the price chart by itself, but the chart combined with the silence around it.
  2. Build positions in tranches. 10-33% of intended size to start. The rest is dry powder for averaging down. If you're early, you want to be glad about it, not panicked.
  3. Hold through the recovery. Three to twelve months is typical. Daily price-checking is the enemy of conviction.
  4. Book profits at technically defined resistance. Not on emotion. Not on "it's been a great run." On a level you wrote down before you entered.
  5. Rotate to the next beaten-down sector. The market always has one.
Well, actually 🧐 — this isn't market timing

Capital rotation is sometimes accused of being "market timing in disguise." It isn't. Market timing predicts the direction of the index — when to be in vs. out of equities entirely. Rotation predicts the relative ranking of sectors within the index while staying near-fully invested.

The difference matters because relative-rank prediction is empirically much easier than absolute-level prediction. Sectors move on identifiable drivers — rate cuts help banks and housing, weak rupee helps IT and pharma, commodity cycles help metals. Predicting "next month, banks will outperform staples" is a far more tractable bet than "next month, the Nifty will be up."

The pattern repeats across every sector and every cycle. Zomato up 70% from entry — book 70%, rotate 30% into Swiggy on the same theme, keep a runner. CDSL/CAMS through their 2023 cycle — same drill. The Smallcap Index in 2024 — 100%+, exited before the 25% correction wiped out latecomers. The rotation isn't the trick. The trick is doing it on cue, every time, without the sentimentality that traps amateurs in losing themes.

Every sector at any moment is in one of four cyclical phases. Misidentifying the phase is the most expensive single error in active investing — buying euphoria, selling capitulation, or worse, both in the same cycle.

By the time RBI's first rate cut landed, they were already past the breakout. IT corrected 35% from peak in mid-2025 and entered Phase 1 with everyone still anti-IT structurally. Healthcare cycled from Phase 3 back to Phase 1 inside twelve months as hospital chains digested their post-COVID overvaluations. Sectors don't move in lockstep. That asynchrony is the entire opportunity.

III.

How should the mix change with the regime?

Asset allocation books tell you to pick a number — 60% equities, 40% bonds — and rebalance to it once a year. That advice was written for a different country in a different decade. In a regime-driven market, the question "what is the right mix?" has no static answer. The right mix changes when the regime changes, and the regime changes more often than annually.

The four buckets that matter, regardless of how you slice equities further:

  • Cash — option value. Pays nothing on its own, but lets you take swings when others can't.
  • Bonds — yield with low drawdown. Outperforms in risk-off; underperforms badly in risk-on.
  • Equities — the long-run engine. Highest drawdown, highest expected return.
  • Commodities — inflation hedge, regime diversifier. Gold and oil don't move with stocks.

Well, actually 🧐 — these aren't backtested numbers

The expected-return and drawdown figures in the widget are illustrative estimates of long-run averages for each asset class, weighted by allocation. They are not the output of a Monte Carlo simulation, and they are not specific to any window of Indian or global market history.

The point isn't precision. The point is the shape: as you push the slider right, expected return climbs roughly linearly, but expected drawdown climbs faster. That is the regime trade-off all allocators face. Pick a mix you can survive in the worst-case regime, not the one you'd love in the best-case.

Look at what the bar does when you slide to -1. Cash dominates. Bonds are the second-largest slice. Equities collapse to a sliver. That's not a permanent recommendation. That's the allocation of a portfolio waiting for a discount. When the discount arrives — when the market is screaming buyer's-market signals — you slide right and the allocation flips. Static allocation is for people who don't read the market.

IV.

How do you trade buy-at-support, sell-at-resistance without lying to yourself?

"Buy low, sell high" is the most over-quoted, under-executed phrase in finance. The reason almost nobody does it is psychological: the moment a stock looks cheap, it usually feels terrible, and the moment it looks expensive, it usually feels great. To execute, you need to commit to entry and exit levels before the trade — and then mechanically honour them.

Concretely: every range-bound stock and every sector index has a support level (the floor it's bounced off multiple times) and a resistance level (the ceiling it's failed at multiple times). On Nifty 50, that range was roughly 21,000 to 26,650 through most of 2024-2025. You buy near the floor, you sell near the ceiling, you wait for the next dip. A 5-8% swing inside the range, executed two or three times a year, compounds to 15-25% annual returns — while a passive holder in the same window earns zero[3].

Well, actually 🧐 — when buy-and-hold wins

Run enough paths and you'll find some where buy-and-hold beats the rotator. Those are typically the paths where the underlying breaks decisively above resistance and never looks back. The rotator sold at the ceiling and watched the stock keep climbing.

This is real, and it's the cost of running the strategy. The defence is partial exits — sell 50-70% at resistance, keep a runner. You give up some upside on breakouts to lock in profits on the more common range-bound paths. In a market that genuinely trends most of the time (US large-cap tech, 2015-2024), the rotator loses. In a market that genuinely chops (India, 2024-2025), the rotator wins. Match the strategy to the regime.

The danger of this strategy is mechanical execution without checking the underlying. If the support breaks because the company is fundamentally deteriorating — fraud, regulator action, irreversible competitive loss — the trade is not "buying support." It's catching a falling knife. The rule is simple: rotate sectors that are temporarily out of favour, not sectors that are dying. The chart tells you where to act. The fundamentals tell you whether to act.

How to identify a valid support level — three filters:

  1. Multiple touches. The level has been tested at least three times and held each time. A single bounce is noise; a third bounce is a level.
  2. Volume confirmation. Selling volume thins as price approaches the level. Buying volume picks up sharply on the bounce. This is the footprint of accumulators absorbing supply.
  3. Fundamental anchor. The price at support corresponds to a sensible valuation — say, the bottom of the historical PE range, or the dividend yield reaches a notable level. The chart and the fundamentals agree.

If all three line up, you have a high-confidence entry. If only one or two, the position size shrinks. If none, you don't take the trade — there is no edge in pretending a level exists when the market is telling you it doesn't.

V.

What signal tells you which style is in favour?

Rotation is not arbitrary. It tracks something measurable. The single best macro signal for which style — growth, value, defensives, cyclicals — is about to outperform is the shape of the yield curve.

The intuition is straightforward. Short rates reflect today's policy stance: the central bank's stamp on the cost of money right now. Long rates reflect the market's expectation of growth and inflation years out. The relationship between the two divides the world into four regimes — and each regime mechanically favours a different equity style.

  • Low short, low long — easy policy, weak growth expectations. Growth stocks win: cheap money funds long-duration cash flows, and the scarcity of growth makes the few growers valuable.
  • Low short, high long — easy policy, rising growth expectations. Cyclicals win: industrials, autos, materials, banks. The yield curve is steepening because the economy is accelerating.
  • High short, low long — tight policy, falling growth expectations. Defensives win: staples, utilities, healthcare. The curve is inverting because a recession is being priced in.
  • High short, high long — tight policy, high inflation. Value wins: short-duration cash flows, dividend payers, commodity producers. Long-duration growth gets crushed by the discount rate.

Well, actually 🧐 — the curve is necessary, not sufficient

The yield-curve map gives you the direction of rotation, not the timing. A steepening curve tells you cyclicals will outperform defensives over the next six-to-eighteen months on average; it does not tell you that Tata Motors will be up next week.

The second-order effects matter too. India's curve doesn't operate in isolation — when the US 10Y rips higher, capital flows out of EM equities regardless of where the Indian curve is. The complete signal stack is: domestic curve + USD direction + commodity backdrop + FII flow. The curve is the first read. The other three calibrate it.

Apply this to mid-2025: the US short rate is around 5.25-5.50%, the 10Y near 4.20%. That's the high-short / low-long quadrant — defensives. RBI has cut once, with more cuts expected; the Indian curve is steepening, pulling India toward the low-short / high-long quadrant — cyclicals. Both maps line up with what actually outperformed: US healthcare and staples held in while growth wobbled; Indian private banks, autos, and industrials caught the rotation as rate cuts approached. None of this required prediction. It required reading the curve.

A few historical regime transitions to anchor the framework:

  • 2020 (COVID emergency cuts). Fed Funds slashed to 0%, 10Y crashed to 0.5%. Pure growth regime. Tech, biotech, anything with long-duration cash flows ripped. Defensives went nowhere despite being defensive — the regime didn't favour them.
  • 2021 (reopening). Short rates pinned near zero, 10Y climbed past 1.5%. Steepening curve — cyclicals quadrant. Banks, autos, materials, commodities led. Growth started underperforming for the first time in years.
  • 2022 (inflation regime change). Fed hiked aggressively, short rates ripped to 4%+, 10Y to 4%. High-high — value quadrant. The energy sector was the only positive S&P sector that year. Growth was crushed by the discount rate; the Nasdaq fell 33%.
  • 2023-2024 (disinflation). Short stays high (5%+), long drifts down to 4%. High-low quadrant — defensives. Healthcare, staples, utilities held in while the market chopped sideways and only the largest mega-cap names broke out.

Notice how each of those transitions happened over months, not days. The yield curve doesn't ask you to be fast. It asks you to be paying attention.

VI.

Why do small, frequent gains beat the multibagger hunt?

Most retail investors chase the 10x. They want the next Polycab, the next Trent, the one stock that vindicates everything. The capital-rotation investor doesn't chase the 10x. They run a process that throws off 1-5% trades, frequently, on a large capital base. The math is unkind to the multibagger hunter.

Suppose you make 1.65% on a single swing trade that took a week. That figure looks unimpressive standing alone. Compound it weekly and the annualised number is (1.0165)52 − 1 = 134%. Nobody actually does 1.65% every week — but the point of the math is to show that capital velocity, not per-trade size, is what builds wealth at scale.

CAGRrotator = (1 + rper-cycle)cycles/yr − 1 where r is the per-cycle return and the exponent is the number of cycles per year

Three concrete templates:

  • Index swing. Identify a Nifty range with 5% upside to resistance. Deploy the full base, exit at the top. Three cycles a year compounds to (1.05)3 − 1 ≈ 15.8% — on the total capital, not on a sliver.
  • Sector rotation. Ride three momentum cycles a year at 15% each. (1.15)3 − 1 ≈ 52%. This is the upper end and requires getting the rotation timing right, but the cycles do happen.
  • Hybrid. One big sector cycle (25%), one Nifty swing (5%), one defensive period in cash. (1.25)(1.05)(1.00) − 1 = 31%. Realistic, repeatable, requires the discipline of holding cash when nothing is cheap.

The structural advantages of running a velocity strategy over a one-shot strategy:

  1. Risk per trade is bounded by sector or index drawdown. A 5% Nifty correction stings. A 50% smallcap blowup ends careers. Swing the index, not the lottery ticket.
  2. Capital never sits idle. The moment one trade hits target, the cash redeploys. In a buy-and-hold strategy, capital can be trapped for two years in a sideways stock.
  3. Tax-loss harvesting becomes natural. Active rotators always have losing positions to harvest against winners. Buy-and-hold investors have to manufacture this artificially.
  4. Compounding frequency increases. Compounding three times a year at 5% beats compounding once every five years at 30% — both deliver roughly 16% IRR, but only the first one frees the capital between events.

The philosophical shift is the part that takes time to internalise. You are not looking for the one stock that makes you rich. You are building a machine that throws off consistent returns through disciplined rotation. Three or four good cycles per year — every year — and you outperform 95% of retail.

VII.

When should you actually book profits?

Buying is the easy half. Selling is where the strategy lives or dies. Almost every amateur error in active investing is a selling error — selling too early, selling too late, or refusing to sell at all because the stock was "going to recover."

Six rules, in order of importance:

Rule 1. Never trim more than 50% in a single move. Unless the underlying business is broken, you scale out, you don't exit. The cost of being wrong about the top is missing the runaway move. A 50% exit captures the win, a 100% exit captures the regret. Zomato to Swiggy: 70% exit on Zomato, 30% runner, fresh position in Swiggy. The runner kept paying when Zomato kept climbing.

Rule 2. Sell at technically defined resistance, not on feel. Every stock and every index has visible resistance levels — prior highs, round numbers, multi-year ceilings. The first major resistance on Nifty 50 was 26,650. That was the exit zone. Pre-commit. Don't argue with yourself in the moment.

Rule 3. Build a profit cushion on volatile names. Stock runs from 100 to 250 (a 150% gain). Book 25% — you take 63 off the table. The remaining 187 you let ride. A 30% correction from here still leaves you net positive because the booked profits already cleared cost basis. This is how you stay invested in a runaway name without going broke when it finally cracks.

Rule 4. Think in terms of capital redeployment, not profit-taking. The right question is never "should I sell this stock?" — it's "is there a better use for this capital right now?" If AMCs are fully valued and private banks are cheap, you trim AMCs into banks. The AMC sale isn't a bearish call on AMCs. It's a relative-value rotation.

Rule 5. In a buyer's market, don't sell. The market alternates between buyer's markets (the whole index is cheap) and seller's markets (the whole index is expensive). In a buyer's market your job is to max out exposure — 85-90% invested — so you have inventory to distribute when the seller's market comes. Most investors do the opposite: panic-sell at the bottom, get euphoric at the top.

Rule 6. Cut losers to fund rotation. If you're stuck in a dying theme — say, railways during a slowdown — exit at a loss to fund the next opportunity. Holding dead money for two years to "avoid booking the loss" is a psychological trade, not a financial one. Selling 11% down hurts. Watching a 25% rotation pass you by hurts more.

If you remember one thing

Profit-booking is not a one-time event. It is a continuous portfolio management discipline. You don't sell stocks because you're sitting on gains. You sell stocks because the capital can earn more elsewhere. The amateur asks "is this a winner?" The professional asks "is this still the best use of my money?"

The hardest part is psychological. You will never sell at the exact top. You will always look back and see that you could have held longer, or that you should have exited sooner. Stop optimising for the perfect exit. Optimise for the systematic exit — pre-defined levels, mechanical execution, disciplined redeployment.

Well, actually 🧐 — the tax implications of frequent rotation

Rotation creates short-term gains that are taxed at slab rates in India, which can hit 30%+ for high-income brackets. Long-term capital gains (held more than one year) get the gentler 12.5% treatment. So shouldn't you hold for a year and a day to get the lower rate?

Two answers. First, the math: a 12% short-term gain taxed at 30% nets 8.4%. A 6% long-term gain taxed at 12.5% nets 5.25%. The post-tax IRR on faster rotation can still win — as long as the per-cycle returns are large enough to overcome the tax differential. Second, the workaround: most active rotators use a hybrid where the swing sleeve is in indices and ETFs (which can be held longer and traded more efficiently) while the core sleeve qualifies for LTCG by design. The tax tail wags the strategy dog less than commonly thought.

VIII.

How do you build a portfolio designed to rotate?

A rotation portfolio is structurally different from a buy-and-hold portfolio. It needs persistent cash, modular sector allocations, clear exit levels for every position, and a core that doesn't trade. Here is the template I run, adapted from years of running this strategy through Indian and US markets.

Layer 1: Asset-class split (the macro skeleton).

My own current split: 70% equities, 25% real estate, 5% crypto (mostly BTC). The equity sleeve is where rotation happens. Real estate gives cash flow plus leverage and doesn't correlate intra-day with equities, which dampens portfolio volatility. Crypto is the asymmetric alpha bet — small allocation, large potential payoff, willingness to write the whole sleeve to zero.

Layer 2: Within equities, core plus swing.

  • Core (40-50% of equity). Three-to-five year positions in structural growth. Hospitals, healthcare, select private banks, premium financials. Criteria: 15%+ ROIC, 15%+ cash flow growth, market leadership. You buy on dips and hold across cycles — these are the chassis, not the cargo.
  • Swing (50-60% of equity). Actively rotated. Nifty swings, sector index trades, tactical entries in beaten-down themes. Pre-defined exit levels. Holding periods measured in months, not years.

Layer 3: Geographic diversification.

My target split is trending toward 40% India / 60% US over time. Why so US-heavy? Because the next wave — robotics, AI, ML, semiconductors — sits in the US. India is overweight financials and consumption; you need US exposure to participate in the technology stack. Diversification across geographies is no longer optional for a serious portfolio.

Layer 4: Cash reserve for tactical deployment.

Always 10-20% in cash. This is your option value — the ability to take a swing when others are forced sellers. The cash isn't dead money. It's the most valuable asset you own when everyone else is panicking. When the budget setup appeared in early 2025 with 1:2 risk-reward (5% downside, 12-13% upside), I deployed that reserve. Without it, I would have been a spectator.

The cash reserve has a strict refill rule: every time a swing position hits target and gets booked, the next-highest priority is replenishing cash back to the 15% target. Only after cash is restored does fresh deployment happen. This stops the very common error of "the trade worked, let me redeploy 100% into the next opportunity" — which leaves you cashless when the better opportunity arrives a month later.

Layer 5: Indices for size, individual stocks for alpha.

When the macro is unclear, start with the Index. Sell targets are clearer, and you can take larger position sizes. I deployed 40 lakhs into the Nifty IT Index as a single swing — I would not have done that with one IT stock. As the picture clarifies, rotate from the index into individual names within the winning sector.

Layer 6: Position sizing for survival.

No single smallcap above 2.5% of total portfolio. Non-negotiable. A 50% drawdown on a 2.5% position is a 1.25% portfolio hit — survivable. A 50% drawdown on a 10% position is a 5% portfolio hit, which sounds fine on a spreadsheet but ruins your decision-making at the moment it matters most. Sector indices can run heavier — 10-30% of equity — because the diversification within the basket caps the idiosyncratic blowup risk.

What do you do on Monday morning?

The thesis through 2026 is unchanged: India trades in a roughly 21,000-25,000 Nifty range for another twelve to eighteen months. Beneath the flat index, sectors rotate. Passive holders earn nothing. Rotators print.

The pivot is psychological more than tactical. You are not switching strategies — you are switching identity. You stop being someone who buys good companies and holds them forever. You become someone who runs a process. A portfolio manager, not a stock picker. The change feels uncomfortable for about three months, then it feels obvious.

The operating routine, by cadence:

Weekly.

  1. Check sector technicals. Which sectors trade below their 200-day moving average? Watchlist for accumulation. Which sit at resistance? Watchlist for profit-booking.
  2. Locate Nifty 50 within its range. Near support is a buy zone. Near resistance is a trim zone. The midpoint is a hold.
  3. Update target prices. Every position has a pre-defined exit. Every watchlist name has a pre-defined entry. No guesswork at the moment of action.

Monthly.

  1. Rebalance check. Has any single sector grown past its target weight? If banks ran from 20% to 35%, trim back to 25% and redeploy.
  2. Macro read. Rate trajectory (RBI and Fed). Rupee direction. FII flows. Commodity backdrop. These drive the next quarter's rotation.
  3. Cash discipline. Maintain the 10-20% reserve. If you're below it, partial-trim the largest winner. If above it, look for fresh entries.

Quarterly.

  1. Played-out themes. Book 50-70% on sectors up 15%+ from entry. Roll the proceeds.
  2. Emerging themes. Scan for sectors down 25%+ from peak with intact fundamentals. Build entry tranches.
  3. Risk-reward audit. For every active position, compute upside to resistance vs. downside to support. Anything below 1:2 ratio gets trimmed.

The sectors that historically offer the cleanest rotation signal in India:

  • Private banks — the chassis. HDFC, Kotak, ICICI cycle between underperformance and catch-up regularly. Anchor positions.
  • IT — pure swing material. Trades with US tech sentiment, large beta to the rupee. Buy after 25%+ corrections, sell after 15%+ bounces.
  • Healthcare and hospitals — structural plus tactical. Insurance penetration is a decade-long tailwind. Buy on dip, hold core, swing the excess.
  • Capital-market plays — CDSL, CAMS, brokerages. Benefit directly from rising market participation. Volatile, rotation-friendly.
  • FMCG and consumption — defensive rotation target. Buy at 200DMA when growth stocks are overheating.
  • Auto — cyclical, rate-sensitive. Outperforms when the curve steepens and rate cuts arrive.

The non-negotiable rules:

  1. Never 100% invested. Always carry tactical cash.
  2. Never more than 2.5% in a single smallcap. Drawdowns kill.
  3. Always know the exit before you enter. If you can't define resistance, you're gambling.
  4. Don't track daily. Weekly for swings, monthly for core. Noise kills conviction.
  5. Diversify geographically. India is one market in a world of markets.
  6. Book and rotate. Don't fall in love. Attachment is the enemy.

For the reader at the beginning of this arc, the on-ramp by portfolio size:

  • Under 25 lakh. Build the chassis first. Core holdings in 3-5 quality names you'd be happy to own across cycles. One Nifty 50 swing per year to learn the mechanics. Don't run a 6-sector rotation portfolio on this base — the per-trade costs and concentration risk dominate.
  • 25 lakh to 2 crore. Now the rotation strategy starts paying for itself. Core / swing split. Two-to-three rotation cycles a year. Start tracking sector positions as a separate sleeve. Introduce US exposure (10-20% of equities).
  • 2 crore to 10 crore. Full rotation portfolio. Six sector themes active. Tactical cash routine. US exposure scaled to 40-60% of equities. This is the size at which the strategy genuinely outperforms the SIP holder by a wide margin.
  • 10 crore-plus. Layer in options (puts on US tech as insurance, covered calls for income). Optimise tax residency where legally appropriate. The rotation engine generates the returns; the tax and hedging layers compound them.
