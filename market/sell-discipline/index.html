I.

I. Why Selling Is the Hardest Skill in Investing

Everyone talks about what to buy. Every YouTube video, every community post, every newsletter – the question is always the same: “Which stock should I buy?” Nobody asks the question that actually determines whether you make money: “When should I sell?”

Here is a truth that took me years to internalize. You do not make money when you sell. You actually make money when you buy right. But buying right is only half the equation. Selling wrong can destroy everything. You can buy the perfect stock at the perfect price, watch it double, and then give back all your gains because you did not have a sell framework. I have seen this happen to hundreds of investors in our community.

The problem is psychological. Buying feels like opportunity. Selling feels like loss – either the loss of future gains if you sell too early, or the locking in of actual losses if you sell too late. Both feelings are paralyzing. And so most retail investors do the worst possible thing: they do nothing. They hold everything forever, calling it “long-term investing,” when what they are really doing is avoiding the discomfort of making a decision.

The stock market has turned from an “investable” asset class to a “speculative” asset class. And in any speculation, it is critical to book profits.

Let me be direct with you. In the Indian market especially, buy-and-hold is a dangerous philosophy that has been promoted at scale by mutual fund houses because it keeps your money in their funds, generating management fees year after year. The game is not buy and forget. The game is capital rotation. And capital rotation requires you to master the one skill that nobody teaches: knowing when to walk away.

This article is my attempt to give you what I have never seen anyone else provide – a complete, rule-based framework for selling. Not vague advice like “sell when fundamentals deteriorate.” Specific rules. Specific percentages. Specific triggers. The kind of sell discipline that separates people who build real wealth from people who just have a portfolio of stocks they are emotionally attached to.

Let me give you the scorecard first, so you understand I am speaking from a position of strength, not theory. Over the last three years, our community portfolio has executed the following sells: Small Cap Index at 100%-plus profit. HDFC AMC at 100%-plus. PNB at 100%-plus. Ujjivan at 100%-plus. Zomato at 100%-plus. Bajaj Finance at 30%-plus. Max at 75%. Nuvama at 35%. IT Index at 30%. DMART at 15-16%. Swiggy at 21%. HangSeng Bees at 20% in three months. And yes, some stocks we sold at a loss – Dreamfolks, Asian Paints exit, Tarsons. Out of 10 bets, 3-4 will lose. That is the game. But the net result has beaten most mutual funds.

The question is not whether these were brilliant stock picks. The question is: how did we know when to walk away? That is what this article teaches.

II.

II. The Never-More-Than-50% Rule

Here is the first and most important rule I follow: never sell more than 50% of a winning position at one time.

Why? Because you cannot predict the future. If a stock is up 100% and you sell everything, you might feel brilliant for a week. But when it runs another 80% after your exit, you will feel sick. And that sickness will make you do something stupid – like chasing it back in at a much higher price.

Let me give you a real example from our portfolio. We had a significant position in HDFC AMC that went up over 100%. The instinct is to sell everything and celebrate. But I sold roughly 50% and held the rest. Why? Because the business was still fundamentally strong. AMCs benefit from a growing market. The earnings trajectory was intact. Selling half locked in real profits – enough to redeploy into the next opportunity – while keeping skin in the game for further upside.

The same logic applied to PNB, where we booked over 100% gains. To Ujjivan, where we exited at 100%-plus and then re-entered months later when the price corrected. To Angel One, CDSL, and CAMS, where we did partial profit booking of around 50% each.

Here is how the 50% rule works in practice:

  • Stock doubles (100% gain): Sell 50%. You have now recovered your entire original investment. The remaining position is playing with house money. Your downside risk on this position is literally zero.
  • Stock gains 30-50%: Sell 20-30%. Reduce exposure, redeploy capital to better opportunities, but do not abandon the thesis if it is still intact.
  • Stock gains 10-20% on a swing trade: This is a different animal. On swing trades with clear technical targets, you can exit fully. I bought NIFTY IT Index, made 5% in a week, and exited completely. Why? Because it was a trade, not an investment. I did not believe in Indian IT long-term; I was capturing a short-term swing.

The percentage you sell depends on your conviction and the opportunity cost. But the ceiling – never more than 50% on a single exit – keeps you from making the catastrophic mistake of being completely out of something that keeps running.

The first major selling target for NIFTY 50 is 26,650. This is a point where I would exit 50% of my NIFTY SWING trades. And, will diversify into other sectors.

Notice the discipline: even on a trade with a specific target, the plan is to sell half, not all. This is not timidity. This is mathematics. The expected value of keeping half your position is positive when the trend is intact.

III.

III. The MOAT Erosion Test -- When to Cut Completely

The 50% rule is for winners. Now let us talk about when to sell everything – even at a loss.

You sell completely when the MOAT erodes. Not when the stock price falls. Not when the market panics. Not when your neighbor tells you the company is doomed. You sell when the fundamental reason you bought the stock no longer exists.

Let me walk you through my framework with real examples:

Tarsons Biosystems: I was at a 6-7% loss. Not a big deal normally. But then Aarti Industries fell 15% in a single day after guidance indicated that China dumping would continue. This was not a one-stock problem; it was a macro shift that directly threatened any Indian company competing with Chinese manufacturing. Tarsons was in the crosshairs. The macro narrative that justified my position – that China dumping would slow down – had changed. So I cut the position entirely.

Was I right? Partially. The stock did bounce back later. But here is the point: as a macro investor, I go by macro narratives. When the narrative breaks, the position breaks. I would rather book a 7% loss on a thesis I no longer believe in than hold on and risk a 30-50% drawdown on a thesis that might be broken.

DMART: I sold DMART completely. Not because the business was bad – DMART is a well-run company. I sold because I could not justify holding a large-cap firm at a PE of 100-plus. The valuation had detached from reality. When the gap between price and value becomes absurd, that is your exit signal, regardless of how much you love the brand.

Gensol: Some community members asked about this one. I never bought it, but my advice to those who held it was simple: it could go the R-Comm way. When a stock shows signs of serious structural distress, it is okay to book losses. Do not take it on your ego.

Here is the MOAT erosion checklist I use:

  1. Has the industry thesis changed? (China dumping accelerating, regulation shifting, technology disrupting the sector)
  2. Are the revenues and profits still growing? If they are declining for two or more quarters, something is wrong.
  3. Is the firm’s competitive position weakening? New entrants with better economics, losing market share, pricing power eroding.
  4. Has the leverage situation worsened? Debt-to-equity climbing while growth slows is a death spiral indicator.

If two or more of these go red simultaneously, I sell. Period. Not 50%. Not 30%. All of it. And I reinvest the capital immediately into something I believe in. The goal is not to recover the loss from the same stock. The goal is to make the best possible move for your portfolio today.

IV.

IV. Rotate Within Themes -- Do Not Just Sell Into Cash

Here is where most investors get selling wrong. They think selling means going to cash. It does not. Selling means rotating to a better opportunity.

India is a capital rotation market. I keep saying this. Let me explain what it means in practice.

Consider City Union Bank. The stock was in my portfolio and went up 30% in three months. A buy-and-hold investor would do nothing. An investor who “takes profits” would sell and sit on cash. Both are suboptimal.

What I did was cut 25% of the position and reinvest immediately into beaten-down stocks like CRISIL. Why CRISIL? The PE was still high, and the growth rate was not explosive. But we were buying below the 200-day moving average, at around 10% of our intended capital, leaving significant room to downward average on a strong business that would revive as India’s capital markets grow.

This is the framework:

  1. Sell a portion of what has run up (not all – remember the 50% rule)
  2. Immediately redeploy into an undervalued asset in a related or emerging theme
  3. Build the new position slowly – 10% of intended capital now, more on dips

Here is another real rotation I executed. I exited SBI Bank completely, booking 20% profits from all public sector banks. Where did the money go? Into six high-growth smaller financials: IDFC First, Armaan Financial, Aavas Financiers, 5 Paisa, Angel One, and Aptus. These were lower-risk small and midcaps likely to benefit from interest rate cuts, with 2-3x upside potential over a long-term horizon.

The logic is straightforward. PSU banks had rallied. They were approaching fair value. The easy money had been made. Meanwhile, these smaller financials were still discounted, with stronger growth rates and more sensitivity to rate cuts. Same sector (finance), but a shift from large and fairly valued to small and undervalued.

Another example: I booked profits on AMC firms and rotated to private banks in India, which were much cheaper. Then I took some of that capital and moved it to US stocks. The rotation was: AMCs (India) –> Private Banks (India) –> US Tech. Each step moved capital from something that had appreciated to something that was relatively cheaper.

Point to be noted: AMC from a long-term is very good. But honestly, I am not a buy and hold type of an investor. I rotate capital where I see opportunities. If you are a buy and hold type, please stay put.

This is a critical distinction. I am not telling you that AMCs are bad businesses. I am telling you that the best use of capital at that specific moment was somewhere else. Selling is not a judgment on the company. It is a judgment on opportunity cost.

V.

V. The Covered Call Strategy -- Selling Without Selling

Now let me teach you something that most people in the Indian retail investing community never touch: the covered call strategy. This is how sophisticated investors generate income from stocks they are willing to sell at a certain price.

Here is how I used it on NIFTY 50:

  1. I bought a large block of NIFTY 50 the previous week. It was already up.
  2. I was happy to sell some of it near the all-time high of 26,200.
  3. You might say: “You are making 1-1.5%, what is the big deal?” The big deal is this was done in a week. If you annualize that return, the CAGR is extraordinary.
  4. On top of the capital appreciation, I sold a CALL option for NIFTY 50 at 26,200 for the October expiry.
  5. Because I was selling a call option, I received a premium upfront.
  6. If NIFTY closes below 26,200 at expiry, I keep my shares AND the premium. Free money.
  7. If NIFTY closes above 26,200, I sell my NIFTY lots at 26,200 – which I was happy to do anyway – and still keep the premium.

This is what a covered call means. You are getting paid for agreeing to sell at a price you would have been willing to sell at anyway. It further amplifies your CAGR on positions you are planning to trim.

But here is the critical warning: do not do naked options. Do not simply sell a call unless you can cover it with shares you actually own. Naked options can lead to unlimited losses. Covered calls are conservative; naked calls are gambling.

Should you do this? I do not know your situation. It is completely okay not to get into options. But for those who have significant holdings and are looking to sell at certain levels anyway, covered calls are one of the most elegant tools in the investor’s toolkit. You are essentially getting paid to have a sell plan.

Let me give you the math on why this matters. Suppose you own 50 lots of NIFTY 50 purchased at 25,500. NIFTY moves to 25,900 – a 1.65% gain. You are now considering your next move. If you just sell at market, you pocket 1.65%. But if you sell a covered call at 26,200 expiring in a week, you collect a premium on top of your position. If NIFTY stays below 26,200, you keep the premium and still own your shares – free money. If NIFTY crosses 26,200, you sell at a level that represents a 2.7% gain in a matter of weeks, plus the premium.

The covered call transforms “should I sell?” from a binary, stressful decision into a mathematical one. You define the price at which you are happy to exit, collect income for being patient, and let the market decide. This is how professionals think about selling. Not as an emotional event, but as a structured, income-generating activity.

One more thing on covered calls that is critical: timing matters. I sell covered calls when the contract expires quickly – days, not months. Short-duration calls give you premium income without locking you into a position for too long. If the market suddenly changes direction, you want flexibility. Weekly or bi-weekly expiry covered calls are the sweet spot for most situations.

VI.

VI. When NOT to Sell -- The Discipline of Holding

Sell discipline is not just about knowing when to sell. It is equally about knowing when NOT to sell. And this is where most people fail.

Here are the situations where I explicitly refused to sell, despite pressure from the community:

SBI Cards at 17% monthly gain: People flooded my comments asking if they should sell. My response was blunt – that is the worst way to invest. That is what noobs do. The question is not “has the stock gone up?” The question is “is the firm becoming more or less valuable with time?” If people are going to use more cards, if the business is going to expand, you stay invested. Your goal on such firms is not to sell – it is to figure out how to buy more when it dips.

CAMS after a run-up: Just because you are sitting on X% profits does not mean you should sell. Amateur investors sell winners too early. A smart investor asks: what is the best use of my money NOW? If you see CAMS going up further, stay invested. If you are unsure, book partial profits – 20-30%. If you are an amateur, book profits and exit. But do not sell just because the stock is green.

Private Banks and Private Bank ETFs after a rally: These had all run up and people wanted to book profits. But export-driven businesses were getting hit by the trade war. There were no massive reinvestment opportunities except maybe US tech. So selling private banks to hold cash made no sense. The answer was simple: I am NOT selling. But in case you wish to book 20% profits, it can be done at current technicals.

Aavas Financiers up 4-5% in a day: Whenever a stock moves by 4-5%, people get the urge to sell. This is exactly wrong. The idea when a stock starts breaking out is to ride the wave for as long as possible. You do not sell the breakout. You sell the exhaustion.

Here are the rules for when NOT to sell:

  1. This is NOT a seller’s market. In a buyer’s market, we generally do not sell. The goal is to max out positions so that later, when the market turns into a seller’s market, we have goods to sell. I have said this many times: just max out positions now (almost 85-90%) and sit tight.

  2. The breakout has just started. There is no point selling right now when the breakout has just begun. From a mid-term point of view, things look good unless the entire market falls.

  3. You do not have a better reinvestment opportunity. If selling the stock means sitting on cash, and you do not have a clear next investment, do not sell. Cash sitting idle is a guaranteed loss to inflation.

  4. The stock is down 10-11% from highs but fundamentals are intact. Selling 10-11% down from the top does not make sense in most cases. Wait for a rebound. It might take six months. But panicking out at the bottom is the single worst mistake retail investors make.

  5. You are in a volatile market and do not have clarity. When NASDAQ moves 6-7% in a single day, when tariff announcements are flying around, when panic is everywhere – do NOT make sudden moves. Do not sell too much or buy too much. The only exception is if you are underinvested, in which case you build big positions. But selling into panic is almost always wrong.

There is a deeper principle here that most investors miss: you do not need to do anything most of the time. If you are not feeling the urge to act in the market right now, then you are learning well. Patience is a massive virtue when it comes to investing. And no, this is not a motivational quote. This is the simple reality. The best investors spend most of their time doing nothing. The urge to act – to sell because the stock moved, to sell because the news is scary, to sell because your WhatsApp group is panicking – is almost always wrong.

The worst thing you can do is sell and go away. This way you will definitely have a loss. In case you wish to exit positions, you should always wait for a better time.

VII.

VII. Selling Losers -- The Ego Problem

This is the section nobody wants to read. But it might be the most important one.

If you have lost 30% on stock ABC, you should NOT try to make that money back from that same stock ABC. This is the worst mistake new investors make. And it comes from ego, not logic.

Here is the framework for handling losing positions:

Out of 10 stocks you buy, 3 will go bad. This is just the base rate of investing. Accept it. Internalize it. If you cannot handle 3 out of 10 bets going wrong, equity investing might not be for you.

When a stock is in the red, you need to analyze – not panic:

  • Does the stock have a chance of revival?
  • Is the industry good?
  • Are the firm’s revenues and profits growing?

If the answers are yes, you hold. If the answers are no, you sell – even at a loss – and rotate the capital to something better.

Let me show you how I handled real losing positions:

Rushil Decor (-8%): Corrected 22-23% from its top, but we bought in tranches so our actual loss was about 8%. Small cap stock in a small/mid index that had corrected 20%. Good profits, strong sales. The real estate cycle was approaching the handover stage, when people buy furniture and decor. Holding. Revival likely post next festive season.

Asian Paints (-9.2%): Same macro thesis as Rushil Decor. Stock corrected 25% from top but the numbers were excellent. Holding.

SW Solar (-35%): This was the tough one. Corrected 50% from its top. All Reliance group companies had corrected, including Reliance itself by 25-26%. SW Solar is a microcap, not even a small cap. Holding – but with eyes wide open. Anything can happen with microcap firms. Position size was capped at 2.5% of portfolio.

PVR (-23%): Stock corrected 50% from its peak. Massive aggregation happening. Absolutely holding. This was already showing signs of a slow grind back, delivering 9% in a single month.

Dreamfolks and Tarsons: These were cut. The thesis had weakened. Dreamfolks faced uncertain business prospects. Tarsons faced the China dumping risk. I booked the losses and moved on.

The key distinction: I held Rushil Decor, Asian Paints, SW Solar, and PVR because the businesses were still sound and the losses were within expected range for the market environment. I cut Tarsons because the macro thesis changed. Selling losers is not about the size of the loss. It is about whether the reason you bought still exists.

And here is one more rule that most people ignore: tax loss harvesting. I sell stocks at a loss specifically to offset capital gains from my winners. If I have made significant profits on HDFC AMC and PNB, I can sell a losing position like Tarsons, book the loss, reduce my tax liability, and buy back later if the thesis improves. This is not emotional selling. This is portfolio engineering.

Let me walk through the math on tax loss harvesting because most people do not understand how powerful it is. Suppose you sold HDFC AMC at a 100% profit – a gain of, say, 5 lakhs. At the STCG rate of 20%, your tax liability is 1 lakh. Now suppose you also hold Tarsons at a 7% loss – a notional loss of, say, 35,000 rupees. If you sell Tarsons and book that 35,000 loss, your taxable gain drops from 5 lakhs to 4.65 lakhs. Your tax bill drops by 7,000 rupees. That 7,000 rupees is real money that stays in your pocket instead of going to the government.

And here is the beautiful part: if you still believe in Tarsons, you can buy it back after 30 days. You get the tax benefit AND you get your position back. You have effectively been paid 7,000 rupees for the inconvenience of being out of the stock for a month.

I had sold a lot of stuff on profit. I am still net profitable for this year. I will reduce my tax liabilities by selling any stocks I have in loss over the next 1 month. And will buy it back.

Most retail investors hold losing stocks and sell winning stocks – the exact opposite of what the math says to do. Smart investors hold winners as long as the thesis is intact and selectively harvest losses to engineer their tax position. This is the difference between emotional selling and strategic selling.

VIII.

VIII. The Range-Trading Framework -- Selling in Sideways Markets

Let me give you an advanced framework for selling in a market that is going nowhere.

Shankar Sharma predicted 0% stock market returns for the next five years. Whether he is right or wrong does not matter to me. What matters is the strategy for a range-bound market. Here is how I think about it:

  1. The market will oscillate in a range. My estimate was 21,500 to 26,500 on the NIFTY (later revised to 23,500 to 27,500).
  2. People who buy at 26,500 will lose money.
  3. People who buy at 22,000 and sell at 26,500 will make money.
  4. When the market falls to the lower end, even good stocks get discounted.
  5. When the market rises to the upper end, it is time to sell.

This is not day-trading. This is structural range trading on a 6-month to 2-year timeframe. And it requires you to have clear sell targets before you buy.

For example, when I bought a big block of NIFTY 50 around Diwali, I immediately defined my exit plan:

  • Target sell zone: Around the budget rally, if tax cuts were announced.
  • CAGR target: 15%. If I could make 15% CAGR on NIFTY 50, I would be happy.
  • Stop loss: If NIFTY fell to 25,500, it became a technical sell.
  • Fallback plan: If the budget rally did not materialize, hold and wait for a revival rally.

Notice the completeness of this plan. Before I bought, I knew exactly when I would sell, at what price, under what conditions, and what I would do if none of those conditions were met. This is what a sell framework looks like. It is not “I will sell when I feel like it” or “I will sell when my portfolio is green.” It is a specific, pre-determined set of conditions.

In a sideways market, the sell framework becomes even more critical because you are not getting bailed out by a rising tide. Here is my approach:

During sideways consolidation: 1. Cut bad stocks entirely – where business prospects are not great. I cut some PSU stocks during this phase. 2. Sell overvalued stocks – I sold DMART because holding a large-cap at PE 100-plus made no sense. 3. Build a cash bucket – and sit on it. Be patient. 4. Deploy cash only at major support levels – if the market hits 23,500, buy the least risky assets. 5. Repeat – buy at support, sell at resistance, rotate between sectors.

If you are overinvested, exit in the upper half of the range. If you are underinvested, buy in the lower half. If you are fairly invested and already have cash positions, just relax and do nothing. The market will reward people who are deeply patient.

There is also the question of what happens when the range breaks. If NIFTY breaks 25,500 to the downside, that becomes a technical sell signal for swing trades. But – and this is important – it does not mean you sell everything. You sell the swing trades. Your long-term positions, your core index holdings, your thematic bets with intact fundamentals, those you hold through the range break and use the cash you freed up from selling the swing trades to buy more.

The S&P 500 trade I executed illustrates this perfectly. I invested around 1 crore on a momentum trade, with the plan to make 4 lakhs in profit. If the trade worked, great. If it did not, the position became a long-term bet. But I also set a stop loss at -3.5%. If S&P 500 fell by 3.5%, I would withdraw 50% of my capital at a loss, and the remaining 50% would become a long-term position. Either way, the downside was controlled because I had defined my sell triggers before entering.

This is what I mean by mixing trading and investing. The entry is the same. The sell framework is what determines whether it is a trade or an investment. And in a range-bound market, every position should start as a trade with a clear sell target, with the option to convert into a long-term investment if the trade does not play out but the thesis remains intact.

IX.

IX. The Complete Sell Playbook -- Rules You Can Use Tomorrow

Let me consolidate everything into a single, actionable sell playbook. Print this. Stick it on your screen. Refer to it every time you feel the urge to sell – or the urge to not sell.

RULE 1: Define your sell target BEFORE you buy. Every purchase needs an exit plan. Is this a swing trade (exit in weeks to months at a specific technical level)? Is this a mid-term investment (exit when fair value is reached or thesis plays out)? Is this a long-term hold (exit only when MOAT erodes)? If you cannot articulate when you will sell, you should not be buying.

RULE 2: Never sell more than 50% of a winning position at once. Lock in profits, but keep exposure. Selling 100% means you lose all optionality on further upside. The 50% rule ensures you are always partially right, no matter what happens next.

RULE 3: Sell on MOAT erosion, not on price drops. The stock falling 20% is not a sell signal. The thesis breaking is a sell signal. Ask: has the industry changed? Are revenues declining? Is the competitive position weakening? Two or more red flags means exit completely.

RULE 4: Rotate, do not go to cash. When you sell, have the next investment ready. Capital sitting in your savings account earning 4% while inflation runs at 6% is a guaranteed loss. Sell and redeploy. Sell and reinvest. Sell and rotate. But do not sell and sit.

RULE 5: In a buyer’s market, do not sell. When the market is discounted, when panic is in the air, when FIIs are exiting and margin calls are forcing liquidation – this is not the time to sell. This is the time to buy with both hands. Save your selling for when the market is euphoric and everyone around you is talking about stocks.

RULE 6: If you are over-concentrated, sell down to safe levels. If any single stock is more than 5% of your total portfolio (more than 2.5% for small and microcaps), trim it. If 10% or more of your portfolio is in one stock, you should sell some and reinvest in a safer asset like NIFTY 50 or an index. Concentration kills portfolios. Position sizing is how you survive the inevitable drawdowns.

RULE 7: Use covered calls on positions you are willing to exit. If you own a large block of an index or a stock and have a price at which you would happily sell, sell covered calls at that strike. Collect premium. Either the stock stays below your target and you keep the premium as bonus income, or it rises above your target and you sell at a price you were happy with anyway. Either outcome is good.

RULE 8: Book losses when the thesis breaks – without ego. Out of 10 stocks, 3 will lose money. This is normal. What is not normal – and what destroys portfolios – is holding onto a broken thesis because you refuse to admit you were wrong. If revenues are declining, if the industry is facing structural headwinds, if management credibility is shot, sell and rotate. The goal is to optimize the portfolio, not to avenge a losing position.

RULE 9: Use tax loss harvesting strategically. If you have realized profits in the financial year, sell losing positions before year-end to offset your tax liability. Then buy back the good ones after 30 days if the thesis still holds. This is not emotional selling – this is financial engineering that puts real money back in your pocket.

RULE 10: Ask the reinvestment question. Before every sell, ask yourself: what will I do with this money if I get it today? If you have a better opportunity, sell. If you do not, hold. Whether you sell or not is ultimately a question of what your capital can do elsewhere.

Whether you sell it now or not is a question of what you will do with the money if you get it today.

A final word. Selling is uncomfortable because it forces you to make a definitive statement about the future. Buying is easy – you are just “adding a position.” Selling requires you to say: I believe the opportunity here is exhausted, or I believe something else is better, or I believe this thesis is broken. That requires conviction, honesty, and the willingness to be wrong.

Most people never develop this skill. They either sell too early out of fear, or they sell too late out of stubbornness. The framework I have laid out in this article sits between those two extremes. It is not perfect – I have sold stocks like Tarsons that later bounced, and I have held stocks like SW Solar through painful drawdowns. But over time, across hundreds of trades and investments, these rules have produced returns that beat most mutual funds and most professional money managers.

The market does not care about your feelings. It does not care that you bought PVR at the top or that you were “so close” to selling HDFC AMC before it corrected. The market is a mechanism. And mechanisms respond to discipline, not hope.

Build your sell framework. Write it down. Follow it even when it hurts. Especially when it hurts. That is how wealth is actually built – not by finding the next multibagger, but by knowing exactly when to walk away from one.
