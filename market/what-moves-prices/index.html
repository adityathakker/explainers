I.

Why did NVDA go up 10×? Was it earnings, or was it sentiment?

In January 2023 NVIDIA closed near $14 (split-adjusted). Two years later it traded near $140 — a 10× run that minted hundreds of billionaires and made every CIO who didn't own more of it look reckless. The story everyone told themselves was: AI. The story that is actually true is more interesting, because it answers a question almost no retail investor asks before clicking buy.

Did NVIDIA's earnings go up 10×? Or did the market's willingness to pay for those earnings go up 10×? Or some mix? Because the same price chart can mean three completely different things depending on which lever did the work — and which lever did the work tells you whether to hold, trim, or get out.

Here is the rough decomposition from the actual filings[1]. In early 2023, NVDA's trailing EPS was about $0.45 (split-adjusted) and the stock traded near a PE of 31. By early 2025, trailing EPS was around $2.85 and the PE sat near 49. The math:

Price2025 / Price2023 = (PE2025 / PE2023) × (EPS2025 / EPS2023)
~10× ≈ 1.58 × 6.33 earnings did most of the heavy lifting; the multiple added another ~58% on top

Translated: NVDA earnings grew about 6×, and the multiple grew another 1.6× on top. The fundamental work did most of it, but a meaningful chunk — about a third of the log-return — was the market deciding to pay more per dollar of profit. That mix is the difference between a great business at a great price and a great business that has already priced in greatness.

Why does this distinction matter when the bank statement shows the same gain either way? Because the two components have wildly different shelf lives. Earnings growth, once delivered, is yours forever — tomorrow's PE can't take back the dollars NVIDIA already reported. Multiple expansion, on the other hand, is fully reversible. The same market that re-rated the stock from PE 31 to PE 49 can revert it to 31 in a single quarter on a guidance scare, wiping out a third of your gain without anything actually changing at the company. Decomposing the return tells you how much of your paper profit is locked in versus on loan.

This article is about doing that decomposition for any stock, any time. Every price move — from a single quarter to a hundred-year index return — resolves into the same three components. Internalise them and you stop being surprised by markets. You start being able to say, before you buy: here is what has to be true for me to make money, and here is what is already priced in.

The exercise is uncomfortable on purpose. It forces you to articulate which lever you're actually betting on, and most retail positions don't have an answer beyond "the stock has been going up." That's not a thesis. That's momentum dressed in fundamentals clothing. By the end of this article you'll have a framework that takes ten minutes per stock and screens out the majority of bad ideas before they become bad positions.

II.

What is the one equation behind every stock price?

If you remember nothing else from this article, remember this. Every share price on every exchange in the world is the product of two numbers:

Price = PE × EPS PE is what the market pays per dollar of earnings; EPS is the dollars of earnings per share

That's it. The identity is true by construction — PE is defined as price divided by EPS, so multiplying it back recovers price. The reason this trivially-true statement matters is what happens when you take the log and differentiate. Returns decompose cleanly:

ln(P1/P0) = ln(PE1/PE0) + ln(EPS1/EPS0)
total return ≈ multiple expansion + earnings growth add dividend yield for total shareholder return; the three drivers are additive in log-space

In English: a stock's return over any period is the sum of three things — how much earnings grew, how much the multiple rebounded or contracted, and how much was paid out as dividends. There are no other categories. "Investor sentiment", "narrative", "AI hype" — these all live inside the PE term. "Real business performance" lives inside the EPS term. Everything else is noise.

Pause on why the log form matters. In percentage space, a 50% gain followed by a 50% loss leaves you with 75% of what you started with — the asymmetric math of compounding distorts the picture. In log space, the same path is +0.405 − 0.693 = −0.288, which exponentiates back to that 75% cleanly. The log decomposition lets you add the contributions of PE and EPS without worrying about the cross-term distortion that mangles percentage arithmetic. It is the difference between an identity that always holds and an approximation that breaks down at the extremes — which is exactly where the most interesting stocks live.

Well, actually 🧐 — buybacks hide inside EPS

EPS is earnings per share. A company can grow EPS without growing earnings at all by buying back stock and shrinking the denominator. Apple has done this aggressively for a decade. So when you see "EPS growth" in the decomposition, ask: was it the numerator (more profit) or the denominator (fewer shares)? Both count as real for the shareholder, but they're qualitatively different signals about the business.

The cleaner decomposition splits EPS growth into revenue growth + margin expansion + buyback yield. That's a four-driver model. For this article we'll fold buybacks into EPS growth because the data is easier to source and the mental model is tighter.

The framework feels obvious once written down, and that's a clue. Every powerful idea in finance reduces to an identity that nobody actively disputes — what people disagree about is which term will do the work next. The mutual fund industry would much rather you focus on a stock's "story" than on which term of PE × EPS is actually paying you. Stories sell SIPs. Math doesn't.

III.

How much of a doubling can come from multiple expansion alone?

The widget below is the article in miniature. Pick a starting price and ending price for any stock you remember. Pick the EPS at each endpoint. The decomposition is automatic.

Try this first: starting price 100, ending price 200, both EPS values 5. The price doubled. Earnings did nothing. The PE doubled from 20 to 40 — that is the entire move. A stock can double on multiple expansion alone with zero help from the business. This was Bata India during its 83-PE peak, every Indian SaaS name in 2021, and a good chunk of the Nasdaq in 1999.

Now do the opposite. Starting price 100, ending price 200, starting EPS 5, ending EPS 10. The PE didn't move at all — the entire double came from the business making twice as much money. This is the cleaner, more durable version of the same return. The shareholder got identical 100% but is sitting on a stock that doesn't need any further help from sentiment.

The market is paying you for earnings Eli Lilly hasn't reported yet. That's a perfectly defensible bet — but it's a very different bet from "I'm buying because earnings grew."

The widget also explains why "double bagger" headlines mislead. A stock that goes from PE 10 to PE 20 with no earnings growth has doubled. A stock that doubles its earnings at a constant PE has doubled. To the headline writer both are "+100%". To you, as the investor still holding tomorrow's risk, only one of them has a foundation under it.

Well, actually 🧐 — the cross term, and why it isn't a rounding error

The clean log-decomposition is ln(P′/P) = ln(PE′/PE) + ln(EPS′/EPS). In linear (percentage) space it's (1+r) = (1+rPE)(1+rEPS), which expands to r ≈ rPE + rEPS + rPE·rEPS. That last interaction term can be 20+ percentage points when both PE and EPS double.

For NVDA's 10× — with PE roughly +58% and EPS roughly +533% — the interaction term alone is about +310 percentage points. It is not a rounding error. The widget displays it as a third slice precisely because pretending it's negligible is what makes journalists write "earnings doubled, so the stock should double" and get it embarrassingly wrong.

IV.

Why is the earnings surprise what moves the stock — not the number?

NVIDIA reports earnings, beats Wall Street by 20%, and the stock falls 8% after-hours. Tesla reports earnings, misses by 5%, and the stock rips 12% the next morning. Retail investors call this "the market is irrational." It isn't. The market is doing exactly what an efficient pricing engine should do.

The stock price already contains everyone's best guess of what earnings will be. By the time a company reports, the consensus EPS number is baked in — it's the central case. What moves the price is the gap between the actual print and what was already discounted. That gap is called the surprise.

Surprise % = (EPSactual − EPSconsensus) ÷ EPSconsensus if you only watch the headline EPS and ignore the surprise, you'll be wrong about direction half the time

The market's reaction function to surprise is well-documented and consistently asymmetric[2]. A 10% beat might add 3–5% to the stock. A 10% miss might cost 8–12%. Misses are punished harder than equivalent beats are rewarded. That isn't a bug — it's a feature of how risk-averse investors process new information. A miss raises the variance of future earnings (maybe management is hiding something, maybe the moat is cracking), and higher variance means a higher discount rate, which means a lower price even if the central case is unchanged.

Two practical lessons from playing with this. First: a "great" earnings print is not enough — it has to beat what was already expected. NVIDIA in Q3 2024 reported revenue up 94% year-over-year and the stock fell because the guide was "only" up 70%. Second: never average down on a miss until you understand why. The asymmetry exists because misses contain information beyond the number itself. If the market is yanking 12% off a 4% miss, the market is saying it no longer trusts the next four quarters of guidance.

The asymmetry also explains the strange post-earnings drift that academic finance has documented for over fifty years. After a positive surprise the stock keeps drifting up for weeks; after a negative surprise it keeps drifting down. The simplest explanation is informational stickiness — analysts revise estimates slowly, institutions rebalance over days not seconds, and the new fundamental signal takes a while to be fully absorbed. For the patient investor this is actionable: a quality business that posts a clean beat and is being punished anyway (because of macro fears or sector rotation) is often a setup where the eventual re-rating happens not on the day of the report but in the weeks after. The market is mostly efficient. It is not instantly efficient.

There is one more pattern worth knowing. Stocks with high implied volatility going into earnings — usually high-PE growth names — have larger absolute reactions in both directions. This is mechanical: a 4% miss on a name that normally moves 1% a day is a 4-sigma event by the market's own pricing, so the post-earnings adjustment has to be commensurately large. The same 4% miss on a low-volatility utility moves the stock 1-2%. If you own a high-multiple growth name, you are implicitly long volatility around earnings — even if you've never thought about it that way.

Well, actually 🧐 — the "whisper number"

Consensus EPS is the published average of analyst estimates. The whisper number is the unwritten figure that institutional buyers are actually positioned for — usually higher than consensus for high-momentum names and lower than consensus for distressed ones. When a stock beats consensus but falls, the surprise relative to the whisper was negative. This is why Bloomberg terminals show "estimate revisions in the last 7 days" as a key field — it's the closest public proxy for where the whisper is moving.

V.

How much of the S&P's return is multiple expansion vs. growth vs. dividends?

You've heard "stocks return 10% a year over the long run." That number is real, but it is a brutal oversimplification of what actually happens. The 10% is a blended figure across vastly different decades — and the mix of where the return came from changes more than the total does.

The S&P 500 (and its predecessor indices going back to 1900) decomposes into three drivers using exactly the framework from Section II:

  • EPS growth — the businesses earned more dollars per share. The underlying engine.
  • Multiple expansion / contraction — the market changed how much it would pay per dollar of those earnings. The mood.
  • Dividend yield — the cash actually returned to shareholders. The boring rent.

The patterns that fall out of this are jarring once you see them.

The 1950s and 1990s were the great multiple-expansion decades. In the 1990s, EPS grew about 7% a year — respectable but not spectacular — while the multiple roughly doubled from a PE of 15 to a PE near 30. Roughly half the decade's return was the market re-rating, not the businesses delivering. When the dot-com bubble burst, the multiple did its mean-reversion, and the 2000s gave back almost a full decade of price appreciation despite EPS continuing to grow.

The 1970s were the opposite. Brutal multiple contraction — PE compressed from 18 to under 8 — while EPS actually grew with inflation. Without dividends, the decade was a disaster. With dividends (which were 4–5% yields in that era), the total return came out positive but mostly cash.

The 2010s were the cleanest "balanced" decade in a century: EPS growth, modest multiple expansion, and 2% dividends roughly summed to a 13% annualised total return with no single driver dominating. That balance is also why nobody saw the 2020s coming — a single year of pandemic, monetary stimulus, and AI hype rewrote the multiple in one direction, then yanked it back, then rebuilt it again.

Notice what the 1930s and the 1970s have in common despite looking entirely different on the surface. Both were dominated by multiple contraction. The 1930s lost half their multiple from deflation and economic collapse; the 1970s lost over half of theirs to inflation and rising rates. The two macro environments are opposites — one is the absence of inflation, the other is too much of it — but the equity-market consequence is the same: the PE term goes negative for a decade, and you need either heroic EPS growth or fat dividends to come out ahead. Most retail investors do not have a mental model for "the multiple itself can compress for ten years." The decade view forces that model on you.

And one more observation that doesn't fit a neat narrative. Dividend yield as a share of total return has been falling steadily for a century. In the 1900s and 1940s, dividends were the dominant component — 5% out of an 8% total. By the 2010s, dividends were 2% out of 13%. This isn't because companies got cheap with cash; it's because they shifted the return-of-capital channel to buybacks (which show up inside EPS as a denominator effect, not as cash to shareholders). So the "EPS growth" bar in modern decades is partly capturing what would historically have been "dividend yield." The total return is still real. The plumbing changed.

The point

"Stocks return 10%" is the average answer to a question that has a different answer every decade. If you buy when multiples are at the top of their century-long range, you're betting on EPS to carry the entire return — the multiple has no further to expand. If you buy when multiples are compressed, you have a tailwind. Where the multiple sits today, relative to history, is the single most important number nobody talks about.

VI.

What is the high-PE trap, and why is time correction worse than a crash?

There are two ways the market punishes you for buying at a stretched multiple. The visible one is the crash — the stock falls 40%, you panic, you sell at the bottom, the story ends. Painful, but at least it's over.

The invisible one is time correction — the stock simply does nothing for six years while the rest of the market doubles. The business keeps growing. The numbers look fine. The PE quietly normalises from 80 to 30, and EPS growth has to claw back every point of multiple contraction before you see a single rupee of return. You don't panic-sell because there's nothing to panic about. You just lose six years.

The math is unambiguous. If you buy a stock at PE 80 and the PE normalises to 30 over six years — a 62.5% multiple contraction — the business has to grow EPS at (1/0.375)1/6 − 1 ≈ 17.7% per year just to keep the stock price flat. To deliver a 10% return on top of that, EPS needs to grow at roughly 28% annually for six straight years. That is heroic. The number of businesses that compound earnings at 28% for six years is in the single digits per decade.

Bata India 2019→2025 was a textbook time correction. So was IRCTC from its 2021 highs. So was every Indian SaaS name that IPO'd at 30× revenue. The investors who held them are not down. They are just deeply behind whoever held a 12% Treasury bill, and they don't fully realise it because their statement says "+2%" instead of "−30%". The opportunity cost is invisible to the same psychology that makes losing $100 hurt more than missing out on $300 of gains.

Well, actually 🧐 — the "justified" PE

The classical Gordon Growth model says a justified forward PE is roughly (1 − b) ÷ (k − g), where b is the earnings retention ratio, k is the cost of equity, and g is the long-run earnings growth rate. With k = 10% and g = 5%, that puts justified PE around 12–15.

When NVDA traded at PE 80 in mid-2024, the implied long-run g needed to justify it was around 8% in perpetuity. Plausible? Maybe, for the next decade. In perpetuity? Mathematically impossible — nothing grows faster than nominal GDP forever. So even when the stock is "right" you're implicitly betting on a finite-but-large window before mean reversion kicks in. Knowing that window matters.

The reason mutual funds don't warn you about high-PE traps is the same reason cigarette companies didn't warn you about cancer in 1965. SIP flows depend on the appearance of constant progress. A frank conversation about "this category is in a multi-year time correction and you should rotate out" is a marketing disaster. So the message gets diluted to "stay invested for the long term" — which is technically not wrong, but routes around the actionable insight.

There is also a survivorship-bias trap embedded in the "high-multiple businesses justify themselves over time" argument. Yes, Amazon traded at PE 100+ for years and the business eventually grew into it. So did Microsoft in the late 1990s — eventually. The ones that didn't grow into their multiples are forgotten. Sun Microsystems traded at PE 70 in 2000; by 2005 it was a takeover target at a fraction of the price, and most retail investors who held it ate ten years of dead money. The lesson is not "never buy high-PE stocks." It's "high-PE stocks need an exceptional, durable, defensible reason for the multiple to hold — and most don't have one."

How do you tell the difference in real time? Three filters that work surprisingly well. First: is the company growing earnings faster than the implied multiple decay required? Run the math like the example above — if normalisation needs 17% EPS growth for six years, does the company actually do 20%+ historically? Second: is the moat structural or narrative? Network effects, scale advantages, regulatory protection survive decades. "First-mover advantage" and "thought leadership" don't. Third: is the high multiple being awarded by sophisticated buyers (sovereign wealth funds, multi-strategy hedge funds) or by retail momentum? Watch the institutional ownership reports. When the smart money quietly trims and retail keeps buying the dip, the time correction has already started — you just can't see it in the price yet.

VII.

When should you bet on PE expansion vs. EPS growth?

Both bets work. Both have a place in a portfolio. They are not interchangeable, and confusing one for the other is the most common analytical error in retail investing.

A PE-expansion bet says: this asset is currently mispriced. The market will eventually agree with me. When sentiment shifts, the multiple rerates, and I capture the gap. This bet is fast when it works — the rerate can happen in months — and it has a hard ceiling determined by what a sensible long-run multiple looks like. It is a bet about other investors, not about the company. Indian IT names trading at 18× in late 2024, when their long-term average was 24×, were a classic PE-expansion setup.

An EPS-growth bet says: the business will compound earnings faster than the market expects, for longer than the market expects. The multiple may not change — in fact, ideally it doesn't, because then the stock price tracks the EPS trajectory directly. This bet is slow but has no ceiling. Microsoft from 2014 to 2024 was a textbook EPS-growth bet: PE roughly stable, EPS roughly 4×, stock roughly 4×. The business did all the work.

The combined bet — both PE expansion and EPS growth — is the rarest and most powerful setup. You buy a quality business at a depressed multiple after a sentiment shock. The earnings keep compounding, and the multiple eventually returns to fair. Returns multiply. Natco Pharma at PE 8 with intact growth was this setup. Meta in late 2022 at PE 12 with intact ad-revenue growth was this setup. They are uncommon — the market is mostly efficient — but they are what generational wealth gets built on.

The combined anti-bet — both PE contraction and EPS deceleration — is what blows up portfolios. The high-multiple growth darling that misses guidance is the textbook case. The PE contracts because the growth story is questioned; the EPS contracts because the growth story was real and is now ending. Both terms work against you simultaneously. Zoom from 2021 to 2023 lost 88% this way[3]. Peloton lost 97%. Their earnings didn't fall 88% — the combination of multiple compression and modest earnings reset did the rest.

So how do you choose, in practice? Match the bet to the market regime, your conviction, and your time horizon. When index multiples are stretched (top quartile of their history), the PE-expansion lever at the index level is exhausted — you have to find individual mispricings, which is hard and frequently wrong. EPS-growth bets dominate in that environment because the only sustainable return source is the business. When index multiples are compressed (bottom quartile), the opposite holds: the PE-expansion tailwind alone can deliver a decade of double-digit returns even on mediocre stock picks. The 2009-2019 S&P run was largely a multiple-expansion phenomenon off a compressed 2009 base — the bull market many people credit to their stock-picking skill was, in aggregate, the market re-rating from 13× to 22×.

The other dimension is your own time horizon. PE-expansion bets resolve fast or not at all — if the rerating doesn't happen within a year or two, the thesis is usually broken (sentiment moved on, the catalyst evaporated). EPS-growth bets resolve slowly — you may need three to five years for the earnings trajectory to fully express itself in the price, because the multiple can move against you in the short term even when the business is delivering. If your money has a one-year horizon, PE-expansion bets are the natural fit. If it has a ten-year horizon, EPS-growth bets compound more reliably. Mismatch the horizon to the bet and you'll end up selling the long-duration thesis at the worst possible moment.

VIII.

What does this mean for the next stock you buy?

If you do nothing else differently after reading this, do this one thing: before any purchase, write down the decomposition.

  1. What is my time horizon? One number, in years.
  2. What EPS growth do I expect over that horizon? Express as a CAGR. Be honest. Compare to the company's last 5-year actual CAGR.
  3. What will the multiple be at the end? Pick a number. Justify it with peer comparisons, the stock's own historical range, and the implied long-run growth rate from the Gordon model.
  4. Multiply the implied future EPS by the implied future PE. That's your target price.
  5. Divide by today's price. Is that the return you need for the risk you're taking?

This exercise takes ten minutes and screens out 70% of the bad ideas before any money moves. Most retail investors skip it because step 3 forces them to articulate a multiple thesis, and most people don't have one — which is itself the signal that they're making a story-based bet rather than a math-based one.

A second rule: match the strategy to the regime. When market multiples sit in the top quartile of their historical range, you cannot count on multiple expansion at the index level — the index has used up its tailwind. In those phases, every dollar of return has to come from EPS growth or from being right about an individual mispricing. That makes stock-picking harder, not easier — and explains why most active managers underperform during stretched-multiple regimes (the universe of mispriced names shrinks just as the difficulty of finding them rises).

When market multiples are compressed — PE under 14 for the S&P, under 18 for the Nifty in modern terms — the index itself is doing some of the work for you. You can buy quality with leverage from the multiple and the business. That is what Warren Buffett means when he says he likes to be greedy when others are fearful. He is not waxing poetic. He is saying: when the multiple is compressed, the PE term flips from headwind to tailwind, and the math works in your favour even on mediocre stock picks.

If you remember one thing

Every stock you own is a bet on PE expansion, EPS growth, or both. If you don't know which one, you don't own a position — you own a story. Stories underperform math over any horizon longer than a news cycle. Pick a lever, write down the thesis, set the time horizon, and check the math against today's price. If the math doesn't work, the story won't save you.

The framework is not a trading system. It will not tell you what to buy tomorrow. What it does is force you to be honest about what you're actually buying when you click the button — the business, the sentiment, or both. Most retail losses come from confusion about which one. Most institutional alpha comes from clarity about it. The gap between those two facts is where 95% of investors live, and it doesn't have to be where you do.

One more piece of advice and then I'll stop. Build the decomposition habit on stocks you already own before you use it for new purchases. Pull up every position in your portfolio. Estimate where the PE was when you bought it, where it is now, and where you think it's going. Estimate the EPS trajectory the same way. Then look at the implied total return you're underwriting for each. You'll find at least one position that doesn't survive the exercise — usually a story stock you bought on momentum and never re-evaluated. Trim it. That single rotation, repeated annually, will compound into more lifetime alpha than any "next big idea" you ever find.

Price = PE × EPS. Decompose. Then decide.
