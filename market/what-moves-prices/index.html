I.

I. The Only Equation You Need to Know

Let me give you the single most important equation in investing. If you understand this deeply, you will beat 95% of fund managers. If you don’t, you will keep buying lottery tickets and calling it “investing.”

Here it is:

Stock Price = PE Ratio x Earnings Per Share (EPS)

That is it. Every stock price movement in the history of financial markets can be decomposed into these two variables. The PE ratio represents how much the market is willing to pay for each rupee of earnings. The EPS represents how much the company actually earns per share.

When a stock goes up, one of two things happened: the PE expanded (investors became more optimistic and willing to pay a higher multiple), or the EPS grew (the company made more money). Often both happen at the same time in a bull market, which is why returns feel magical. And when both contract simultaneously, you get the bloodbaths that make people swear off equities forever.

Stock prices grow for exactly 2 reasons: PE expansion or EPS growth. This is not my opinion. This is math. And the sooner you internalize this equation, the sooner you stop gambling and start investing.

Let me show you why this matters with a real example. Bata India in 2019 had a PE of 83 and a stock price of 1,250. In 2025, six years later, Bata India’s stock price was still 1,250. The PE had halved to 43. The sales went from 3,000 crore to 3,500 crore. Profits went from 330 crore to 350 crore. The EPS barely moved. The PE got cut in half.

In 6 years, Bata India lost 6 years. And half its PE.

The business grew a little. But the market decided it was no longer willing to pay 83 times earnings for a shoe company. The PE contracted, the EPS inched up, and the net result was zero returns for six years. If you had understood the equation, you would have seen this coming. At 83 PE, you were betting almost entirely on the market continuing to pay a premium. The business itself could not save you.

This is the foundational concept. Everything else in this article builds on it. So let me say it one more time:

If you are picking companies, you have to understand whether you are betting on PE expansion, EPS growth, or both. If you don’t know which one, you are gambling.

II.

II. PE Expansion: When the Crowd Gets Optimistic

PE expansion is when the market decides a company, a sector, or an entire country is worth paying more for. It is driven by sentiment, narratives, capital flows, and growth expectations. It has very little to do with what the company is actually earning right now.

Let me walk you through the Indian market’s PE expansion cycle so you understand how this works at a macro level.

In 2015, something big happened. A new government came in with a fresh promise of fast reforms, efficiency, and economic transformation. Investors saw “growth” and poured money into Indian equities. PE/VC funds deployed billions. These institutional players operate on 10-year cycles, and 2015 was the start of a massive pumping phase.

Between 2015 and 2019, the PE of the Indian market expanded significantly. Investors were not buying because earnings were exploding. They were buying because they believed earnings would explode in the future. That belief, expressed through higher PE multiples, drove stock prices up.

Then came the exit phase. Post-2020, foreign investors and PE/VC funds started selling. Mutual funds became the buyers. Foreign investors became the sellers. This phase has been continuing. The PE expansion narrative played out. The people who bought early on the PE expansion made their money. The people who bought at the top of the PE expansion are still waiting.

Now look at this pattern carefully. When PE expanded from 2015 to 2021, the Nifty went on a tear. When PE started to contract from the highs, even though corporate earnings were growing, the market went sideways or fell. This is the core mechanism you must understand.

Here is another real example: Railway stocks in India. In the last couple of years, railway stocks saw massive PE re-rating. The market decided that the government’s infrastructure push would mean massive growth for these companies. PEs went from 15-20 to 50-80 in some cases. Did earnings justify those multiples? Not even close. The market was pricing in five years of growth in advance. That is PE expansion at work.

And when PE expansion reverses, it is brutal. Because the business has not actually changed. Only the market’s willingness to pay has changed. You can wake up one morning and your stock is down 30% with no change in the company’s fundamentals. That is PE contraction. It is the silent wealth destroyer.

PE expansion on the market happens when investors see more “growth” opportunities. PE contraction happens when that growth does not materialize, or when there are simply better opportunities elsewhere.

Right now, fund managers in India are saying we are in a “bottom up” market, be stock specific. What does this really mean? It means they are fundamentally telling you that the era of broad PE expansion in India is over for now. You cannot just buy the index and expect the rising tide to lift your boat. You need to find individual stocks where EPS growth will compensate for the ongoing PE contraction.

There is another dimension to PE expansion that most people miss: it is heavily influenced by interest rates. When interest rates go down, firms borrow more. A fresh CAPEX cycle starts. Investors are willing to pay higher PE multiples because the discount rate on future earnings drops. This is why rate cut expectations can trigger PE expansion even before earnings actually improve. The current correction is NOT like the 2022 crash. In 2022, valuations were bloated and interest rates were expected to rise because we were in a high inflation year. Now, inflation is already low and rates are expected to be cut. This is a huge difference for PE expansion potential.

III.

III. EPS Growth: When the Business Actually Delivers

If PE expansion is about narratives and sentiment, EPS growth is about cold, hard numbers. Revenue goes up. Margins expand. Profits increase. Earnings per share grow. This is the bedrock of long-term wealth creation.

Let me explain why EPS growth is more reliable than PE expansion. A company’s PE can swing wildly based on market mood, but EPS is tied to actual business performance. If a company is growing revenues at 20% a year and expanding margins, EPS will grow regardless of what the market thinks about it in the short term. Eventually, the stock price follows.

Stock price follows earnings. I keep repeating this because it is the most important insight in investing.

Consider firms like Google, Meta, and Microsoft. These are compounding their earnings at around 18-20% CAGR in US dollar terms. For context, Indian large caps typically do this at about 14% in INR terms, which translates to 9-10% in USD terms. When you buy these US tech giants, you are primarily buying EPS growth. The PE might fluctuate, but the underlying earnings machine keeps grinding higher.

Now look at Mercado Libre (MELI). A long-term study pegs MELI’s EPS CAGR at about 59% over the past 5 years and 36.9% over the past 10 years. That is extraordinary earnings growth driven by real business expansion across e-commerce, payments, and lending in Latin America. In LATAM, MELI has 30%+ market share in e-commerce. In Argentina, Mercado Pago controls around 74-80% of the digital wallet float. Every new user or transaction makes the system stronger. More sales give them better credit data, which boosts wallet usage. That keeps merchants hooked, increases order volume, cuts costs, and attracts even more users. It is a self-reinforcing loop that just keeps accelerating.

Between March 2018 and 2021, the stock grew 350%. Since then, it consolidated for 4 years. But the EPS kept growing at 30%+ annually.

Here is the critical insight: it is unlikely that when the EPS grows in excess of 30%+, the stock price curtails forever. The stock may go sideways for years as the PE normalizes, but eventually the earnings growth forces the stock price to catch up. Different valuation models show MELI is undervalued by 30-60% from current prices. That is EPS growth pulling the stock price forward.

Let me give you the Indian counterpart. Max Healthcare has been posting 20% earnings growth, which is exceptional in the Indian market. Hospital stocks in general benefit from a structural tailwind: India is moving from informal to formal healthcare, unit economics work at high price points, there are massive barriers to entry, and margins expand with time. When you buy a hospital stock at reasonable valuations, you are betting on EPS growth, and that is a high-confidence bet.

Compare this to buying a stock at 100 PE where you need the narrative to stay alive. With EPS growth, you need the business to keep performing. One is within your circle of analysis. The other is a prayer.

The beauty of EPS growth is that it works even in sideways markets. When India’s market PE is contracting, stocks with genuine EPS expansion still reward you. The PE might compress from 25 to 20, but if EPS grows 40% in that same period, you still come out ahead. This is what “bottom up” stock picking actually means: finding companies where the earnings engine is strong enough to overcome the headwind of market-wide PE contraction.

Natco Pharma is an interesting example from the other direction. The firm had been growing fast with healthy year-on-year revenues and expanding EPS. Then one quarter, results spooked investors with a major revenue shrink, possibly due to tariff impacts on exports. The stock fell to a PE of 8-9. At that PE, with the growth trajectory expected to resume, you are getting the EPS growth almost for free. The market is pricing in permanent damage, but if the earnings recover, you make money on both PE expansion (from 8 back to 15-20) and EPS growth. That is the best possible setup.

IV.

IV. The Dangerous Trap: High PE Without High Growth

Now we get to the part where most investors lose serious money. This is the trap that fund managers will not warn you about because it would hurt their SIP flows.

There is zero point in buying very high PE firms where earnings growth cannot justify the multiple. Let me explain this with several real examples from the Indian market.

DMART trades at a PE of around 100. In a world where people are shifting to Blinkit, Swiggy Instamart, and other quick commerce platforms, DMART’s growth model faces real headwinds. The way DMART grew in its last cycle was by adding massive numbers of stores when borrowing rates were low around 2020-2021. Now borrowing rates are high, store expansion speed slows down, and the only way to justify a PE of 100 is to keep growing earnings at breakneck speed. In an environment where general economic growth is slowing, this is nearly impossible.

The PE will contract. It has to. It is math. Either the earnings catch up to justify the multiple, or the price comes down until the PE reaches a more reasonable level. As an investor, you are essentially praying for one of two things: either the company pulls off exceptional growth in a tough environment, or greater fools keep buying at these elevated prices. Neither is a sound investment thesis.

Lenskart is another case study. In July 2025, the founder took a 200 crore loan to buy Lenskart stock when the valuation was 8,500 crore. Three months later, the company was valued at 70,000 crore. That is 8X returns in months, which tells you everything about how bloated the valuations are. The founder was asked why the PE of the stock is 200. There was no sensible answer. The company’s profit margin is 1.7%. Not 17%. 1.7%. No proprietary tech, no world domination plans, but getting a 200 PE. This is speculation, not investing.

Eternal (Zomato’s parent) at 1500 PE. CDSL at 67 PE. Adani Enterprise at PE of 100 with a 3.3 lakh crore market cap. These are all examples where the PE is disconnected from any reasonable earnings trajectory.

A high PE is not really a problem if the growth of the industry, country, or firm matches up. But if the growth slows down, you can be in for a very long correction. That is where A LOT of money is lost.

Here is why this trap is so dangerous: when the stock starts falling, your instinct is to downward average. The firm is good, you think, so you keep buying the dip. But the PE contraction can continue for years. The firm can post decent results quarter after quarter, and the stock still goes nowhere because the PE is normalizing from absurd levels. You end up with dead money for half a decade.

The way to avoid this trap: before buying any stock, calculate how much EPS growth is required to justify the current PE. If the company needs to grow earnings at 30%+ for the next 5 years just to bring the PE to a reasonable level, and the economy around it is growing at 6-7%, you are making a heroic assumption. Do not do it.

DMART versus Zomato is actually an instructive comparison. Zomato can undertake vertical and horizontal integrations rapidly. They deliver food, but can sell tickets, start their own restaurant business, move into new categories. Every new business line expands the Total Addressable Market. DMART, on the other hand, can only grow by opening more physical stores. There is a hard limit on physical infrastructure expansion. Therefore, paying a high PE for Zomato has some logic because the growth optionality is real. Paying the same high PE for DMART makes much less sense because the growth ceiling is visible.

Let me also address why Indian stocks in general command higher PEs than US or China. Three reasons: India’s growth expectation was around 7-8%+, so paying a higher PE made sense for investors; India’s market was getting organized from unorganized (think HUL taking business from local manufacturers, bringing scale and efficiency); and businesses that could premiumize would benefit as India’s middle class grew.

But what has happened? GDP has structurally slowed down to 6-6.5%. Something strange has occurred: savings for the typical middle class (people earning 5-15 lakhs) have gone down. This undermines the premiumization thesis. So many of those high-PE stocks are sitting on assumptions that no longer hold. Anything that is discretionary, trading at a high PE, and caters primarily to the typical middle class is a dangerous combination to hold.

V.

V. Price Correction vs Time Correction: The Two Wealth Destroyers

As investors, you need to understand that there are two types of corrections in the market. Most people only think about price correction. The smarter investors also prepare for time correction.

Price correction is when the stock price falls sharply. This usually happens to firms that are fundamentally bad, or where the business has deteriorated. The firm loses stock value over time. This hurts investors visibly, and they stop investing if it happens to them frequently.

Time correction is the silent killer. This usually happens when a PE re-rating is underway. The stock does not lose much value, but it moves sideways for a frustratingly long period. Your money is not losing value on paper. But it is losing its most precious resource: time.

The Bata India example I mentioned earlier is a textbook illustration of time correction. Six years. Zero returns. The business was fine. Sales grew from 3,000 to 3,500 crore. But the PE went from 83 to 43, and the stock went absolutely nowhere.

ITC is another classic case. The stock has been in a valuation correction for years. Earnings have consistently disappointed investors. The PE keeps adjusting downward. At a PE of 21, it is not discounted enough for a bulk buy. The stock is decent. The business is fine. But you would be buying into a time correction that can drag on further.

IRCTC makes this point even more starkly. It will always be a great revenue-making firm. But one way or another, the increase in revenues is NOT systematically passed onto shareholders. The stock is back to its 2021 price. So from a shareholder returns perspective, you have had years of dead money despite a fundamentally sound business. That is time correction in its purest form.

Jio Financial Services is a live example. After its 2023 rally, the stock peaked at a PE of around 400. Since then, the PE has been correcting. At 120 PE, is it expensive? Nobody can definitively say what the right PE for Jio Fin is. But whenever it starts its next growth phase, it is likely a 20%+ CAGR business. The key is not buying at extremely premium valuations and getting trapped in a multi-year time correction.

In bull markets, you can easily get trapped with high PE stocks where the chances of time correction are very high. The only reason to hold through a time correction is if the EPS growth will eventually pull the stock price to new highs.

Here is how to think about this practically. The NIFTY itself goes through these phases. It averages around 12-13% returns per year, which means the NIFTY should double every 5.5-6 years. Before COVID, it was at 12,500. By 2025, it was around 24,000 in roughly 5 years. That means it was somewhat fairly valued. Not overvalued, not undervalued. Just fair.

What happens when the market is fairly valued? You get time correction. The market does not crash, but it does not run either. It consolidates in a range. During the last valuation correction from October 2021 to roughly May 2023, rotating capital was the only way to make sensible returns. If you bought and held through that phase, you experienced time correction. Your money sat idle while inflation eroded its purchasing power.

The practical lesson: when markets are fairly valued or overvalued, keep 15-20% cash positions. When the inevitable correction comes – whether a price correction or a time correction that creates pockets of undervaluation – you deploy that cash into the best opportunities. The people who have cash when fear is maximum make the most money.

And do not underestimate the power of this approach. During one such correction, I invested close to 5 crore by buying at supports and booking at resistance, generating 37-40 lakhs of profit within a month. This is not trading. This is capital rotation based on understanding where PE stands relative to its cycle.

The only reason a capital rotation strategy fails is if the market goes into a structural decline. This rarely happens, especially when firms are growing their earnings. Keep a check on earnings. If earnings are growing, the market will eventually follow. The only question is when.

VI.

VI. How to Identify What You Are Actually Betting On

Now let me give you a practical framework for every stock you look at. Before you invest a single rupee, answer these three questions:

Question 1: Am I betting on PE expansion, EPS growth, or both?

If you are buying Indian IT stocks right now, you are making a bet on PE expansion. These stocks have been undervalued relative to their fundamentals. PPFAS, one of India’s most respected mutual funds, has been increasing its allocation to IT stocks since January. The bet is that when sentiment shifts, the PE re-rates upward, and you capture that expansion. This is smart money making a PE expansion bet on a sector with decent fundamentals.

If you are buying Meta, Amazon, or Microsoft, you are primarily making an EPS growth bet. All three trade below their long-term PE ratios, so you are actually NOT buying expensive from a PE standpoint. What you are buying is 20% CAGR earnings growth in industries that are likely to outperform GDP. Bill Ackman put $2 billion on Meta stock. This is not a narrative bet. This is a math bet. The earnings will grow, and the stock price will follow.

If you are buying a hospital stock like Max Healthcare at a reasonable valuation, you are betting on both. Hospitals benefit from structural tailwinds that should drive PE expansion (more investor interest in healthcare as a theme) AND EPS growth (20% earnings growth driven by India’s formalization of healthcare).

Look at this chart carefully. Stock A is a pure PE expansion bet: if the PE goes from 20 to 30 over 5 years, you make 50% even with flat earnings. Nice, but it requires the market to cooperate. Stock B is a pure EPS growth bet: 18% annual EPS growth with no PE change gives you 129% returns over 5 years. The business does all the work. Stock C is the trap: 15% EPS growth sounds great, but if the PE contracts from 40 to 25, you barely break even after 5 years. This is the DMART scenario. This is the high-PE trap. The earnings grow, but the PE compression eats all your returns.

Question 2: What is the margin of safety if my PE expansion bet does not work?

This is critical. If you buy a stock at PE 20 betting on PE expansion to 30, and the PE stays at 20, what happens? If the EPS is growing at 15%, you still make 15% returns. Your floor is EPS growth. That is margin of safety.

But if you buy a stock at PE 100 betting on PE expansion to 120, and the PE contracts to 60, even 20% EPS growth will not save you. Your EPS goes up 20%, but your PE drops 40%. Net result: you lose money despite the company doing well. No margin of safety.

Question 3: What are the alternative opportunities?

This is what most investors forget. You do not invest in a vacuum. CDSL at 67 PE might be a fine business, but there are much better risk-adjusted stocks in the US. Amazon at a reasonable PE with 20%+ earnings growth is simply a better bet. When you buy CDSL at 67 PE, you need everything to go right. When you buy Amazon at a fair PE, you need the world’s largest e-commerce and cloud company to keep doing what it has been doing for 20 years.

Here is a quick framework I use for evaluating Indian stocks specifically. I look for 6 things:

  1. Unit economics – is this a needed product at a sensible price point?
  2. Expansion/contraction cycle – is the industry still expanding or has it matured?
  3. Growth rate – at least 15% profit growth for the next 3 years
  4. Competition – can the firm survive and defend its position?
  5. Valuation – some kind of stress, discount, or market mis-pricing
  6. Margins – will they expand or shrink going forward?

If a stock passes all six filters and is available at a reasonable PE, you are likely making an EPS growth bet with a margin of safety. If it fails on valuation but passes everything else, you are making a PE expansion bet without a safety net. Know the difference.

VII.

VII. The Macro Overlay: Why Market-Level PE Matters More Than Your Stock Pick

Here is an important conceptual point that most stock pickers miss entirely: if the overall market is undergoing PE contraction, even your good stock picks will do nothing.

Let me explain. If you buy good stocks in the Indian market, but the “market” as a whole undergoes PE correction, those good stocks stagnate or fall. This is because institutional capital flows drive the broad market PE. When FIIs are selling Indian equities (which has been happening for an extended period), even fundamentally strong stocks get dragged down.

Right now in India, the average PE of mid and small caps is in excess of 40+. This means valuations are still elevated at the index level. Even good mid and small cap stocks will get hit in a broad correction. Aavas Financiers is a perfect example. The numbers are fine. DII, FII, and promoters have all built positions. They have not cut their stakes. Revenues and profits are all up. But the stock is going through a valuation correction because the entire small and mid cap segment is correcting.

This brings us to a crucial comparison between Indian and US markets.

In India, the top 7 profit generators – Reliance, SBI, HDFC Bank, ONGC, TCS, ICICI Bank, IOCL – together earn roughly 40-45% of the total net profits of all NIFTY 50 companies. Their average PE is 18.7. The remaining 43 firms on NIFTY 50 have an average PE of 25-26X. For NIFTY 50 to be considered undervalued, the average PE should be around 22 or less.

Now here is the problem: if you invest in the top 7 profit-making firms in India, the average CAGR of stock price over various time periods is:

  • 1-year: around 10-12%
  • 3-year: around 11-13%
  • 5-year: around 11-13%
  • 7-year: around 11-13%
  • 10-year: around 13-15%

If you go beyond the top 7 to the remaining 43 firms, you are essentially buying “overvalued.” If you go to small and mid caps too early, the Sharpe ratio is not sensible compared to Nasdaq.

These are facts. Zero mutual fund managers will tell you this because it would hurt their SIP flows. If you take any timeframe – 5, 7, 10, or 20 years – and adjust for currency, Nasdaq Composite beats Nifty 50. This means that by investing in a lower risk asset (Nasdaq), you get almost equal or better returns than Indian small and mid caps, with less volatility.

The macro overlay matters enormously for your returns. You can pick the perfect stock, but if you are in the wrong market at the wrong valuation level, your returns will disappoint. This is why thinking about asset allocation across markets is not optional. It is essential.

India struggled in 2025 (stock market wise) because the upper income segment is not big enough and AI is not getting built in India. The AI revolution and upper income spending added most to the US GDP. Reliance pausing its plan to build lithium batteries due to lack of technical knowhow is a reality check. As an investor, you should be where the growth is. And right now, more growth is happening in US tech than in most Indian sectors.

But this does not mean abandon India entirely. It means be strategic about which PE x EPS bets you take:

  1. Buy AI stocks in the US when available at reasonable prices – these are EPS growth bets
  2. In India, buy industries growing at least 50% faster than general economic growth (like energy trading, fintech) – these are EPS growth bets
  3. Buy firms where PE expansion is likely (like IT firms currently undervalued) – these are PE expansion bets with earnings support
  4. Buy firms where operating profit margins are likely to expand (like hospitals) – these are EPS growth bets with potential PE expansion kicker
  5. Buy firms where the bottom line is turning positive (like select turnaround stories at low P/S ratios) – these are EPS inflection bets

The investment landscape right now is less about individual stocks and more about asset class allocation. Allocate your capital poorly across markets and themes, and you will see bad results regardless of how well you pick individual names.

VIII.

VIII. Valuation Tools Beyond PE: Matching the Metric to the Business

PE is the most common valuation metric, but it is not always the right one. Using the wrong metric for the wrong type of business will lead you to wrong conclusions. Let me help you understand when to use what.

PE Ratio (Price to Earnings): Best for profitable, stable companies with consistent earnings. Use PE when comparing firms within the same industry. For large cap, dividend-paying, profit-generating companies, PE works well. Banks, IT companies, FMCG players – PE is your primary tool here. When I say HDFC Bank is undervalued or Meta trades below its long-term PE, the PE ratio is the right lens.

However, PE has clear limitations. For firms like VBL (Varun Beverages), which is more of an infrastructure and bottling play, EV/EBIT is more appropriate. VBL’s EV/EBIT of roughly 38.7X versus a historic average of 30 tells us the firm has expanded in size but is not making enough earnings relative to that expansion. PE would miss this nuance because it does not account for the firm’s capital structure and debt.

EV/EBITDA (Enterprise Value to EBITDA): Use this for capital-intensive businesses like hospitals, real estate, and infrastructure companies. PE only includes equity in its calculation. EV includes equity, debt, and cash on hand, giving a more holistic view of the total enterprise. For hospitals and real estate firms, EV/EBITDA is the correct metric. As one of our community members correctly pointed out, analyzing hospital stocks through PE alone gives you an incomplete picture.

P/S (Price to Sales): Use this when earnings are negative or highly volatile. PVR is a perfect example. The PE went negative because the firm was losing money post-COVID. But you can still evaluate the stock using P/S. When the P/S is contracting and reaches a sensible level, it signals potential value even though the PE ratio is meaningless. This is how you find turnaround candidates that PE-only investors completely miss.

For small cap and growth-stage companies, PE ratios are often misleading. Duolingo is a small cap company. You simply cannot judge it by PE ratios. It has more to do with the growth rate and trajectory. The firm fell 20% after-hours despite beating earnings estimates because it pivoted its marketing model from paid back to freemium. This creates uncertainty around near-term earnings. But if you focus only on PE, you miss the bigger picture: Duolingo is positioning itself as the leading AI educational app, which explodes the Total Addressable Market. For such companies, look at revenue growth rates, DAU growth, and TAM expansion potential.

The key insight is this: valuation metrics are tools, not answers. A PE of 18 for Dreamfolks is cheap because the growth is there. A PE of 100 for DMART is expensive because the growth trajectory cannot justify it. A PE of 67 for CDSL could go either way: either the firm expands revenues really fast, or it will see PE erosion. Context is everything.

One powerful approach that has worked well for me: look at where the current PE is relative to the stock’s own historical range. Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft all three trade below their long-term PE ratios right now. This means you are STILL not buying expensive, even though the absolute PE might look high to someone unfamiliar with these businesses. The market is giving you a discount on proven earnings compounders. When proven compounders trade below their own historical PE, and their earnings growth rate has not slowed, you invest aggressively.

Conversely, when a stock like Whirlpool India trades at 50 PE and its own CEO says “if we have a business trading at 50 times multiple, and your own company trades a lot lower, it is basically an asset arbitrage” – that is management telling you the PE is too high. When the parent company is selling because the Indian subsidiary’s PE is elevated, listen to them. They know their business better than any analyst.

IX.

IX. Your Investment Action Plan: Putting PE x EPS to Work

Let me synthesize everything into an actionable framework you can use starting today.

Step 1: Decompose every investment into PE and EPS components.

Before you buy anything, ask: where will my returns come from? If you are buying Indian IT, you are betting on PE expansion. If you are buying US tech, you are betting on EPS growth. If you are buying hospitals, you are betting on both. Be honest about this. Do not tell yourself you are a “long-term investor” when you are actually making a short-term PE expansion bet on an overvalued stock.

Step 2: Avoid the high-PE trap ruthlessly.

Anything that is discretionary, trading at a high PE, and caters primarily to the typical middle class is a dangerous combination. The savings rate for people earning 5-15 lakhs has gone down. Premiumization assumptions from 5 years ago may not hold. If you are holding high PE consumer stocks, ask yourself: can this company grow earnings fast enough to justify the multiple? If the answer requires heroic assumptions, exit. I say the same boring things repeatedly: DMART at 100 PE, Lenskart at 200 PE, Eternal at 1500 PE – these are speculative positions, not investments.

Step 3: Build positions in tranches, not all at once.

General advice: always buy any stock in 3-5 tranches. This is not a risk management gimmick. It is the single most practical way to handle the uncertainty of PE expansion and contraction. If you bulk buy at the wrong PE level, you are stuck for years. If you build in tranches, your average cost smooths out the PE volatility. For example, with Jio Fin: invest a fixed budget monthly, but only buy if the stock is at or below its trading channel. If it is above, skip that month. This disciplined approach prevents you from buying at peak PE.

Step 4: Keep 15-20% cash when the market is fairly valued.

Big money does not come when things are fairly priced. It comes when things are discounted. Keeping 15-20% cash when markets are fair means you have ammunition when markets turn fearful. The most important decision is to keep emotions aside and follow simple math of risk management. The biggest loss in a sideways market is time correction loss. Cash positions help you avoid that.

When the valuation of the market is not crazy high, you should buy it. But the valuation can go from discounted to hyper-discounted. Handling the fall is the hard part. If your portfolio falls from 100 to 85 (that is a 15% loss), invest MORE. Bring the loss down to -10%. Average at supports. Keep the losses in check. At some point the market reverts, and you win.

Step 5: Think globally, allocate across markets.

Your portfolio should be at least 25% in AI and global tech. If you do not know much about investing, simply buy Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft. These have close to zero chance of getting disrupted by AI because they ARE the AI companies. They trade below long-term PE ratios. Will they move tomorrow? I do not know. Will they move in a month? I do not know. But will they outperform the economy after 3.5 years? 99% chance that they do.

With the trade wars increasing regionalism in the world, you need to think about multiple asset classes: how much in neutral currencies like Gold and BTC, how much in US vs India equities, whether to explore new emerging markets like UAE, how much in real estate, and whether to buy value economies like Europe. We worry too much about picking great individual stocks but forget that if emerging markets go down 40%, every stock within them goes down too.

Step 6: Use different strategies for different market phases.

In bull markets, ride the PE expansion but keep trailing stops. In sideways markets, rotate capital and sell options for income.
