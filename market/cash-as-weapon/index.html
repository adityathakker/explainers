I.

What is the most expensive mistake you'll ever make?

There is one mistake that destroys more retail portfolios than buying bad stocks, more than chasing momentum, more than ignoring valuations. It is running out of money to buy the dip.

I have seen this play out hundreds of times. The market falls 10–15%. Stocks you have been watching for months are finally at levels that make the risk-reward compelling. Private banks sit at multi-year lows. US tech is down 20–30%. The whole small-cap index is hammered. And you are fully invested, with zero cash, watching the opportunity of the cycle pass you by.

This is not a hypothetical. During the 2024–2025 correction in Indian markets, NIFTY fell over 14% from peak. Trump tariffs, ₹71,000 crore of FII outflows in a single month, and a real structural slowdown combined to create the kind of buying window that arrives once every few years. Investors who had held a 20–25% cash position were buying Nifty 50 at support, picking up HDFC Bank and ICICI Bank at depressed valuations, and entering US tech names at meaningful discounts. The ones who were 100% invested through the bull run? Panic-selling into the fall, or sitting frozen, watching unrealized gains evaporate with no ammunition to deploy.

The difference between these two groups is not intelligence, not stock-picking ability, not information access. It is portfolio construction. Specifically, whether or not they had a disciplined cash hedge in place before the correction arrived.

If you run out of money to buy the dips, it results from poor portfolio planning. A portfolio does not only consist of stocks; it consists of cash flow, emergency assets, and some form of diversification.

That captures the core idea of this article: cash is not idle money. It is your most powerful hedge. The next eight sections will quantify that claim — with sliders you can move and simulations you can rerun — until "20% cash" stops sounding like timid advice and starts looking like the math problem it actually is.

II.

Why is cash worth more than its yield?

The mutual fund industry has spent decades drilling a single message into Indian retail investors: "time in the market beats timing the market." Repeated endlessly, the phrase carries a subtle implication: being fully invested at all times is optimal. Holding cash is indecision. Every rupee not deployed in equities is a rupee underperforming.

This is marketing strategy disguised as investment advice. A mutual fund charges fees on assets under management. Every rupee you keep in cash is a rupee they cannot charge fees on. They have a structural incentive to keep you 100% invested. And they have built an entire narrative around why that supposedly serves you.

Now look at what these same funds actually do with their own books. According to Economic Times data from July 2024, SBI MF was sitting on ₹25,857 crore in cash; Quant MF on ₹12,269 crore; ICICI MF on ₹33,790 crore; HDFC MF on ₹23,545 crore; PPFAS MF on ₹12,367 crore. PPFAS's cash represents about 15% of their entire AUM[1].

These are not nervous retail investors hoarding money. They are professional managers running tens of thousands of crores, deliberately holding 10–15%+ in cash even though it drags near-term performance. Why? Because they understand something the "always be invested" crowd does not: cash is optionality.

The yield on a liquid debt fund is roughly 6.5% in India, 5% on a US T-bill. That is the carry. But the real value of cash is what it lets you do when the market hands you a 20%-off sale. Formally, if drawdowns of average depth d arrive with annual probability p, an extra rupee of cash earns its carry plus an expected option payoff. Roughly:

Value of $1 in cash = (1 + rcash) + p · d · (1 + rrecovery) − 1 where p is the annual probability of a drawdown of depth d, and rrecovery is the post-trough return earned by capital deployed at the bottom. The second term is the option value of dry powder; it grows fastest when d is large and recoveries are sharp.

Plug in even pessimistic numbers — p = 0.25, d = 0.15, rrecovery = 0.25 — and the optionality term contributes ~4.7% per year on top of the 6.5% carry. That is why a professional accepts the cash drag: they are not measuring cash against the bull-market return on equities, they are measuring it against missing the next dip. One thing the model can't capture: the behavioural value of cash. An investor who knows they have dry powder is far less likely to panic-sell during a crash. That behavioural edge alone is worth more than the 1–2% of carry drag for most retail investors.

What was our community doing while Buffett trimmed Apple and built a record cash pile? Maintaining 20% cash positions. Aligned not by copying him, but by following the same logic. Cash is a position. When you hold 20% cash, you are making an active investment decision: the marginal return of deploying that capital today is lower than the expected return of deploying it at better prices later. That is not laziness. That is disciplined capital allocation.

III.

How much cash should you actually hold?

The framework is simple. But "simple" does not mean "one-size-fits-all." The right percentage depends on where the market is, where you are in your investment journey, and your personal cash flows.

The baseline split is between growth assets (equities, real estate, BTC/gold) and a cash hedge. The ratio shifts with valuation:

  • Market clearly undervalued: 100% invested. No cash hedge. Deploy everything.
  • Market fairly valued: 90/10. Hold 10% cash as a baseline hedge.
  • Market towards overvalued: 80/20. This is where most of the cycle is spent.
  • Market in euphoria: 70/30. Become a net seller and build maximum cash reserves.

This is the framework I personally follow. When the market moved toward overvaluation I shifted to 80/20. When the rate-cut cycle seemed fully priced in and small-caps and mid-caps had elevated PEs, I went to 70/30. At one point my cash relative to equity portfolio hit 30% — meaning on a ₹10 crore equity book, I was sitting on ₹3 crore in cash, locked into risk-free assets, ready to deploy.

"But what about the opportunity cost?" If you are 80% invested and the market goes up 20%, your portfolio gains 16%. You have "missed" 4% by holding cash. Yes — and completely irrelevant. If the market instead falls 20% and you deploy your cash at the bottom, you buy at prices 20% cheaper. When the market recovers, those positions are up 25% from the trough. The math is asymmetric. The cost of holding cash in a bull market is linear. The benefit of having cash in a bear market is exponential.

The question this leaves open is: which percentage is best? 10? 20? 30? The honest answer is "it depends on the regime," and the next widget lets you see exactly how. The cash itself does not sit in a savings account earning 3.5%. It belongs in instruments that are liquid, safe, and generating a reasonable return: liquid debt mutual funds (in India), short-term fixed deposits (easily breakable), international debt funds (if you want currency diversification), or in the US, money-market funds and short T-bills currently yielding 5%+. The return is secondary. Its primary job is to be available when you need it.

IV.

When and how do you actually deploy the bucket?

Having cash is only half the strategy. The half most people get wrong is knowing when and how to deploy it. When a correction begins, you do not dump cash on day one. You deploy in tranches, calibrated to the depth of the fall.

Here is the framework I use:

  • At 5% from peak: deploy 25% of the cash bucket. Quality names at a modest discount.
  • At 10% from peak (normal correction): deploy another 35% — you are now 60% deployed. History says most corrections stabilise around here.
  • At 15–20% from peak (bear territory): deploy another 30%. Fear is at its maximum, the best opportunities appear, you still hold 10% in reserve.
  • Beyond 20% (crisis-level): deploy the remaining 10%. COVID-level, 2008-level events — rare, but they happen.

Let me show how this played out in real time. When NIFTY broke the 25,000 psychological level and we entered official correction territory, what went out to the community was:

NIFTY 25K psychological level is now broken. This means we are officially in a correction zone. Now, don't rush to buy. Do your 1 bulk 25% thing and sit tight.

That was the first tranche. 25% of the cash bucket, deployed at the first major support. Then we waited. We let the market tell us what it wanted to do. A few weeks later, when the market had settled further:

NIFTY 50 has corrected 5% from its peak. It is a good time to use 25% of the money from your cash bucket that we have been saving for the last several months. And invest in NIFTY 50. In case the market corrects more, we will use the rest of the money for downward averaging.

The discipline is critical. You are not trying to catch the exact bottom. Nobody can. You are systematically deploying capital at progressively better prices, ensuring your average entry sits somewhere in the lower half of the correction range. The strategy works whether the dip is 5% or 25%.

What to buy when you deploy. When the bucket opens, you do not scatter-gun. You buy in priority order: (1) index funds first — Nifty 50, S&P 500, broad exposure, no single-stock risk; (2) undervalued quality — private banks that have underperformed but have solid corporate numbers, housing finance firms, businesses whose fundamentals haven't changed while the price has fallen; (3) growth at a discount — IT stocks, brokerages, AMCs, structural-tailwind businesses caught in the downdraft (this is what PPFAS did starting January 2025); (4) international markets — if Indian markets are falling but US tech holds up or vice versa, your cash gives you the flexibility to deploy where risk-reward is best.

Do not use more than 30% of your opportunity fund now.

That line — a real-time instruction during the LTCG/STCG tax-change sell-off — illustrates the discipline. Even in an acute fall, you deploy a portion, not all of it. You always keep reserves.

Two macro signals help calibrate. The Insider Sell-to-Buy Ratio (STB) — how aggressively CEOs, founders, and promoters are selling their own company stock in aggregate — peaked before 2008, before March 2020, and before the latest correction cycle. When the people who know the most are reducing exposure, that's information[2]. Use it not to predict exact crash dates (no one can) but to calibrate: when the ratio peaks, tighten the hedge from 20% to 25–30%; when it normalises, deploy more aggressively.

The second distinction is between price correction and time correction. A price correction is when NIFTY drops 10–15% quickly — painful but creates obvious buying opportunities. A time correction is different and trickier: the market does not lose much value but moves sideways for months or even years, oscillating in a 15–20% range. During a time correction, having cash lets you buy at the lower end of the range and stay patient. Your tranche framework works perfectly for sharp falls; for time corrections, deploy smaller amounts at the bottom of the range and wait. The cash bucket absorbs shocks in both.

V.

What do Buffett, PPFAS, and Quant MF do with their cash?

Understanding how institutional investors handle cash sharpens your own approach. These are not theoretical models. These are real allocation decisions made by people running tens of thousands of crores.

Warren Buffett and Berkshire Hathaway. Buffett's cash management is a masterclass. When he sold half his Apple position, the popular narrative was "turning bearish." The reality is more nuanced. Buffett recognised that Apple, after its run-up, offered lower forward returns relative to the opportunity cost of holding cash. With short-term US Treasury yields at 5%+, cash was generating attractive risk-free returns. More importantly, he wanted the firepower for the next dislocation. The same logic applies at smaller scale: when you keep 20% in liquid funds earning 6–7%, you are not leaving money on the table. You are earning a reasonable return while maintaining the ability to deploy at significantly better prices.

PPFAS Mutual Fund. One of the most respected funds in India. With ₹12,367 crore in cash against ₹75,000 crore AUM — ~15% — they were signalling something important: even a fund supposed to be fully invested was choosing significant dry powder. Then, starting January 2025, PPFAS began aggressively adding IT stocks. They had waited, built the position, and deployed when they saw genuine value in a sector that had been beaten down. Cash-as-weapon, at the institutional level.

PPFAS has increased its allocation for IT stocks. Now on one hand, most retail trusts funds like PPFAS. On the other: they find it hard to take actions that the fund itself is taking.

Retail investors say they trust PPFAS, but when PPFAS sits on 15% cash, those same retail investors are 100% invested. When PPFAS deploys aggressively into IT, retail investors are still hesitating. The lesson is not to copy PPFAS trade-for-trade. The lesson is to adopt their discipline around cash management.

Quant Mutual Fund has been even more aggressive. When they pushed cash holdings well above the industry standard of 5%, the signal was clear. Mutual funds hate holding cash — it drags short-term numbers, and short-term numbers are how they attract inflows. For a fund to accept that drag voluntarily, the conviction that better opportunities lay ahead must be very strong.

Many Mutual Funds, including Quant, have increased their cash holding recently. MFs usually keep 5% cash. The only reason why MF will increase their cash positions 10% or above is: if they are expecting the markets to crash, or if they are going to rotate money into a new segment.

Well, actually 🧐 — cash isn't always a bullish signal

Be careful when reading institutional cash levels. A fund can hold cash because (a) they expect a drawdown and want optionality, (b) they are mid-rotation between sectors, or (c) they've been hit with redemptions and the cash is operational, not strategic. Only (a) and (b) carry signal.

The cleanest read is from closed-end vehicles like Berkshire, where Buffett can't be forced to sell, so a rising cash pile reflects a deliberate allocation choice. For open-end mutual funds, cross-check against net flows: if PPFAS is on 15% cash and taking inflows, that's a strong signal. 15% cash on the back of redemptions is a different story.

The point: cash hedging is not a retail crutch. It is a professional capital allocation strategy. The best funds in the world do it. The greatest investor in history does it. And you should too.

VI.

How should the cash ratio shift across market cycles?

The 20% rule is not static. It shifts with where you are in the cycle. Understanding when to raise cash, when to lower it, and when to flip from net buyer to net seller is what separates sophisticated investors from those who just follow a fixed allocation.

In a bull market (greed phase). When markets are making new all-time highs consistently:

  1. Don't over-invest. Maintain the 20–25% cash hedge. This is the time to be disciplined, not excited.
  2. Don't under-invest either. Whatever new money you are earning, keep investing — as long as your cash hedge ratio stays intact.
  3. Buy quality. Not the market for speculative small-caps. Firms with 15% CAGR growth potential, low risk.

The temptation in a bull market is to go 100% invested because "everything is going up." Resist this. Everything goes up until it does not, and when it stops, the speed of the reversal catches most investors completely off guard.

In a correction (fear phase). This is where your cash earns its keep. At the first support level, deploy 25% of the bucket — index funds, private banks, high-quality names that have fallen with the market. If the first support breaks, deploy another 25–35%. Then wait for consolidation. Do not chase falling knives. Let the market form a base before deploying more.

In a sideways market (consolidation phase). This is actually the trickiest phase — the market is not crashing but not rallying. It moves in a wide range (15–20%) but goes nowhere net. Keep the 20% hedge intact. If your cash bucket starts growing (new income), and the market dips to the lower end of the range, deploy the excess. Do not let the cash position grow beyond 30% in a sideways market — that is too much sitting out when there are real buying opportunities at the lower end.

If your 20% cash position goes up, you should go ahead and invest at minor falls. For example: if your portfolio size is 1 crore, you have kept 20 lakh on the sidelines. Now you are getting more cash flows: let's say you earned 5 lakh more. Since your cash bucket is already full, you should go ahead and invest that 5 lakh.

This is nuanced but critical. The cash hedge has a target size. New income above target gets invested. You are not hoarding indefinitely — you are maintaining a specific reserve ratio and deploying everything above it.

In euphoria (danger phase). When a single Yamaha showroom in NCR files for an SME IPO raising 12 crore and receives bids of 4,700 crore, you are in euphoria. When MamaEarth announces a subsidiary merger and its market cap jumps by 2,000 crore for a move that changes nothing about the business, you are in euphoria. In euphoria: raise the cash hedge to 30%+; become a net seller; take profits on overvalued positions; lock gains into risk-free instruments; be extremely selective about any new purchases.

VII.

What does the Step-Down SIP actually buy you?

Most Indian retail investors have been taught that SIPs are the ultimate set-and-forget investment strategy. For people who cannot be bothered to learn investing, that is fine. For anyone reading this article, there is a strictly better approach: the Step-Down SIP with a Cash Bucket.

The traditional SIP: you invest ₹1 lakh per month into mutual funds or stocks, regardless of market conditions. In a bull market you buy high; in a bear market you buy low. Over time, you get a blended average. Fine, but not optimal.

The Step-Down SIP with Cash Bucket: instead of investing the full ₹1 lakh every month, you invest ₹80,000 and put ₹20,000 into your cash bucket (liquid debt fund or FD). When the market falls 10%+, you empty that bucket and deploy in bulk at distressed prices.

Instead of putting 1 lakh per month, put 80k. Rest 20k, in liquid debt funds or FDs. This is a step-down SIP, very similar to cash hedges.

The math is powerful. Over 12 months: a traditional SIP commits ₹12 lakh at average price X. A Step-Down SIP commits ₹9.6 lakh monthly at price X, plus ₹2.4 lakh deployed in bulk at a 15% discount (price 0.85X). The blended average cost is lower. And if no correction happens? You still have ₹2.4 lakh in liquid funds earning 6–7%, ready for later deployment. Downside is minimal; upside is significant.

The cash-bucket system for direct stock investors is the same idea, scaled. Determine your total investable portfolio (say ₹10 lakh). Allocate 80% to equities (₹8 lakh across 15–20 stocks). Allocate 20% to your cash bucket (₹2 lakh in liquid funds). Any new income first refills the bucket to 20%, then goes into equities. When the market falls to a major support, deploy 25% of the bucket. After deployment, refill with new income. The system is self-correcting: as your portfolio grows, the bucket grows proportionally; as you deploy in corrections, new income refills.

Walk through it with real numbers. Total portfolio ₹1 crore: ₹80 lakh equity, ₹20 lakh cash bucket. Market falls 10%. You deploy ₹5 lakh (25% of bucket) into Nifty 50 and select private bank stocks. Now: ₹85 lakh equity (including the ₹5 lakh at discount), ₹15 lakh cash. Cash ratio has dropped to ~15%; new monthly income works to bring it back to 20%. Market falls another 5%. You deploy ₹7 lakh. Now: ₹92 lakh equity (significant portion at 10–15% discounts), ₹8 lakh cash. You are now heavily invested, but your average cost is meaningfully lower than someone who was 100% invested from the start.

VIII.

Where else can dry powder live?

The 20% cash rule exists within a broader construction framework. Cash is one form of non-equity hedge, but the smartest investors think about hedging across multiple asset classes. A portfolio does not only consist of stocks. It consists of equities (Indian, US, China), cash and liquid instruments (the 20% hedge), real estate (cash-flow assets), and gold and BTC (insurance assets).

Each serves a different function. Equities are your growth engine. Cash is your ammunition. Real estate provides cash flows that fund equity buying during drawdowns. Gold and BTC are insurance against fiat debasement and systemic uncertainty.

Here is how I think about my own allocation: total portfolio — 60% stocks, 30% real estate, 10% BTC/gold. Within stocks: 65% US/China, 35% India. Within India stocks: 80% large caps, 20% mid and small caps. Cash hedge: 20–30% of equity portfolio, depending on market conditions.

Since 2022 I have been buying real estate aggressively. Many questioned it. But during the 2024–2025 correction the diversification did three things: (1) provided rental income to invest on the dip; (2) kept me in an asset class that hardly corrects, so I could liquidate one property to bulk-invest in stocks; (3) gave certainty about making income next month regardless of what stocks did.

This is a point that I picked up in 2021: that it is critical to be asset class diversified. At that stage, I used to be almost 100% stocks, 0% anything else. The steps I took in 2022 are helping me now.

For investors who cannot afford real estate, gold and BTC serve a similar function. If you have 5% in gold and the equity market crashes 20%, you can liquidate the gold (which typically rises during equity selloffs) and deploy that capital into stocks at depressed prices. This is cross-asset rebalancing — one of the most powerful wealth-building strategies available to retail investors. BTC performs well in high-liquidity environments (when central banks print, BTC rises); gold performs well in uncertainty (when geopolitical risk is elevated, gold rises). A 5%/5% split is a 10% non-correlated hedge that expands your effective cash bucket when you need it most[3].

Why companies hoard cash too. There is a useful corporate parallel. The best-run companies in the world — Apple, Google, Microsoft — sit on enormous cash piles. Why? Same reason you should: optionality. One key use is stock buybacks. When Apple buys back its own shares from the open market, it is doing exactly what we are discussing: deploying cash at attractive prices. When the stock falls, the company uses cash reserves to buy shares, creating a counter-buyer position that supports the price and rewards long-term shareholders. One of the key reasons mega-cap tech stocks recover faster from drawdowns — they have an internal cash weapon smaller companies do not.

Firms like Apple and Google are right now cash-rich firms. One of the key reasons why the stock price of these firms keeps going up is they buy their own stocks from the open market when it falls and bring the stock price back up.

As a retail investor you are doing the same with your portfolio. When the market falls, you deploy cash to buy more of your best positions at lower prices. Same idea, smaller scale. This also tells you which companies to own. Cash-rich companies that buy back shares are structurally more resilient in downturns. They have the same weapon you have. In a portfolio where both you and the companies you own are deploying cash strategically during drawdowns, the compounding effect over a decade is extraordinary.

The point: your "cash" strategy is not just the 20% in liquid funds. It is having multiple sources of deployable capital. Your salary is one. Your liquid funds are another. Your gold allocation is a third. Your rental income is a fourth. The more sources of dry powder, the more aggressively you can buy during corrections, and the better your long-term returns.

IX.

What is the action plan, in six steps?

Distil everything into a concrete plan you can implement starting today.

Step 1: Audit your current position. Look at your portfolio right now. What percentage is in equities? In cash or liquid instruments? If you are 100% in equities with zero cash reserve, you are exposed. Build the buffer before the next correction, not during. If your equity book has positions where trimming 10–20% of overvalued holdings makes sense, do it. Sell the stocks where you cannot confidently say "If this falls 20%, I will buy more." Those are the positions to trim.

Step 2: Set your target cash ratio. Use the framework: undervalued 0–10%, fairly valued 10–15%, overvalued 20–25%, euphoria 25–30%+. Assess honestly. Elevated PEs despite small/mid-cap corrections, average earnings growth, bullishness without fundamental backing → 20–25% is the right number.

Step 3: Implement the Step-Down SIP. If you invest monthly, reduce your equity SIP by 20% and redirect it into a liquid debt fund. This builds the bucket automatically without requiring any selling decisions.

Step 4: Define your deployment triggers. Write them down. Before the market falls. Not during.

  • At what NIFTY level (or percentage decline) will you deploy your first 25%?
  • At what level will you deploy the next 35%?
  • What specific stocks or indices will you buy?

Having this written before a correction removes emotion from the equation. When the market is falling and everyone around you is panicking, you pull out your plan and execute mechanically.

Step 5: Replenish after deployment. After you deploy cash into a correction, the bucket is depleted. Immediately begin refilling with new income. The goal is to always maintain your target ratio, because you never know when the next opportunity will come.

Step 6: Diversify your sources of dry powder. Beyond your liquid fund cash bucket: rental income from real estate; gold or BTC that can be liquidated in extreme scenarios; fixed deposits that can be broken; even borrowing against real estate at low interest rates to deploy into deeply discounted equities (advanced, not for everyone).

The stress test

If the market falls 20% tomorrow, do you have cash to buy? If your best stock falls 30%, can you add to it? If a once-in-a-decade buying opportunity appears, do you have enough capital to take a meaningful position? If the answer to any of these is "no," your portfolio is not properly constructed. Fix it now, while the market is giving you the luxury of time.

Why this matters more now. The world is running on liquidity. If you track the Global Liquidity Index, you'll see it jacked up dramatically post-COVID. Since then, governments have tried to keep liquidity in check to prevent inflation from spiraling. But the key insight: no government can afford to actually withdraw liquidity from the market. They can slow the growth of liquidity, but net liquidity has to go up over time — there is no other option.

The world is screwed. We are surviving on fake liquidity, inflation cannot be controlled at least for a decade. We have no option but to do high-risk investing in this time frame. And, deal with the drawdowns.

The drawdowns will keep coming, but they will be followed by liquidity-driven recoveries. Investors with cash to deploy during drawdowns capture the recoveries. Investors who are fully invested ride the drawdown down and the recovery back up — getting nowhere net — while the cash-deployers buy the bottom and ride only the recovery. When RBI pushed ₹60,000 crore into the economy through Open Market Operations during the 2025 correction — firefighting against ₹71,000 crore of FII selling — the injection created a bottom. Cash-ready investors bought at almost exactly the point of maximum opportunity. Without cash, you were a spectator watching liquidity lift stocks you already owned back to where they were.

Indian markets went 6× from 1999 to 2008. They went 2× from 2010 to 2019. Over any decade-long period, equity markets go up. The question is not whether you make money — it is how much. And the investors who make the most are not the ones who picked the best stocks. They are the ones who had cash to deploy when everyone else was running scared.

Think of your portfolio as a business. Every business needs working capital — money set aside to seize opportunities, weather downturns, fund growth. A business that deploys 100% into inventory with zero working capital is one bad quarter from bankruptcy. Your portfolio is the same. The 20% cash hedge is your working capital. It is what keeps your portfolio alive and growing through every type of market.

If I simply take the profits out now, put it in an FD, do nothing for the next 2 years: I would still beat 90%+ funds out there. I am not saying this to boast. Any retail guy can beat big funds. Why? Because we have massive advantages: we can rotate capital, invest small amounts, sit or not sit on cash. These are all structural flexibilities we have, which many large funds do not have. So take advantage.

That structural flexibility — the ability to hold cash, deploy it on your own timeline, and not answer to quarterly performance pressure — is the single biggest edge retail investors have over institutions. Mutual funds cannot sit on 30% cash for six months without their AUM shrinking as panicked investors redeem. You can. Hedge funds cannot explain to their LPs why they are 80/20 for an extended period. You do not have to explain to anyone.

Cash is not a drag on your portfolio. It is not money earning 6% when it could be earning 15%. It is the most powerful weapon in your arsenal, sitting quietly in the corner, waiting for the moment when it can multiply your wealth. The 20% cash rule is not about being cautious. It is about being ready. It is about having the courage to act when the market gives you a gift, and the discipline to wait until it does.
