I.

Why does anyone buy options at all?

Say "options" and most retail investors picture one of two things: a labyrinth too complicated to bother with, or a degenerate gambler blowing up a brokerage account on weekly expiries. Both pictures are wrong. Both are costing you money.

Here is the reality almost nobody will say out loud, because it is either too complex for a financial-media segment or it threatens the simplistic SIP narrative: options are insurance contracts. That is the entire concept. Buy a put on your portfolio, and you have bought insurance against a crash. Sell a covered call against shares you already own, and you have rented them out for premium income. Every serious investor — Buffett, Cuban, Altman, every hedge fund, every family office — uses options as ordinary portfolio plumbing.

Options get a bad reputation for the same reason cars get blamed for accidents. It is not the instrument; it is how people use it. Buying out-of-the-money weekly calls on a meme stock with the whole account is gambling. Buying a twelve-month put on a $1 million portfolio to cap the worst case at 14.5% is risk management. These are two completely different activities that happen to share a name.

Before any math, look at the four contracts you can hold and what they each commit you to. Every option strategy on earth is built from these four shapes.

If you take 100 units of risk to make 10 units of return, you might win that one trade and lose at the system. Almost guaranteed. The point of this article is to show you the better system.

II.

What does a put actually do to your portfolio in real dollars?

Abstract payoff curves are easy to nod along to. To feel them, you have to put a real portfolio behind one.

I had a $1 million US portfolio sitting on 35% paper profits. Most of it in big-cap tech. The portfolio had served its purpose — the next job was to keep it. So I bought insurance:

  1. 17 lots of QQQ puts. QQQ is the ETF tracking the Nasdaq 100; that is the right benchmark for the portfolio's beta.
  2. Strike: 545, roughly 10% below the spot at the time[1].
  3. Premium paid: ~$45,000, which is 4.5% of portfolio value.
  4. Expiry: September 2026 — a full year of coverage.

The arithmetic of what that $45,000 buys, scenario by scenario, is what makes the case. The maximum loss on the insured leg is bounded the moment the contract is opened:

Max loss = (1 − K/S0) + premium/S0 where S0 is spot at purchase, K is strike, premium is what you paid

For QQQ at spot 605, strike 545, premium 4.5% of portfolio: (1 − 545/605) + 0.045 = 0.099 + 0.045 = 14.4%. No matter what happens between now and September 2026 — tariff war, AI bubble bursting, geopolitical surprise — the portfolio cannot lose more than 14.4%. The downside has a floor.

Well, actually 🧐 — the put doesn't perfectly mirror QQQ

The simulator above treats the portfolio as if it moves 1:1 with QQQ. In reality, your individual stocks have a beta to QQQ that is rarely exactly 1.0. If you hold concentrated NVIDIA and the put is on the ETF, your portfolio falls faster than QQQ in a tech selloff — so the put is partial coverage, not exact.

The fix is either to hedge with options on the individual names (more expensive, more precise) or to size up the index hedge to cover beta > 1. Most professional hedgers do the latter and accept some basis risk in exchange for tighter spreads and deeper liquidity.

At the time I bought this insurance I was sitting on 35% gains. Even in a 50% crash, the worst case is 35% − 14.4% = a still-positive 20%. I paid 4.5% of the portfolio to guarantee no market event could push me below 20% net. That is the entire trade.

Gambling is having a $1 million portfolio with no downside protection and hoping nothing bad happens. The person without put options is taking more risk than the person with them — even though only one of them is technically "doing options."

When markets did fall — tariff anxiety, DeepSeek, rate uncertainty — the insured investor is not panicking. They are calmly evaluating whether to buy the dip. That is the difference between an insured and an uninsured portfolio. One panics, the other plans.

III.

Where does the premium come from? The five forces of price

If options are insurance, the premium is what the insurance company charges. The same way a home-insurance quote depends on the house's value, the neighborhood, the term, and the deductible — an option premium depends on exactly five inputs:

  • S — the spot price of the underlying.
  • K — the strike, the price you have the right to transact at.
  • T — the time remaining until expiry.
  • σ — the volatility of the underlying (how much it tends to move per day).
  • r — the risk-free interest rate.

Plug those five into the Black-Scholes formula and out comes a price. The formula looks intimidating; the intuition is not[2].

C = S · N(d1) − K · e−rT · N(d2)
d1 = [ln(S/K) + (r + σ2/2)T] ÷ (σ√T) N() is the standard normal CDF; the put price follows from put-call parity

You do not need to derive it. You need to feel how the five levers move it. Each one has its own Greek letter, because each one is a partial derivative of the price with respect to that input.

  • Delta = ∂C/∂S — how much the option moves for a $1 move in the underlying.
  • Gamma = ∂²C/∂S² — how much delta itself moves; the curvature.
  • Vega = ∂C/∂σ — how much premium moves per 1% bump in vol.
  • Theta = ∂C/∂T — how much premium decays per day passing.
  • Rho = ∂C/∂r — how much premium moves per 1% bump in rates (small for short-dated options).

Well, actually 🧐 — the formula assumes things that aren't true

Black and Scholes assumed constant volatility, no jumps, frictionless trading, no dividends, and log-normal returns. Every one of those is wrong in the real world. Vol smiles (Section VII) exist precisely because the market knows the formula is wrong about the tails.

It still gets used because (a) for at-the-money, short-dated options it's empirically close enough, and (b) market makers quote in implied volatility, not in price — so the formula is reduced to a translator between vol and dollars, with the actual disagreement happening in vol space.

IV.

Why does an option lose value when nothing happens?

Hold an option overnight. The stock closes exactly where it opened. The option is worth less the next morning. Why?

Because every option has two components — intrinsic value and time value. Intrinsic value is "what is the option worth if I exercised right now": max(S − K, 0) for a call, max(K − S, 0) for a put. That part does not erode from the calendar; it erodes only if the spot moves against you.

Time value is what the buyer pays for the chance the spot will move in their favor before expiry. Every day that passes, there is one fewer day for that chance to play out. Time value can only go down. By definition, on expiry day, time value is zero — whatever is left in the option's price is purely intrinsic.

That daily erosion is theta. And theta is not linear. It accelerates as expiry approaches.

Well, actually 🧐 — why theta accelerates

The mathematical reason: time value is proportional to σ · √T. At-the-money, the price of an option is roughly 0.4 · S · σ · √T (a Black-Scholes approximation). When T goes from 30 days to 29, you lose a tiny sliver of √T. When T goes from 2 days to 1, you lose half of what remains. The square-root function is steep near zero.

Practical consequence: an option buyer should generally avoid the last 30 days unless directionally certain, because that is where theta does most of its damage. An option seller often targets exactly that window because that is where the premium drips out fastest.

Theta is the option buyer's enemy and the option seller's friend. You are paying or being paid for the passage of time. The QQQ put I bought in Section II had a full year to run — by paying for time, I bought a slow theta drag, not a sudden one. If I had bought a one-week put instead, the daily decay would have been savage.

V.

What does volatility actually mean in dollars?

"NVIDIA has a 40% implied vol." What does that mean? It does not mean NVIDIA will be 40% higher or lower a year from now. It means the market expects log-returns to have a standard deviation of 40% over a one-year horizon, with the price evolving as a random walk in the meantime.

Translate that into a one-day expected move:

One-day σ in dollars ≈ S · σannual · √(1/252) 252 trading days in a year

NVIDIA at $180 with 40% annual vol: expected one-day standard deviation is 180 × 0.40 × √(1/252) = $4.54. About two-thirds of trading days should see moves smaller than that. About 5% of days should see moves bigger than $9 (two sigma). And about 0.3% of days should see moves bigger than $13.50 (three sigma). That is what "40 vol" actually buys you.

Now run that random walk forward many times and look at the cloud of where the price could land. Each path is one possible future; the histogram on the right is where they all end up.

Well, actually 🧐 — log-normal, not normal

The widget uses geometric Brownian motion: log-returns are normal, prices are log-normal. This matters because a stock can lose 50% but cannot lose 110% — price has a hard floor at zero, and the distribution has to be skewed right to reflect that.

You can see this in the histogram: it's not symmetric around the starting price. There are more paths ending modestly above the start than modestly below, but the left tail (catastrophes) is also fatter than the right tail (moon shots). That asymmetry is exactly why puts cost more than equidistant calls — a fact called skew, which we'll come back to in Section VII.

This is also the answer to "is the option fairly priced?" Run the simulation many times. Compute the average payoff at expiry. Discount that back to today at the risk-free rate. That number is the theoretical fair value — and on average, that is what Black-Scholes spits out. The market price you actually pay differs because traders disagree with the formula's assumptions about σ (and, for crashes, about whether the random walk has fat tails it shouldn't).

VI.

Why is selling options like running an insurance company?

So far we have been on the buyer's side — paying premium for protection. Flip the table. Sell that same put, and you are the insurance company. You collect premium every day, you hope the claim never happens, and you keep a reserve in case it does. That is the whole business.

The mapping is one-to-one:

Insurance companyOption seller
Premium collected upfrontOption premium credited to account
Probability of claimProbability of expiring in-the-money
Severity of claimHow deep ITM the option ends up
Reserve held against claimCollateral / margin / underlying shares
Reinsurance for tail eventsBuying further OTM options to cap loss
Hurricane wiping out a year of profitsMarch 2020, August 2024 — multi-sigma days

The two real-world flavors most retail-friendly investors use are covered calls (rent on stocks you own) and cash-secured puts (paid to wait for a dip). Both are "covered" — you are not exposed to unbounded loss because the underlying obligation is fully backed by stock or cash on your balance sheet. That is the discipline that turns option selling from gambling into insurance.

A covered call concrete example. You own 100 shares of Meta at $640, total cost basis $64,000. You write a 6-month call at strike $760 (about 19% out-of-the-money). The premium you collect: roughly $2,850, or 4.5% in six months — ~9% annualized just on the option side. If Meta closes above $760 at expiry, you sell at $760 (a 19% gain on the underlying you wanted to take anyway, plus the 4.5% premium). If Meta stays below $760, you keep the shares and the premium. The worst case is Meta crashing — in which case your $2,850 cushion lowers your effective cost basis from $640 to roughly $611.

Three regimes. Below strike: you outperform by the premium. At strike: maximum upside. Far above strike: you cap out and the covered-call line stops climbing — you gave away the moonshot in exchange for the rent.

The cash-secured-put twin. You have $38,000 in cash. You sell one Microsoft put at strike $380 (10% below the current spot of around $420). You receive perhaps $500 in premium. If MSFT stays above $380, the put expires worthless — you keep $500 and do nothing. If MSFT falls below $380, you are assigned: you spend your $38,000 and end up owning 100 shares at an effective cost of $375 ($380 strike minus $5 premium per share). The position you actually wanted, at a price you actually wanted, with $500 of paid waiting on top.

You are not becoming an options trader. You are buying and selling options on things you already wanted to own at the prices you already wanted to own them at. There is a huge difference between that and being a gambler.

The two together are a complete cycle. Sell puts to get paid while waiting to buy. Once assigned and holding shares, sell calls to get paid while waiting to sell. In a sideways market — the worst environment for buy-and-hold investors — this cycle generates 8–15% annualized income on top of whatever the underlying does.

VII.

The Indian options trap and the US opportunity

Everything in this article is structured around US-listed options for one reason: the Indian options market is, for retail, designed to lose[3].

Problem 1: taxes. Options profits in India are taxed as business or speculative income at slab rates — meaningfully worse than long-term capital gains. The tax drag means a strategy that nets 12% gross in the US might net 6–7% in India after the tax bite.

Problem 2: instruments. Liquid Indian options exist on Nifty, Bank Nifty, FINNIFTY, and a thin layer of individual names. You cannot hedge your IT-heavy portfolio with a put on TCS at meaningful size. The US gives you deep options markets on thousands of stocks and ETFs with tight spreads.

Problem 3: margin. Indian brokers demand large margins on option positions — far more than the actual position risk warrants. Capital efficiency is poor, which compounds the tax drag.

Problem 4: weekly expiry casino. The Indian retail F&O market is dominated by weekly Nifty/BankNifty buyers chasing intraday moves. SEBI's own 2024 study found 9 out of 10 individual F&O traders are net losers, with average annual losses around ₹1.1 lakh per trader. That is not an options problem — it is a structurally rigged casino dressed up as one.

In US markets the dynamics are inverted. Most retail flow is on individual tech stocks, where high-net-worth concentrated holders have a genuine need to hedge — and they are willing to pay for it. That demand creates organic, persistent premium for sellers to harvest. The infrastructure is world-class. Tax treatment is more favourable, especially if you can route through a 0%-CGT jurisdiction. The ecosystem is built for option strategies to work, instead of being built to bleed retail.

The path for an Indian investor who wants the option machine:

  1. Build a real portfolio in clean US large-caps via a legitimate US-brokerage route.
  2. Let it compound for several years — minimum ~₹3 crore base.
  3. Optimise tax residency where legally appropriate.
  4. Layer options strategies on the US-listed positions, not on Nifty weeklies.

Skip this sequence and you are doing options in the wrong market with the wrong tax regime. That is the trap.

VIII.

Five rules that keep this from blowing up

Options used carelessly will wipe a portfolio faster than any crash. The strategies in this article work precisely because they live inside strict rules. Break the rules and insurance becomes speculation.

Rule 1. Never naked. Don't write a call unless 100 shares of the underlying are sitting in your account. Don't write a put unless the full strike-times-100 in cash is parked aside. Naked short calls have theoretically unlimited loss — if the stock triples overnight, you owe the difference. This is how option sellers actually go bust.

Rule 2. Only on names you'd hold anyway. A cash-secured put commits you to buy at the strike. A covered call commits you to sell at the strike. If you wouldn't buy this stock at strike X with cash, you have no business selling a put at X. If you wouldn't sell at strike Y, you have no business writing a call at Y. The option layer should ratify your underlying conviction, not invent one.

Rule 3. Match the strategy to the regime.

  • Sideways: sell covered calls and cash-secured puts aggressively. Premiums are your alpha.
  • Bull, but stretched: buy protective puts. Sleep premium is worth it.
  • Bear: cash up. Buy puts. Sell puts only at strikes you genuinely want to own.
  • High vol: premiums are fat — great for sellers — but the realised move expectations are also fat. Size down.

Rule 4. Accept that insurance has a cost. The $45,000 in QQQ-put premium is not a "loss" any more than your home insurance premium is a loss when your house doesn't burn down. You bought the right to think clearly during a crash. Even when unexercised, that right has value.

Rule 5. Position size to survive being wrong. No single option position should ever be more than 5% of total portfolio value. If a trade goes against you, it should sting, not bankrupt. The investors who blow up on options are universally the ones who took position sizes that look insane on a portfolio chart. The instrument did not destroy them. The position size did.

If you remember one thing

Never sell volatility you can't afford to be assigned on. The premium looks like free money until the day it isn't. Run every short option position assuming you will be assigned and asking whether the resulting stock position is one you wanted anyway. If yes, fine. If no, you're not running an insurance company — you're picking up pennies in front of a steamroller.

The retirement machine and your next move

Stack everything together and you get a machine almost nobody in Indian retail finance will describe out loud, because it threatens both the mutual-fund complex and the SIP-marketing pipeline.

That is ₹33–37 lakh per year in options income alone, before any appreciation in the underlying. In a 0%-CGT jurisdiction, it stays in your pocket. The underlying stocks meanwhile do whatever they do — the income is layered on top, not in place of, the equity return.

Compare this to the alternative most Indian retail follows: a SIP into Nifty 50. Twenty-year nominal CAGR around 12.5%. Post-tax 10.5%. Adjusted for INR depreciation, 6.5–7%. Adjusted for real dollar inflation, 0–1%. The treadmill we covered in the Real Returns explainer.

So — given all of the above — what should you actually do?

If options are completely new: trade nothing yet. Spend a month watching the options chain on a stock you already own. Notice how premiums change with implied vol, time, and price. Build intuition before positions. Get the underlying core portfolio right first; options are layered on top of conviction, not in place of it.

If you understand the basics: write one covered call on a stock you already own at 15–20% OTM, 30–60 days out. Collect the premium. Watch what happens. Repeat three or four times before adding any other strategy. Feel the mechanics before you scale them.

If you already trade options: build the full insurance-plus-income machine. Protective puts on the index. Covered calls at resistance. Cash-secured puts at support. Track net premium income as a separate line on your portfolio. The goal is to offset 60–80% of insurance cost with selling income, leaving a portfolio with bounded downside and consistent yield.

If you have a 3-crore-plus portfolio: start planning the full system — US equities, options layer, residency optimisation. This is a multi-year arc, not a quarter's project. The earlier you start mapping the path, the more flexibility you have when it's time to execute.

The mental shift is the actual product of this article: options are not a separate asset class. They are a management layer. Every serious portfolio in the world — hedge fund, family office, endowment, sovereign wealth — runs one. The retail story that options are "too risky" or "too complicated" is a defensive narrative from the institutions that profit from keeping you on the treadmill.

You don't need to start trading options tomorrow. But you cannot afford not to understand them. The top decile of investors plays a fundamentally different game, and that game has options in it as ordinary plumbing — not as a speculative toy.

Build better systems. The most powerful move in investing is not finding the next multibagger. It is protecting what you've already built, and generating income from it, while it compounds.

That is what options are for. Not gambling. Insurance.
