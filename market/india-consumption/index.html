I.

I. India Has Only One Investable Theme Left

Let me be blunt with you. After studying Indian markets for years, rotating capital across sectors, tracking every macro variable that matters, I have arrived at a conclusion that most people are not ready to hear.

India is a domestic consumption story. That is it. That is the entire thesis. Everything else – Make in India, data centers, defence indigenization, semiconductor fabs – is noise. Some of it might become signal in ten years. But right now, if you are allocating capital to Indian equities, the only structural growth driver that actually translates into investable opportunities is the fact that 1.4 billion people need to eat, get treated when they fall sick, buy shampoo, take loans, watch movies, and order food on their phones.

This is not pessimism. This is arithmetic.

An economy grows for four reasons: net trade (exports minus imports), consumption, private investment, and government expenditure. Let me walk you through why three of these four are dead or dying in India, and why the fourth – consumption – is the only lever that works.

Net trade: In the 2014-2018 phase, the entire national strategy was built around Make in India. It is quite evident now that this is not taking off to the extent anyone would like. We do not have a winning advantage – low cost, labour advantage, capital cost – in manufacturing at a global scale. We are not China. We are not going to become China. Our trade deficit is structural, softened only when crude oil prices fall below $70, since over 80% of our import bill is energy-driven.

Government expenditure: There was a phase in the last three years where government capex spending surged. Railways, defence, highways. The stock market rallied primarily on the back of this spending. PSU stocks went vertical. Defence stocks went parabolic. But here is the problem: this spending is not leading to GFCF (Gross Fixed Capital Formation). The government pumped money in, but GDP is stuck at 6-6.5%. The capex for FY26 is not higher but slightly lower. Government spending as a growth driver has saturated. If you are still holding PSU railways and defence stocks hoping for another leg up, you are fighting a thesis that has already played out.

Private investment: This has been low because taxes in India, net-net compared to the world, are still high. Businesses are not rushing to set up new capacity. The real wage growth at the national level from Q2 FY20 to Q1 FY25 was essentially flat – about 0.01% per year, according to India Ratings and Research using PLFS data. When wages do not grow, private investment does not accelerate.

So what is left? Consumption. A huge population will consume basic things, all protected by regulations. Banks, FMCG, healthcare, quick commerce, entertainment – this is where the money flows. This is the game until the world settles down.

India is a domestic consumption game now. Huge population will consume basic things, all protected by regulations. Banks, FMCG, Healthcare makes sense.

Understanding which consumers, buying what products, at what price points, and financed by whom – this is the entire framework you need to invest in India. Everything else follows from this.

II.

II. The 4-Tier Customer Framework

Here is the single most important framework for understanding Indian consumption. I divide India into four customer buckets plus a fifth category that is really government spending in disguise. Once you internalize this segmentation, every investment decision in India becomes dramatically clearer.

Tier 1 – Upper Class: Top 1 Crore Customers. These are people who can afford to buy luxury outright. No loans needed. No EMI calculations. They buy Mercedes, invest in luxury real estate, fly business class, stay at five-star hotels, and eat at premium restaurants. This segment is untouched by economic slowdowns. There is no dip here. When the rest of India is struggling with demand contraction, these 1 crore customers keep spending.

But here is the catch: our products are not competitive in this segment. This market would continue to grow for Mercedes, luxury real estate developers, premium hospitality – but the Indian companies serving this segment often face competition from global luxury brands that are simply better at what they do. The investable opportunities here are narrow: think hospitals (where even the rich get sick), premium entertainment, and financial services for the wealthy.

Tier 2 – Middle Class: Top 10 Crore Customers. This is the segment that drives the narrative of “India’s rising middle class.”[1] These are people earning 5 to 20 lakh per year. They take loans to buy luxury. The car is on EMI. The phone is on EMI. The vacation is on a credit card. Their consumption is entirely dependent on two things: job market health and interest rates.

This is also the segment that has been absolutely devastated in the last three years. Something strange has happened: savings for the typical middle class (people earning 5-15 lakh) has gone down, while for the lower segment it has actually gone up. The demand slowdown in India is not coming from the poor – it is coming from this middle class. Two-wheelers, paints, entertainment, consumer durables – all of these have been hit because the middle class stopped spending. Real wage growth at 0.01% per year means this segment has been slowly getting squeezed.

When people say “consumption is down in India,” they mean Tier 2 is struggling. Not Tier 1. Not Tier 4. Specifically, the 10 crore customers who are supposed to be the backbone of premiumization.

Tier 3 – Lower Class A: The Next 15 Crore Customers. These are people who cannot afford to buy luxury even on loans. They are not taking EMIs for iPhones. They are buying the cheapest smartphone that works. Their consumption is basic – necessities, FMCG staples, two-wheelers at the entry level. This segment competes primarily on price, which makes it a tough investment thesis. When firms compete on price, margins compress, and margin compression is never good for equity investors.

Tier 4 – Lower Class B: Survival Consumption. This is the base of the pyramid. Government freebies, subsidies, and direct benefit transfers keep this segment consuming. Rural consumption in India is largely driven by government redistribution. A lot of freebies are distributed, and this is unlikely to change regardless of which government is in power. The good part is that it frees up some money to be spent – meaning it does drive rural consumption for staples. HUL, for instance, generates roughly 40% of its revenue from rural and lower-segment consumers.

Tier 5 – Government Spending. This is not really a consumer segment, but it has functioned as one for years. When the government builds highways, orders trains, buys defence equipment – that spending creates demand. But as I explained, this engine has stalled. Capex is not leading to productive capital formation. Government spending is slowing. So there is no point picking firms that depend primarily on government orders.

The recent demand slowdown – the demand has actually slowed down from the middle class, not necessarily the lower class. This means 2W, paints, entertainment products have been hit due to demand contraction from Tier 2.

This framework is not academic theory. It directly tells you which stocks to buy and which to avoid. Upper class consumption plays work regardless of the cycle. Middle class plays only work when interest rates fall and jobs revive. Lower class plays are price wars – avoid unless you are buying the absolute market leader at a discount. Government-dependent plays are dead money until the next capex cycle, which could be years away.

Well, actually 🧐 — the "rising middle class" thesis is mostly a marketing artefact

McKinsey, Bain, and Goldman have all published "India's middle class will hit X by 20YY" reports for two decades running. Each one revises the prior estimate down. The current consensus (~2024) is that the genuinely discretionary-spending middle class — households with > ₹15 lakh disposable income — is closer to 30–40 million people, not 300 million.

This matters for valuation. Indian consumer staples trade at 50–80x PE on the assumption that the customer base 5x's by 2035. If the actual ramp is 1.5–2x, those multiples cannot be defended. The de-rating risk in HUL, Asian Paints, and Trent over 2023–2025 is precisely this assumption getting marked-to-market.

III.

III. Why the Middle Class Squeeze Changes Everything

Let me give you the data that matters. India Ratings and Research, using PLFS data, found that overall real wage growth at the national level from Q2 FY20 to Q1 FY25 was essentially flat at about 0.01% per year. Let that sink in. Five years of near-zero real wage growth for the median Indian worker.

This single data point explains why every consumption stock in your portfolio has been bleeding. It explains why Asian Paints is down 31.63% from its peak. Why Avenue Supermarts (DMART) is down 36%. Why Hero MotoCorp is down 34.39%. Why Colgate-Palmolive is down 33.85%. These are not individual stock stories – this is a macro story playing out across every company that depends on Tier 2 middle class spending.

Now, the typical analyst response is: “consumption is cyclical, it will come back.” True. But you need to understand the mechanism by which it comes back, because that determines what you buy and when.

Three things need to happen for the middle class consumption revival:

First, interest rate cuts. The RBI cutting rates puts more disposable income into the hands of EMI-paying consumers. If you have a 50 lakh home loan and rates drop by 100 basis points, that is roughly 40,000-50,000 rupees per year freed up. Multiply that across crores of borrowers, and you get a meaningful consumption stimulus. This is why I have been saying: when the rate cut cycle begins, two-wheeler companies, paint companies, and consumer durable firms will be the first beneficiaries.

Second, job market revival. This is harder to engineer and harder to time. Without genuine employment growth in sectors that pay middle-class wages – IT services, financial services, manufacturing – the consumption squeeze continues. Indian IT is undergoing a PE correction and faces structural questions about whether AI replaces the labor arbitrage model that built the industry. Manufacturing has not scaled. The job market recovery is the most uncertain variable in the entire thesis.

Third, tax relief. The Union Budget made income up to 12 lakh tax-free (with caveats). This puts discretionary income into the hands of people – at least a little. Approximately 80,000 to 1 lakh rupees is going to come back to close to 1.4 lakh crore people. Money also has a multiplier effect, which in the Indian economy is roughly 5x[2]. So essentially: 1.4 lakh crore multiplied by 5 equals 7 lakh crore of capital that will get diverted to private consumption over time.

ΔGDP = MPC × (1 / (1 − MPC)) × ΔSpend classical Keynesian multiplier; for India MPC is ~0.8 implying a fiscal multiplier of ~5×

This is why the budget was consumption-focused. It was very Bihar-focused, heavy on redistribution. To be honest, there seems to be no plan to accelerate manufacturing or services. Foreign investors are unlikely to get excited with the existing growth prospects. But domestic consumption? That is the one lever the government can pull, and they pulled it.

Now here is the critical investment implication: anything that is discretionary and trades at a high PE and caters primarily to the typical middle class is a dangerous combination to hold. This is a triple threat. Discretionary means it gets cut first when wallets tighten. High PE means the market is pricing in growth that is not materializing. Middle class dependence means you are betting on the weakest consumer segment.

Trent at 130 PE going up 11% in a single day after the budget – that is the market being insane. Please be careful with valuations. Growth is slowing down in the world generally. India will not be isolated from this. Buying super-high PE stocks is a very dangerous game.

Well, actually 🧐 — the demographic dividend is real but front-loaded

India's working-age population peaks around 2045–2050 and starts declining. That is the demographic dividend everyone cites. But the per-capita-productivity growth that converts demographics into consumption is what's missing — the median Indian worker today produces about 12% of what the median US worker produces, and the gap is closing too slowly to drive the bull case timelines.

If you anchor on demographics alone, you'll overpay for consumption stocks. If you anchor on per-capita-output convergence, the math gets much more sober.

IV.

IV. Banks -- The Backbone of Every Indian Portfolio

If you have been following my work, you know that most of my India portfolio is finance. It has been that way for a while, and there is a very good reason for it. Almost 70-75% of my Indian stocks were finance at one point. Many of these have done quite well – Nuvama, HDFC, Kotak, ICICI.

Banks are the purest domestic consumption play in India. Think about it. When a Tier 2 middle class customer buys a car on EMI, the bank makes money. When a Tier 1 upper class customer parks money in a wealth management product, the bank makes money. When the government distributes subsidies through Jan Dhan accounts, the bank makes money. When an FMCG company takes a working capital loan to distribute products to rural India, the bank makes money. Every single layer of Indian consumption flows through the banking system.

Here is the structural thesis. With the ban on betting apps, fewer F&O participants, and tighter SEBI regulations, money will flow back to banks. India’s savings rate relative to GDP has been at a historic low. If there is a turnaround on this ratio, banks benefit immensely. Private banks in particular are still the most undervalued domain in Indian stocks. Results have been quite positive for most private banks. The RBI has done a great job ensuring there is no credit default crisis. There is no reason these will not grow at 15-16% CAGR or more, conservatively speaking.

The specific allocation I have been moving toward:

  • Banks/Finance: 40% of Indian portfolio (down from 70%, because I am rotating into hospitals)
  • Private banks like HDFC, Kotak, ICICI, Axis – these are 15% compounders. Safe, reliable, available at 3-year-old prices. If you study how themes run in India, when the banking theme moves, TCS-Infy style, they all move together. So just buy the top 3-4 and be done.
  • Loan-giving firms: Bajaj Finance, Jio Financial, SBI Cards, housing finance companies. The addiction to EMIs has just begun. This is one of the critical levers to grow the economy.

But here is the nuance on Bajaj Finance specifically. It is a great company with 22%+ CAGR, roughly 55% share in consumer durable loans, 7% share of unsecured personal loans, and 23-24% share in unsecured SME loans. All impressive. But think from a game theory perspective – almost all firms from Jio to Groww are growing their lending businesses. Bajaj Finance has only one way to go: market share will decline. Not because Bajaj is bad, but because this is a high-profit-margin business and big players are not going to leave it alone. Jio Financial’s strategy is digital-first, embedded inside the Reliance ecosystem – a lower cost customer acquisition strategy that wins long term. Bajaj Finance depends on its legacy point-of-sale distribution, which is far more expensive. This is a “buy on dips” stock, not a “chase on rise” stock.

For brokerages and capital market plays – Angel One, CAMS, CDSL, Nuvama, DreamFolks – these are domestic capital and investment consumption themes. They all go up as the market improves. We had been able to time a good cycle and book profits on AMCs, CAMS, CDSL types – more than 50% on each. The key is to treat these as cyclical plays within the finance bucket.

People get too excited about manufacturing, but honestly: finance is right now the most solid industry in India.

V. Healthcare -- The 5-Year Mega Bet

On a five-year basis, hospital stocks are becoming my number one bet. I know explaining five-year plans is challenging, but let me try.

Here is why I am buying more hospital stocks. Oncology – the study of cancer – is the growth engine. India is quickly becoming the cancer capital of the world. The rate of growth in cancer patients is around 3-3.5% CAGR each year, and those are just official reports. The consumption of processed food is at an all-time high. Even “safe” food like eggs is contaminated. The TAM (total addressable market) of the disease is growing, and hospitals are already investing in capacity for this segment.

Unlike many other diseases, cancer treatment is primarily done by organized hospitals. This cannot be done by secondary and tertiary healthcare facilities. So the growth in oncology patients flows directly and exclusively to the listed hospital chains – Apollo, Fortis, Max. Long story short: there is a clear growth driver where hospitals would make serious money.

But oncology is only one leg of the thesis. Here are the structural tailwinds:

Insurance penetration. Healthcare in India is getting more organized. With insurance coming in, hospitals will make more money per client. My dad had a kidney stone operation – the doctor said, “If you have insurance, I will keep you for 3 days; if not, I will send you home in 2 days.” As healthcare plus insurance gets more organized, profit margins will go up. The FDI hike to 100% for insurance is actually good news for good insurance firms and, by extension, for hospitals. As foreign insurance companies set up shop here, the entire ecosystem gets more sophisticated and more profitable.

Demographics. Our population is young and will age with time. Spending in this sector will only go up. There is unlikely to be a decline in revenues – ever. This is a structural one-way bet on the direction of healthcare spending.

Medical tourism. My bet is that medical tourism will add serious money to our economy. India already has a cost advantage for complex procedures. As hospital chains build more capacity and reputation, this becomes a meaningful revenue stream.

Real estate as a moat. Given how much real estate prices have gone up, we are unlikely to get massive new competition in this space. Building a new 500-bed hospital in a metro city requires land that is either unavailable or prohibitively expensive. Apollo, Fortis, Max – these are all strong because their physical infrastructure is essentially irreplicable at current land prices.

I had built positions on Max at around 90-95% profits. Hospital stocks look bloated PE-wise. But if you consider a 5-10 year vision, this is one of the most sensible bets you can take. I am comfortable paying high PE for healthcare because these are critical products. When someone needs cancer treatment, they do not comparison-shop for the cheapest option.

The allocation: I am moving from Banks/Finance at 70% to a target of 40% finance, 25% hospitals. That is a massive rotation, and I am executing it over the next 12-18 months. Do not expose more than 3-5% of your overall portfolio on any individual hospital stock, but the sector allocation can be much higher.

If you are uncomfortable taking this long-term bet, avoid it. When I say 5 years, I mean 5 years. Do not track the prices from tomorrow.

VI.

VI. Quick Commerce, Food Delivery, and the Consumption Platform Thesis

India is a consumption-driven play. Zomato and Swiggy have become too big in an industry that is very likely to survive for the next 5-10 years. This is a duopoly. Study how duopolies work – two dominant firms in one growth industry. Food delivery is a sure-shot business in India. It is unlikely to see any new competition. The unit economics are hard to achieve, and the two dominant players are already set.

Now, I have had a complex relationship with these stocks. I bought Zomato at 55, made 110-120% returns, sold 60%, then sold another 20%. I bought Swiggy pre-IPO at around 11 billion dollar valuation. I exited all positions on Zomato and Swiggy at one point when the Q-commerce war intensified. Then I rebuilt positions.

Why the back and forth? Because the thesis and the valuation are two different things. The business thesis on Zomato is rock solid. If I have to pick 5 businesses in India for the next 5-6 years, Zomato would be in it. The firm can undertake vertical and horizontal integrations really fast – they deliver food, but if they see opportunities to sell tickets, they can. If they decide to start their own restaurant business, they can. Their ad revenues will only grow in time. Open your Swiggy or Zomato app – out of 10 restaurants you are shown, 5 are ads. The platform fee every quarter is likely to keep jumping up.

But the valuation? The firm is at 600 crore of profits with a market cap of 2.3 lakh crore. Even if profits triple to 3,000 crore, that is still a PE of close to 80. That is very high. And with the listing of Swiggy, the monopoly premium that Zomato commands will shrink because this is now a proper duopoly.

The expectation around Zomato is that its EPS will go from 0.4 to almost 2.5 – a 6x increase. That means PE could fall 6 times over the next 2 years. Is it expensive? Maybe. Hard to say. But my bet is that Zomato might become the most valuable company in India given their massive expansion opportunities.

On Swiggy: it is the number two player in the same space, and there is enough room. Swiggy announced it wants to raise 10,000 crore rupees, got 2,400 crore from selling its Rapido stake, and already had 4,605 crore in cash. They are preparing for war on Q-commerce. Competition is heating up with BigBasket, Reliance Retail, Amazon, and Flipkart all entering the space.

10-minute delivery is likely to create some multi-baggers. The only issue is the valuation at which you pick these up. Their profitability is irrelevant right now – with the revenues they are doing, they will reach profitability eventually. Product-market fit is already proven. With better tech and ops evolution, profitability will come.

The key insight for investors: these are buy-on-dips stocks, not chase-on-rise stocks. Keep 5-8% of your Indian portfolio here. And apply the concept of price stickiness – once a stock reaches a price, if the business is surviving and growing, it will cut that price again. Swiggy reached 600, fell 25%, but if the firm survives 5 years, it hits 600 again. That is substantial gain.

In markets like India, you will not get a full-toss on stocks where the firm is safe, profits are expanding, and it is also available at a great price. You have to look behind the scenes, apply brain, take your 5% bet and stay put.

VII.

VII. The FMCG and Paints Paradox

FMCG and paints stocks present a fascinating paradox in India. They are simultaneously the safest consumption plays and the most frustrating ones to hold.

Take HUL. You would hardly see it fall below 50 PE. Why has it commanded such a premium historically? Three reasons. First, India’s growth expectation was around 7-8%, so paying higher PE made sense. Second, the Indian market was getting organized – HUL taking business from local manufacturers means scale, efficiency, and more profits. Third, premiumization – if HUL can sell a 120 rupee shampoo next year instead of a 100 rupee one with no change in volume, that is pure margin expansion.

But what actually happened? GDP structurally slowed to 6-6.5%. The premiumization thesis for the middle class stalled because savings rates dropped. And the companies that were supposed to ride the organized-beats-unorganized wave found that the wave was losing momentum.

Here is why I still hold these names. HUL generates 40% of its revenue from rural and lower segments. Despite being high PE, it is fine because FIIs eventually build positions in such firms in India since they can hedge in futures markets. If you are buying HUL or Nestle below their 200 DMA, you will not lose money – but you need to hold for 3-5 years. These are consistent 15% growth type firms. I personally add these because almost 70-80% of my portfolio is finance, so I need to hedge it by buying such firms.

Asian Paints and Indigo Paints are a more interesting case. The domestic paints business is hard to disrupt. Despite increased competition, Asian Paints posted highest-ever sales in 2024. Profit margins will improve now, especially with crude oil below $70 – remember, crude prices directly affect paint raw material costs. With easing of crude, this will reflect in the balance sheets of Asian Paints and Indigo Paints over the next 3-4 quarters and should translate to higher stock prices.

But I have been cutting positions here. Consumption has become a slow-growth industry in India, so I cut some positions on Asian Paints and Indigo Paints – around 40% – and moved to faster-growing segments. Post this change, I hold around 1.25% on Indigo Paints and 2% on Asian Paints as a part of my portfolio.

The real estate connection is important. RE sales have been at an all-time high. The next step is usually home decor – paints, furniture, MDF boards. This should start reflecting in the results of companies like Asian Paints, Indigo Paints, Greenpanel, and Rushil Decor. But the timing is uncertain, and these are all competing on price in a market where the middle class buyer is squeezed.

For auto, here is my view: Hero MotoCorp and Bajaj Auto are consumption plays. Two-wheelers primarily, benefiting from interest rate cuts and more discretionary income. For Bajaj, 68% of sales come from India, rest outside. These benefit from the positive consumer cycle. But Tata Motors? Almost 70% of its business is outside India, multiple issues in Europe – it is not really an India consumption play. And I am not bullish on 4W EVs in India. The day the Indian market opens up to Chinese and US firms, legacy automakers like Tata Motors will struggle. So far, industrialists in India have been defending their moat using government connections. But given our evolving foreign policy, we run a real risk of opening up our economy to foreign players.

VIII.

VIII. The Organized vs. Unorganized Transition -- And Why It Is Slowing

Here is a macro trend I have been researching, and it has serious implications for how you think about Indian consumption stocks over the next decade.

If we look at the phase from 2000 to 2019, a lot of unorganized businesses in India were getting organized. Organized means: instead of 1,000 kirana shops (unorganized), you have 1 DMART (organized). Instead of 1,000 small jewellery shops, you have 1 Titan. Right from Titan to Zomato, Swiggy, Angel One, LensKart, OYO, Zerodha, retail businesses – almost everything gained scale from unorganized to organized. As a result, the smallcap and midcap index in the last 20 years has done fairly well.

But here is the question I am investigating: will this trend continue?

Look at the US. The Russell 2000 (small cap index), in a market that is mostly organized already, has not done well at all. If India’s transition from unorganized to organized slows down, our small and midcap indices could face a similar fate.

Two scenarios can play out:

  1. The rate of growth from unorganized to organized businesses stays up. In this case, small and midcap stocks continue to do well. New companies emerge, take share from fragmented local players, and their stocks compound.

  2. The transition slows. In this case, we move to 2-3 top players in each growing segment, and those will do well, but the broad small/midcap index becomes a poor investment. The winners have already won.

I have not reached a definitive conclusion, but I lean toward scenario two. The easy transitions have happened. Food delivery is a duopoly. Online broking is dominated by 3-4 players. Organized retail has DMART and Reliance. Paints have Asian Paints and the entrenched players. The next wave of organization – in healthcare, insurance, wealth management – will benefit specific companies, not the broad index.

This is why I have been selective about smallcaps. PVR, DreamFolks, Aavas – these are monopoly smallcaps. Even if only one survives, it will build wealth. PVR is posting record revenues, profit margins are not sliding, operating cash flows have improved dramatically, and the firm is likely to become net positive soon. When this happens, the market usually reacts very positively – a similar story played out on Zomato earlier.

DreamFolks is at 18 PE, which is low. Both DreamFolks and PVR benefit from domestic consumption and are less prone to external shocks. There are a handful of profitable, small cap firms in the luxury play category, and these two are in that zone.

But do not confuse these individual stock picks with a broad bet on smallcaps. I exited the Smallcap 250 index before the major fall. The index has corrected 25-30%. Individual good smallcaps get dragged down when the index falls, but they recover when the thesis is intact. The index itself? That is a coin flip now, and I would rather take specific bets than make a blanket smallcap allocation.

One of the most critical lessons I have learnt in the last 3-4 years is: go where growth is. The world is becoming a giant casino. And people are obsessive gamblers who will chase growth. So being patient, investing in growth assets and riding narratives is critical.

IX.

IX. Putting It All Together -- Your India Consumption Playbook

Let me synthesize everything into a clear, actionable framework. India’s stock market is structurally slowing. Nifty 50 delivered 4x from 1999-2009, 2.5x from 2009-2019, and roughly 2x from 2020-2025. Expecting the 2020-2030 phase to be 3x is likely, which translates to around 10% nominal market returns. After accounting for 10% effective inflation in India, the real return on a buy-and-hold Nifty strategy is essentially inflation plus 2-3%. You are not building wealth. You are preserving it.

So how do you actually make money in India? Three principles:

Principle 1: Buy domestic demand-driven firms that are critical. “Critical” means people cannot stop consuming the product regardless of the economic cycle. Hospitals – people get cancer whether GDP grows at 5% or 8%. Banks – people need savings accounts and loans regardless. Staple FMCG – people buy soap and toothpaste no matter what. Two-wheelers – for a massive portion of India, this is primary transportation, not discretionary spending. These are the anchor positions in your portfolio.

Principle 2: Be deeply selective on consumption. Do not buy the consumption basket – buy specific segments at specific valuations. Buy luxury and necessary consumption-oriented products. Hospitals, entertainment that serves the upper class (like PVR), monopoly smallcaps in luxury niches (like DreamFolks). Sell non-critical products where there is a price war. Be cautious on consumption firms with very high valuations – Swiggy, Zomato are buys on dips, not at current levels unless you are building a tiny position for the very long term.

Principle 3: Use consumption as one leg of a diversified portfolio, not the whole thing. My target India portfolio allocation tells the full story:

  • Banks/Finance: 40% – the structural backbone
  • Hospitals/Healthcare: 25% – the 5-year mega bet
  • IT: 0% (all tech allocation goes to US and China)
  • Consumption basket: 20% – FMCG, paints, 2W auto, quick commerce, entertainment
  • Valuation plays / cash: 15% – if opportunities like DMART at a 4-year low or Swiggy at a deep discount come up, deploy this capital

And critically: I am shifting 50-60% of my total portfolio outside India over time. Better real growth options are available. US tech – Amazon, Meta, Microsoft, Netflix, NVIDIA – these are structurally superior growth assets. When I look at 10 years, the Indian market gives me hospitals and domestic consumption. The US market gives me AI, cloud, autonomous vehicles, and global platform monopolies. It is not even a comparison.

For India specifically, here is the concrete playbook:

Stocks to hold / accumulate: - Private banks: HDFC, Kotak, ICICI, Axis – 15% compounders, available at good valuations - Hospitals: Max, Apollo, Fortis – 5-year holds, accumulate on corrections - HUL, Nestle – below 200 DMA buys, hold 3-5 years as portfolio hedge - Hero MotoCorp, Bajaj Auto – domestic 2W consumption, benefit from rate cuts - PVR, DreamFolks – monopoly smallcaps in luxury consumption

Stocks to buy only on deep dips: - Zomato, Swiggy – great businesses at expensive valuations - Bajaj Finance – set for disruption from Jio Financial and digital lenders - DMART – MOAT-oriented business, wait for it to become truly cheap - Jio Financial – speculative but in a lucrative industry, 0 or 5x in 10 years

Stocks to avoid: - Anything discretionary at high PE catering to the middle class – the triple threat - Government capex dependent firms – PSU railways, defence - 4W auto with heavy EV exposure – Tata Motors faces existential competition risk - Indian IT for long-term holding – the thesis has shifted to US tech entirely

The final point I want to leave you with is this: Indian markets are not dead. Equity in India still generates the best post-tax returns of any domestic asset class. But the easy money is over. The PE expansion phase that made SIPs look like a guaranteed wealth machine in the 2010s is spent. What remains is a consumption-driven economy where 1.4 billion people will continue to eat, borrow, get treated, and order food on their phones. Your job as an investor is to own the pipes through which that consumption flows – the banks that lend, the hospitals that treat, the platforms that deliver, and the brands that people refuse to substitute.

Do not try to be a hero. Pick the top 3-4 firms in each segment. Keep position sizes disciplined – no more than 2.5% on any individual smallcap, no more than 5% on any individual largecap. Buy when things are cheap, rotate when they are fairly valued, and always keep asking: is this the best use of my capital right now?

If you get this framework right, you will outperform 90% of mutual funds in India. Not because you are smarter. Because you understand who the Indian consumer actually is.
