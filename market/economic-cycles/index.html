I.

I. The Five Stages Nobody Teaches You

Every economy on the planet runs through the same five stages. Over and over. Without exception. And yet, most investors – including people who have been putting money into markets for a decade or more – cannot name them. They cannot tell you where we are in the cycle. They cannot tell you what comes next. And because they cannot do this, they keep making the same mistake: buying when they should be rotating, holding when they should be hedging, and panicking when they should be loading up.

Here are the five stages:

  1. Crash (example: 2020 COVID, 2008 financial crisis)
  2. Liquidity injection – more money printing, more government spending (2021-2022)
  3. Consolidation – debt repayment, sideways movement, reduced spending
  4. Euphoria / Run-up – markets overshoot on sentiment
  5. Crash – and the cycle resets

This is not theory. This is the observable, repeatable pattern that has played out in every major economy for over a century. The specifics change – the trigger for each crash is different, the duration of each phase varies, the exact magnitude of each rally differs – but the sequence does not change. Crash, liquidity, consolidation, euphoria, crash.

The single most valuable skill you can develop as an investor is the ability to identify which stage we are in right now. Everything else – stock selection, position sizing, asset allocation – flows from that one determination.

Let me walk you through each stage using real examples, so this becomes something you can actually use rather than just another framework you nod at and forget.

Stage 1: The Crash. In 2020, COVID hit. Markets around the world collapsed. The Nifty fell from about 12,400 to 7,500 – a 40% drawdown in roughly one month. The NASDAQ fell over 30%. Nobody saw it coming. Nobody could have seen it coming. That is the nature of crashes – they are triggered by genuinely unexpected events. The 2008 crisis was a banking system failure that most retail investors did not understand until it was far too late. These events are sharp, parabolic, and terrifying.

Stage 2: Liquidity injection. Governments respond to crashes the same way every time: they print money. From 2020 to 2022, the total US money supply increased from $15 trillion to $18.75 trillion – roughly a 20% increase. India’s RBI cut rates aggressively. Central banks globally flooded their economies with cheap capital. This money has to go somewhere, and it flows into assets – stocks, real estate, crypto, commodities. This is when the V-shaped recovery happens. This is when the people who bought during Stage 1 start looking like geniuses.

Stage 3: Consolidation. After the money is printed and the debt is accumulated, someone has to pay for it. The US will be repaying $1 trillion in debt interest payments this year alone. If they borrow more, their yearly payments go up further. How do you control this? You borrow less. That means you either tax more or spend less. Both reduce liquidity. Both slow the economy. Both create what looks like a sideways market with wide swings. This is where most investors get crushed – not by losses, but by boredom and frustration. The market goes up 8%, then falls 6%, then goes up 5%, then falls 7%. Over 18 months, you have made almost nothing. People lose patience. They sell. They make emotional decisions. This is the trap.

Stage 4: Euphoria. When a Yamaha showroom in NCR files for an SME IPO seeking to raise 12 crores and receives bids worth 4,700 crores, you are in euphoria. When MamaEarth announces a merger of its own subsidiaries and its market cap jumps by 2,000 crores despite no change in the underlying business, you are in euphoria. Signs of pure madness become evident. Markets can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent – which is precisely why your cash position and cash flows are king during this phase.

Stage 5: Crash. And it begins again.

The people who understand these five stages do not need 50 different trading strategies. They need three or four good cycles. That is it. Three or four cycles executed properly can generate enough wealth to change your life permanently. But to run those cycles, you need extreme preparation. And you will not get there by simply SIP-ing your way through, regardless of what investment pundits tell you.

II.

II. Three Types of Falls -- And Why Confusing Them Will Cost You

Not all market declines are created equal. This is one of the most critical distinctions you can make as an investor, and almost nobody makes it. People see red on their screens and react the same way regardless of whether the market is down 5% because of a tariff announcement or down 40% because of a systemic banking failure. These require completely different responses.

There are three types of falls:

Type 1: Sharp parabolic fall. This happens in situations like COVID or the 2008 financial crisis. It is completely unexpected. Nobody can predict it. The market drops 20-40% in a matter of weeks. The characteristic feature of a parabolic fall is speed – the decline is so fast that by the time you process what is happening, a significant portion of the damage is already done.

Here is what most people miss about parabolic falls: in the modern era, they exhibit V-shaped recoveries. Markets these days recover in a V-shape rather than the slow decay that characterised 2008. The 2020 crash recovered almost entirely within six months. This changes the calculus dramatically. If you know that the recovery will be fast, the correct response to a parabolic fall is not to run – it is to buy aggressively. This is what Adani, Ambani, and Zuckerberg did during the 2020 crash. They went leverage. Not because they are reckless, but because they understood the type of fall they were witnessing.

Type 2: Valuation correction. This is what mid and small caps experienced in 2024-2025, with 40-50% drawdowns that wiped years of hard-earned returns for many investors. Valuation corrections happen when stocks are priced at PEs that the earnings cannot justify. The correction continues until prices come down to sensible value levels. This can easily last 2-3 years. The last significant valuation correction in the Indian market ran from roughly October 2021 to May 2023.

The critical difference between a valuation correction and a parabolic crash is duration. A parabolic crash is violent but brief. A valuation correction is slow, grinding, and psychologically devastating. You wake up each morning hoping for recovery, and instead you get another 2% decline. Then a small bounce that gives you hope. Then another decline. This goes on for months, sometimes years. The stocks that experience valuation corrections are typically those that had their PEs inflated during the euphoria phase – the exact stocks that everyone was excited about six months ago.

Type 3: Slow decay. This is the most misunderstood type of fall, and it is the one I believe we have been in recently. Slow decay is an economic growth forecast problem. It happens because there is too much debt in the system and it needs to be reduced. Governments spend less. Corporate growth slows. The market does not crash dramatically – it just bleeds, slowly, for an extended period.

The practical manifestation of slow decay is a sideways market with wide range volatility – meaning swings of 20% up and down within a broader range that goes nowhere over 18-24 months. This is the type of market that destroys buy-and-hold investors, because they sit through 18 months of volatility and end up with 0% returns. Meanwhile, traders who understand capital rotation capture those 20% swings and do extremely well.

Understanding which type of fall you are in determines your entire strategy. In a parabolic crash, you buy aggressively. In a valuation correction, you wait for sensible value to emerge. In a slow decay, you rotate capital. Applying the wrong strategy to the wrong type of fall is how serious money gets destroyed.

III.

III. Recession Is Not What You Think It Is

Every time the word “recession” appears in the news, retail investors panic. Goldman Sachs raises the probability of a US recession from 20% to 35%, and suddenly your WhatsApp groups are flooded with messages about the end of the world. Media channels run recession segments around the clock. Podcasters who have never invested a single rupee in their lives start telling you that the US is going bankrupt.

Stop. Take a breath. And understand what a recession actually is.

A recession is two consecutive quarters of GDP slowdown or negative growth in GDP. Let me give you a simple example to make this precise. If GDP goes from 100 to 110, that is 10% growth. If GDP then goes from 110 to 120, that is approximately 9% growth. The GDP grew. But the rate of growth slowed. This slowdown – this deceleration in growth – is what constitutes a recession. A recession is NOT necessarily negative GDP. This is what most people confuse.

Now here is the part that matters for your money: recession is a lagging indicator. It is backward-looking. By the time the official recession announcement comes, the economic contraction has already happened. Stock market prices, on the other hand, are leading indicators. They try to predict what will happen 6-12 months from now. This creates a paradox that most people cannot wrap their heads around: the stock market often bottoms before the recession is officially declared, and by the time the recession announcement hits the news, markets are already recovering.

This is why the people who wait for the recession to be “confirmed” before adjusting their portfolios are always too late. The market has already priced it in. The opportunity has already passed.

Look at the actual data. The last two significant recessions were in 2008 and 2020. In both cases, if you had bought the dip during these phases, you would have made extraordinary money. The 2020 recession lasted exactly two months. Two months! The market recovered in a V-shape and went on to new highs within the year. People who sold in panic during March 2020 locked in their losses. People who bought in panic – who had the courage and the cash to buy when everyone was running – made generational wealth.

The current environment in 2025-2026 is instructive. The US is growing at approximately 3.8% money supply expansion per year, which means GDP growth of at least 3.8%. There are recessionary concerns being discussed constantly in the media. But look at the actual recession indicators:

  • Gold has gone down recently (in a real recession, gold goes up)
  • Bond yields have gone up (in a real recession, bond yields go down)
  • The DXY dollar index has corrected (in a real recession, the dollar strengthens)
  • Consumer demand is still up (in a real recession, consumer demand falls)

The only recession signal that checks out is that firms are not expanding as aggressively – but that is because they are being forced to restructure contracts due to tariff uncertainty, not because of fundamental demand collapse.

This is NOT a recession. This is a manufactured problem. It is an ego war over tariffs that can be solved with a stroke of a pen. And these manufactured crises are precisely the buying opportunities that build real wealth over time.

Recession these days simply means a sideways market. Which is already happening. So ignore the noise, learn to invest in a sideways market, and stop reading headlines from people who have no stake in the game.

IV.

IV. The Liquidity Engine: How Money Printing Drives Everything

If there is one mechanism that every investor needs to understand at a deep level, it is the relationship between money supply, interest rates, and asset prices. This is the engine that drives the entire economic cycle, and once you see it clearly, a lot of market behaviour that seems random or irrational starts making perfect sense.

Here is the basic chain:

  1. A crisis happens (crash)
  2. Central banks respond by cutting interest rates and printing money
  3. Cheap money floods into the economy – firms borrow more, consumers spend more, a fresh CAPEX cycle starts
  4. Asset prices rise because all that liquidity needs somewhere to go
  5. Eventually, the debt accumulated during the spending spree becomes unsustainable
  6. Central banks raise rates or reduce spending to control the debt
  7. Liquidity decreases, growth slows, markets consolidate or correct
  8. And the cycle repeats

From 2020 to 2022, the US increased its money supply from $15 trillion to $18.75 trillion. That is a 20% increase in roughly two years. Now, you might ask: if money supply increased by 20%, why didn’t GDP also increase by 20%? The answer is simple – GDP shrunk during COVID because things stopped moving. But if you adjust for that contraction, the money supply expansion did indeed flow through into economic activity.

The US plan is straightforward: print money, maintain a certain growth rate in GDP, aggregate the means of production – semiconductors, land, strategic resources like ports – to gain competitive advantage, and add real value to the economy over time. When the US prints dollars, it is essentially creating internal debt that it can easily handle because the dollar is the global reserve currency.

Currently, the US is increasing its money supply by approximately 3.8% per year. This means baseline GDP growth of at least 3.8%. It also means that asset prices will continue to appreciate – maybe 10-15% per year in nominal terms. After taxes, currency depreciation, and real inflation, the actual wealth growth for people who invest will be 2-3%. For people who do not invest, the cost-of-living crisis will be devastating.

Interest rate cuts are the trigger for the next phase. When the FED cuts rates, it signals that they are trying to accelerate the economy by increasing liquidity. The nature of the market does not change – we remain in a liquidity-driven rally. The good part is that stocks and investments continue to behave the way they have for the last several years: they go up, but with significant volatility.

When the FED cut rates by 0.5% instead of the expected 0.25%, it indicated that the US government was worried about growth and wanted to accelerate the recovery fast. A fast FED cut is typically very good for markets. We enter the euphoria stage after such cuts.

But here is the deeper point that most people miss: this liquidity-driven growth fundamentally weakens the economy long term. It will burst at some point. The question is not whether it will burst – it is when, how, and who precisely will suffer. The US will successfully export inflation to the world because there is no currency that can replace the dollar right now. We will witness recurring cost-of-living crises. Inflation might spike 7-8% for 2-3 years, then settle, then spike again. The cycle gets faster each time.

People who wait for asset prices to correct significantly before buying will keep waiting. The surprise will be that asset prices keep going up, and people keep paying higher and higher prices driven by this manufactured growth. The window to buy at “sensible” prices is during the consolidation and early correction phases – exactly when the headlines are most terrifying and the urge to stay on the sidelines is strongest.

V.

V. Reading the Room: Where Are We Right Now?

This is the question that matters more than any individual stock pick or trading strategy. Where are we in the cycle? Everything you should be doing with your money depends on the answer.

As of the current market environment, here is my assessment. We are in the consolidation phase of the economy, with characteristics of a slow-decay correction playing out simultaneously across multiple geographies.

The evidence:

In the US: Interest rates have been lowered from 5.5% at their peak to 4.5%, with expectations of further cuts to 3.5% by year end. The overall growth forecast is slow – 1.7% growth rate, which qualifies as slow growth by American standards. The FED is slowing its selling of securities, indicating it wants to maintain liquidity. Bad news like tariffs has been factored in. We are in a situation where the market is going sideways and consolidating in a range.

In India: The markets have given negative returns over the last 1.5-2 years when adjusted for INR depreciation. Small and mid caps have exhibited corrections of 40-50%. The Nifty is in a range-bound market with the possibility of moving between 21,000 and 25,000. Large caps are at a discount. SIP-driven flows provide a floor, but the broader direction is sideways.

Globally: We are in a “risk-off” phase. People want to bet on certain assets, low-risk economies, and low-risk segments. This is why gold and metals have been relatively stable while stocks worldwide have corrected. The correction is particularly severe in emerging markets. A firm like MercadoLibre has grown revenues at 30%+ CAGR for the last 30 quarters – the only listed company in the world to achieve this – yet the stock has not rallied. Why? Because we are in a risk-off phase, and people do not want to bet on Latin America right now regardless of fundamentals.

Here is what this phase looks like practically:

  • Almost all high-growth assets are declining in value: Gold down 10%, Silver down 30%, stocks like Palantir and SoFi down 30%, Bitcoin down 40-45%
  • We are NOT in euphoria yet
  • The 2025 tariff war is NOT a crisis like COVID or the 2008 bank crisis – it is an ego war that can be resolved with negotiations
  • The current crash is NOT like the 2022 crash, where valuations were bloated and interest rates were expected to rise. Now, inflation is already low and rates are expected to be cut. When rates go down, firms borrow more, and a fresh CAPEX cycle starts.

My assessment is that interest rates in the US will be cut soon. And due to the midterm elections, Trump will institute more market-friendly policies. We might see euphoria by the end of the year. There is significant headroom for quality assets like Amazon, Microsoft, and Meta to grow from here.

We are nowhere close to a bubble. In fact, there is massive panic in the market. These are exactly the times you invest more.

VI. The Sideways Market Playbook

Most investing education assumes a bull market. Buy great companies. Hold forever. Watch your wealth compound. The entire SIP narrative is built on the assumption that markets trend upward over long periods.

But what happens when the market goes sideways for 1.5 to 2 years? What happens when the Nifty oscillates between 21,000 and 25,000, making 20% swings up and down but ending up roughly where it started? This is the environment that destroys the buy-and-hold strategy, and it is the environment we find ourselves in today.

In the last valuation correction, which ran from October 2021 to roughly May 2023, rotating capital between corrections and rallies was the only way to make sensible returns. I do not see value in buy-and-forget strategies right now unless there is a clear bull run. The next 2-3 years are going to be volatile, and capital rotation is not just a nice-to-have – it is the only strategy that works.

Here is how I am approaching it:

Step 1: Maintain a cash bucket. I keep 15-20% of my portfolio in cash at all times during a sideways market. This cash is not idle money – it is ammunition. When the market falls 10% (which has happened at least four times in the Indian market in the last 2.5 years), that cash gets deployed aggressively. When the market bounces back to resistance levels, I book 15-20% of the position and replenish the cash bucket.

Take a specific example. I had invested close to 5 crores. The Nifty hit first resistance, and I was up 37-40 lakhs (roughly 6% in a month). I booked those profits and went 15% cash. Why? Because in a sideways market, money does not come when things are fairly priced. It comes when things are discounted. Keeping 15% cash ensures I am ready for the next discount.

Step 2: Buy at supports, sell at resistances. This sounds simple. It is not. It requires you to have a clear view of where the technical support and resistance levels are, and it requires the discipline to actually sell when things are going up (which feels terrible) and buy when things are going down (which feels even worse).

The framework I use is thirds. If I want to invest 10 lakhs in an asset, I invest 3.3 lakhs at the first support level. If the market falls to the second support, I invest another 3.3 lakhs. If it falls to the third support, I deploy the remainder. This way, I cannot time the bottom perfectly – nobody can – but I am averaging into the position at progressively lower prices.

Step 3: Never stop investing. If your cash bucket is full (say, 20 lakhs sitting aside for a 1 crore portfolio) and you earn more money, that new money should go into the market at minor dips. Do not let your cash position grow beyond 20%. The risk of a sideways market is not that you lose money – it is that you waste time. Time correction is the real enemy.

Step 4: Know when to shift gears. The only scenario where this strategy changes is when the market moves to euphoria. Then we become net sellers. And the only scenario where we go all-in with leverage is a genuine parabolic crash – a 20%+ fall driven by a truly unexpected event, not a tariff announcement.

For context, here is my actual allocation during this phase: India portfolio is 95% invested in safe instruments – index funds, private banks, healthcare stocks. US portfolio is being built up, currently at around 30-55% depending on the month, with the rest in cash waiting for better entry points. I do zero SIPs. I rotate capital instead.

Step 5: Accept the volatility. Here is what nobody tells you about sideways markets: the daily experience is miserable. You buy something, it drops 3% the next day. You hold through the drop, it recovers, then drops again. Your WhatsApp groups are full of panic. The headlines are terrible. You feel like an idiot for buying.

This is normal. This is what it feels like to be positioned correctly during consolidation. The pain is the price of admission. If you go out and stop investing, you lose the game. The biggest loss in this market is not a price loss – it is a time loss. And I am trying to help you avoid that.

VII.

VII. The Insider Signal and Other Leading Indicators

One of the most reliable signals for reading the economic cycle is something called the Insider Sell-to-Buy Ratio (STB). In simple words, it tells you how much the insiders – CEOs, founders, promoters of companies – are selling at an aggregate level.

If you backtest the data, it works out quite accurately. The STB ratio peaked before the 2008 crash. It peaked before the 2020 crash. Each time the red line spikes, the market eventually corrects. The signal is not perfect – the timing can be off by months – but the direction is remarkably consistent. When the people who know the most about their own companies are selling aggressively, you should be paying attention.

The STB ratio peaked again recently. Now, the counter-argument is that due to US elections and Chinese quantitative easing, the market has not crashed but instead gone sideways. This is an important nuance. People backtest indicators and assume the future will work exactly like the past. It rarely does. The market does one unpredictable thing every decade, and guessing that one thing right is where the real money is made.

Recession indicators are another useful framework. There are five classic signs of a recession:

  1. Gold goes up
  2. Bond yields go down
  3. Money flows to stronger currencies like USD (so they strengthen)
  4. Consumer demand goes down
  5. Firms stop expanding

When these five conditions are present simultaneously, you are either in a recession or heading into one. When only one or two are present, the media will still scream “recession” but the probability is low. As of right now, most of these indicators are not flashing red. Gold has gone down. Bond yields have gone up. The dollar index has corrected. Consumer demand is still holding. Only the firm expansion indicator is negative, and that is driven by tariff-related restructuring rather than fundamental demand weakness.

The risk-on / risk-off cycle is another leading indicator. When we are in a risk-off phase, capital flows out of emerging markets (India, Latin America, Southeast Asia) and into safe havens (US treasuries, gold, the dollar). Stocks in emerging markets can correct severely even when the underlying businesses are growing rapidly. This is what happened to MELI – 30% CAGR revenue growth for 30 consecutive quarters, and the stock went nowhere, because global capital was in risk-off mode.

The shift from risk-off to risk-on is what triggers the next bull run in emerging markets. When that shift happens, the stocks that were beaten down during the risk-off phase rally hardest. This is why you accumulate quality assets during risk-off phases rather than selling them. The buying opportunity is when nobody wants to touch emerging markets. By the time the risk-on narrative returns, prices will be 30-40% higher than where they are today.

PE re-rating is the mechanism through which individual stocks participate in the cycle. When a stock’s PE gets positively re-rated, the stock price moves up – this is what happened to railway stocks in India during the infrastructure theme, or IT stocks post-COVID. When PE gets negatively re-rated, the stock moves down even if earnings are stable. Understanding whether a stock is going through PE compression or PE expansion tells you whether you are early, on time, or late to the trade.

VIII.

VIII. How to Actually Buy the Dip Without Losing Your Mind

Everyone talks about buying the dip. Almost nobody does it correctly. The reason is psychological: when markets are falling, every instinct in your body tells you to run. The headlines are catastrophic. Your portfolio is deep red. Your friends and family are telling you to sell before it gets worse. Buying more in this environment requires a specific mindset and a specific process.

Let me give you the process I use.

First, determine the type of fall. Is this a parabolic crash (Type 1), a valuation correction (Type 2), or slow decay (Type 3)? Each requires different position sizing and different time horizons. In a parabolic crash, you can be very aggressive because the recovery will be fast – V-shaped and sharp. In a valuation correction, you need patience measured in years, not months. In slow decay, you need capital rotation skills.

Second, identify the support levels. For the Nifty, I typically work with three levels. The first support is a 5% correction from the peak – this is where I deploy 25% of my cash bucket. The second support is a 10% correction – this is where I deploy another 35%. The third support is a 15-20% correction (bear market territory) – this is where I go all in and potentially add leverage.

Take a concrete example. When the NASDAQ fell 20% from its peak, that was the start of a US bear market. At that point, I said: invest at least 50% of your intended US capital. Not because I knew the bottom was in – nobody knows that – but because a 20% discount on the world’s strongest equity market is a statistically excellent entry point over a 3-5 year horizon.

For individual stocks, the 200-day moving average (200DMA) is my primary reference. I buy stocks when they are trading below their 200DMA. When a stock goes 10-15% above its 200DMA, I book at least 50% profits.

Third, size your positions deliberately. If you want to invest 1 lakh in a stock, do not put 1 lakh in on day one. Put in 20,000 when the stock hits first support. If it falls to 200DMA, put in another 30,000. If it falls further, add more. The maximum exposure to any single stock should be 5% of your total portfolio. This is non-negotiable. I have seen people put 25% of their portfolio in a single small-cap stock and lose half their net worth in a valuation correction. Do not be that person.

Fourth, understand the Meta example. In February 2021, Meta had significant issues. Revenues were declining, valuations were extremely high, ad spend from clients was declining because interest rates were rising, and there was a broad tech correction in the US market. Meta dropped from a stock price of roughly $400 to $80. An 80% drawdown.

Now, the remarkable part: if you had done absolutely nothing – held the stock at $400, endured the correction, and simply waited until 2025 – the stock went to $800. That is a 19% CAGR despite riding through an 80% drawdown. If someone had bought the dip, traded along the way, added options, the CAGR would have been 25-30% easily.

When you see golden stocks fall, buy. Do not hesitate. And be okay with the correction. The current 2025 drop in Meta does not even have shrinking revenues or financial stress. The firm is set to expand capacity. The fundamentals are better than they were in 2021, and the stock is more reasonably priced.

Fifth, use the leverage window wisely. Leverage is not bad. You need to know WHEN to take it. The when is very specific: only during a genuine 20%+ crash (Type 1 fall) where the market exhibits V-shaped recovery characteristics. During a valuation correction or slow decay, leverage will destroy you because the timeline to recovery is too long and the carrying cost of the leverage will eat into your returns.

Why does leverage make sense during crashes? Because markets these days exhibit V-shaped recoveries rather than the slow decay of 2008. As long as you are playing within your limits and not taking leverage too early, you will do exceptionally well. This is exactly what the wealthy do – Adani, Ambani, Zuckerberg all leveraged up during the 2020 crash. They understood the type of fall, they understood the likely recovery pattern, and they acted while everyone else froze.

Sixth, do NOT chase rallies. This is the flip side of buying dips, and it is equally important. People who chased the Bitcoin rally are suffering now. People who chased the small and mid cap rally are suffering now. People who will chase the metals rally will eventually suffer as well. The rule is simple: buy more when panic is more. If you have cash to buy when the market is in panic, do it. And stop tracking your portfolio every day.

IX.

IX. Building Your Cycle-Proof Portfolio

Let me pull everything together into a practical framework. The goal is not to predict the future – it is to build a portfolio that performs well regardless of which phase of the cycle we are in, and that is positioned to capture the maximum upside when the next phase transition happens.

Principle 1: Geographic diversification is more important than stock selection.

Most Indian investors spend enormous amounts of time debating whether to buy IndusInd Bank or Kotak Bank. A far more impactful question is: how much should you allocate between India, the US, and China? Your geographic allocation will have a bigger impact on your portfolio than constantly hunting for trading opportunities within a single market.

The US is structurally the strongest economy in the world and is far from dying. Having at least 20% of your portfolio in the US – if nothing else, a simple index fund – is a basic requirement. AI is the trade of the decade, and most of the AI value creation is happening in US growth stocks like Amazon, Microsoft, and Meta. If you miss this, you might pay a heavy price.

India has pockets of over and undervaluation. IT is undervalued. Healthcare and hospitals are undergoing valuation corrections but remain fundamentally sound. Quick-commerce and delivery seem hyped. Banks have not moved correspondingly with the Nifty and represent the last segment likely to move before euphoria.

Principle 2: Match your strategy to the cycle phase.

  • During consolidation (now): Rotate capital. Keep 15-20% cash. Buy supports. Sell resistances. Focus on safe assets – Nifty 50, private banks, healthcare. No small/mid cap aggression until the valuations reset.
  • During early euphoria: Start becoming a net seller. Book 20-50% of profits on positions that have run up. Increase cash allocation. Do not chase momentum.
  • During crashes: Deploy aggressively. Use the cash bucket built during consolidation and euphoria. Consider leverage for Type 1 parabolic falls. Buy the strongest assets – not speculative names, but market leaders that will recover fastest.
  • During liquidity injection phase: Stay fully invested. Ride the wave. This is when buy-and-hold works perfectly, and the SIP narrative is actually correct.

Principle 3: Understand price correction vs. time correction.

Price correction is when the price of a stock falls. Time correction is when the stock does not lose much value but moves sideways for a long period. Both cost you money – price correction costs you capital, time correction costs you opportunity.

In the current environment, time correction is the bigger risk. The market may not fall dramatically – but it may go nowhere for 18 months. The people who sat through the October 2021 to May 2023 valuation correction with fully invested buy-and-hold portfolios made zero returns over that period. The people who rotated capital captured 10-15% gains each rotation.

HDFC Bank is an instructive example. When it fell 4% in a single day, people panicked. But HDFC does not have a business problem (price correction risk) or a valuation problem (PE compression risk). In fact, the chance of PE re-rating to the higher side is significant. It will happen – we just do not know when. For stocks like this, the strategy is simple: hold, buy more during dips, and wait for the re-rating.

Principle 4: Build your portfolio in layers.

Layer 1 is your core – 60-70% of your portfolio in high-conviction, geographically diversified large-cap positions. Index funds, top private banks, US tech leaders. These are the positions you do not touch through a cycle.

Layer 2 is your rotation capital – 15-20% that you actively trade between support and resistance levels. This is where your sideways-market returns come from.

Layer 3 is your cash bucket – 15-20% that sits on the sidelines waiting for the next 10%+ correction. This is your ammunition, and it is the single most important tool for outperformance in a volatile market.

Layer 4 is your opportunistic allocation – 5-10% for contrarian bets, small and mid caps that have been beaten down to aggregation zones, and new themes like robotics, autonomous vehicles, data centres, biotherapeutics, and space exploration. These are 3-5 year bets that you put into a separate brokerage account and do not check frequently.

Principle 5: You only need 3-4 good cycles.

This is the most important principle, and it is the one that should give you patience and perspective when the market feels terrible. You do not need to be right every day, every week, or even every quarter. You need 3-4 cycles executed correctly to build generational wealth. One crash where you had the courage and cash to buy aggressively. One euphoria phase where you had the discipline to take profits. One consolidation period where you rotated capital instead of sitting idle. One liquidity injection phase where you stayed fully invested and rode the wave.

Three or four of these sequences, executed with discipline, will outperform 95% of fund managers. This is not hypothetical – it is exactly what our community has done over the last three years.

The preparation for each cycle happens during the boring periods. Right now, when the market is going sideways and nothing exciting is happening, is when you should be studying interest rate cycles, understanding PE re-rating dynamics, learning options strategies for generating income in sideways markets, and building the analytical frameworks that will help you recognise the next phase transition before it is obvious to everyone else.

Investing is a skill. The last 1.5 years, people have made 0% returns – negative when adjusted for INR depreciation. If you believe in simple narratives like “buy and hold forever” or “buy quality monopoly stocks,” those narratives have been run by SIP advocates whose funds are now suffering at scale. God has given you a brain. Do not give up on your critical thinking skills. Analyse, build different strategies, and improve each day as an investor.

The cycle always turns. The question is whether you will be ready when it does.

Buy when panic is high. Sell when euphoria takes over. Rotate when the market goes sideways. And never, ever stop learning. That is the entire game.
