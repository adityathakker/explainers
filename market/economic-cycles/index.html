I.

What are the five stages every economy walks through?

Every economy on the planet runs through the same five stages. Over and over. Without exception. And yet, most investors — including people who have been buying mutual funds for a decade or more — cannot name them. They cannot tell you where we are in the cycle. They cannot tell you what comes next. So they keep making the same mistake: buying when they should be rotating, holding when they should be hedging, panicking when they should be loading up.

The sequence is always the same. The trigger changes, the duration changes, the magnitude changes. The order does not.

  1. Crash. An unexpected event takes 20–40% off the index in weeks. 2008. March 2020. The character is speed.
  2. Liquidity injection. Central banks cut rates and print. From 2020 to 2022 the US money supply went from $15T to $18.75T — a 20% expansion in two years[1]. The V-shape recovery starts here.
  3. Consolidation. Debt has to be repaid. Spending slows. The market grinds sideways with wide swings — up 8%, down 6%, up 5%, down 7% — for 18–24 months. The phase that bores most investors into surrendering.
  4. Euphoria. A Yamaha showroom raises SME IPO bids worth 4,700 crores against a 12-crore ask. A holding company "merger" of its own subsidiaries adds 2,000 crores in market cap with zero change to the business. The phase where price detaches from value.
  5. Crash. And the cycle resets.

The single most valuable skill you can develop as an investor is the ability to identify which stage we are in right now. Stock selection, position sizing, asset allocation — all of it flows from that one determination.

This is not theory. This is the observable, repeatable pattern that has played out in every major economy for over a century. Three or four cycles, executed properly, will generate enough wealth to change a life permanently. But you have to recognise the cycle to play it — and you will not get there by SIP-ing your way through with your eyes closed.

A short tour through each stage with real examples makes this concrete rather than abstract.

Stage 1 in practice. In March 2020 the Nifty fell from 12,400 to 7,500 — a 40% drawdown in roughly one month. The NASDAQ fell over 30% in the same window. Nobody saw it coming. Nobody could have. That is the defining feature of a crash — the trigger is genuinely unexpected and the unwind is faster than anyone's reaction time. By the time most retail investors processed the headlines, the bulk of the damage had already been priced in.

Stage 2 in practice. Through 2020 and 2021 the Fed dropped rates to zero, expanded its balance sheet by trillions, and the US Treasury cut $5 trillion of fiscal cheques. The RBI followed with aggressive cuts and OMOs. The money had to land somewhere and it landed in financial assets — equities first, then housing, then crypto, then long-dated growth. This is the phase where the people who bought during Stage 1 start looking like geniuses, and the people who sat out start chasing.

Stage 3 in practice. After the money was printed and the debt was issued, someone had to pay for the carry. The US is now spending more than $1T per year on debt interest alone. The only way that stops is by borrowing less — which means either taxing more or spending less. Either of those slows the economy. The market goes sideways with wide swings: up 8%, down 6%, up 5%, down 7%. Eighteen months of motion, zero net return. This is where most investors get crushed — not by losses, but by boredom and frustration. It is also the trap that this article exists to help you avoid.

Stage 4 in practice. When a Yamaha showroom files an SME IPO for 12 crores and receives bids worth 4,700 crores, that is euphoria. When a holding company merges its own subsidiaries and its market cap jumps by 2,000 crores despite zero change in the underlying business, that is euphoria. The signs are loud, and they accumulate fast. Markets can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent — which is precisely why a cash position and steady cash flow become king during this phase.

II.

Where are we in the cycle right now?

The honest answer in 2025–2026: late consolidation, with the first credible whiff of an early-expansion turn. Not euphoria. Not a crash. The phase where most investors give up — right before the phase where most wealth is made.

The evidence, market by market:

In the US: The policy rate has been walked down from 5.5% at the peak to 4.5%, with another 100 bps of cuts priced in by year end. Growth forecast around 1.7% — slow by US standards but positive. The Fed has slowed its balance-sheet runoff, which is a deliberate signal that it wants liquidity to come back. Tariffs are now in the prices. The market grinds in a range.

In India: Negative real returns over the last 1.5–2 years once you adjust for INR depreciation. Small and mid caps have done 40–50% drawdowns. The Nifty oscillates between roughly 21,000 and 25,000. Large caps are at a discount; SIP flows put a floor under the index; the broader direction is sideways[2].

Globally: Risk-off. Capital wants the safety of US treasuries, gold (until recently), and the dollar. A company like MercadoLibre — 30%+ revenue CAGR for thirty consecutive quarters, the only listed business on earth to manage that — has a stock that goes nowhere. Not because the business is broken. Because global capital does not want to bet on Latin America during a risk-off phase regardless of fundamentals.

Well, actually 🧐 — "risk-off" isn't just a mood

Risk-off is a measurable rotation: gold/USD/treasuries up, EM equities and high-yield credit down. The cleanest single proxy is the MOVE index (Treasury vol) divided by the VIX (equity vol). When MOVE/VIX rises, bond markets are pricing in policy uncertainty faster than stock markets — classic late-cycle risk-off behaviour. As of late 2025 the ratio is still elevated, but coming off the highs.

The shift from risk-off to risk-on is what triggers the next bull run in EM. The stocks that were beaten down hardest during risk-off rally hardest when the rotation completes. That is why you accumulate quality assets during the unloved phase — and why MELI, an obvious 5-year compounder, is on every smart desk's quiet buy list right now.

What this is not: it is not a 2022-style correction. In 2022, inflation was running 9% and rates were going up. Today inflation is at 3% and rates are coming down. When rates go down, firms borrow more, fresh capex starts, and the cycle moves forward. We are nowhere near a bubble. We are well past the bottom. The textbook answer is to accumulate.

The asymmetry is the point. A correct call here — that we are exiting consolidation toward early expansion — with appropriate sizing nets +20–40% over the next 18 months. A wrong call — that we are still in consolidation when we are actually entering euphoria — nets you missing out on the upside but not losing capital. The downside is opportunity cost. The upside is the next cycle's wealth. When the risk-reward is that lopsided, the discipline is to bias toward action.

The Fed is expected to cut into year-end and through 2026. Midterm-election dynamics in the US almost always pull policy toward growth-friendly stances regardless of the party in power. There is real headroom for quality compounders — Amazon, Microsoft, Meta, and a handful of Indian financials — to re-rate from here. Cheap is not the same as good, but quality at a fair price is the textbook signal to scale in.

There is massive panic in the market — gold off 10%, silver off 30%, Palantir and SoFi off 30%, Bitcoin off 40–45%. These are exactly the conditions where the next leg of wealth is set up. Bull markets are not loud.

III.

What's the difference between a correction, a bear, and a regime break?

Not all market declines are equal. This is one of the most expensive distinctions retail investors fail to make. People see red on a screen and react identically whether the index is down 5% on a tariff headline or down 40% on a systemic banking failure. The two situations require opposite trades.

There are three types of falls, and they map cleanly onto depth, duration, and recovery.

Type 1 — the normal correction. A 10–15% drawdown. Driven by sentiment, positioning, or a manageable shock. Recovers in weeks to a few months. Examples are dime-a-dozen: the 2018 Q4 wobble, summer-2024 yen carry unwind, every tariff-headline-driven pullback of the last two years. The right move is almost always to do nothing, or to add a tiny bit at the lows.

Type 2 — the bear market. A 20–35% drawdown. Driven by a real economic deceleration or a valuation reset. Takes 12–18 months to recover. 2022 (Fed-tightening bear) is the canonical recent example: NASDAQ down 33%, Bitcoin down 65%, growth stocks gutted. The right move is to deploy cash into quality at the second and third support levels. Patience, not heroism.

Type 3 — the structural break. A 40%+ drawdown driven by a regime change — a banking crisis, a war, a pandemic. The depth alone doesn't characterise it; the recovery time does. The post-2008 recovery to a new high took almost 6 years for the S&P 500. The Nikkei from its 1989 peak took 34 years. The COVID drawdown was structurally unusual — same depth (-34% on the S&P), but the policy response was so aggressive it compressed the recovery into 5 months, the only V-shape in this category since the Second World War.

Well, actually 🧐 — why recovery scales non-linearly with depth

If a stock falls 50%, it needs to rise 100% to get back to where it was. If it falls 80%, it needs to rise 400%. The arithmetic asymmetry of percentage returns is brutal: recovery = 1 / (1 − drawdown) − 1. A 10% fall needs an 11% gain. A 40% fall needs a 67% gain. A 67% fall needs a 200% gain.

That is the mechanical part. The behavioural part — that deep drawdowns shake out forced sellers and re-price risk for years — layers on top. Both effects compound. This is why "buy and hold" sounds simple and is actually punishing in regimes when the rare 40%+ event lands on your account.

The clearest historical evidence for non-linear recovery is the cluster of 40%+ drawdowns the S&P 500 has experienced since 1929. The 1929 crash took 25 years to recover in nominal terms. The 1973–74 oil shock took 7 years. The 2000 dot-com unwind took 7 years. The 2008 financial crisis took ~5.5 years. The 2020 COVID drawdown took 5 months. Same depth class — vastly different recoveries — because the policy response differed by orders of magnitude. The depth alone tells you about the danger; the policy response tells you about the duration.

Understanding which type of fall you are in determines your entire strategy. Correction: do nothing. Bear: deploy in thirds at support. Break: the only fall where leverage and aggression are arguably correct — but only if you are early enough to ride the policy response.

IV.

Is the recession everyone is talking about even a real recession?

Every time the word "recession" appears on a finance channel, retail investors panic. Goldman raises the US recession probability from 20% to 35% and the WhatsApp groups light up. Channels run recession segments around the clock. Podcasters who have never invested a single rupee start explaining that the US is going bankrupt.

Stop. Breathe. Understand what the word actually means.

Two technical definitions are in play. The textbook one: two consecutive quarters of negative real GDP growth. The NBER one (the body that actually dates US recessions): a significant decline in economic activity spread across the economy, lasting more than a few months. Neither matches the panic-headline version. Neither requires "negative GDP" in the sense most people picture.

If GDP goes from 100 to 110, that is 10% growth. If GDP then goes from 110 to 120, that is roughly 9% growth. GDP grew. The rate of growth slowed. That deceleration is what gets called a recession when it persists. Negative numbers are not required — only a sustained loss of velocity.

NBER recession ≈ (−) Δ(employment) ∪ (−) Δ(real income) ∪ (−) Δ(industrial production) ∪ ... for > ~6 months a basket of monthly series, not a single quarterly GDP number

Now the part that matters for money: recession is a lagging indicator. By the time the official announcement lands, the contraction has already played out. Stock prices, meanwhile, are leading indicators — they try to price in what happens 6–12 months from now. So markets routinely bottom before the recession is even declared, and by the time the headline hits, the cycle is already turning.

That is why the people who wait for the "official" recession to adjust their portfolios are always too late. The market has already priced it in. The opportunity has already passed.

Look at the actual data. The last two significant US recessions were 2008 and 2020. In both cases, if you bought during the pain, you made extraordinary money. The 2020 recession lasted exactly two months — the shortest on record. The market recovered in a V and hit new highs within the year. People who sold in panic in March 2020 locked in losses. People who bought made generational wealth.

The current 2025–2026 noise is louder than it deserves to be. The US is growing money supply at 3.8% a year, which floors nominal GDP growth at roughly that. Most of the classic recession indicators are not even pointing the same direction:

  • Gold has corrected (a real recession sends gold up).
  • Bond yields are up (a real recession pulls yields down).
  • The DXY has weakened (a real recession strengthens the dollar).
  • Consumer demand is still positive (a real recession crushes it).

The only signal that checks out is that firms have slowed capex — and that is a tariff-uncertainty story, not a fundamental demand collapse. This is not a recession. It is a manufactured slowdown that can be undone with a stroke of a pen. These are exactly the manufactured crises that build real wealth for the people who refuse to panic.

"Recession" in the modern press is closer to a vibe than a definition. The technical version — sustained, broad-based decline in activity — is rare, dated in retrospect, and almost always already priced in by the time it makes the front page. The vibe version is loud, frequent, and routinely wrong. Calibrate accordingly.

V.

How does money printing drive every asset price you own?

If there is a single mechanism every investor needs to internalise, it is the relationship between money supply, interest rates, and asset prices. Once you see it cleanly, half of what looks like random market behaviour starts to make sense.

The simplest way to see it is to follow a single rupee or dollar of newly created money through the system. The central bank credits a bank's reserves. The bank, holding more reserves than it needs, lends to a firm. The firm uses the loan to fund payroll, capex, or to buy back its own stock. The recipient of the cash (employee, supplier, shareholder) spends some and saves the rest. The saved portion lands in someone else's bank account. That bank, again, has more reserves than it needs — and the cycle of fractional lending repeats. By the time the original unit of newly created money is exhausted, it has touched five or six balance sheets. Each one moved the corresponding asset prices.

The chain in compressed form:

  1. A crisis hits. Markets crash.
  2. Central banks cut rates and create reserves. Cheap money floods the economy.
  3. Firms borrow more. Consumers spend more. Capex restarts. Asset prices rise because the liquidity has to go somewhere.
  4. Debt accumulates. At some point the carrying cost becomes uncomfortable.
  5. Central banks raise rates or shrink the balance sheet. Liquidity drains.
  6. Growth slows. Markets consolidate or correct.
  7. Repeat.

From 2020 to 2022 the US grew M2 from $15T to $18.75T — a 20% expansion in two years. "Why didn't GDP grow 20% then?" Because GDP shrank in 2020 when activity froze. If you re-base for the COVID contraction, the money expansion did flow through into nominal activity. It just got delivered as asset-price inflation first, then goods inflation, then a slow normalisation. Always in that order.

The US plan, stripped of euphemism, is straightforward: print enough to keep nominal GDP growing, aggregate the strategically important means of production (semiconductors, land, ports, energy), and add enough real value to absorb the dollar dilution. Internal debt in the world's reserve currency is the cheapest possible way to fund a hegemon's transition.

Right now the US is growing money supply at 3.8% per year. That floors nominal GDP at roughly 3.8%. It also floors nominal asset-price appreciation at 10–15% per year in the major US indices. After tax, currency depreciation, and real inflation, that is 2–3% of real wealth growth for people who invest. For people who don't, it is a deepening cost-of-living crisis[3].

Rate cuts are the trigger for the next phase. A Fed cut signals an intent to accelerate liquidity. The character of the market doesn't change — we remain in a liquidity-driven cycle — but the velocity does. When the Fed cut by 50 bps in September 2024 instead of the expected 25 bps, that was a deliberate signal that growth concerns outweighed inflation concerns. Markets re-priced for euphoria the same week.

The deeper problem nobody on financial TV says out loud: this kind of liquidity-driven growth structurally weakens the long-run economy. It will burst. The question is just when, how, and who exactly gets hurt. The US will export inflation to the world because no currency can replace the dollar today. Cost-of-living crises will recur. Inflation will spike 7–8% for 2–3 years, settle, then spike again. The amplitude grows. The cycle gets faster.

People waiting for prices to "correct meaningfully" before they buy will wait forever. The surprise of the next decade is that asset prices keep climbing because the denominator (money) keeps debasing. The window for sensible entries is during consolidations and early corrections — exactly when the headlines are most terrifying and the urge to sit on cash is strongest.

The transmission mechanism, in one line

Central bank prints → reserves up → bond yields down → equity discount rate down → long-duration assets up → real estate up → goods inflation up (12–24 months later) → rates raised → reverse. Every step is mechanical. The only judgement call is figuring out which step the chain is on right now — which is exactly what the dashboard in the next section is built to help with.

VI.

How do you read leading indicators without spinning out on every headline?

If recession is a lagging indicator, what are the leading ones — and how do you put them together without becoming a slave to every Goldman PDF?

Four signals do most of the work. They are not perfect individually. As a basket they have historically led every US recession of the last 40 years by 6–18 months.

  • Inverted yield curve — when the 10y treasury yields less than the 3-month bill, the bond market is forecasting a slowdown. Inverted since mid-2022; un-inverted in late 2024. That un-inversion is, paradoxically, when recessions historically start.
  • Unemployment trend — the Sahm Rule fires when the 3-month average jobless rate rises 0.5pp above its 12-month low[4]. Triggered briefly in summer 2024, has since cooled.
  • ISM manufacturing PMI — sub-50 means contraction in the goods economy. Sub-45 historically aligns with recession. Currently mid-40s — cautionary, not catastrophic.
  • Credit spreads — the gap between high-yield bond yields and treasuries. Above 600 bps means the market is pricing in defaults. Currently around 300 bps — benign.

Well, actually 🧐 — why no single indicator works alone

The inverted yield curve has predicted 11 of the last 7 recessions (with apologies to Paul Samuelson). The Sahm Rule fired in 2024 and we still have not had a recession. ISM was below 50 for most of 2023 with the economy growing 2.5%. Credit spreads stay benign right until they don't.

Each indicator has a different failure mode, which is why you weight them together. The composite reduces the false-positive rate dramatically — if 3 of 4 signals are in the same colour zone, the historical hit rate jumps from ~60% (any one indicator) to over 85%.

The other useful signal is the Insider Sell-to-Buy Ratio — how aggressively insiders (CEOs, founders, promoters) are selling at an aggregate level. It peaked before the 2008 crash. It peaked before the 2020 crash. It peaked again recently. Timing has been off by months; direction has been remarkably consistent. When the people who know the most about their own companies are selling aggressively, it pays to listen — even if the price keeps going up for another quarter.

The classic five signs of recession, viewed simultaneously, also still pay rent. Gold rising. Bond yields falling. Money flowing to stronger currencies (dollar strengthening). Consumer demand falling. Firms stopping expansion. When all five fire at once, the probability of an imminent recession is high. When only one or two are firing, the press will scream "recession" but the probability is low. Right now four of the five point the wrong direction. The headlines do not.

One more useful framing: the risk-on / risk-off cycle itself is a leading indicator. In risk-off, capital flows out of EM (India, Latin America, SE Asia) and into safe havens (US treasuries, gold, the dollar). EM equities can correct severely even when the underlying businesses are growing rapidly — MELI is the textbook case. The shift back from risk-off to risk-on is the trigger for the next EM bull run, and the stocks that were beaten down hardest during risk-off rally hardest when the rotation flips. The buying opportunity is when nobody wants to touch EM — by the time the risk-on narrative returns to the press, prices are already 30–40% higher.

VII.

How do you make money when the market goes nowhere for two years?

Most investing education assumes a bull market. Buy great companies. Hold forever. Watch the wealth compound. The SIP industry is built entirely on the assumption that markets trend upward over long enough periods.

That assumption breaks during consolidation. When the Nifty oscillates between 21,000 and 25,000 for 24 months — making 20% swings up and down but ending where it started — buy-and-hold delivers zero. The October 2021 to May 2023 valuation correction was exactly this. Passive investors made nothing. Rotators captured 10–15% per leg.

Here is the practical playbook.

Step 1: Keep a real cash bucket. 15–20% of the portfolio in cash during consolidation. Not idle — ammunition. When the market falls 10% (which the Indian market did four times in the last 2.5 years), the cash gets deployed. When the market bounces back to resistance, 15–20% of the position gets booked and the cash bucket replenished.

Step 2: Buy at supports, sell at resistances. Simple to say, brutal to execute. You have to sell when things are going up (which feels terrible) and buy when things are going down (which feels worse). The framework I use is thirds. To invest 10 lakhs, deploy 3.3 lakhs at the first support, 3.3 lakhs at the second, the remainder at the third. You cannot time the bottom. Nobody can. But you average in at progressively lower prices.

Step 3: Never stop investing. If your cash bucket is already at 20% and fresh income arrives, that money goes in at minor dips. Do not let cash drift above 20%. The real risk in a sideways market is not capital loss. It is time loss. The market may not fall — it may simply go nowhere for 18 months — and a portfolio that earns 0% for 18 months has lost real purchasing power.

Step 4: Know when to shift gears. The only scenarios where this playbook changes:

  • Euphoria signals firing (insider STB peak, SME IPO mania, holding-co mergers) — become a net seller. Book aggressively. Build cash.
  • Type-1 parabolic crash (20%+ in weeks, unexpected trigger, policy response visible within days) — deploy aggressively, consider modest leverage on quality.

Step 5: Accept the daily misery. Nobody tells you that being positioned correctly during consolidation feels awful. You buy something, it drops 3% the next day. Headlines are catastrophic. Your WhatsApp groups are full of panic. You feel like an idiot. This is what being early to the right trade looks like. The pain is the price of admission. The investors who quit here are the ones who do not see the eventual leg up.

A concrete example of the rotation in action. A 5-crore Indian portfolio. Nifty hits first resistance after a 6% rally in a month — that is roughly 30 lakhs of unrealised gain. The book-and-rotate rule fires: sell ~15% of the position, lock in the 30 lakhs, redirect the cash to the bucket. Two months later the Nifty corrects 8% on a tariff headline. The cash bucket is fully topped up; the next 25% of cash deploys at the first support level. Six weeks later the position is back to the previous high, and the lower entry price means an additional 5–7 lakhs of profit captured purely from the rotation. Over a 24-month consolidation, four such rotations stack to 25–30% on the rotating capital — while the buy-and-hold neighbour is still sitting at zero net return.

The biggest loss in a sideways market is not a price loss. It is a time loss. And the cure for time loss is structured capital rotation — not a SIP.

A useful behavioural tool to fight the urge to hold winners through resistance: tag every buy at support with a pre-decided sell level (e.g. "sell 25% at +12%, 25% at +20%, hold the rest"). The decision is then mechanical, not emotional. The instant the price prints the level, the order fires. Removing the decision is the entire game in a sideways market — because the moment you make the call by feel, the disposition effect (selling winners too early, holding losers too long) takes over and the rotation breaks down.

VIII.

How do you actually buy the dip without losing your mind?

Everyone talks about buying the dip. Almost nobody does it. The reason is biological: when markets fall, every instinct says run. Headlines are catastrophic. Your portfolio is red. Friends are telling you to sell before it gets worse. To buy in that environment requires a specific process, not willpower.

First, classify the fall. Use Widget 2 from Section III. Is this a correction, a bear, or a structural break? Each has a different optimal response.

  • Correction. Do nothing meaningful. Maybe add 10% of cash bucket at the lows. Most of these reverse within weeks.
  • Bear. Deploy in thirds at −10%, −15%, −20%. Patience measured in quarters.
  • Structural break. If the policy response is visible and aggressive (2020), buy aggressively — the V-shape is faster than headlines say. If it is not (2008, Japan 1990), wait for credit conditions to stabilise first.

Second, define the support levels in advance. For the Nifty I work with three: −5% (deploy 25% of cash bucket), −10% (deploy another 35%), −15–20% (deploy the rest, consider leverage). For individual stocks, the 200-day moving average is the primary anchor. Buy below 200DMA. Book 50% of the position when price runs 10–15% above 200DMA.

Third, size positions deliberately. To invest 1 lakh in a single stock, deploy 20k at first support, 30k at 200DMA, 50k if it falls further. Max exposure to any one name: 5% of portfolio. Non-negotiable. The people who blow up on options or single-stock concentration are universally the ones who held 25% positions and prayed.

Fourth, the Meta case study. February 2022 to October 2022 — Meta dropped from $384 to $88. An 80% drawdown. Revenues were genuinely declining. Ad spend was contracting. Tech was in a Fed-tightening bear. If you held through and did nothing — just sat on shares bought at the top — by 2024 Meta was at $600. That is a 13% CAGR despite riding through an 80% drawdown. If you bought the dip in thirds, the same period returned 35–40% CAGR. Same underlying. Same time window. Different process.

The Meta example is instructive because the fundamentals genuinely did get worse during the drawdown. Reality Labs was burning $10B+ annually with no revenue. Ad spend was contracting. The market was not punishing a healthy business unfairly. It was correctly re-pricing a business that had become less valuable. And yet the stock recovered, because (a) the secular ad business was structurally intact, (b) management cut costs aggressively, and (c) the AI capex story added a new growth vector. The lesson is not "buy any drawdown." It is "buy quality drawdowns where the underlying franchise survives the shock." The two look identical for a few quarters and diverge dramatically thereafter.

Fifth, leverage has exactly one window. Only during a Type-1 parabolic crash with a visible policy response. Not during a valuation correction. Not during a slow decay. The carrying cost of leverage will eat you alive over a multi-quarter bear. The wealthy who used leverage in March 2020 (Adani, Ambani, Zuckerberg all on the record) understood the type of fall and the recovery pattern. They moved while everyone else froze. That is the whole game.

Sixth, do not chase rallies. The flip side. The people chasing Bitcoin at $69k in late 2021 suffered for two years. The people chasing Indian small caps in late 2024 are suffering now. The rule, stated as flatly as possible: buy when panic is loudest, sell when celebration is loudest, and check your portfolio less often than you check the weather.

One closing note on the 200DMA reference above. There is nothing special about 200 days as a window — a 150-day or 250-day average performs almost identically across long backtests. The 200DMA is just the convention — widely watched, widely used, and therefore self-reinforcing when institutional players adjust positioning around it. The deeper signal it captures is mean-reversion at the multi-quarter horizon. Stocks that drift far above their long-term moving average are usually rich relative to their own history. Stocks that drift far below are usually cheap. Neither relationship is exact; both have been statistically reliable over decades, which is enough to anchor a process around.

IX.

What does a cycle-proof portfolio look like?

Pulling everything together. The goal is not to predict the next move — it is to build a portfolio that performs reasonably in every phase and is positioned to capture the maximum upside at every phase transition.

Principle 1: Geographic mix dominates stock selection. Most Indian investors burn hours debating IndusInd vs. Kotak. The decision that actually matters is how much sits in India vs. the US vs. China vs. cash. Geographic allocation outweighs single-stock picking by an order of magnitude. The US is the strongest economy in the world and is not dying. At least 20% in US large-cap, even via a plain index fund, is a baseline requirement, not an aggressive call. India itself has pockets of mispricing within the market. IT services are undervalued after two years of compression. Healthcare and hospitals are mid-correction but fundamentally sound. The decision of which India to own — not whether to own India — is where the next two years of relative returns will be made.

Principle 3: Price correction vs. time correction. Price correction takes your capital. Time correction takes your opportunity. Both cost real money. In the current environment, time correction is the bigger risk. The market may not fall meaningfully — it may simply do nothing for 18 months.
