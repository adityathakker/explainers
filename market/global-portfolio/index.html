I.

I. The Question You Are Not Asking

Here is the most dangerous assumption Indian investors make: that investing in India is sufficient.

You run your SIPs in Nifty 50. Maybe you have some midcap and smallcap mutual funds. You feel diversified because you own banks, IT companies, hospitals, and an FMCG stock or two. Your portfolio looks spread out across sectors, across market caps, across fund managers. And yet, in the most fundamental sense, you are making a single concentrated bet – a bet that the Indian rupee will hold its value, that the Indian economy will outgrow its structural challenges, and that the firms listed on Indian exchanges will capture a meaningful share of the global value creation happening right now.

That bet, on every metric that matters, has been underperforming for years.

Consider the basic arithmetic that most investors never bother to compute. Nifty 50 has given approximately 12.5% CAGR over the last 20 years in nominal INR terms. Post long-term capital gains tax (which has marched steadily from 0% before 2018, to 10%, then to 12.5%), that drops to roughly 10.5%. Now adjust for the rupee’s depreciation against the dollar – a consistent 3-4% per year erosion. You are now at 6.5-7% in dollar terms. And what is the global inflation rate in real terms? At minimum 7-8% in dollar-denominated purchasing power.

Your Indian equity portfolio, the thing you have poured years of SIPs into, has barely kept pace with global inflation. That is not wealth creation. That is a treadmill.

Now compare this to the Nasdaq. QQQ’s last 15-year CAGR has been 19.5%. Its last 30-year CAGR has been 13.97%. Its last 10-year CAGR has been 19%. Even on a 5-year basis – which includes the 2022 crash, the inflation scare, and the interest rate hiking cycle – QQQ has returned approximately 82%, which translates to roughly 12.95% CAGR in US dollar terms. No currency adjustment needed. No rupee depreciation to subtract.

The question you should be asking is not “Should I invest globally?” The question is: “Why have I been concentrating all my wealth in one country?”

This article will walk you through the numbers, the logic, the mechanics, and the exact framework for building a globally diversified portfolio. Everything here is drawn from years of real investing experience – not textbook theory, but actual positions taken, actual returns earned, and actual lessons learned the hard way.

II.

II. The Currency Problem Nobody Talks About

Let me put this bluntly: most Indian investors do not understand currency risk, and it is costing them dearly.

There is a common argument that goes: “I earn in rupees, I spend in rupees, so why should I care about the dollar?” This sounds sensible. It is also completely wrong.

Here is why. From October 2021 to February 2025, the Nifty gave 19% returns in nominal terms. During the exact same period, the Indian rupee eroded by 18% against the US dollar. So the effective net gain in dollar terms over those four years was approximately 1%. That is a 0.23% CAGR. Four years of investing, four years of market volatility, four years of stress – for essentially zero real wealth creation[1].

ReturnUSD ≈ (1 + ReturnINR) × (1 + ΔINR/USD) − 1 Indian-market returns translated into a global currency — subtract this, not the Nifty number, from your real-return goal

And this is not an anomaly. The INR has consistently depreciated by 3-4% per year against the dollar over long periods. In October 2024, the exchange rate was 84 INR to 1 USD. By early 2025, it had hit 87.03 – a 3.5% fall in just a few months. Add the fact that when the Indian stock market falls (say 12% from its peak), the effective drawdown in dollar terms becomes 15.5% because you are hit by both the market decline and the currency depreciation simultaneously.

When Indian equities fall, the rupee often falls with them. You get a double hit that most investors never see because their brokerage account shows only the INR number.

Now, why does this matter if you plan to spend in India? Because we live in a globally connected world. The supply chain efficiencies of modern commerce ensure that comparable-quality goods cost roughly the same across nations. Go to Starbucks in the UAE and pay 16 AED for an espresso – that is about 376 INR. In India, the same espresso costs around 250 INR. Adjust for India’s lower tax environment and you arrive at nearly the same effective cost.

This convergence applies to everything that defines quality of life: healthcare, international education, electronics, travel, even domestic real estate in tier-1 cities that is increasingly priced relative to global benchmarks. Your wealth is not the number on your screen. Your wealth is what that number can buy you in real terms, and in real terms, the rupee buys you less every single year.

The US Dollar Index (DXY) measures the strength of the dollar against six major currencies – Euro (57.6%), Japanese Yen (13.6%), Pound Sterling (11.9%), Canadian Dollar (9.1%), Swedish Krona (4.2%), and Swiss Franc (3.6%). While the INR is not directly a part of this basket, there is a strong indirect relationship. When DXY strengthens, the rupee weakens. When DXY consolidates, the INR gets some breathing room. Between 2020 and 2022, DXY grew significantly, and during that period, the rupee depreciation accelerated. Understanding this relationship is critical for timing your dollar-denominated investments.

The solution is not to stop investing in India. The solution is to own assets denominated in stronger currencies – primarily the US dollar – so that part of your portfolio benefits from, rather than suffers from, the structural currency differential.

Well, actually 🧐 — home bias is a real, documented behavioural error

Indian retail investors hold >95% of their financial wealth in INR-denominated assets, despite India representing <3% of global market cap. Vanguard's research across 20 countries finds the same pattern everywhere: investors overweight their home market by 50–90 percentage points relative to market-cap-weighted optimal. The explanation isn't ignorance — it's familiarity bias plus a refusal to pay the operational friction of cross-border investing.

The fix is mechanical: open a US brokerage account (LRS limit is $250k/year per resident), park 30–50% of new savings there, and let the structural rebalance happen over years rather than trying to time it.

III.

III. The US Compounding Machine

There is a reason I keep coming back to the US market, and it has nothing to do with hype or trend-following. It is pure math.

Firms like Google, Meta, and Microsoft are compounding their earnings at 18-20% CAGR in US dollar terms. For context, Indian large caps typically grow earnings at about 14% in INR terms. Convert that to dollar terms and you are looking at 9-10% earnings growth. Stock prices follow earnings over the long run – this is not a theory, it is an accounting identity. If US tech companies are growing their earnings nearly twice as fast in hard currency terms, their stock prices will outperform over any meaningful time horizon.

And it is not just about today’s numbers. We are in the early innings of the most significant productivity revolution since the internet. Consider self-driving cars: Google’s Waymo cars now operate 24 hours a day, 7 days a week. A human driver could handle maybe 12 hours. That is a 2x productivity gain from a single AI application. It is expected that 25% of the world’s GDP will be AI-driven by 2030, and this is already playing out.

Now ask yourself: which firms will capture the majority of this value? The top 7 profit generators on the Nifty 50 – Reliance, SBI, HDFC Bank, ONGC, TCS, ICICI Bank, and IOCL – together earn roughly 40-45% of the total net profits of all Nifty 50 companies. Their average PE is about 18.7. The remaining 43 firms on the Nifty 50 have an average PE of 25-26x, meaning most of the index is overvalued relative to its earnings power.

If you decide to invest in only those top 7 profit-making Indian firms, here is your expected return profile across timeframes:

  • 1-year: 10-12%
  • 3-year: 11-13%
  • 5-year: 11-13%
  • 7-year: 11-13%
  • 10-year: 13-15%

All in INR terms. Convert to dollars and subtract another 3-4%. You are looking at 7-11% in real terms from the best Indian large caps.

Meanwhile, the Nasdaq composite in USD terms delivers performance roughly equal to India’s small and mid-caps – but with significantly lower risk and a far better Sharpe ratio. This is a 10-year data point you can verify yourself. You are getting small-cap-like returns from a large-cap US index, with none of the governance risk, liquidity risk, or information asymmetry that plagues Indian small-caps.

The US market is not just another place to put money. It is the global compounding machine – the place where the world’s most innovative companies are listed, where capital allocation is most efficient, where corporate governance standards are highest, and where earnings growth in dollar terms consistently outpaces anything available in emerging markets[2].

Well, actually 🧐 — currency hedging usually destroys the case for going global

The textbook answer to currency risk is "hedge it". Buy US assets, but hedge the INR exposure via forwards or NDFs. In practice, this is a terrible idea for the Indian retail investor.

The forward INR/USD curve already prices in the expected ~3.5%/year depreciation. So if you hedge, you pay the depreciation cost up front in the forward premium — meaning your hedged USD return ends up identical to the INR return you would have gotten by staying home. The whole point of going global is the unhedged exposure: you want the rupee to weaken because it adds to your return. Hedging eliminates the structural advantage you went global to capture.

During Trump’s first term, the Nasdaq gave 22-23% average annual returns, compared to its long-term 15-year CAGR of 16-17%. Market-friendly administrations push equity markets aggressively because the wealth of the political class is directly tied to stock prices. The lesson is simple: if you tie your fortunes to the rich, your chances of getting richer go up significantly.

IV.

IV. Why India Is a Trading Market, Not an Investment Market

I want to be very direct about something that will upset a lot of people: the Indian market, as it stands today, is primarily a trading market. It is not an investment market in the way the US market is.

What do I mean by this? An investment market is one where you can buy high-quality firms, hold them for 5-10 years, and be confident that earnings growth will be strong enough to generate meaningful real returns over that period. A trading market is one where you make money by timing entries and exits – buying undervalued bets, riding momentum, and flipping positions when they reach fair value.

The Indian market has become the latter for several reasons.

First, value migration. The most significant economic shift happening right now is value flowing from every other part of the world into the US, driven primarily by AI and technology. Our entire Indian economy is now hostage to the US – our markets move not because of something India is doing, but because of what the US is doing. That is a classic hostage situation, and it tells you where the real value creation is happening. The number one reason to pick US stocks is value migration: value is flowing more to the US due to AI, rather than India.

Second, structural policy failures. Our economic policies have not kept pace with the global shift. While the US is investing trillions in AI infrastructure, semiconductor fabs, and next-generation computing, India’s policy toolkit is still largely oriented around consumption subsidies and incremental manufacturing incentives. The “marketing premium” that India commanded as a growth story is eroding. Our economic policies have been poor, and now it is time to pay the price for that.

Third, the valuation problem. The Nifty 50’s average PE needs to be around 22 or below for the market to be genuinely undervalued. But strip out the top 7 earners (which trade at 18.7x PE), and the remaining 43 companies are at 25-26x PE. If you want to invest in Indian large caps beyond the top 7, you are essentially buying overvalued stocks. And if you go to small and midcaps too early, the natural problem is that the Sharpe ratio is not sensible compared to Nasdaq.

Fourth, the tax trajectory. Before 2018, LTCG on equities was 0%. Then it went to 10%. Then to 12.5%. Where do you think it will be in 2045? Short-term trading tax is already at 20% plus additional charges, making buying stocks for less than one year a really bad strategy. The government’s pattern is clear: cultivate adoption first, then progressively increase the tax take. This makes long-term holding in India increasingly expensive relative to holding US stocks where you may not book profits for 15 years – and who knows where you will be a tax resident in 15 years?

If you don’t diversify, you will have zero options, even later in life. Fifteen years ago I was working at a non-profit teaching English to kids and hated money. The big bad world made me a capitalist. Point being: your future is more unpredictable than you think.

So what should you do? Here is my framework:

  • Invest for the long-term in US markets – this is where the compounding happens
  • Take undervalued, broken bets on good Indian firms – for example, IT companies trading at long-term support levels
  • Buy Indian small and midcaps only when deeply discounted – not at current elevated valuations where earnings are not growing that fast
  • Buy Indian large caps where margin expansion is likely – sectors that grow faster than the overall economy, like hospitals
  • Trade and rotate capital in the Indian markets – use India for momentum plays and tactical allocation, not for your core long-term wealth building

Do NOT invest in Indian equities for 3, 5, or 10 years with a “set and forget” mentality. That era is over. Until valuations become genuinely cheap in the Indian market, there is more value in the US market, period.

V.

V. The 50% Rule: How to Structure a Global Portfolio

I get asked constantly: “What percentage should I invest internationally?” Here is my answer, and it has evolved over the years as I have gained more experience.

Every investor should have at least 20% exposure to the US or at least one developed market. This is the absolute minimum. But if you are serious about wealth building, the number should be closer to 50%.

Diversify at least 50% of your wealth globally. And you can do all this legally. Just read up a bit. This is one of the most critical decisions you will have to make right now.

Up until two years ago, even I was heavily invested in India – almost everything was in Indian equities. But slowly I started to realise that it is critical to be internationally diversified. I have always been honest about my positions, and this evolution in my thinking came from confronting the data rather than the narrative. The data said that US markets were better for long-term investing. I followed the data.

Right from day one of starting my YouTube channel, I have been a firm advocate of diversification. It is fairly easy to do it now: if you struggle to buy individual stocks, just simply buy US Index, Indian Index, China Index. You do not even need a fourth asset. If you cannot buy real estate, buy Gold. If you do not want to buy much Gold, buy BTC.

Here is a simple allocation framework that balances growth, protection, and diversification:

  • 50-60% Indian equities (large caps, select midcaps, tactical small-cap positions)
  • 20-30% US equities (indexes like QQQ, S&P 500, or individual high-quality tech firms)
  • 5-10% China/Europe exposure (for value diversification)
  • 5-10% Gold/BTC (store of value and inflation hedge)

Why this split? Because no one can predict with certainty that any single market will generate an alpha over the next decade. We all have home biases – we like investing money in our own country. But this violates the principles of diversification. By spreading across geographies, you ensure that if Indian markets do not perform structurally well, the rest of your portfolio compensates. And if India does well? You still participate with your 50-60% allocation.

The specific instruments are straightforward:

  • US: Buy QQQ (Nasdaq 100), S&P 500 ETF, or SOXX (semiconductor ETF). For individual stocks: Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, Google, CrowdStrike, and Novo Nordisk are solid starter positions that are likely to do well going forward.
  • China: CQQQ (China tech ETF) or Hang Seng Index. Chinese stocks are not long-term investable in the same way US stocks are, but they are good for trading. China is a powerhouse right now and can outdo anyone in the world on literally anything. Not investing there at least 10% on a long-term basis is foolishness. China has dramatically increased its capex on tech in the last 3 years. Chinese tech stocks are being looked at as a hedge for US stocks.
  • Europe: Vanguard European Stock Index Fund – particularly relevant now as European nations are loosening monetary policies and borrowing aggressively to fund growth. Germany and other European nations have recently offered high interest rates on long-term bonds, signaling they will throw liquidity into their markets.

The most common objection I hear is about taxes and remittance charges. Let me address this directly: the taxation on US stocks for Indian residents is 20% LTCG and slab-rate STCG. The holding period for long-term classification is 2 years for foreign equities. Since you are doing SIPs and long-term investing, you are holding for more than 2 years anyway, so the tax treatment is comparable. Remittance charges through platforms are minimal. The friction is mostly psychological, not financial.

Here is an important nuance most people miss: you pay capital gains only when you book profits. If you are buying high-quality US firms, maybe you will not book profits for the next 15 years. In that time, who knows where you will be a tax resident? The optionality of holding unrealized gains in dollar-denominated assets is enormously valuable.

Most importantly, if you speak with some advisors, they will just make you invest in India. The reason might be that they do not know how to invest abroad, since they themselves have never done it. I am someone who is doing this at scale and gaining practical experience. Do not let someone else’s ignorance limit your portfolio.

If you are over-invested in India right now, you do not need to sell everything and rebalance overnight. Just redirect your new investment flows internationally for a few months until you reach a better balance. Whatever you sell in India, deploy immediately in the US. This is not a time to withdraw money from markets entirely – it is a time to rebalance geographically.

VI.

VI. Buying the Dip: Position Sizing for Global Markets

One of the biggest mistakes I see investors make when they start investing globally is getting the position sizing wrong. They either put too much money in too early (driven by FOMO) or they put in too little (driven by fear of the unfamiliar). Both are costly errors.

Here is how position building actually works in practice, especially in volatile US markets.

The Nasdaq and US indices like SOXX are extremely volatile. They can swing 6-7% in a single day – I have watched the Nasdaq move in a 6-7% range in a single session, which is enormous. Amazon fell 55% in the 2021-2023 correction. NVIDIA dropped 20% in just two days during the DeepSeek panic, when a Chinese AI platform called DeepSeek emerged as a competitor to OpenAI that was 95% cheaper and far more efficient, causing the US market to question the entire AI capex thesis. If you go all-in at one price point, you are taking enormous risk that you catch a local top and then watch your capital erode for months before the recovery comes.

The correct approach is to build positions in tranches. Here is a practical framework:

Step 1: Decide your total intended capital allocation to the US market. Let us say it is 10 lakhs.

Step 2: Divide this into at least 3 equal tranches of 3.33 lakhs each.

Step 3: Deploy the first tranche at the first major support level. If the Nasdaq is at a critical support right now, put your first 3.33 lakhs in.

Step 4: If the market falls further to the next support level, deploy the second tranche.

Step 5: Continue until fully invested. If the market never gives you a second dip, you still have meaningful exposure from your first tranche.

This approach works because you cannot time the bottom – nobody can. But you can build positions systematically at progressively lower prices, which gives you a favorable average entry point. You buy in at least 3 dips. That is the rule. The stronger recession probabilities grow, the more the market will react, and more support levels will be hit. We keep buying until we run out of capital.

I will share something from my own portfolio management. When the Nasdaq fell 20% from its peak – the start of a US bear market – I had deliberately maintained 75% cash and only 25% invested in the US. This was by design. As the market corrected, I started deploying capital aggressively – building positions to 35%, then 60%, with the remaining 40% still in reserve for further dips. By the time I was done, my portfolio was showing 16% alpha compared to the Nasdaq index, beating 99% of fund managers. This is what disciplined position building looks like.

For SIP investors, the logic is even simpler. Common sense tells us: if you run SIPs in slow-moving products, you will get slower returns. So if you are already doing monthly SIPs, why not run them in faster-growth products like QQQ instead of Nifty 50? The data shows that on every timeframe longer than 5 years, SIPs in Nasdaq outperform SIPs in Nifty 50, S&P 500, and Gold. Even if you take a longer horizon of more than 10 years, the math still stands.

For hedging larger positions, there are three strategies I use:

Cash reserve strategy: Keep 15-20% of your intended US portfolio in cash at all times. This gives you ammunition to buy dips without needing to sell existing positions. For the US markets, I recommend keeping at least 10-15% cash for downward averaging at all times.

Options strategy: If you have the expertise, buy protective puts on QQQ at 10% below current market price with long-dated expiry (6-12 months). This caps your maximum loss while letting you stay fully invested. I have personally used this approach – buying 17 lots of QQQ puts at an exercise price of 545 (10% below market), paying roughly 45K USD in premium, selling call options to recover some cost, with September 2026 expiry. The result: my maximum loss scenario was capped at 14% from peak, while I was sitting on 34% profits. Worst case: 20% net profit even if Nasdaq corrected by 50%.

Double-down leverage strategy: Keep aggressive leverage pre-arranged so that when the market corrects 15% or more, you can deploy additional capital into growth assets. This works exceptionally well for V-shaped recoveries but is a poor strategy in sideways markets. I have already arranged for aggressive leverage and will use it whenever QQQ corrects by 15% or more from any given level.

These are not theoretical exercises. These are actual strategies deployed in live market conditions with real capital at stake. Pick the one that matches your portfolio size and expertise.

VII.

VII. The Global Monopoly Thesis

Here is a mental model that has served me exceptionally well and should guide your global investing: buy global monopolies.

What is a global monopoly? It is a company that dominates its market worldwide, has an effectively insurmountable competitive moat, generates massive free cash flow, and will exist and thrive regardless of which country’s economy is up or down in any given year.

Google is the monopoly on search and digital advertising. Meta is the monopoly on social networking. Amazon is the monopoly on e-commerce infrastructure and cloud computing. Microsoft is the monopoly on enterprise software. Netflix is the monopoly on streaming entertainment. NVIDIA is the monopoly on AI computing chips. These firms are going nowhere. They are the infrastructure of the modern economy in the same way that railways and telegraph companies were the infrastructure of the 19th century.

When Netflix stock dropped 44% from its peak because it was negotiating to acquire Warner Bros, the deal fell through – and Netflix still remained the best brand in entertainment, now 2.8 billion dollars richer from the breakup fee. The original deal was announced at $27.75 per share with an enterprise value of around $82.7 billion, with mutual termination fees built in. WBD ended up accepting Paramount’s upgraded $31/share all-cash bid, triggering the $2.8 billion fee to Netflix. Netflix stock jumped 10% in a single day on that news. That is what a monopoly looks like. Even when the thesis temporarily breaks, the underlying business is so dominant that it recovers and compounds forward.

The beauty of global monopolies is that they are remarkably easy to identify, unlike in India where finding genuinely high-quality large caps requires significant research and still comes with governance risk, promoter risk, and regulatory risk. Anyone can buy Google. Anyone can buy Meta. Unlike US tech which is easy to invest into – anyone can buy these companies – good Indian large caps are extremely difficult to identify. The information is transparent, the financials are audited to global standards, and the businesses are understood by billions of users worldwide.

Here is how I think about it practically:

  • If a firm has fallen 40% from its peak and your portfolio shows -20% on it, you have done well. This means you bought at better levels than the peak, which is exactly what tranche investing accomplishes.
  • If the firm is a global monopoly, the 40% fall is temporary. The earnings power, the competitive moat, the user base – none of that went away because the stock price dropped.
  • When these firms start rising, you will have an extremely hard time chasing them. This is why you build positions during drawdowns, not during rallies.
  • A high-quality asset is something you are okay owning even if it falls another 20% from the current level. If you would panic and sell at -20%, just buy the index instead of individual stocks.

Right now, there are many solid firms in the US market that have corrected due to absolutely ridiculous short-term reasons – tariff fears, trade wars, Fed rhetoric, NVIDIA chip export restrictions to China, geopolitical noise. The underlying businesses have not deteriorated. The earnings trajectories have not changed. NVIDIA and AMD corrected aggressively because of chip export concerns, but these are temporary trade negotiation tools, not structural business impairments.

In a risky market, we do not take more risk. We reduce our risks. Buy high-quality assets only. Do not speculate. The firms correcting now are the same firms that will lead the next rally. When the dust settles – and it always does – these positions will be the foundation of serious wealth.

VIII.

VIII. The Multi-Geography, Multi-Asset-Class Framework

Investing globally is not just about stocks. It is part of a broader philosophy of asset class diversification across geographies that protects your wealth through any economic cycle.

Here is a hard truth I learned in 2021: I used to be almost 100% in stocks and 0% in anything else. That was a mistake. When markets corrected, I had no cash flow to buy the dip. I had no uncorrelated assets to liquidate and redeploy. I was fully exposed to a single asset class in a single geography, and it limited my ability to capitalize on the very opportunities that market drawdowns create.

A portfolio does not only consist of stocks. It consists of cash flow, emergency assets, and some form of diversification. We run out of money to buy the dips because of poor portfolio planning, and then we watch helplessly as the best buying opportunities of the decade slip past us.

Since 2022, I have deliberately restructured across multiple asset classes and multiple geographies:

  • US equities for long-term compounding (QQQ, S&P 500, individual tech monopolies)
  • Indian equities for tactical plays and select long-term positions (banks, hospitals, deeply discounted value bets)
  • China/Europe equities for value diversification (CQQQ, European index funds)
  • Real estate for cash flow generation (properties in India and internationally)
  • Bitcoin for asymmetric upside and strategic reserve exposure (5-10% allocation)
  • Gold for store-of-value diversification

The reason this framework works is not just because diversification reduces volatility. It works because different asset classes give you options during drawdowns. When stocks are falling, your real estate generates rental cash flow that you can invest at depressed prices. When one geography is in crisis, another is likely performing. When equities are expensive, your gold or BTC allocation may have appreciated and can be partially liquidated to fund equity purchases.

Since 2022, I was buying real estate aggressively. Many people laughed at me. But here is what that allowed me to do when markets corrected:

  • Get consistent cash flows that I could invest during dips
  • Stay in an asset class that hardly corrects, so I could liquidate if I needed bulk capital for stocks
  • Have certainty that I would generate income next month regardless of what markets did

If you cannot afford real estate, gold or BTC serves the same purpose. The point is to have at least one uncorrelated asset class that you can tap when equity markets create once-in-a-cycle buying opportunities. Vanguard, which for years dismissed Bitcoin as too speculative, opened its multi-trillion-dollar brokerage platform to crypto ETFs in December 2025. On day one, aggregate trading in Bitcoin ETFs hit the $1 billion mark in the first half hour. The cost of missing BTC is too high. Even a 5% allocation makes sense – if the bet turns out right, that capital could go 10x easily.

We are also witnessing a fundamental shift in strategic reserves globally. The US is moving from USD-only reserves toward fixed-supply reserves like Gold and BTC. The newly announced BTC strategic reserve means that a large part of long-term strategic reserves are likely to shift from USD to BTC. The US will buy BTC with USD and control these reserves – similar to how countries maintain FOREX reserves to indicate the strength of their currency. This is the simple game going on, and understanding it helps you stay ahead of the curve.

We are moving from a globalized world to a more localized, multipolar world. Russia has demonstrated that countries can be cut off from the US SWIFT system overnight. Tariff wars are reshaping trade flows. Geopolitical blocs are forming. In this environment, concentrating all your wealth in one country, one currency, and one asset class is not prudent investing – it is gambling that nothing will go wrong in your specific geography over the next 20-30 years.

Being asset class diversified is not a nice-to-have. It is critical. It allows you to buy equities on big drawdowns, which is where the real money is made. Investing is a very long-term game. The more asset classes and geographies you have access to, the better your outcomes will be over decades. Going forward, it makes sense to learn as much about other asset classes as we learn about equities.

Your wealth erodes due to your inability to protect it. You might be worth 100 crores in India today. If inflation is 10-12% for you and your ability to generate returns is 5% (FD returns, after tax), you are losing 5-7% a year. At 7% annual erosion, your 100 crore purchasing power halves in 10 years. The solution is not higher returns from one asset class – it is diversification across asset classes and currencies that structurally protect your purchasing power.

IX.

IX. Making the Move: Your Global Investing Action Plan

Let me close with something practical. I have given you the data, the logic, and the framework. Now here is exactly how to implement this.

If you are starting from zero international exposure:

  1. Open an account on a platform that allows direct US stock and ETF purchases. There are several options available now with partnerships with major Indian banks. Pick whichever you are comfortable with.
  2. Start with a simple SIP into QQQ (Nasdaq 100 ETF). This single instrument gives you exposure to the top 100 non-financial US companies, including every major tech monopoly. You can literally just buy QQQ and the top 20 tech firms. You do not need any course or any expert for this.
  3. Allocate your first tranche – ideally 20-30% of your new monthly investment flow – to QQQ. Let this accumulate for 6-12 months before expanding into individual stocks or other ETFs.

If you already have some US exposure but are under-allocated:

  1. Stop adding new capital to Indian equities temporarily. Redirect all new investment flows to the US for the next 3-6 months until you reach at least 20-30% US allocation.
  2. If India looks overvalued and US is at discounted levels, consider trimming some Nifty 50 exposure and redeploying immediately into US indexes. Whatever you sell in India, buy in the US the same day. Do not let the capital sit idle.
  3. Add SOXX (semiconductor ETF) as a second position for concentrated AI and semiconductor exposure.

If you want to build a full global portfolio:

  1. Target allocation: 50% India, 30% US, 10% China/Europe, 10% Gold/BTC
  2. Build US positions using the tranche method described earlier – divide intended capital into 3 parts and deploy at major support levels
  3. For China, buy CQQQ at 5% allocation and treat it as a tactical trade, not a permanent hold. Monitor the 50-day, 100-day, and 200-day moving averages for momentum confirmation. When the 50 DMA crosses above the 100 DMA which crosses above the 200 DMA, you have a confirmed momentum shift.
  4. For Europe, consider the Vanguard European Stock Index Fund, especially as European nations are loosening monetary policies and borrowing aggressively to fund infrastructure and growth.
  5. Maintain 10-15% cash across the total portfolio for opportunistic buying during global drawdowns.

Key principles to remember:

  • Compare all returns in USD terms. The argument that “we consume in INR” does not hold. India is a net importer country. Your investments need to be benchmarked against a global currency base. You need to compare returns in USD terms, period.
  • Buy quality, not excitement. In volatile markets, stick to indexes and global monopolies. Do not speculate with risky stocks during a trade war or macro uncertainty. Just buy decent firms and stay put.
  • Recessions are buying opportunities. The last two major recessions (2008, 2020) generated the best buying opportunities in modern market history. If a US recession hits, India will get hit too – in 2008, a pure US-centric real estate crisis caused an almost equal drawdown in Indian equities. So you are not avoiding risk by staying in India. You are just getting worse returns for the same risk.
  • Diversification means across everything. Not just stocks and bonds, but geographies, currencies, and asset classes. Use multiple brokers too – if you have a portfolio above 10 lakhs, I would encourage using multiple DMAT accounts and platforms. I trust none of them individually. But I trust diversification.
  • Think in decades, not months. If you are buying high-quality US firms, you may not book profits for 15 years. Who knows where you will be a tax resident by then? If you do not diversify now, you will have zero options later in life.
  • Unless you buy a downtrend, you will not make money on an uptrend. The best time to build positions is when everyone else is panicking. These are exactly the times where, if you are sensible, you add massive value to your portfolio.

The world is changing rapidly. AI is concentrating value creation in the hands of a few global firms, most of them American. Trade wars are reshaping supply chains. Countries are building strategic reserves in gold and Bitcoin. The old playbook of parking all your money in Indian equities and running SIPs for 20 years simply does not work anymore in a world where the rupee depreciates 3-4% annually, where LTCG taxes keep climbing, and where the real growth in global GDP is being captured by firms listed on US exchanges.

The data is unambiguous. Nasdaq beats Nifty on every timeframe – 5-year, 10-year, 15-year, 30-year – in dollar terms. Indian equities barely beat dollar inflation after taxes and currency adjustment. The firms driving the AI revolution, the semiconductor boom, and the next wave of global productivity gains are overwhelmingly listed in the US. And you can access all of this legally, easily, and at minimal cost from India.

Right from day one, I have been a firm advocate of diversification. The numbers support it. The logic supports it. The geopolitical environment demands it. The only thing standing between you and a globally diversified portfolio is the decision to start.

Start today. Buy the index. Build your positions across geographies. Your future self will thank you for it.
