I. The Most Expensive Mistake in Investing

Let me tell you something that will save you more money than any stock tip ever will: the price at which you buy a stock matters more than which stock you buy.

This sounds counterintuitive. Every investing YouTube channel, every Telegram group, every business news anchor spends 90% of their time telling you what to buy. The remaining 10% is spent on when to sell. Almost nobody talks about the discipline of entry – the precise moment, the exact price level, the technical conditions under which you deploy your capital.

And that gap in education is where most retail investors bleed money.

I have seen this pattern play out hundreds of times in our community. Someone buys HDFC Bank – a fundamentally excellent stock, no debate there. They buy it because a friend told them to, or because they read a bullish article, or because they had some spare cash sitting in their savings account and felt the itch to invest. They buy it at the top of a run-up, when the RSI is screaming overbought and the price is kissing the upper Bollinger Band. Then the stock consolidates sideways for eight months. They sit on a 10-15% drawdown, watching their money do nothing while the market moves on without them.

This happened to me on HDFC. We eventually made money. But those first few months were painful. The stock went sideways for a long time because I bought too much too early, before the technical picture confirmed it was time to enter. I had seen a massive rally and jumped in. If you buy too much too early after a big rally, you can see a sideways consolidation for a long time. I am careful now.

The lesson was seared into my brain: entry price is not a detail. It is the decision.

Consider two investors buying the same stock – say Nvidia. Investor A buys at the peak, full of excitement after reading about AI growth. Investor B waits for a 17% pullback, buys when the price is at the middle of the Bollinger Band, and builds the position in tranches. Both own the same company. Both have the same fundamental thesis. But Investor B has a 17% margin of safety built into the position from day one. If the stock falls another 10%, Investor B is down 10% from a discounted base. Investor A is down 27% and questioning every life decision that led them to open a brokerage account.

The margin of safety – that gap between what an asset is worth and what you pay for it – is not a luxury. It is the single most important variable in determining whether your investment makes money or destroys capital. Even the best stocks in the world will ruin your returns if you buy them at the wrong price.

This is what we are going to work through in this piece. Not which stocks to buy – you already know the good ones. Meta, Amazon, Visa, HDFC Bank, Kotak, the hospital chains. The real skill, the one that separates profitable investors from people who just talk about investing, is knowing when to buy them. The tools are not complicated: Bollinger Bands, support and resistance levels, moving averages, RSI. But using them together with discipline and patience – that is where the money is made.

Understanding such simple strategies leads to 2-3% alpha per year. You would say: what is the big deal? The big deal is that on a 30-40 year basis, this is a doubling of your portfolio compared to someone who buys randomly. The point of studying technical patterns is that you figure out the times when to bulk buy. And that timing is everything.

II. Bollinger Bands: Your First Line of Defense

Let me teach you something new. Open any charting tool and pull up Bollinger Bands on any stock or index you track. You will see three lines: an upper band, a middle band, and a lower band. The middle line is simply the 20-day moving average of the price. The upper and lower bands sit two standard deviations above and below that average.

Here is what these three lines tell you in plain English:

Upper Band: The stock is running hot. It is trading at the high end of its recent range. If the price is touching or exceeding the upper band, there is a strong statistical case that a pullback is coming. This does not mean the stock will crash. It means the odds of continued upward movement in the short term are low.

Middle Band (Mean): This is the equilibrium zone. The stock is trading at its average recent price. This is often a sensible entry point for investors who have a medium-term view and there is already some margin of safety from the peak.

Lower Band: The stock is oversold. It has been beaten down relative to its recent trading range. This is where the bargains live – if the fundamentals are intact.

When I shared the Nifty 50 analysis with our community, I pointed out that the Bollinger Band was hitting the top line while RSI was hitting 70. Both indicators were flashing the same signal: short-term peaking. I told the community not to be scared if we saw a 1-2% drop, because that is exactly what the technicals were predicting. The Bollinger Band was likely to contract, and then give a breakout – exactly when, I did not know. But the point was clear: the markets, as per RSI and Bollinger Bands, had peaked in the short term. So unless you had at least a mid-term view, putting more money in at that level was a mistake.

I also laid out the mid-term support and resistance levels from that analysis. The breakout point was 25,500 – that became the new support. The top was around 29,200. If the top was achieved, it would be amazing. But you only win if you are able to hold the short-term volatility. The Bollinger Band combined with the RSI gave us the confidence to know: this is not the time to panic, and it is also not the time to chase.

Now let me show you how I actually use this in practice with a real trade – Nvidia.

Nvidia was 17% down from its peak. It was trading at the middle part of the Bollinger Band – the mean. Here is exactly how I thought through the position:

  1. Decide the maximum allocation: my limit for Nvidia was 10% of my portfolio
  2. At the time, I was at about 4%
  3. At the mean, with a 17% margin of safety from the top, it made sense to build to about 5%
  4. If Nvidia falls more and reaches another technical level – the lower Bollinger Band, a strong historic support, the 200 DMA, or an oversold RSI – that becomes the next major buying point, and I take positions to 7-7.5%
  5. Repeat, and max out at 10%
  6. This might take months to play out. We are simply following a system.

I received messages from people in the community saying they had bought Nvidia on a Friday and it fell 2-3% by the next week. My response was direct: if you cannot handle 10-20% falls on stocks and have no system or plan to handle this, stock investing is not the right game for you. Stocks falling is very natural. The system accounts for it.

The Bollinger Band is not a crystal ball. It does not tell you the exact bottom. What it does is give you a statistical framework for understanding where the price sits relative to its recent history. When you combine that with RSI and support levels, you get a much clearer picture of whether this is a good time to deploy capital or a time to keep your powder dry.

If you combine Bollinger Bands and RSI, you get an idea if an asset is overbought or oversold. The Bollinger Band is likely to contract, and then give a breakout. Exactly when? I don’t know. But the point is: the markets have peaked in the short-term. So unless you have a mid-term view at least – don’t invest more.

III. Support and Resistance: Where the Market Draws Its Lines

If Bollinger Bands tell you whether a stock is statistically cheap or expensive relative to itself, support and resistance levels tell you where the market has drawn its lines in the sand.

A support level is a price point where buyers have historically stepped in aggressively. Every time the stock falls to this price, demand materializes, and the price bounces. A resistance level is the opposite – a ceiling where sellers emerge, and the price struggles to break through.

These are not arbitrary lines. They represent the collective memory of every market participant. When Amazon has traded in a channel between two price levels for two years, bouncing between support and resistance like a ball between two walls, that channel is real. It is backed by trillions of dollars of market consensus.

Here is how I explained this to the community with a concrete example. I drew a channel on a chart – two lines, one at support, one at resistance. The stock since its last major trend change in 2023 had been moving in this channel. The range was 10-12%. On average, the stock touched support and resistance about two times per year.

Now do the math:

  • Buy at support, sell at resistance: 10% gain per cycle
  • Two cycles per year: 10% x 2 = 20% minimum annual return
  • Add covered calls at resistance and put selling at support on roughly 50% of your holding: another 5% per year
  • Total potential: 25% per year on a stock that the market considers “boring”

This is capital rotation. This is how you make money even when the market is not giving you a raging bull run. And this is exactly the reason I added Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, and Google quite aggressively during that period. The stocks were moving in predictable channels. And every time they touched support, it was like the market was handing you a gift.

There is also a concept I call psychological supports. These are round numbers – like 25,000 on Nifty – that become pivotal levels in the market’s collective mind. In markets there is a concept called psychological supports. This indicates pivotal levels. If the market breaks this level, it can sharply correct. But it will revert to this base psychological level at every chance it gets. So anything below a psychological support is a great point to buy for the long term. If the market holds above it and stays there, it turns into not just a support, but an important psychological support.

The same principle applies to indices. When Nifty 50 hit its first critical support during a correction, I told the community exactly what to do. If you wanted to invest 10 lakhs in the NASDAQ, you should deploy at least 3.33 lakhs at the first support. Can it fall more? Yes. Then we buy more at the next support. You cannot time the bottom. But you can systematically deploy capital at levels where the probability of a bounce is highest.

We always buy at support. We buy things where principal loss will not happen. Check the chart, identify the next buying point – the technicals tell you when. We only downward average from support points according to technicals.

The critical insight: support and resistance are not just lines on a chart. They are the market’s verdict on fair value. When a stock falls to support and holds, the market is telling you that buyers believe in the stock at this price. When Kotak Bank is trading at support and the fundamentals are solid, that is all the hint I can give you. Use it.

IV. The 200 DMA and Moving Averages: Reading the Market's Long-Term Pulse

Moving averages are the backbone of technical analysis for long-term investors. The three I track most closely are:

  • 50 DMA (Day Moving Average): The short-term trend – the average price over the last 50 trading days
  • 150 DMA: The medium-term trend
  • 200 DMA: The long-term trend – and the single most important line on any chart for a buy-and-hold investor

The 200 DMA is the most important of the three. When a stock or index is trading below its 200 DMA, it is in a long-term downtrend. From an investment perspective, this is a bulk buying zone for good quality assets. If you are a buy-and-hold investor and the market is below its 200 DMA, just buy solid things and stay put.

I explained this to the community during a critical market juncture. The red line was the 50 DMA. Green was the 150 DMA. Purple was the 200 DMA. The market was below the 200 DMA. Here is what I told investors:

For buy-and-hold investors: Just pick top 2-3 players from each sector – private banks (you already know the names), FMCG, auto, finance firms, insurance, hospitals. These industries grow faster than the Nifty typically, so you are likely to get a 15% CAGR on these. You do not need to do anything fancy.

For swing traders: Wait for a clear trend reversal. This will happen when the red line (50 DMA) goes above the green (150 DMA) and both change their incline – that would be the start of a bullish momentum.

For new investors: If you do not have much money invested and the market has most likely bottomed out near or below the 200 DMA, good time to start building positions right away.

Now let me teach you about the death crossover. On December 19, 2024, there was a death crossover on the Indian markets – the 50 DMA crossed below the 150 DMA. This indicates a trend change. We saw a drawdown, and the downtrend continued until there was more clarity on tariffs. I also demonstrated this at a sectoral level – Hero MotoCorp, for example, showed a clear crossover between the 50 DMA and 150 DMA, indicating the start of a bearish trend. Since then, the price had corrected a lot.

But here is the key point that most people miss: the death crossover is not a signal to sell. It is a signal to prepare to buy. The downtrend creates the very conditions under which future returns are generated. Unless you buy a downtrend, you will not make money on an uptrend. This is the single most counterintuitive idea in investing, and it is also the most profitable one.

I told the community: repeat after me – buy the downtrend. If you do not have the guts when the market is down, you will NOT make money when the market goes up.

The reverse is the trend reversal signal. I studied Kotak Bank’s moving averages and told the community that a trend reversal was forming. The 50 DMA was changing direction. A breakout had already happened. Going forward, the pattern was likely to be: 50 DMA above 150 DMA, both above 200 DMA. Post this alignment, the stock would be technically very strong. Since fundamentally the stock was already strong, the technical confirmation meant it was time to be fully invested and wait.

Similarly, when Bank Nifty was sitting on its 50 DMA, I pointed out that the data showed it typically takes support at this level. Could it fall further? Yes. Chances of fall? Low. Is this guaranteed returns? No. But if you study the data and there is a 20% margin of safety to resistance – that is enough for an informed investor to act.

The market right now is below the 200 DMA. So from a long-term perspective, this is a bulk buying zone. If you are a buy-and-hold investor: just buy good solid things now. And stay put.

V. Position Building: The Art of Deploying Capital in Tranches

Understanding the right technical level to buy is only half the battle. The other half is knowing how much to buy and how to stage your purchases. This is position building, and getting it right is the difference between a good investor and a great one.

Here are the rules I follow, distilled from years of real portfolio management:

Rule 1: Never exceed 5% of your total portfolio on a single stock. An index or ETF is different – you can go heavier there. But a single stock should be capped at 5%, with only the highest conviction positions stretching to 10%. When I was building my Nvidia position, the limit was 10% – and it took months to get there through staged buying.

Rule 2: Buy in at least 2-3 tranches. Never put your entire allocation into one purchase. If you want to invest 1000 rupees in a stock, split it into at least 3 buckets of 333 each. Buy at support using channels or moving averages. This is the absolute minimum you should do – the absolute basic of position building.

When I was buying Nvidia, my position went from 4% to 5% at the Bollinger Band mean. If it fell to the lower band or the 200 DMA, I planned to build to 7-7.5%. The final tranche to 10% would come at the strongest support level. This is systematic, disciplined, and takes the emotion out of investing.

Rule 3: Manage the investment-to-cash ratio. I keep a running ratio of how much I am invested versus how much dry powder I am holding. Right now in India, my ratio is 90-10 – if I have 9 lakh invested, I can invest 1 lakh more. For people investing over 3-5 years, I recommend 80-20 – 80% invested, 20% cash which is to be deployed at major support points. I will take the community along.

The best thing you can do is 20% cash targeting. If you have 10 lakhs as your portfolio size, create a 2 lakh cash bucket. This you can invest on the dips. In a panicked market, do not use more than 30% of your opportunity fund at the first support. Save the rest for deeper levels.

Rule 4: Loss targeting on portfolio. This is a technique most people have never heard of. Let us say you invested 100 units and the portfolio falls to 85 – that is a 15% loss. You invest MORE at supports. You bring the effective loss down to -10%. You keep averaging at supports and keeping the losses in check. At some point the market reverts, and you win.

The only reason this strategy fails is if the market goes into a structural decline. This rarely happens, especially when firms are growing their earnings. Keep a check on earnings. And stick to the system.

Rule 5: The Microsoft dip example. When Microsoft dipped, I told the community: you can start with 5% of your portfolio. And keep room to take this up to 10-12% of your portfolio, over time. Not all at once. When the market correction hit in early 2026 with gold down 10%, silver down 30%, Palantir and SoFi down 30%, and Bitcoin down 40-45%, I told the community: this is a good pullback to buy. There is significant headroom for good assets like Amazon, Microsoft, and Meta to grow. Just make sure the assets you are buying are worth buying.

That week turned out to be my highest buying week in the last 3-3.5 years. I was 60% done on my portfolio, most of it bought on a downtrend. My live portfolio showed a 16% alpha compared to NASDAQ – beating 99% of fund managers. I will invest the remaining 40% as we go along.

Position building means: never exceed 5% on one stock. Put money in at least 3 buckets. Buy at support. This is the absolute minimum. The absolute basic of position building. Learn about at least these points.

VI. Buying Uncertainty: The Counterintuitive Edge

Here is the hardest lesson in investing, and it is the one that separates people who talk about making money from people who actually make it:

You would only make serious money if you are buying equity during uncertainty.

This looks dangerous. It is not. Markets always cut their previous peak. This is not a hope – it is a statistical fact backed by decades of data. The Indian markets went 6x from 1999 to 2008. They went 2x from 2010 to 2019. Take a decadal view on things, and the direction has always been up.

The problem is that uncertainty feels terrible. When the market is bleeding, your portfolio is bleeding too. Every news channel is running doomsday headlines. Goldman Sachs is raising recession probability from 20% to 35%. Trump is threatening tariffs. The rupee is falling. And every instinct in your body is telling you to sell everything and hide.

This is precisely the moment when you should be buying.

During times of uncertainty, equity does badly. But you would only make serious money if you are buying during that uncertainty. It looks dangerous but it is not – markets always cut their previous peak. If the valuation of the market is not crazy high, you should buy it. The valuation can go from discounted to hyper-discounted. That is called handling the fall. If the market is bleeding, your portfolio will bleed too. If that hurts and you cannot handle this, equity investing might not be for you. But if you have a plan to handle the drawdown, you will win.

Let me give you a concrete example. During the tariff panic, the media was in full fear mode. Almost all high-growth assets were going down. I told the community: we are not in a euphoria yet. My assessment was that interest rates in the US would be cut soon, and due to midterms, Trump would institute more market-friendly policies. Stocks like Meta, Amazon, Netflix, and the software stack were trading at a significant discount. Yes, a 10% dip could come any day. But there was headroom for a 20-25% run-up.

When the breakout eventually came, it confirmed what I had been saying. It is often said that if you miss 10 best investment days in the market, you will miss 50% of the gains on your portfolio. The breakout day was one such day. The people who had bought during the uncertainty were rewarded. The people who had sold in panic missed it entirely.

Here is another principle that reinforces this: the market does not create a structural scare on the same news twice. On the first COVID dip, the market corrected aggressively. On the second COVID dip, the market did not correct much at all. Like humans, markets learn from experience. If there is a path to deal with a problem, it only creates a very short-term impact.

We stayed pretty much inactive for almost a year in the Indian market during one period. Guess what? That was the best thing to do. Most people were restless and they lost money. We did not. That is what matters. When we finally deployed, we bought banks at deeply undervalued levels. Every fund manager was now running after bank stocks, but our community already had decent positions – already on good profits.

The lesson: patience is not passive. Patience combined with a buying system at supports is the most aggressive form of investing there is. It means you have the discipline to wait for your price while everyone else is paying a premium for the privilege of acting impulsively. And when the opportunity comes, you deploy big.

I love to state this statistic: 1999-2008, Indian markets went 6x. 2010-2019, Indian markets went 2x. Take a decadal view on things. Buy the downtrend. If you don’t have the guts when the market is down, you will NOT make money when the market goes up.

VII. Capital Rotation: Making Money in a Sideways Market

Most people think you need a roaring bull market to make money in stocks. That is completely wrong. Some of the best returns come from capital rotation in a sideways market – buying at support, selling at resistance, and repeating the cycle.

My long-term thesis for India was clear: the market would be sideways for roughly 1.5-2 years. This means that people who do not rotate capital will suffer. But for investors who understand support and resistance, a sideways market is a goldmine.

The game is simple:

  1. Buy at support. Wait for the stock to move to resistance.
  2. Book partial profits at resistance.
  3. Redeploy that capital into another asset that is now at its own support level.
  4. Repeat.

Around Diwali, I took a huge block of Nifty 50. In a month, the trade gave 2.24% on a very large capital base. You would say: what is the big deal? The big deal is that almost all my free capital was in this trade. I invested 8 crores. That capital gave around 16 lakhs – technically a CAGR of 25%+. People do not see the power in this. But if you trade with huge capital safely, the absolute additional capital gained is massive.

Then I liquidated 20% of this and moved to riskier stocks. Now, the 16 lakhs becomes your risk capital. You go buy speculative things with profits, not with your principal.

It is better to put 10x capital and make 15% through safe trades, rather than x capital and try to make 30% on a speculative bet. The math is obvious: safe, repeatable returns on large capital will always beat speculative returns on small capital.

For momentum trading, the strategy is to ride momentum 2-3 times per year and make 15% each cycle. That is 15% times 3 equals 45% returns. Momentum trading means doing channel trades on strong stocks and rotating capital 2-3 times. I did this quite aggressively in 2021, and the same opportunities were opening up again.

When Aavas went up 20% in a month, I told the community: when fast run-ups happen, you should book around 15-20% profits as it has hit a resistance point. Carry forward with the rest of the positions. When Asian Paint’s breakout sustained, the upside was 10-15% after a long consolidation. When Bajaj Finance gave a technical breakout after 4 years of consolidation, the idea was to ride the uptrend for as long as possible.

On the other hand, when Indigo Paints and Asian Paints had run up and the RSI was overbought, I told the community: in case you have too many positions, consider cutting some. I personally cut around 40% on both because consumption had become a slow-growth industry. Moved to faster-growing segments.

The action points for capital rotation:

  1. Keep 20% cash for deployment at supports
  2. Sell bad stocks or rotate at the top of the range. Buy undervalued assets at the low range
  3. Track channels on your core holdings. Know where support and resistance sit
  4. Book profits at resistance. Do not be greedy
  5. Rotate into the next theme approaching support. When banks hit targets, move to hospitals. When IT runs up, rotate to financials.

People who don’t rotate capital will suffer in a sideways market. But for investors who understand support and resistance, a sideways market is a goldmine.

VIII. Scenario Analysis: Planning for Every Outcome

One of the most valuable frameworks I teach our community is scenario analysis using support and resistance levels. Instead of trying to predict exactly what will happen – which is impossible – you map out 2-3 realistic scenarios and prepare a plan for each one.

Here is how I did this during a period of uncertainty on the Indian market. Looking at monthly candles, I identified three scenarios:

Scenario 1 – Slow Bleed (highest probability): The market consolidates around the first major support for the next few months. Returns are 0%, but we make money if we are able to rotate capital within the range. My portfolio positioning: 95% invested, new cash held as reserve.

Scenario 2 – Sharp Fall and V-Shaped Recovery (moderate probability): The first support breaks, and we fall to the second major support – about 10% below. This should happen fast if it happens at all, and we can then expect a V-shaped recovery. This is where the cash reserve pays for itself – you deploy aggressively at the second support.

Scenario 3 – Breakdown (lowest probability): A completely new, unseen negative event plays out. The market breaks through even the strongest support. This rarely happens, but if it does, you deploy whatever capital you have left at generational entry points.

Similarly, for the US market, I gave the community multiple positive triggers to watch for. Tariff situation getting cleared – the trend changes and you ride it for 40%. Interest rate cuts – this saves the market in case of further drawdown, since all governments are in a good position to execute now. Russia-Ukraine clarity – helps Europe, accelerates growth. The point: there were enough positive triggers left for the markets. Do not just focus on the negative headlines.

The same framework works at the stock level. When HDFC Bank fell 12% from its high, I mapped the scenario:

  • Mid-term technical basis: nothing wrong, 26% upside potential
  • Long-term technically: fine
  • Fundamentally: very strong, the bank just needs big institutional buyers
  • If you do not need the money for 2-3 years, deeply undervalued

When IDFC First Bank was technically weak but fundamentally solid, I held all positions. My thesis: the market cap would grow from 60,000 crore to around 120,000 crore over 2-3 years – roughly a 30% CAGR. The bank had grown branches from 800 in 2022 to 950 in 2024, a 19% growth. Fixed asset formation was up 25%. If delivery volumes showed 55% delivery at 76 rupees, that told me long-term buyers were aggregating at that price. The technicals matched the fundamentals perfectly.

One of the primary goals of this community is to avoid panic. If I can help you do just this one thing, you eventually would make very good money. Now just because I say “don’t panic” would not change a thing. So you need a framework – the scenario analysis – to give you the confidence to act rationally when everyone around you is losing their minds.

The critical formula is simple. When the Nifty had 15-16% upside to its previous high, and a likely downside of 4-5% from current levels, the risk-reward was clear: seeing 4-5% pain is worth 15-20% gains. That is a margin of safety you can work with. So to a sensible investor, this is a buying opportunity.

We always buy at support. We buy things where principal loss won’t be there. If you have a plan to handle the drawdown, you will win. The only reason the strategy fails is structural decline. This rarely happens, especially when firms are growing their earnings.

IX.

IX. The System: Bringing It All Together

Let me distill everything into a system that you can actually execute. This is the framework I follow across my Indian and US portfolios, and it has delivered consistent results over multiple market cycles.

Step 1: Identify what to buy using fundamentals. The stock should be making money. Revenues should be growing. The business model should be sound. Fundamentally it should be making money. Technically it should be sound – charts should tell us to buy. And sentimentally it should be weak – that is where the opportunity is. When I studied Motilal Oswal Financial Services, it had highest-ever sales and profits, was 35-40% from its top, and there were lots of attacks on the firm in the news. Fundamentally strong, sentimentally weak, technically approaching support. That is the trifecta.

Step 2: Check the Bollinger Bands and RSI. Where is the price relative to the upper, middle, and lower bands? RSI above 70 with price at upper band means overbought – do not buy. RSI between 40-60 with price at the mean – moderate buy with a tranche. RSI below 30 with price at the lower band – aggressive buy if fundamentals are intact.

Step 3: Identify support and resistance levels. Draw the channel. Map at least 2-3 supports below the current price. Calculate the risk-reward: distance to resistance divided by distance to support. Anything where the upside significantly exceeds the downside is actionable.

Step 4: Check the moving averages. Is the price above or below the 200 DMA? If below, this is a bulk buying zone. Has there been a death crossover (50 DMA below 150 DMA) or is a golden crossover forming? For swing traders, wait for the 50 DMA to cross above the 150 DMA with an upward incline.

Step 5: Build positions in tranches. Deploy the first 33% at the current support. Hold 33% for the next support. Keep the final 33% for an extreme pullback or a confirmed breakout. This might take months. You are simply following a system.

Step 6: Maintain cash reserves. Keep 15-20% of your portfolio as deployment ammunition. Replenish after every profitable exit. Best thing you can do is 20% cash targeting.

Step 7: Execute the capital rotation. In a sideways market, buy at support, sell or trim at resistance, and rotate into the next opportunity. Ride momentum for as long as possible when a breakout happens. Cut losers, let winners run.

Step 8: Hold through the noise. The market will try to shake you out. There will be 2-5% drops that feel like the world is ending. Most people are too emotional to study the data. They keep reacting to every small fall. If you have followed steps 1-7, you can ignore the daily noise. You have a plan. Execute the plan.

Here is the final checklist I keep coming back to:

  1. Buy at supports. Always.
  2. If the market is discounted, keep buying for as long as possible.
  3. During times of uncertainty, equity does badly – but that is when you make serious money.
  4. If the valuation is not crazy high, you should buy.
  5. Have a plan to handle drawdowns – use loss targeting, average at supports, keep losses in check.
  6. Keep a check on earnings. The strategy only fails if there is a structural earnings decline.
  7. Give yourself at least 2-3 years. On that basis, this money is very likely to give more than 13% CAGR.
  8. Stop tracking your portfolio daily. When there is a war going on, even if you are the best warrior, things on the battlefield always look ugly.

The discipline of entry is not glamorous. Nobody will praise you on social media for waiting six months to buy a stock at the right price. Nobody writes headlines about the investor who sat in cash while the market was running, then deployed at supports when everyone else was panicking.

But that investor makes money. Consistently. Across cycles. In bull markets and bear markets and the long, grinding sideways stretches that make up most of market history.

There are three reasons stock prices move: fundamentals, technicals, and sentiment. Indian markets are being deeply driven by sentiment right now. Excessive retail participation, excessive DII support, and very little FII investment. This creates both pros and cons. But it means that the investors who stay disciplined – who buy at supports, who build in tranches, who hold through the panic and sell at resistance – will outperform dramatically over any multi-year period.
