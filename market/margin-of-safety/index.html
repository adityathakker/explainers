I.

Why does entry price matter more than the stock you pick?

Almost every retail-finance channel spends 90% of its airtime telling you what to buy. The remaining 10% is reserved for when to sell. Almost no one teaches the discipline of entry — the exact price, the technical setup, the precise moment you commit capital. That gap is where most retail investors bleed.

Here is the claim, stated plainly: the price at which you buy a stock matters more than which stock you buy. Counterintuitive, true, expensive to ignore.

I have watched this pattern repeat hundreds of times. Someone buys HDFC Bank — a fundamentally excellent business, no debate. They buy because a friend mentioned it, or a bullish article ran, or they had cash burning a hole. They buy near a local top, when the RSI is screaming overbought and the price is kissing the upper Bollinger Band. Eight months of sideways consolidation later, they are sitting on a 10–15% drawdown wondering what went wrong. The thesis was right. The price was wrong.

It happened to me on HDFC, in fact. We eventually made money, but those first few months were painful because I bought too much too early after a big rally. The stock chopped sideways while my capital did nothing. The fix — once burned — was a system: entry price is not a detail; it is the decision.

Picture two investors buying Nvidia. Investor A buys at the peak, full of AI excitement. Investor B waits for a 17% pullback, buys at the middle Bollinger Band, builds the position in tranches. Same company. Same thesis. But Investor B has a built-in 17% margin of safety before day one. If the stock falls another 10%, B is down 10% from a discounted base. A is down 27% and questioning every life decision that led to opening a brokerage account.

The margin of safety — the gap between what an asset is worth and what you pay for it — is not a luxury. It is the single biggest determinant of whether your investment compounds or destroys capital. Even the best stocks in the world will ruin your returns if you buy them at the wrong price[1].

Compounding makes this brutal. A disciplined entry that adds 2–3% of alpha per year sounds like rounding error — until you let it run for thirty or forty years. That little annual edge roughly doubles your terminal portfolio versus the investor who bought at random prices. The math is unforgiving in both directions.

The rest of this piece is the system. Not which stocks to buy — you already know the good ones: Meta, Amazon, Visa, HDFC Bank, Kotak, the hospital chains, the top-three FMCG names. The skill that separates profitable investors from people who only talk about investing is knowing when to buy them. The tools are not complicated — intrinsic value, Bollinger Bands, support and resistance, moving averages, tranche-sizing. The discipline of stringing them together is everything.

II.

What is a stock actually worth — and how do you compute it?

Before you can talk about margin of safety, you need a number to be safe from. That number is intrinsic value — what the business is worth on its cash-flow merits, independent of what the ticker is doing this week.

The clean way to estimate it is a discounted cash flow model. You project the free cash flow the business will throw off each year, discount each year's cash back to today using a rate that reflects its risk, and add a "terminal value" representing every year of cash flow beyond your forecast horizon. The formula is uglier than it is hard:

IV = ∑t=1..N FCFt ÷ (1 + r)t   +   TV ÷ (1 + r)N
TV = FCFN · (1 + g) ÷ (r − g) FCFt = free cash flow in year t; r = discount rate (WACC); g = terminal growth; N = forecast horizon

Read that as: add up the present value of every future dollar of cash the business will generate, including a perpetuity at the end. The fair stock price is that sum divided by shares outstanding[2].

You don't need to derive it. You need to feel which lever moves the answer the most. Spend two minutes pushing the sliders below — the bar chart shows the present value of each year's cash, with the terminal-value bar at the end in a different colour. Push the discount rate up and watch the right side of the chart shrink. Push terminal growth up and watch it explode.

Well, actually 🧐 — the terminal value can dominate too much

If 70–85% of your intrinsic value is sitting in the terminal bar, your DCF is really a bet on perpetuity assumptions, not on the next decade of execution. That's why analysts spend so much time on g (terminal growth) and r − g (the denominator of the perpetuity). A 1% bump in g with r held constant can swing intrinsic value 20%.

The discipline: never let g exceed long-run nominal GDP growth (~4–5% for the US, ~6–8% for India). A business cannot, in the limit, grow faster than the economy it sits inside — otherwise it eventually is the economy.

DCF is not how I price every position day-to-day. It is how I anchor the conversation. If the market price is materially below my intrinsic value — say, 25–40% lower — then technical signals become an entry timing tool, not a green-light/red-light judgement on the business. If the market price is at or above my intrinsic value, I do not care how oversold the RSI is. I do not buy expensive things just because they got slightly less expensive.

III.

What does a Bollinger Band actually mean?

Open any charting tool and overlay Bollinger Bands on any stock you follow. You will see three lines: an upper band, a middle band, and a lower band. The middle line is the 20-day simple moving average. The upper and lower bands sit two standard deviations above and below that average.

That last sentence is the whole concept. The 20-day mean is the local centre of gravity. The ±2σ envelope captures roughly 95% of trading days under a normal-returns assumption. So when price pierces the upper band, the stock is statistically running hot. When it touches the lower band, it is statistically oversold.

Upper = SMA20 + 2σ20  ·  Middle = SMA20  ·  Lower = SMA20 − 2σ20 where σ20 is the rolling 20-day standard deviation of close prices

In plain English, three regimes:

  • Upper band touch — the stock is hot relative to its recent range. Odds favour a pullback or at least a pause. Not a sell signal in itself; a "don't add" signal.
  • Middle (mean) — equilibrium. A defensible entry if your thesis is medium-term and the fundamental case is intact.
  • Lower band touch — statistically oversold. If fundamentals are unchanged, this is where the bargains live.

Well, actually 🧐 — returns aren't actually normal

Bollinger's framework assumes price changes follow a roughly normal distribution. They don't — equity returns are leptokurtic (fat-tailed) and negatively skewed. The market crashes harder than a Gaussian predicts and bounces more dramatically than one allows. A "3σ event" in equities happens far more often than 0.3% of the time.

Practical consequence: a Bollinger Band touch on the way down is sometimes the start of a real fall, not an oversold reversion. That is why the rule is to combine Bollinger Bands with at least one independent confirmation — a horizontal support, a 200 DMA bounce, a divergence in RSI — before sizing up.

Here is how I actually used this on Nvidia. NVDA was 17% off its peak and trading near the middle Bollinger Band. My allocation cap was 10% of portfolio; I was at 4%. Mean-band re-entry made sense for a small tranche — up to 5%. If it kept falling and reached the lower band, 200 DMA, or a horizontal support I had drawn earlier, the next tranche pushes me toward 7–7.5%. The final tranche to 10% only comes at a confluence event — an oversold RSI inside the lower band on top of historic support. This could take months. That is the point.

Community messages started flowing in: "I bought Nvidia Friday and it fell 2–3% by Monday." My response was always the same. If you cannot handle 10–20% intra-position drawdowns and you have no system for them, stock investing is the wrong game. Stocks fall. The system accounts for it.

IV.

Where does the market draw its support and resistance lines?

If Bollinger Bands tell you whether a stock is statistically cheap or expensive relative to itself, support and resistance levels tell you where the market has drawn its lines in the sand.

A support is a price where buyers have historically stepped in aggressively. Every time the stock fell to it, demand materialised and the price bounced. Resistance is the mirror — a ceiling where sellers consistently emerge and the price struggles to break through. These aren't arbitrary lines; they encode the collective memory of every trader who has held the stock at that level. When Amazon spends two years bouncing in a channel between two prices, that channel is real, backed by trillions of dollars of consensus.

Now do the math on a name that is sitting inside a clean range. Suppose the channel is 10–12% wide and the stock taps both walls roughly twice per year:

  • Buy at support, trim at resistance: 10% per cycle
  • Two cycles per year: 20% from price action alone
  • Layer covered calls at resistance and cash-secured puts at support on half the position: another 5% of annualised premium
  • Total: ~25% per year on a stock the market thinks is "boring"

This is capital rotation. It is how you make money in markets that aren't giving you a screaming bull run. It is also why I added Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, and Google aggressively during one consolidation stretch — the channels were clean, the touches were regular, and every visit to support was the market handing you a gift.

There is a related concept worth naming: psychological supports. Round numbers — 25,000 on Nifty, $100 on a popular tech stock, $50,000 on Bitcoin — become pivotal levels in the market's collective mind. If a market breaks below a psychological support, it can correct sharply, but it will repeatedly try to revert above it. Anything below a strong psychological support is, by default, a long-term buy zone. Anything that holds above one turns the level into a structural floor.

Most names are not, in fact, in clean channels. Most names are above intrinsic value and above support most of the time. V.

Why is the 200 DMA the most important line on the chart?

Moving averages are the backbone of trend-following. Three windows are worth tracking:

  • 50 DMA — the short-term trend, ten weeks of price action.
  • 150 DMA — the medium-term trend.
  • 200 DMA — the long-term trend, roughly ten months of price. The single most important line for a buy-and-hold investor.

When a stock or index is trading below its 200 DMA, it is in a long-term downtrend. For a buy-and-hold investor in a quality name, that is a bulk buying zone — not a sell signal. Names rarely stay below their 200 DMA forever; the act of selling has already happened by the time the price gets there.

Two patterns worth naming:

  • Death cross — the 50 DMA crosses below the 150 (or 200) DMA. Indicates a regime change to a downtrend. December 19, 2024 printed one on the Indian indices and the drawdown that followed lasted months.
  • Golden cross — the 50 DMA crosses back above the 150 DMA, both rising. Indicates trend reversal to an uptrend. Aggressive swing traders wait for this before sizing up; long-term investors don't need to.

Here is the counterintuitive part most people miss: the death cross is not a sell signal for a long-term holder. It is a signal to prepare to buy. The downtrend creates the very conditions under which future returns are generated. Unless you buy the downtrend, you cannot make money on the eventual uptrend. This is the single most uncomfortable idea in investing and it is also the most profitable one.

The reverse pattern — "200 DMA bounce" — is one of the cleanest setups in trend-following. Price falls toward the 200 DMA from above, taps or briefly pierces it, then bounces back above. Well, actually 🧐 — the 200 DMA only works in trending markets

The 200 DMA bounce is a real pattern, but it is a conditional pattern. It works beautifully when the underlying drift is positive — i.e., the market is in a secular bull, just experiencing a correction. It works badly inside a structural bear (think 2000–2002 NASDAQ), because each "bounce" gets killed by the next leg down.

Push the annual-drift slider to −5% in the widget above and watch the win rate collapse. The signal is not magic — it is a particular way of betting on mean reversion. Mean reversion fails when the mean itself is falling.

The mechanism: when a quality name kisses its 200 DMA, every long-term holder who bought above it is now sitting at break-even or modestly underwater. Selling pressure exhausts itself. Quietly, the institutions that have been waiting for a discount step in. Volume picks up. The next move is up. That is the bounce.

VI.

How should you stage the buying once a level prints?

Knowing the right technical level is half the battle. Knowing how much to buy and how to stage the purchases is the other half. This is position-building, and it is the difference between a good investor and a great one.

Rule 1 — Never exceed 5% on a single stock. Indices and ETFs can carry more; a single name should cap at 5%, with the very highest-conviction positions stretching to 10%. The Nvidia cap was 10%, and it took months of staged buys to get there.

Rule 2 — Always at least 2–3 tranches. Never deploy a full allocation in one trade. Split it into thirds at a minimum. Anchor each tranche to a technical level: mean band, lower band, 200 DMA, horizontal support. This is the absolute basic.

Rule 3 — Manage the invested-vs-cash ratio explicitly. Run a target. In India today, mine is 90/10. For investors with a 3–5 year horizon, 80/20 is healthier — 80% deployed, 20% dry powder reserved for major supports. The 20% rule is the most useful thing you can do. On a 10 lakh portfolio, that is a 2 lakh "opportunity bucket" you do not touch until a real discount appears.

Rule 4 — Loss-targeting on the portfolio. Suppose the portfolio falls from 100 to 85 — a 15% loss. You deploy fresh capital at the next confirmed support. The effective loss compresses to ~10%. You keep averaging at supports, keeping losses in check, until the market reverts. The only way this strategy actually fails is a structural earnings decline — which is rare for diversified portfolios of quality businesses.

Rule 5 — Cap the opportunity bucket per event. Don't use more than 30% of your dry powder at the first support of a panic. Save the rest for deeper levels. There is almost always a deeper level.

A concrete example: during the early-2026 correction (gold −10%, silver −30%, Palantir −30%, SoFi −30%, Bitcoin −45%), I told the community this was a good pullback to buy. Quality names — Amazon, Microsoft, Meta — had real headroom. That week became my highest buying week in three-plus years. The portfolio ended ~60% deployed, most of it bought into a downtrend. The live track record showed a 16% alpha versus NASDAQ — beating roughly 99% of fund managers. The remaining 40% is reserved for the next setup. The system is the reason.

Never exceed 5% on one stock. Put the money in at least three buckets. Buy at support. That is the absolute minimum. The absolute basic of position building. Learn at least these.

VII.

Why does buying uncertainty work when buying euphoria doesn't?

The hardest lesson in investing, and the one that separates people who talk about making money from people who actually do: you only make serious money buying equity during uncertainty.

It looks dangerous. It is not. Markets always cut their previous peak — not a hope, a fact backed by decades of data. The Indian markets went 6x from 1999 to 2008. They went 2x from 2010 to 2019. Take a decadal view of any major index and the direction is up. The problem is that uncertainty feels terrible. When the market is bleeding, your portfolio is bleeding too. Every news anchor is running doomsday graphics, Goldman is raising the recession-probability number, the rupee is sliding, and every instinct in your body is screaming sell.

That is precisely the moment you buy. Not because you're a contrarian for its own sake — but because the math of long-horizon equity returns demands that you acquire most of your shares while everyone else is panicking and discounting them for you.

A concrete example from the tariff panic: media was in full fear mode, almost all growth assets were being marked down, and the community was nervous. My read: we were nowhere near euphoria, US rate cuts were coming, midterm-cycle politics would push policy market-friendly, and Meta, Amazon, Netflix and the software stack were at meaningful discounts. Yes, another 10% drop could land any day. There was also headroom for a 20–25% run-up. The asymmetry was clearly in favour of buying.

When the breakout eventually came, it confirmed the read. There is a well-known statistic — if you miss the ten best market days in a decade, you forfeit roughly half your returns[3]. The breakout day was one of those days. People who bought during the uncertainty were rewarded. People who sold in panic missed it entirely. There is no way to time the bounce, but there is a way to be already positioned for it.

One more principle worth internalising: the market does not create a structural scare on the same news twice. On the first COVID dip in March 2020, the market corrected aggressively. On the second COVID wave, it barely flinched. Like humans, markets learn. If there is a known path to deal with a problem, the problem produces only a brief shock.

Patience here is not passivity. Patience combined with a buying system at supports is the most aggressive form of investing there is. It means you have the discipline to wait for your price while everyone else is paying a premium for the privilege of acting impulsively. When the opportunity comes, you deploy big. We sat almost completely inactive for nearly a year through one Indian-market stretch. The restless lost money. We didn't. When we finally deployed, we bought private banks at deeply undervalued levels — right before every fund manager started chasing the same names.

If you remember one thing

Buy the downtrend. If you don't have the guts when the market is down, you will not make the money when the market goes up. The two are the same trade, viewed from opposite ends of the cycle.

VIII.

How do you make money in a sideways market?

Most people assume you need a roaring bull market to make money in stocks. Completely wrong. Some of the best returns come from capital rotation in a sideways market — buying at support, trimming at resistance, and recycling the capital.

My long-term thesis for India during one stretch was that the market would chop sideways for 18–24 months. People who don't rotate suffer in that environment. People who understand support and resistance treat it as a goldmine. The mechanic is simple:

  1. Buy at support; wait for the move to resistance.
  2. Book partial profits at resistance.
  3. Redeploy that capital into another quality name that is now at its own support.
  4. Repeat.

A concrete number from one Diwali-window trade: I sized a Nifty 50 block at 8 crore. In one month it gave 2.24% — about 16 lakh of P&L. That works out to an annualised return of 25%+ on the deployed capital. Then I liquidated 20% of the position and rotated the gain into riskier names. The 16 lakh becomes the risk capital — speculation with profits, never with principal.

The math is unflattering for traders chasing 30%+ on small capital. Safe, repeatable returns on large capital beat speculative returns on small capital, every time. A 15% rotation on 10x capital is dollar-for-dollar three times better than a 30% speculative bet on 1x capital — with a fraction of the risk.

Hands-on examples that came up in the community: when Aavas ran 20% in a month I told the group to book ~15–20% of the position into the resistance — trim, carry the rest. When Asian Paints' breakout sustained, the upside was a clean 10–15% from a long base. When Bajaj Finance broke out of four years of consolidation, the playbook was to ride the move for as long as possible. Conversely, when Indigo Paints and Asian Paints ran into overbought RSI in consumption (a slow-growth industry), I trimmed ~40% on both and rotated into faster-growing sectors. The rule is the same in all directions: take what the market gives you, and don't be greedy at obvious resistance.

IX.

What is the full system, and what do you do tomorrow?

Stack everything from the previous chapters and you get an executable system. Eight steps, in order.

Step 1 — Pick what to buy on fundamentals. Revenues growing. Margins stable or expanding. Reasonable balance sheet. Business you'd be happy to own for five years even if the ticker went dark. Bonus if the stock is also sentimentally weak — under attack in the press, ignored by retail — while still fundamentally sound. That trifecta (fundamentally strong, sentimentally weak, technically approaching support) is the cleanest setup there is.

Step 2 — Anchor with intrinsic value. Run a DCF (Section II). Note the IV/share. If price is at or above IV, you're not buying today — wait. If price is meaningfully below IV (25%+ discount), move to Step 3.

Step 3 — Check Bollinger Bands and RSI. Upper band + RSI > 70 = overbought, don't buy. Mean band + RSI 40–60 = moderate first tranche. Lower band + RSI < 30 + fundamentals intact = aggressive add.

Step 4 — Identify support and resistance. Draw the channel. Mark at least 2–3 supports below the current price. Compute distance-to-resistance over distance-to-next-support; anything where upside meaningfully exceeds downside is actionable.

Step 5 — Check the moving averages. Is price above or below the 200 DMA? Below = bulk buying zone for quality. Is there a death cross? Is a golden cross forming? Swing traders wait for the golden cross; long-term investors do not need to.

Step 6 — Build in tranches. 33% at the current support, 33% reserved for the next support, 33% reserved for extreme pullback or confirmed breakout. Might take months. That is the system.

Step 7 — Maintain the 20% cash reserve. Replenish after every profitable exit. Never let opportunity-bucket capital fall below 15% of total portfolio.

Step 8 — Hold through the noise. The market will try to shake you out. There will be 2–5% drops that feel terminal. If you've followed steps 1–7, you have a plan. Execute the plan.

The action card I keep coming back to:

  1. Buy at supports. Always.
  2. If the market is discounted, keep buying.
  3. Equity does badly during uncertainty — that is when serious money is made.
  4. If valuations aren't insane, you should be buying.
  5. Plan for drawdowns. Use loss-targeting. Average at supports. Cap losses.
  6. Monitor earnings — the system only fails on structural earnings decline.
  7. Give every position 2–3 years minimum. Over that horizon a disciplined book targets >13% CAGR.
  8. Stop checking the portfolio daily. Battlefields always look ugly from inside the trench.

The discipline of entry is not glamorous. Nobody will praise you on social media for waiting six months to buy a stock at the right price. Nobody writes headlines about the investor who sat in cash while the market ran — then deployed at supports while everyone else panicked. But that investor makes money. Consistently. Across cycles. In bull markets, bear markets, and the long sideways grinds that make up most of market history.

Three forces move stock prices: fundamentals, technicals, sentiment. Indian markets in particular are heavily driven by sentiment right now — excessive retail flow, heavy DII support, very little FII. That creates noise but also creates opportunity for investors who stay structured. Buy at supports. Build in tranches. Hold through the panic. Sell at resistance. Give the system time to work.

The price at which you buy is not a detail to figure out later. It is the first and most important decision you make. Get it right and even an average portfolio compounds exceptionally. Get it wrong and even the best stocks in the world will leave you underwater, frustrated, and quietly questioning every life decision that led you to open a brokerage account.

That gap — between intrinsic value and price — is the margin of safety. That is where all the money is made.
