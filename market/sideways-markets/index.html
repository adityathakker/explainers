I.

Range trading, covered calls, and the cash deployment playbook

Most investors know only two emotions: euphoria when markets go up, and panic when markets go down. But there is a third state that the market spends an enormous amount of time in – and almost nobody is trained for it. That state is sideways.

Markets go nowhere. The Nifty 50 oscillates between 22,000 and 26,500. The S&P 500 churns within a 15-20% band. Your portfolio value at the end of the year is roughly where it was at the beginning. Your SIPs keep running, your money keeps flowing in, and your returns are functionally zero.

This is not an anomaly. This is the normal condition of a consolidating market. And right now, as of early 2026, this is exactly where we are – both in India and in the US. Excessive debt, slowing GDP growth, tariff wars, and central banks trying to thread the needle between inflation control and liquidity maintenance have created the perfect recipe for a rangebound market that could last 12 to 18 months or longer.

Shankar Sharma has predicted 0% stock market returns for the next five years. Whether that exact prediction comes true or not is irrelevant. What matters is this: if you only know how to make money in a bull market, you are going to have a very frustrating few years. And if you learn to make money in a sideways market, you will outperform 90% of investors who are sitting on their hands waiting for the “next rally.”

This article is about that skill. The skill of making money when markets go absolutely nowhere.

II.

I. What a Sideways Market Actually Is

Let us be precise about definitions, because sloppy thinking about market states leads to sloppy investing decisions.

A sideways market – also called a consolidation or rangebound market – is a market that oscillates between a defined support level and a defined resistance level without making a sustained breakout in either direction. The key word is “sustained.” A sideways market can have sharp 5-7% moves in either direction. It can have days that feel like a crash and weeks that feel like a bull run. But zoom out to the monthly chart, and you will see price bouncing between two horizontal lines like a ball trapped in a corridor.

There are three types of market declines you need to understand, because each produces a different kind of sideways:

Type 1: Sharp Parabolic Fall. This is COVID-2020, the 2008 crisis. Completely unexpected, violently fast, and usually followed by a V-shaped recovery. You rarely get a long sideways market after this. The crash happens, the recovery happens, and within 6-12 months you are back to making new highs.

Type 2: Valuation Correction. This is what Indian mid and small caps went through in late 2024 and 2025. Prices got too expensive relative to earnings. The market corrects by going sideways or grinding lower until valuations become reasonable again. This can easily last 2-3 years. Time does the work that price cannot.

Type 3: Slow Decay. This is an economic growth forecast problem. There is too much debt in the system, governments need to reduce it, so they spend less, and that reduced spending eventually reflects in poor corporate earnings growth. The market does not crash – it just drifts sideways with wide swings.

My assessment right now is that we are in a Type 3 environment. The US is repaying $1 trillion in debt this year. If they borrow more, their yearly debt service goes up. The only ways to control this are to tax more or spend less – both of which decrease liquidity. India, as an emerging market, benefits in growth phases but struggles in consolidation phases. We are in the consolidation phase.

An economy goes through predictable cycles: crash, increased liquidity through money printing and government spending, consolidation, euphoria and run-up, and then crash again. After the 2020 crash and the 2021-2022 liquidity expansion, the world is firmly in the consolidation phase. The excess money printing created excessive debt, and now that debt needs to be worked off. This takes time. And during that time, markets go sideways.

The markets are going to go sideways and consolidate in a range. I am betting on a sideways market with 20% rangebound movement for over 1.5 years. And designing my portfolio accordingly.

This is not a guess. This is a structural read based on the debt cycle, the liquidity cycle, and the interest rate trajectory. Interest rates in the US have been lowered from 5.5% at their peak to 4.5% now, with expectations of reaching 3.5% by year end. But until those rate cuts fully transmit into the real economy, the market has no catalyst for a sustained breakout.

Let me walk you through the macro scorecard as it stands:

  • Tariff news: largely factored in (positive)
  • Growth forecast: slower – 1.7% GDP growth in the US is “slow” by any standard (negative)
  • Short-term interest rate cuts: unlikely (negative)
  • Medium-term rate cuts: expected by next year (positive)
  • Inflation: by and large under control, though tariff-related supply disruptions could push it higher (neutral)
  • US Fed liquidity policy: slowing down the selling of securities, which means the Fed wants to maintain liquidity in the market (positive for stability)

Add it up and you get a picture that is neither bullish nor bearish. It is sideways. The floor is held up by liquidity maintenance and eventual rate cuts. The ceiling is capped by slow growth, high debt, and persistent inflation fears. In between, the market oscillates. And that oscillation is where we make money.

III.

II. The Anatomy of Support and Resistance

If you are going to trade a range, you need to understand the two lines that define it. These are the most fundamental concepts in technical analysis, and I am going to explain them in a way that actually helps you make money rather than just pass an exam.

Support is a price level where buying pressure is strong enough to prevent the price from falling further. Think of it as a floor. Every time the stock or index falls to this level, buyers step in, demand absorbs the selling, and the price bounces. The more times a support level is tested and holds, the stronger it becomes.

Resistance is a price level where selling pressure is strong enough to prevent the price from rising further. Think of it as a ceiling. Every time the price rallies to this level, sellers step in, supply overwhelms demand, and the price drops. Again, the more times it is tested without breaking, the stronger it becomes.

In the current Indian market context, the Nifty 50 has a well-defined range. The first major support sits around 22,500. Below that, the second support is at approximately 21,900. On the upside, resistance is in the 25,500-26,500 zone. The total range is roughly 4,000-4,500 points, which translates to about 18-20% from bottom to top.

Now here is the concept that separates amateurs from professionals: psychological support levels. These are round-number pivotal levels – like 25,000 on the Nifty – that carry outsized importance because of how humans think about numbers. When I wrote about this to the community, I explained it in two parts:

  1. If the market breaks this psychological level, it can sharply correct. But it will revert to this base level at every chance it gets. So anything below this is a great buying point for the long term.
  2. If the market does not break this level and stays above it, the level transforms from ordinary support into a powerful psychological support.

This matters for your execution. When the Nifty is at 22,500 and the psychological support at 25,000 has already been broken, you know two things: first, you are getting the market roughly 14% cheap from recent highs. That is a solid margin of safety. Second, there is likely to be a magnetic pull back towards that psychological level over the next 3-6 months, which gives you a defined upside target.

Let me give you a real example. When the Nifty was trading around 25,000, I told the community: deploy 25% of your bulk buy from cash positions at this level and stay put. On a 2-3 year basis, money deployed at that level is very likely to give more than 13% CAGR. And generating a 13% CAGR with new money today is genuinely tough, because for the next big rally we need more liquidity, and that might take time to materialize. The support gave us a defined entry, and the expected reversion to the mean gave us a defined exit thesis.

In any sideways market, you also need to understand channel patterns. A channel is drawn with two parallel lines – one connecting the support levels and one connecting the resistance levels. When you see price bouncing between these lines on a weekly or monthly chart, you have a tradeable channel. The size of candles at the extremes tells you about momentum: large candles at support mean strong buying, small candles (called Doji patterns) at resistance mean indecisiveness – the market is running out of steam.

Understanding such simple strategies leads to 2-3% alpha. And on a 30-40 year basis, this is DOUBLING of your portfolio.

IV.

III. The Capital Rotation Playbook for Sideways Markets

Here is where the money is actually made. In a sideways market, buy-and-hold is dead. SIPs are suboptimal. The strategy that works is capital rotation – buying at support, selling at resistance, and redeploying the freed-up capital into the next opportunity.

Let me break this down with real math from a stock I have been tracking.

Take a large-cap stock – let us use Amazon as the example. Since its last major trend change in 2023, the stock has been moving in a well-defined channel. The range of that channel is 10-12%. On average, the stock touches support and resistance about 2 times per year.

If you successfully buy at support and sell at resistance, that is 10% times 2 = 20% minimum per year. In a market that is going “nowhere,” you are generating 20% returns simply by respecting the channel.

Now add options on top. Sell a covered call at resistance on a portion of your holding – say 50% of your position. Sell a put option at support. The premiums from these options trades add roughly another 10% per year on that 50% allocation. That translates to an additional 5% on your total holding. Total potential: 25% per year. On a stock in a “sideways” market.

This is why I have added Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, and Google quite aggressively. These are not moonshot bets. These are channel-trading opportunities with options enhancement. The range is defined. The support and resistance levels are clear. And the options market gives you additional income on top of the capital gains from trading the range.

The same principle applies to the Indian market. Take the Nifty 50 range I described earlier: 21,500 to 26,500. That is roughly a 23% range from bottom to top. If you deploy capital at 22,000 and the index reverts to 25,000-26,000 over the next 3-6 months, you have made 14-18% on that deployment. If you then take some profits at the upper end and wait for the next dip to support, you can repeat the cycle.

Consider a concrete example from the community. Aavas, a small-cap stock, ran up 20% in a single month. Small-caps move fast. But when fast run-ups happen, you should book around 15-20% profits, because it has hit a resistance point. You carry forward with the rest of the position. That booked profit becomes cash – ammunition for the next support-level buy. This is the rotation engine in action.

Or take NIFTY IT. We picked it at extremely negative sentiments. The community was already in profits. At that point, the correct move was not to add more, but to expect a range to form and trade that range. In a sideways market, the discipline is not in the buying – it is in the selling. You must be willing to let go of positions at resistance, even when the stock feels like it still has momentum.

Anyone can do this. This is the reason why I keep saying: Indian market right now is NOT buy and hold. It makes sense to rotate capital.

The key execution rules for capital rotation in a sideways market are:

  1. Never go all-in at once. Buy in at least 2-3 tranches. If support is at 22,000, put your first tranche in at 22,500, your second at 22,000, and hold your third in reserve for a potential break below support.

  2. No single stock should exceed 5% of your total portfolio. Indexes and ETFs are a different category – you can go heavier on those. But individual stock positions must be size-controlled.

  3. Book 15-20% profits on fast run-ups. When a stock hits resistance or gives you a quick 15-20% gain, take some off the table. You can always carry forward with the rest of the position, but locking in partial gains is how you compound in a rangebound market.

  4. Keep 20-25% cash at all times. This is your ammunition for the next dip to support. The biggest mistake investors make in sideways markets is being fully invested at the wrong time.

V.

IV. The Art of Buying at Support -- Tranches, Timing, and Discipline

Knowing that you should buy at support is the easy part. Actually doing it – with real money, when the market is falling and fear is everywhere – is the hard part. Let me give you the exact framework I use.

Step 1: Identify the support level. This comes from drawing channels on weekly and monthly charts. Look at where price has bounced at least 2-3 times. The more touches, the stronger the support. For the Nifty 50 right now, the first support is around 22,500 and the second support is around 21,900.

Step 2: Decide your total allocation. Let us say you have 10 lakhs of cash earmarked for deployment. Do not put all 10 lakhs in at once.

Step 3: Deploy in tranches. Split your allocation into at least 3 buckets of roughly 33% each.

  • Tranche 1 (33%): Deploy when the market reaches the first support level. This is your “high conviction” buy. The market has already corrected and you are buying at a defined level.
  • Tranche 2 (33%): Deploy if the market falls further, ideally at the second support level. This averages down your cost significantly.
  • Tranche 3 (33%): Hold in reserve. This is your insurance against the “breakdown scenario” – the small probability event where support is broken entirely.

Step 4: Choose what to buy. In a panicked market, buy the safest possible assets. That means the Nifty 50 or Sensex through an ETF or index fund. You do not need to experiment with individual small-cap stock picks when the market is in fear mode. The index gives you broad exposure and ensures you benefit when the recovery comes.

In times of panic, follow the basics. Buy at supports. Just simply buy Nifty 50 if you want lower risk. You do not need to experiment in a panicked market.

Step 5: Wait. This is the hardest part. After deploying your first tranche, the market might continue falling. You will feel like an idiot. Your unrealized P&L will be red. News channels will be screaming about recession. Your friends will tell you the market is going to zero.

Ignore all of it. You bought at a defined support level with only one-third of your allocated capital. You have two more tranches ready. Your downside is limited and your upside – when the market reverts to the upper end of the range over the next 3-6 months – is substantial.

On a 2-3 year basis, money deployed at these support levels is very likely to give you more than 13% CAGR. And generating a 13% CAGR with new money in today’s market is genuinely difficult, because for the next big rally we need more liquidity, and that takes time to materialize.

In Week 6, it dips below to the second support zone – that is your Tranche 2 entry. From Week 7 onwards, the market begins reverting toward the upper half of the range, and your deployed capital starts working.

VI.

V. Covered Calls and Puts -- The Options Income Layer

This section is for intermediate and advanced investors. If you have never traded options before, you can skip ahead to Section VI – but I strongly recommend learning this, because options income is the difference between a 15% year and a 25% year in a sideways market.

The basic concept is simple. In a sideways market, there is a high probability that a stock will NOT break above resistance or below support in any given month. Options traders can monetize this probability by selling options at those levels and collecting premiums.

Covered Calls: Selling at the Ceiling

A covered call means you own the stock (or index ETF) and you sell a call option at the resistance level. Here is how it works step by step:

  1. You own 100 shares of Amazon, bought at $180 (near support).
  2. You sell a call option with a strike price at $200 (near resistance), expiring in 30-45 days.
  3. You collect a premium – let us say $4 per share, or $400 total.
  4. Scenario A: Amazon stays below $200. The option expires worthless. You keep the $400 premium AND your 100 shares. You can sell another call next month.
  5. Scenario B: Amazon rises above $200. Your shares get “called away” – sold at $200. You made $20 per share in capital gains ($2,000) PLUS the $400 premium ($2,400 total). You hit resistance anyway, so you would have been selling here regardless.

In a sideways market, Scenario A happens most of the time. You are essentially collecting rent on your stock position every single month. Over a year, this premium income can add 8-12% to your returns on the shares you sell calls against.

Cash-Secured Puts: Buying the Floor

A cash-secured put means you sell a put option at the support level while holding enough cash to buy the shares if assigned. Here is the logic:

  1. Amazon is currently trading at $190. Support is at $175.
  2. You sell a put option with a strike price of $175, expiring in 30-45 days.
  3. You collect a premium – say $3 per share.
  4. Scenario A: Amazon stays above $175. The option expires worthless. You keep the premium. You wanted to buy at support anyway, and the market did not give you the chance – but you still made money.
  5. Scenario B: Amazon drops to $175 or below. You are assigned and buy the shares at $175. This is exactly the price you wanted to buy at. And your effective cost basis is $175 minus the $3 premium = $172.

Do you see the beauty of this? In a sideways market, you are getting paid to wait for the price you already wanted. You either collect income from the puts expiring worthless, or you get to buy the stock at your ideal entry price at an even lower effective cost.

Combining Both Strategies

Now here is the full picture, and this is what generates that 25% annual potential I described earlier:

  • You hold a core position in a large-cap stock (bought at support)
  • You sell covered calls on 50% of your position at resistance
  • You sell cash-secured puts at support when you have dry powder

The capital gains from trading the range give you 10-12% (two round trips per year at 5-6% each). The options premiums on 50% of your position add another 5-8%. The cash-secured put premiums on your reserve cash add another 2-3%.

Total: 17-23% per year. In a market going “nowhere.”

This is exactly the math on stocks like Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, and Google. These companies have massive, liquid options markets. Their channels are well-defined. And the premiums are rich enough to make this strategy genuinely profitable.

VII.

VI. What to Buy in a Sideways Market

Not everything works in a sideways market. The stock selection criteria change dramatically compared to a bull market. Here is how to think about it.

The Index is Your Safest Bet

When there are a lot of uncertainties, buy the safest possible assets. In the Indian market, this is Nifty 50 or Sensex through an ETF. In the US market, this is QQQ or SPY. The index gives you automatic diversification, eliminates single-stock risk, and ensures you participate in any recovery without having to pick the exact right stock.

Between QQQ (US index) and the Indian index, I would pick the US index for now. Why? Multiple reasons: oil crisis is likely to hit the US less, more stable currency, and more earnings stability. The US has structural advantages in a consolidation phase that India does not.

Large-Cap Quality Stocks at Support

If you want individual stocks, stick to the highest quality names and buy them ONLY at support levels. Here are five names I have identified for 2026:

  1. Meta – dominant AI infrastructure play, 41% operating margins
  2. Amazon – cloud computing and e-commerce duopoly, clear 10-12% channel
  3. Visa – global payment rails, steady compounder
  4. MercadoLibre (MELI) – Latin American e-commerce leader, massive TAM
  5. Top Indian hospitals (top 3-4 by market cap) – domestic consumption play

The common thread: these are businesses with structural earnings growth, liquid options markets for income enhancement, and well-defined trading ranges.

Sectors to Watch in India

  • Private Banks (HDFC Bank, ICICI, SBI): Still undervalued relative to their earnings power. HDFC Bank is sitting on its best profits and revenues, but money has not flowed into the stock yet. When FIIs return and SIP money has no other option but to get invested, banks will move.
  • IT: NIFTY IT broke into bullish momentum after we picked it at extremely negative sentiments. But do not chase it now – wait for the range to form, then trade that range.
  • Healthcare/Pharma: Good long-term bets. If you can avoid short-term noise and stay invested, you are likely to make money.

What to Avoid

  • Mid and small caps at current valuations. In India, small and mid-cap valuations are still rich. There is still a good chance for more correction. Wait for these to come down to sensible valuations before deploying serious capital. There has been so much negativity on small and mid-caps that even MF managers are fighting with each other about the category. This tells you everything about the current state of confusion.
  • Stocks trading at extreme PEs without earnings growth. Tesla is a prime example – the stock already trades at a higher PE, the recent numbers have not been great, and there is key-man risk with Elon. If you enter in a bad cycle, you could lose a lot of money. Be picky about entering such stocks.
  • SIPs in the traditional sense. In the current volatile, sideways environment, I do not see much value in mechanical SIPs. Given how much the markets have become volatile, trading the channel – buying at support and selling at resistance – makes more sense. Even when the market was going up, the volatility was way too high for SIPs to be the optimal strategy. SIPs work in secular bull markets. This is not one.
  • Anything where you do not understand the thesis. If you do not know exactly why you should be holding a stock, exit. Loss and wins are a part of the stock market, but confusion about your own thesis is unforgivable.

If I don’t know “why” exactly I should be holding a stock, I will exit.

One more important filter: stocks go through distinct growth phases. A stock does not go up in a straight line – it moves through phases of growth followed by periods of consolidation. If you hold a stock through 6-7 growth phases, it can become a 10x-15x stock. But this takes a long time. In a sideways market, the key question is: which phase is the stock in? If one clear phase of growth is over and the stock is entering consolidation, you have two choices – hold through the consolidation if the business fundamentals are intact, or rotate your capital into something at the beginning of its growth phase. Both are valid strategies. What is NOT valid is buying a stock that has just completed a growth phase and expecting immediate returns.

VIII.

VII. The Cash Hedge -- Why 20% Cash is Non-Negotiable

Here is a rule I follow that most retail investors find counterintuitive: in a sideways market, I always keep 20-25% of my portfolio in cash.

Not invested. Not “earning returns.” Just sitting there. Waiting.

This feels wasteful in a bull market. But in a sideways market, cash is not a drag on returns – it is a weapon. Here is why.

When markets consolidate in a wide range, there are periodic sharp dips of 5-8% that happen fast and recover fast. The investor who is 100% invested cannot take advantage of these dips. The investor with 20-25% cash can deploy aggressively at support and capture the subsequent rebound.

Let me illustrate with the scenario analysis I shared with the community:

Scenario 1: “Slow Bleed.” The market consolidates around the current range for the next few months. Returns are 0%. But if you are able to rotate capital – buying at the low end and selling at the high end – you make money. Your cash position enables this rotation.

Scenario 2: “Sharp Fall and V-Shaped Recovery.” The first support is broken, and the market falls to the second support – about 10% below current levels. This happens quickly. And then there is a V-shaped recovery. The investor with cash can deploy at the bottom and ride the recovery. The investor who was 100% invested at the top just went through a roller coaster and ended up roughly where they started.

Scenario 3: “Breakdown.” This has the least probability. It requires a completely unseen global event. But if it happens, the investor with cash can buy at generational discounts. The investor without cash can only watch.

My current allocation reflects this thinking: 95% invested in India (very safe instruments), around 30% built in the US portfolio, and new money being held as cash to see how far the sideways movement goes.

Build a simple 20-25% cash hedge to take advantage of sideways movement. This is a very complex market to invest now.

The insider data backs this up. The Insider Sell-to-Buy Ratio (STB) – which tracks how much company insiders at an aggregate level are selling versus buying – has recently peaked. Historically, when this ratio peaks, the market either crashes or goes sideways. Given the current macroeconomic environment (US elections, Chinese QE providing a floor, tariffs providing a ceiling), the sideways scenario is more likely.

People who aggregate good stocks at good prices and keep rotating capital will do very well in this environment. People who are 100% invested and hoping for a bull run will be frustrated for a long time.

There is another dimension to the cash hedge that most people miss: asymmetric opportunity capture. In a sideways market, there are periodic moments – usually driven by sentiment, not fundamentals – when high-quality stocks correct by 20% or more alongside the broader market. These are temporary dislocations. The business has not changed, but the price has. The investor with a 20% cash position can aggressively deploy into these dislocations.

For example, during the December 2025 correction, several high-quality firms corrected by roughly 20%. If you were a long-term holder with cash on the side, the process was simple: check which themes you want to own (in my case, more healthcare in India), check if the stock is trading at support (Max Healthcare was trading at support), and then build a position. How much depends on your portfolio construct, but a typical rule is 5% of your portfolio per stock, deployed in at least 2 tranches.

Without cash, you cannot execute this. You are fully invested, watching the correction with no ability to act. With a 20% cash hedge, every correction becomes a controlled deployment opportunity. Over the course of a 12-18 month sideways period, you might get 3-4 such opportunities. Capturing even two of them at the right support levels can add 5-8% to your annual returns.

IX.

VIII. The Psychology of Sideways -- Why Most Investors Fail Here

The hardest part of navigating a sideways market is not the strategy. The strategy is straightforward: buy at support, sell at resistance, keep cash, use options. A teenager could understand it.

The hard part is the psychology. And the psychology destroys most investors in sideways markets. Here is why.

Problem 1: You will feel the urge to do something. Markets go up and you want to buy more. Markets go down and you want to sell everything. In a sideways market, the correct action is often to do nothing – to sit with your position and wait for the channel to reach the other extreme. But doing nothing feels lazy. It feels unproductive. It feels like you are missing out.

You are not. The most profitable action in a sideways market is patience. Wait for the support. Wait for the resistance. Execute your plan. And then wait again.

Problem 2: You will mistake consolidation for breakdown. Every time the market dips 3-5% within the range, financial media will scream about crashes. Your friends will panic. Twitter will be full of “I told you so” bears. And you will start questioning whether the support will hold.

This is normal. On any given correction day, there need not be any specific reason for the fall. Do not waste your time looking for one. Technicals dominate in consolidation phases. The support is either going to hold or it is not. Watching CNBC will not change that outcome, but it will change your emotional state – for the worse.

Problem 3: You will anchor to past returns. If you made 40% in 2023 or 30% in 2024, you will feel entitled to similar returns in 2025 and 2026. But the market does not owe you anything. A 15-20% return in a sideways market through capital rotation is an exceptional outcome. Comparing it to bull market returns is a recipe for bad decisions.

Problem 4: You will confuse stock price movement with business quality. HDFC Bank has fallen 6% in a month while sitting on its best-ever profits and revenues. Does that mean HDFC Bank is a bad stock? Absolutely not. Stock prices move because money comes or goes out of a stock. When a business grows and gives good results, it becomes attractive for money to flow in. But until that money actually flows in, the stock price will not move up.

A business can keep posting good results but its stock price would not move. Possible? Yes. But this cannot go on indefinitely. If a business keeps posting good results, its PE keeps falling. And this range of price cannot be manipulated artificially for too long.

This is why time horizon matters enormously in a sideways market. If you buy a good business at a reasonable price and can wait 2-3 years, you will make good money. It happens almost every time. The stock market is not broken. It is just consolidating. And consolidation ends.

Problem 5: You will want to time the breakout. Everyone in a sideways market is secretly waiting for the breakout – that glorious moment when the index smashes through resistance and begins a new bull run. The temptation is to stay 100% invested so you do not “miss” it.

But here is what I have observed over decades: markets fall when you least expect them to fall. People are rejoicing that tariff news is factored in and from now on there will be a bull run. If you have been investing even for the last 2-3 years, you know this is usually the moment before the next unexpected drop. Stay disciplined. Stay in the range-trading framework. If the breakout comes, you will still participate – you just will not be leveraged to the max when it does.

Problem 6: You will second-guess your sells. This is the flip side of anchoring. You sell a stock at resistance, book 15% profit, and two weeks later the stock rallies another 5%. You feel like an idiot. You swear you will never sell too early again. And then the next time resistance is hit, you hold on – and the stock drops back to support, erasing all your gains.

This happened with real examples in the community. Tarsons was sold, and then it gave a breakout. Was that a mistake? Looking back, perhaps. But the reason for selling was to reduce risk, and the thesis around China dumping was still not clear. If I were to go back in time and change it, I would not. Because the goal of selling at resistance is not to capture every last rupee of upside. The goal is to generate consistent, repeatable returns. And consistent returns come from following your system, not from trying to be perfect on every trade.

The market does one unpredictable thing every decade. Guessing that one thing right is where generational wealth is made. But your job is not to guess the unpredictable thing. Your job is to systematically capture the predictable thing – the range, the support, the resistance, the premium. Do this consistently, and the unpredictable things become noise rather than portfolio-defining events.

X.

IX. Your Sideways Market Action Plan

Let me pull everything together into a concrete, executable plan. This is what I am doing with my own portfolio, and it is what I recommend for anyone navigating the next 12-18 months.

Step 1: Define your range. For the Nifty 50, the current range is approximately 21,500-26,500. For the S&P 500, identify the equivalent channel on a weekly chart. For individual stocks (Meta, Amazon, HDFC Bank, etc.), draw the channel using at least 6 months of price data.

Step 2: Set your cash allocation. Keep 20-25% of your total portfolio in cash or liquid funds. This is not idle money – it is your deployment reserve. It is the ammunition that lets you be aggressive when others are fearful.

Step 3: Build your buy list. Identify 5-7 high-quality stocks or index ETFs that you want to own. For each one, note the support and resistance levels. Only buy when the price is in the lower third of the range.

Step 4: Deploy in tranches. When a stock or index hits support: - Deploy 25-33% of your intended allocation at the first support - Deploy another 25-33% if it falls to the second support - Hold the rest in reserve

Step 5: Book profits at resistance. When a stock or index approaches resistance: - Book 15-20% of your position - Sell covered calls on another 30-50% of your position - Let the remaining core position ride (in case this is the real breakout)

Step 6: Sell puts at support. When price is near the top of the range and you have cash reserves: - Sell cash-secured puts at the support level - Collect premiums while waiting for the price to come back down - If assigned, you are buying at your ideal entry price

Step 7: Diversify across asset classes. Do not put everything into equities. My current allocation: 60-70% equities, 20-25% real estate, 5% crypto (purely Bitcoin). The exact mix depends on your age, risk tolerance, and income stability. But the principle is the same: in a consolidation phase, concentration kills.

Step 8: Review monthly, not daily. Stop watching your portfolio every day. In a sideways market, daily price movements are noise. The signal is on the monthly chart. Check your levels once a week. Execute your plan when a level is hit. Otherwise, close the app and go live your life.
