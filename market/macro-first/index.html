I.

I. The One Variable That Explains Everything

Most investors spend their evenings reading annual reports, studying candlestick patterns, and debating whether Company X will beat earnings estimates by two percent or four. They obsess over the micro. They memorize P/E ratios. They build elaborate DCF models with seventeen tabs in Excel.

And then the Federal Reserve raises interest rates by 50 basis points, and every single one of those carefully constructed theses gets demolished in a week.

This is the single most important lesson I can teach you: macro drives everything. The direction of interest rates, the volume of liquidity flowing through global markets, the policy decisions made by central banks in Washington, Mumbai, Tokyo, and Beijing – these forces determine whether your portfolio goes up 40% or down 25% over the next two years. Individual stock selection matters, but it matters within a framework that is entirely set by macroeconomic conditions. You can pick the best stock in the world, and if the macro turns against you, that stock will fall. You can pick an average stock, and if the macro is screaming in your favour, that stock will rally.

I have been a macro investor for years. Most of our community’s best calls – right from Zomato to the Small Cap Index, from private banks to US tech – were macro bets. Not bottom-up stock picks. Not tips from someone’s cousin’s chartered accountant. Macro bets. We identified where the liquidity was flowing, which sectors would benefit from rate cuts, where governments were forced to inject money into the system, and we positioned accordingly.

Point being: macro investing works. The goal should NOT be to trade, but to see where the macros are heading. And, how you can reposition your portfolio for the next 2-3 years. That is where REAL money is made.

In this article, I am going to walk you through the entire causal chain – from a central bank’s decision to move interest rates, to the liquidity that floods or drains from the system, to the specific sectors and stocks that rise or fall as a result. By the end, you will understand why I check the Fed statement before I check any earnings call, why I track the Global Liquidity Index before I open a single stock chart, and why the best investment framework starts from the top down, never from the bottom up.

II.

II. The Interest Rate Machine: How Central Banks Set the Rules of the Game

Let me start with the most fundamental mechanism in all of finance. Interest rates are the price of money. When a central bank – the US Federal Reserve, the Reserve Bank of India, the Bank of Japan – sets its benchmark rate, it is literally deciding how expensive it is for every person, business, and government in that economy to borrow.

When interest rates are low, loans get cheaper. More money flows. More economic activity happens. Businesses borrow to expand. Consumers borrow to spend. The entire economy accelerates, and the stock market typically rallies. When interest rates are high, the opposite happens. Borrowing becomes expensive. Businesses postpone expansion. Consumers tighten their belts. Growth slows. And stock prices fall.

This is not abstract theory. Let me give you real numbers.

The US Federal Reserve took interest rates from near-zero to 5.5% between 2022 and 2023. The result? The S&P 500 fell 25% from its peak. The Nasdaq got hammered even harder. In India, the Nifty corrected 17% from its highs during the same period. Every single growth stock, every high-beta tech name, every leveraged small-cap got destroyed. Not because their businesses suddenly became bad. Not because their products stopped working. Because the price of money changed.

Then consider what happened when the conversation shifted to rate cuts. Interest rates were lowered from 5.5% at the peak to 4.5%. The expectation that rates would go down further to 3.5% by year-end started pushing small-cap stocks in the US market higher. Markets began pricing in cheaper money before it even arrived. This is critical to understand: markets move on the expectation of rate changes, not the actual changes themselves.

The US Fed has already announced rate cuts – and once those cuts actually happen, the immediate market reaction is often muted, because the move was priced in months ago. It is the second rate cut, the third rate cut, the acceleration of the cutting cycle that drives the real rally. The first cut is old news by the time it arrives.

Now here is something that looks counter-intuitive but is precisely the kind of insight you need as an advanced investor. When rate cuts actually happen, the market sometimes falls 10-15% initially. Why? Because:

  • The rate cuts are already factored into prices
  • When cuts happen, borrowing picks up
  • This leads to fixed asset formation for firms – companies like Meta borrow more to push R&D
  • Those investments take 1-2 years to produce meaningful results
  • So there is a gap between the cut and the payoff, and markets can sell off during that gap

This is why I have always said: don’t trade the rate cut announcement. Position your portfolio for the 6-12 month period after the cutting cycle is well underway. That is when the real money is made.

A fast fed cut is usually good for the markets. We are likely to enter the Euphoria stage post this.

III.

III. Liquidity: The Invisible Force That Moves Every Asset on Earth

If interest rates are the price of money, liquidity is the quantity of money. And in today’s markets, liquidity might be the single most important variable driving asset prices. Nothing else comes close.

Let me be blunt: nothing – absolutely nothing – fundamentally has changed on the markets in recent years. The movements are purely liquidity driven now. When liquidity expands, every asset class goes up. When it contracts, everything falls. Individual company fundamentals matter at the margins, but the tide of liquidity lifts or sinks all boats.

You can track this with the Global Liquidity Index (GLI). I have studied this extensively, and the correlation between GLI and asset prices – particularly Bitcoin, but also equities broadly – is remarkably tight. When the 3-month GLI moves up, markets rally. When it turns down, markets fall. The relationship is almost mechanical.

But here is a nuance that most people miss: it is the expectation of liquidity changes that moves markets, not the actual change itself. We saw this clearly when Bitcoin fell even though GLI had not yet decreased. The market was pricing in the expectation that global liquidity would shrink. Investing is a forward-looking game, always.

Let me walk you through the liquidity story of the last few years to show you how this works in practice.

2020-2022: The Great Liquidity Surge. The US money supply (M1) increased from $15 trillion to $18.75 trillion – roughly a 20% increase. Governments around the world printed unprecedented amounts of money in response to COVID. Global liquidity went vertical. The result? Every asset on the planet exploded higher. Stocks, real estate, crypto, commodities – all of it. The Nasdaq doubled. Bitcoin went from $5,000 to $69,000. Indian small-caps went on a multi-year tear.

2022-2023: The Withdrawal. Central banks, terrified by the inflation they had created, began tightening. The Fed jacked rates to 5.5%. Liquidity began draining from the system. The result was an ugly correction across all markets – stocks, bonds, and crypto fell simultaneously. The traditional inverse relationship between equities and bonds broke down entirely.

2024-2025: The Managed Middle. Governments learned they could not withdraw liquidity without crashing the economy. We were already in a slow-growth phase globally – if liquidity growth slowed further, growth would collapse entirely. So they kept liquidity sideways at worst, with occasional injections when things got stressed.

March 2025: The Trade War Injection. When the tariff war escalated, liquidity shot up dramatically as central banks intervened to prevent a market collapse. On the GLI chart, this showed up as the yellow curve going up sharply.

The pattern on the GLI chart is telling: it is making higher lows, which is a bullish pattern. The world is surviving on manufactured liquidity. Inflation cannot be controlled for at least a decade. We have no option but to do high-risk investing in this timeframe and deal with the drawdowns as they come.

Here is the most important point about liquidity: no government on earth can afford to withdraw it permanently. If they do, economies collapse. If they do not, inflation stays elevated. This is the fundamental trap that every government is stuck in, and it is the reason that asset prices will continue to trend higher in nominal terms over the long run – because the alternative is economic collapse, and no politician will allow that on their watch.

The long story short: the world is in a complicated place. We are surviving on manufactured liquidity. But that liquidity has to keep flowing, and that means asset prices have to keep going up. Your job as an investor is to position yourself on the right side of this liquidity flow.

IV.

IV. The Causal Chain: From Central Bank to Your Portfolio

Now let me connect the dots and show you the complete causal chain that runs from a central bank decision to the price of a stock in your portfolio. Understanding this chain is the difference between reacting to markets and anticipating them.

Step 1: Central bank sets interest rates. The Fed, RBI, BOJ, or PBOC decides to raise, hold, or cut rates based on their assessment of inflation, growth, and employment.

Step 2: Interest rates determine borrowing costs. When rates go down, every loan in the economy gets cheaper – home loans, business loans, government borrowing, margin loans for traders. When rates go up, everything gets more expensive.

Step 3: Borrowing costs determine liquidity. Cheaper borrowing means more money flowing through the system. More money supply (M1) means more economic activity, more consumer spending, more business investment, more buying of financial assets.

Step 4: Liquidity determines asset prices. More money chasing the same pool of assets means higher prices. This is not complicated. When you increase the supply of money by 20% (as the US did from 2020-2022), asset prices will reflect that. The GDP growth rate in the US is at least going to match the money supply growth rate – right now the US is increasing money supply by 3.8% a year, which puts a floor under GDP growth.

Step 5: Asset prices determine sector rotation. Not all sectors respond equally to rate changes. When rates are being cut: - Housing finance firms benefit because their borrowing costs drop (companies like Aavas, Aptus) - Banks benefit because cheaper rates stimulate lending activity - IT and tech firms benefit because growth companies are valued on future cash flows, and lower rates make those future cash flows worth more today - Real estate benefits because mortgage rates drop and demand increases - Brokerage firms benefit because more trading activity happens in rising markets - Leveraged small-caps benefit because their debt burden decreases

When rates are being raised, the exact opposite happens. High-beta stocks get destroyed first. Growth stocks with no near-term earnings get hammered hardest. Safe-haven assets and value stocks outperform.

Step 6: The Equity Risk Premium adjusts. This is a concept that ties everything together. The Equity Risk Premium (ERP) is the extra return investors demand for holding stocks instead of bonds. When bond yields are high (because rates are high), the ERP goes up. Stocks become a higher-risk, higher-reward game. When bond yields are low, the ERP shrinks, and stocks become relatively more attractive.

Here is a practical way to think about it: US markets used to give roughly 10% CAGR with 50 units of volatility. Now, as inflation stays elevated and bond yields remain high, US equity markets are likely to give 10.5% CAGR – but with 80 units of volatility. The return is slightly higher, but the ride is much rougher. This matters enormously for portfolio construction and position sizing.

The point I am trying to make is: if X units of money is printed, this money will be spent in the form of consumption, expenditure, investment etc. And, it will reflect in GDP. When the US prints dollars, one could argue it is an “internal” debt for the US. This they can easily handle.

V.

V. The Three Central Banks That Matter Most

You need to be watching three central banks. Everything else is noise.

The US Federal Reserve

The Fed is the most important institution in global finance. Full stop. When the Fed moves, every market on earth responds. Here is why: the US dollar is the world’s reserve currency. US Treasuries are the global benchmark for “risk-free” returns. When the Fed raises or lowers rates, it changes the baseline against which every other investment in the world is measured.

Jerome Powell was expected to be replaced in May 2026 by someone who could cut rates faster. This was incredibly bullish for markets – a dovish Fed chair means cheaper money, more liquidity, higher asset prices. Add midterm elections on top (where the ruling party needs a strong economy to win votes), and you have a setup where the Fed is politically incentivized to keep rates low and markets high.

The US government has to refinance $7 trillion of debt. There is a theory that the government actually wants lower yields on Treasuries, which means they need money to flow into bonds (pushing prices up and yields down). Some people have speculated that Trump is deliberately engineering stock market volatility to push investors from equities into bonds, which would bring down the government’s borrowing costs. The US government pays $1 trillion per year in interest payments alone. Getting that cost down is a strategic priority.

Whether or not you believe that theory, the structural reality is clear: the US government is deeply incentivized to keep rates low and liquidity abundant. Their entire fiscal strategy depends on it.

The Reserve Bank of India

The RBI operates in a different context but follows the same logic. High interest rates in India have been maintained for the last 3 years, and as a result, most small and mid-sized businesses have suffered. Growth has massively slowed down. The economy is quite leveraged at the consumer level, and with a lack of jobs, consumption has structurally suffered.

But here is the constraint: if the RBI cuts rates too fast, India will have a delinquency loan problem. Too many people who borrowed at variable rates will suddenly be encouraged to borrow even more, and the system cannot absorb that. So the RBI has been slow and deliberate – strengthening the financial system even at the cost of growth.

The RBI has been announcing Open Market Operations (OMO) – purchasing securities to push liquidity into the market. When FIIs sold 71,000 crores in a month, the RBI responded by injecting 60,000 crores through OMO. This is firefighting, but it shows a clear signal: the RBI will not let liquidity dry up. When the RBI starts injecting liquidity, it means they are committed to continuing such actions if the fall continues.

RBI rate cuts help specific sectors massively. Private banks, housing finance companies like Aavas (whose revenues are growing at 20% CAGR and directly benefit from lower borrowing costs), NBFCs like Armaan Financials, and even IT firms – all of these are macro plays on the rate-cutting cycle.

The Bank of Japan

This is the one most Indian investors completely ignore, and it is a serious mistake. Japan’s interest rate decisions have massive global implications because of the Yen carry trade.

Here is how it works: for decades, Japan maintained near-zero interest rates. Global investors borrowed in yen at extremely low rates and invested that money in higher-yielding assets – US Treasuries, US tech stocks, emerging market equities. This created an enormous flow of capital from Japan into global markets, artificially boosting asset prices everywhere.

When Japan increases interest rates, several things happen simultaneously: - The yield gap between Japanese government bonds and US Treasuries narrows - Japanese institutions repatriate capital, selling foreign assets to invest at home - This puts downward pressure on US Treasury prices and upward pressure on yields - Higher US yields increase borrowing costs across the entire economy – from federal debt to mortgages - The carry trade unwinds, which means all that borrowed yen-money exits global markets

We saw this play out when Japan raised rates and Indian markets fell even though there was no direct connection. NVIDIA had announced stellar results, and yet markets corrected. The reason was not NVIDIA. The reason was the Yen carry trade unwinding, combined with expectations of reduced global liquidity.

Japan fears inflation, so they increased rates. This points to a few things: lack of liquidity, and the aggravation of Yen carry trade unwinding. People used to borrow in Yen at low interest rates and dump it into US assets which gave higher rates. But since Japan has increased IR, this makes Japanese debt attractive for even global players.

VI.

VI. Reading the Macro Signals: DXY, Bond Yields, Gold, and What They Tell You

You do not need a PhD in economics to be a macro investor. You need to track four or five indicators and understand what they are telling you. Here are the ones I watch most closely.

The US Dollar Index (DXY)

The DXY measures the strength of the US dollar against six major currencies: the Euro (57.6% weight), Japanese Yen (13.6%), British Pound (11.9%), Canadian Dollar (9.1%), Swedish Krona (4.2%), and Swiss Franc (3.6%).

While the INR is not directly included, there is an indirect relationship. If DXY is falling or consolidating, the INR is likely to strengthen or consolidate. This matters enormously for FII flows into India.

Here is why: FIIs invest in foreign currency (say USD), make their returns in INR, and then convert that INR back to USD when they take capital home. If the INR is constantly falling, this lowers their returns. A weak rupee is a direct disincentive for foreign investment in Indian markets.

In the period 2020-2022, DXY grew significantly as the US tightened monetary policy. More recently, DXY has been correcting, partly because China and Europe (which have invested in Gold) are likely to loosen their monetary policies and sell USD. Russia has demonstrated that a major economy can function while limiting USD usage since 2021 – and other countries are taking note, fearing they too could be thrown off the SWIFT system. So doing this preparation is critical for several nations.

A weaker DXY is good for Indian markets. It means the INR stabilises, which improves FII returns, which brings capital back into India. It also means USD-denominated assets become cheaper for international investors, potentially driving more money into US stocks from abroad.

Bond Yields: The 30-Year Treasury

The 30-year Treasury yield indicates whether foreign investors are interested in buying US debt. When it goes high, investors are demanding higher returns for holding US debt. A sudden spike typically means a counterparty might be selling – for example, China dumping Treasuries – and higher yields must be offered to find new buyers.

When you see bond yields rising alongside a falling DXY, it tells you something important: USD is getting cheaper, and people are moving out of bonds simultaneously. This has critical implications:

  1. International investors might buy more US-denominated equities (as USD gets cheaper, it is more lucrative to buy USD assets)
  2. Long-term demand for Treasuries is declining
  3. The ERP goes higher, making stocks a higher-risk, higher-reward proposition

The earlier inverse relationship between equities and bonds – where one acted as a hedge against the other – is now broken. In the new regime, both can fall together. This is one of the most important structural changes in global markets, and it means the old 60/40 portfolio no longer works as advertised.

Gold as a Recession Signal

Gold is one of the clearest macro signals available. Some classic signs of a recession include: - Gold goes up - Bond yields go down - Money flows to stronger currencies like USD (so they strengthen) - Consumer demand goes down - Firms stop expanding

When Gold goes down, bond yields go up, and DXY corrects – as we have seen recently – it tells you this is NOT a recession. It is a manufactured problem, likely driven by policy decisions (tariffs, trade wars) rather than fundamental economic weakness. These manufactured problems tend to be temporary. Real recessions are structural.

Gold also functions as an insurance asset. When fiat-denominated assets (INR, USD) go down, gold typically does well. BTC does well in a high-liquidity environment. Gold does well in an uncertainty environment. They serve different purposes in your portfolio, and understanding the macro context tells you which one to lean into.

Money Supply and GDP: The Direct Relationship

Here is a relationship that most investors ignore but is incredibly powerful. If X units of money is printed, this money will be spent in the form of consumption, expenditure, and investment – and it will reflect in GDP.

The US increased its money supply from $15 trillion to $18.75 trillion between 2020 and 2022 – roughly 20%. You would expect GDP to grow by roughly 20% if you adjust for the COVID contraction (during which GDP shrunk because economic activity stopped). And that is exactly what happened when you make the adjustment.

Right now, the US is increasing its money supply by 3.8% per year. This puts a floor under GDP growth at approximately 3.8%. This is why I keep saying a major recession is NOT coming in the US. The money supply growth rate gives you a baseline for how fast the economy will grow, and right now that baseline is positive and stable.

The US strategy is straightforward: print money, maintain a certain GDP growth rate, aggregate the means of production (semiconductors, strategic resources, competitive advantages in AI), and add real value to the economy over time. As long as they are printing in their own currency, this is internal debt that they can manage.

VII.

VII. Geopolitics Through the Macro Lens

Every few months, some geopolitical crisis sends markets into a panic. Israel-Iran. Russia-Ukraine. US-China tariffs. The new Fed chair appointment. Every time, retail investors rush to sell and “wait for clarity.”

This is exactly the wrong approach.

As a macro investor, you need to evaluate geopolitical events through one simple filter: does this fundamentally change the liquidity or interest rate environment? If the answer is no, the event is noise. If the answer is yes, you need to act.

Take the Israel-Iran situation. This is not a new conflict – it has been ongoing for years. Whatever is not a real surprise for the market does not impact it much. Yes, it might be used as a reason for a 10% correction. But from a bigger picture perspective, governments have enough firepower to cut interest rates fast and balance the market if needed. The global stock market survived Russia-Ukraine. It survived Israel-Iran. Geopolitical events that do not disrupt the fundamental flow of liquidity are buying opportunities, not selling signals.

The tariff war is more interesting because it actually does affect the macro picture. When the US slaps tariffs on Chinese goods, it disrupts supply chains, which raises input costs, which increases inflation, which constrains the Fed’s ability to cut rates. This is a real macro impact – not because tariffs themselves are important, but because they feed into the inflation and interest rate equation.

But even here, the analysis comes back to liquidity. When tariffs created economic stress, central banks responded by injecting liquidity. The trade war injection of March 2025 showed up as a dramatic spike in the GLI. The playbook is always the same: create a problem, inject liquidity to solve it, and asset prices recover.

The US-China spending war on AI is perhaps the most important geopolitical dynamic for investors right now. Whoever wins this AI race gets to export their tech stack globally. The US is already exporting to the UAE, where the tech infrastructure will be used to build data center capacity, optimized by hyperscalers like Amazon and Meta. China is targeting Africa and Southeast Asia. Just in 2026 alone, hyperscalers planned to spend $600 billion on this race. Most of this spending comes from existing free cash flows – it is not unreasonable capital being raised through IPOs. This is real money being deployed by profitable companies, and it underpins a massive growth story.

China’s announcement of its own version of QE – printing more money and rolling out a corporate buyback program – sent the Chinese stock market up 4% in a single day. Alibaba, commodity-driven stocks, and broad Chinese indices surged. When a major nation prints money, it is the start of another round of asset price appreciation. Period.

In simple words: when a major nation prints money, it is a start of another round of asset price appreciation. This will get reflected in the broader market.

There was massive panic when the new US Fed Chair was appointed. Markets expected tightening. High-beta stocks, especially tech, corrected sharply. But this panic was overdone. With midterm elections approaching and the US expected to post record GDP growth, the setup was bullish. The lesson: do not react to personnel changes at central banks. React to what they actually do with policy. And what they will do, in almost every case, is what the political incentives demand – which is to keep money flowing.

The point is simple: geopolitics matters, but only insofar as it affects the liquidity and interest rate environment. Everything else is theatre. Your job is to cut through the noise and ask: is money flowing into the system, or out of it? The answer to that question tells you more than a hundred hours of cable news analysis.

VIII.

VIII. The Bubble Framework: When Does This End?

If I am telling you that liquidity drives everything and that governments cannot afford to withdraw it, you might reasonably ask: does this ever end? Is this a bubble? And if so, when does it burst?

This is the most important question in macro investing, and I want to give you a sophisticated framework for thinking about it.

Raghuram Rajan (former RBI Governor) recently spoke about a U-shaped curve on credit spending. His argument is that when interest rates are cut, debt gets built into the system. This is the good phase – the left side of the U. But the more this stretches, the worse the situation it creates in the long term. Eventually, you reach the right side of the U, where the system is so overleveraged that the central bank is forced to raise rates to prevent a complete loss of monetary control. This is what happened in 2021-2022 when the Fed was forced to hike from near-zero to 5.5%.

Ray Dalio added an important nuance: bubbles do not burst automatically. Something has to prick them. The bubble expands until an external force – usually a rate-hiking cycle – punctures it. The bubble does not know it is a bubble until it pops.

Dr. Rajan’s theory on this seems sound. The pricking of the bubble will likely happen when interest rates are jacked up again. And interest rates are jacked up when inflation becomes politically intolerable.

So the sequence looks like this:

  1. Central banks cut rates aggressively
  2. Liquidity floods the system
  3. Asset prices soar
  4. Inflation rises as a result of all this cheap money
  5. Central banks are eventually forced to raise rates to fight inflation
  6. The rate hike pricks the bubble
  7. Markets crash
  8. Central banks cut rates again to cushion the crash
  9. The cycle repeats

We saw this exact cycle play out between 2020 and 2023. And it is starting again.

We are currently in phases 1-3. Interest rates are being cut (or will be cut aggressively soon), liquidity is expanding, and asset prices are rising. My expectation is that 2026 will be a year when rate cuts go aggressive, the stock market soars, and inflation rises fast. This sets the ground for an eventual crash – but that crash is likely at least 2-3 years away, depending on how fast inflation accelerates.

The probability of the bubble expanding right now is significantly higher than the probability of it bursting. Why?

  • China and the US have strategic reasons to keep spending (the AI race)
  • Hyperscalers can afford to spend $600 billion from free cash flows
  • Macro conditions favour keeping markets elevated (midterm elections, $7 trillion debt refinancing needs)
  • No government can afford to withdraw liquidity without causing a depression
  • We are already in a slow-growth phase of the world – if liquidity growth slows down, the growth would collapse further

Professor Damodaran made another deeply insightful observation: interest rates in the economy go up when expected inflation goes up. The Fed keeps rates low to add more money and increase velocity in the economy. But once that money supply is increased, it translates into demand for higher interest rates if inflation is expected to be high. This creates the tension that eventually resolves through a bond market breakdown and, eventually, a rate-hiking cycle.

What should you do with this information? Invest, and keep a track of macros. Ride the wave while it lasts. Keep 20-30% cash for corrections. Monitor inflation numbers, central bank language, and the GLI. When you see the Fed starting to talk about rate hikes again – that is when you start getting defensive.

The expansion of the bubble has a much higher chance than the busting of the bubble right now. Bubble burst or slowdown is likely when interest rates go up again.

IX.

IX. Building Your Macro-First Portfolio

Now let me give you the practical playbook for implementing everything I have discussed. This is the synthesis – how you translate macro understanding into actual portfolio decisions.

The Geographic Allocation Decision

The single most impactful decision you will make is not which stock to buy. It is how to allocate your money across geographies. People focus too much on questions like “Should I buy IndusInd Bank?” A far better question is: how much should you split your money between India, the US, and China? This geographic allocation is going to have a much bigger impact on your portfolio than constantly hunting for individual trading opportunities.

My framework: - At least 20% in the US – if nothing else, just buy a simple index like the Nasdaq or S&P 500. The US is far from dying. They have the tech infrastructure, the AI leadership, and the political will to keep growing. Nasdaq has a much faster earnings growth rate compared to Nifty 50. - 5-10% in China when macro conditions favour it – specifically when China announces QE or monetary easing. Chinese markets are cyclical and non-transparent, so keep the allocation small. - Core allocation in India with the right sector rotation based on the rate cycle. - 5% in alternative assets – BTC as a high-liquidity-environment insurance, Gold as an uncertainty-environment insurance. I have a 5% BTC allocation in my public portfolio. I have never been a Bitcoin maximalist, but like all assets, BTC has a use case.

Sector Rotation Based on Rate Cycles

This is where your macro knowledge becomes directly actionable. Based on my study of interest rate cut history, here are the sectors that benefit 6-12 months after rate cuts begin:

  1. Private Banks – These have significantly underperformed the Indian market for the last 5 years. Corporate numbers are good, growth rates are fine. RBI rate cuts help private banks massively by reducing their cost of funds and stimulating lending activity. Stay put. Next 2-3 years look good.

  2. Housing Finance (Aavas, Aptus) – Lower rates directly reduce their borrowing costs and stimulate demand for home loans. Aavas is growing revenues at 20% CAGR and is in an accumulation pattern. The PE ratio seems sensible and is likely to improve further due to higher earnings.

  3. IT and US Tech – Growth stocks benefit from lower discount rates. As interest rates go lower, there is a very good chance that SMEs and new-age businesses would look to upgrade their enterprise capabilities, and this pushes firms like Salesforce, TCS, and the Mag-7 higher. This is the primary engine for world growth. Not investing in strong tech firms is a bad mistake.

  4. Brokerage Firms – Lower rates bring more participants into the market, increasing trading volumes and fees.

  5. Select Manufacturing and Infra – Lower credit costs help leveraged manufacturers and infrastructure firms expand. But this cycle takes time to play out.

  6. NBFCs (Armaan Financials and others) – With interest rate cuts, the entire NBFC space benefits from cheaper wholesale funding. I am expecting 25-30% CAGR collectively from a basket of quality NBFCs and housing finance names.

The Cash Bucket Strategy

This is non-negotiable in my framework. You must always maintain a 20-30% cash position that you deploy only during corrections. In the last 2.5 years, the Indian market has corrected by 10% or more at least four times. Each of those was a bulk-buy opportunity.

The discipline is simple: - When the market is at or near highs with no margin of safety: do NOT keep building new positions. Wait. - When you get a 10% correction: deploy your cash bucket aggressively into your highest-conviction names. - After deploying, rebuild the cash bucket over the next few months. - Best thing you can do is 20% cash targeting. If you have 10 lakhs as your portfolio size, create a 2 lakh cash bucket to invest on dips.

My current investment-to-cash ratio in India is 90:10. That means if I have 9 lakhs invested, I keep 1 lakh ready to deploy. The exact ratio depends on your personal cash flows and risk tolerance, but the principle is universal: stay 80-85% invested in a negative trend, and keep the remaining cash ready for opportunities.

I would also say this: I will personally not do SIPs anymore. I see no point. I would rather save money for a few months, and then go big during corrections. If you have the discipline to maintain a cash bucket and deploy it at the right time, you will dramatically outperform any SIP strategy.

Macro Discipline: Cutting Losers

Here is something most people will not tell you. I cut Tarsons at a 6-7% loss because the China dumping macro changed. When Aarti Industries fell 15% on guidance that China dumping would continue, it was a warning sign for any stock exposed to that risk. I am a macro investor – I go by macro narratives. When the macro thesis changes, I change my positions. There is no ego in this.

Similarly, I sold my entire Apple stake when they projected earnings contraction. Not because Apple is a bad company, but because the macro signal (slowing consumer spending, contraction in a key product line) told me to move capital elsewhere. I rotated into names like Microsoft, Fortinet, Palo Alto Networks, and LVMH that had better macro tailwinds.

The Decadal View

I love to state this statistic: - 1999-2008: Indian markets went 6X - 2010-2019: Indian markets went 2X

Each decade delivers diminishing multiples as the market matures. If you take a decadal view on things, you can realign your portfolio for the structural trends rather than chasing weekly or monthly noise.

Right now, the three positive triggers for markets going forward are: 1. Tariff resolution – the trend will change, you ride the trend, make your 40% 2. Interest rate cuts – this will save the market in case of further drawdown. All governments are in a good position to execute this now. 3. Russia-Ukraine clarity – helps Europe, accelerates Europe’s growth

There are enough positive triggers left. Do not focus solely on the negative headlines.

BUY THE DOWNTREND. If you don’t have the guts when the market is down, you will NOT make money when the market goes up.

What To Avoid

Stay away from: - Bloated PSU stocks with no earnings growth - Random themes like hydrogen that have no macro tailwind - Extremely safe assets beyond your emergency fund (just keep them for emergencies) - Overtrading around events like budgets or elections (the next major rally happens at rate cut announcements, not budget day) - Stocks without macro alignment – no matter how good the company looks on paper, if the macro is against it, move on

The Final Framework

Let me leave you with the complete mental model:

Step 1: Read the macro. Check the Fed, RBI, and BOJ positioning. Track the GLI. Monitor DXY and bond yields. Know where we are in the rate cycle.

Step 2: Determine the regime. Are we in an easing cycle (buy growth, buy risk) or a tightening cycle (buy safety, raise cash)?

Step 3: Allocate geographically. How much India vs US vs China vs alternatives? This is your biggest alpha generator. Are you well diversified across at least India, US, and China?

Step 4: Rotate sectors. Within each geography, which sectors benefit from the current macro regime? In rate-cutting cycles: banks, housing finance, IT, NBFCs. In rate-hiking cycles: defensive, cash-flow-positive businesses.

Step 5: Pick stocks within those sectors. This is the LAST step, not the first. Only after you have the macro, the geography, and the sector right do you start looking at individual names.

Step 6: Manage cash. Always maintain your 20-30% cash bucket. Deploy on corrections. Rebuild during rallies.

Step 7: Monitor and adjust. Macro conditions change. Your portfolio should change with them. If the macro thesis shifts, cut your losers and rotate without hesitation. That is discipline.

This is the macro-first framework. It will not make you money every single week. But over 2-3 year cycles, it will put you on the right side of the biggest moves in the market. And those big moves – the 40% rallies, the sector rotations, the geographic allocation shifts – are where real wealth is built.

If you study complicated things, you get a knowledge alpha. And macro is the most complicated, most rewarding thing you can study as an investor. No one will teach you these strategies. But I hope that with this knowledge, you will feel a bit more at ease with all the uncertainties around – and a bit more confident about where to put your money.

Invest. Keep a track of macros. And enjoy the ride.
