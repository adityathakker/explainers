I.

Why do rates matter more than earnings most of the time?

Most investors spend their evenings reading annual reports, studying candlestick patterns, and debating whether a company will beat earnings by two percent or four. They obsess over the micro. They memorise P/E ratios. They build seventeen-tab DCFs.

Then the Federal Reserve raises rates by 50 basis points and every one of those carefully constructed theses gets demolished in a week.

This is the single most important lesson in investing: macro drives everything. The direction of interest rates, the volume of liquidity flowing through global markets, the policy decisions made in Washington, Mumbai, Tokyo, and Beijing — these forces determine whether your portfolio compounds at 15% or bleeds 25% over the next two years. Individual stock selection matters, but only within a framework that is entirely set by macroeconomic conditions. Pick the best stock in the world, and if the macro turns, that stock falls. Pick an average stock with the macro at its back, and it rallies.

The proof is in the numbers most retail investors carefully avoid memorising. The Fed took rates from near-zero to 5.5% between 2022 and 2023. The S&P 500 fell 25% from its peak. The Nasdaq got hammered worse. The Nifty corrected 17% from its highs. Every growth stock, every high-beta tech name, every leveraged small-cap got destroyed — not because the businesses changed, but because the price of money changed.

Once you internalise the magnitude of that move, the framework shifts. A Fed press conference is no longer background noise. It is the central event of every quarter. An earnings beat that comes alongside a hawkish dot plot is meaningless. An earnings miss that comes alongside a dovish surprise is a buying opportunity. The signal that matters is always the one upstream — and the most upstream signal in modern markets is the central bank.

The community of macro investors has been remarkably consistent on the calls that matter. Right from Zomato to the Small Cap Index, from private banks to US tech, the highest-conviction trades were not bottom-up stock picks. They were macro bets — identifying where liquidity was flowing, which sectors would benefit from rate cuts, where governments were forced to inject money — and positioning around the flow. The retail framing of "investing as stock-picking" misses that the alpha sits at the level of regimes, geographies, and rate cycles, not at the level of individual names. Get the macro right, then the stocks within that macro perform. Get the macro wrong, and the best stock picks in the world cannot save you.

The goal is not to trade. The goal is to see where the macros are heading and reposition your portfolio for the next 2–3 years. That is where the real money is made.

This article walks the entire causal chain — from a central bank's rate decision, to the liquidity that floods or drains from the system, to the specific assets that rise or fall as a result. By the end you will understand why you should check the Fed statement before any earnings call, why the Global Liquidity Index matters more than any single stock chart, and why every serious portfolio is built top-down, not bottom-up.

The bottom-up tradition has a long pedigree — Graham, Buffett, Greenblatt — and the case for it is real. Pick wonderful businesses at fair prices and hold them forever; the macro will sort itself out over thirty years. But the assumption underneath is that you have thirty years. Most investors do not. Most investors care about the next five to ten years — the windows where a bad macro regime can take a 40% bite out of compounding that never gets recovered in time to matter. Inside those windows, macro is not a backdrop. It is the dominant variable. And the framework in this article is built for those windows.

Even Buffett, the patron saint of bottom-up investing, holds enormous amounts of cash through expensive macro regimes. Berkshire Hathaway sat on a record $325B+ cash pile entering 2025 — not because Buffett ran out of stock ideas, but because the macro setup made cash a defensible position. The greatest bottom-up investor of all time is still, at the margin, making macro calls about when to be invested and when to wait. The dichotomy between "macro investor" and "fundamental investor" is largely artificial. Every serious investor is both. The framework in this article just makes the macro half explicit.

II.

How does the price of money set the price of everything else?

Interest rates are the price of money. When a central bank sets its benchmark rate, it is literally deciding how expensive it is for every person, business, and government in that economy to borrow. Every other price in the system — mortgages, bond yields, equity multiples, even the dollar — floats on top of that one number.

When rates are low, loans get cheaper. More money flows. Businesses borrow to expand. Consumers borrow to spend. The economy accelerates and equities typically rally. When rates are high, the mechanism runs in reverse. Borrowing becomes expensive. Investment is postponed. Multiples compress. Growth slows.

What most retail investors miss: markets do not move on the rate change. They move on the expected path of rates. The first cut in a cycle is old news by the time it arrives; the second and third cuts are what drive the real rally. The Fed announced cuts and the immediate reaction was muted — because the move had already been priced in months earlier through forward-looking discount-rate math.

Look at it counter-intuitively: when rate cuts do actually start, markets sometimes fall 10–15%. Why?

  • The cuts are already factored into prices.
  • When cuts arrive, borrowing picks up — firms like Meta or Microsoft go on capex sprees.
  • Those investments take 12–24 months to produce earnings.
  • There is a gap between the cut and the payoff, and markets sell off into that gap.

That gap is where the macro investor positions. Not on the cut announcement. Not on the press conference. On the 6–12 month window after the cutting cycle is well underway, when the second-order effects start landing in earnings.

The 2019 cycle is the cleanest historical case study. The Fed cut three times in July, September, and October. The S&P had already rallied 17% through the year by the time of the first cut, on anticipation alone. Between the first cut and Q1 2020 (before COVID), the S&P added another 11% — not on actual earnings, but on multiple expansion as the discount rate fell. The lesson is mechanical: the announcement is the smallest part of the move; the multiple expansion that follows is the larger part; and the earnings boost from cheaper capital is the slowest but largest leg of all. The patient macro investor positions for all three, not just the headline.

Well, actually 🧐 — the Fed Funds rate isn't really the lever investors care about

The Fed sets the overnight Federal Funds rate, but almost no economic activity actually happens at that maturity. Mortgages are priced off the 10-year Treasury. Corporate borrowing is priced off the 5-year. What matters for assets is the shape of the yield curve, not the overnight rate.

The Fed's real tool is forward guidance: convince the market that overnight rates will be X for the next two years, and the entire curve moves. That's why a press conference can move markets 3% with zero actual rate change — the market is repricing the path, not the level.

So: do not trade the rate-cut announcement. Position for the cycle that follows it. The first move in a new regime is psychological; the durable move is mechanical, and it shows up over quarters, not days.

The asymmetry is worth dwelling on. Over the last forty years, the average S&P 500 return in the twelve months before a first rate cut is roughly +6%. The average return in the twelve months after a first rate cut is roughly +14%. The market does not wait. It anticipates, prices in, then runs the second leg as actual earnings benefits land. Anyone trading purely on the rate-cut headline is essentially buying the dip from a year ago.

There is one caveat: the post-cut return is sensitive to why rates were cut. Cuts that arrive ahead of growth slowdown (the "soft landing" scenario) deliver the +14% pattern reliably. Cuts that arrive into an unfolding recession (the "hard landing" scenario) often deliver flat-to-negative returns for the first 12 months as earnings collapse outpace multiple expansion. The macro investor reads the cut alongside the unemployment trend and the credit-spread trend — not in isolation. A cut into a recession with credit spreads already widening is bearish; a cut into stable employment with tight credit spreads is bullish. Same announcement, very different trades.

That same asymmetry is why we obsess over the dot plot, the Fed minutes, and the chairman's word choice. None of that information is about today. All of it is about the expected path. A single phrase — "data-dependent," "patience," "transitory" — can swing trillions in market value because it shifts the curve the market is discounting against. This is forward-looking machinery, and the macro investor is paid to read it earlier than everyone else.

III.

Why does a 100bp move reprice the whole market overnight?

To feel why rates matter so much, look at how a stock is actually valued. Every equity is, in theory, the present value of its future cash flows discounted back to today at some rate r. That rate is built on top of the risk-free yield. Move the risk-free yield by 100 basis points and every dollar of future cash flow gets multiplied by a different number.

PV = Σt=1..∞ CFt ÷ (1 + r)t
For a growing perpetuity:  PV = CF1 ÷ (r − g) where r is the discount rate and g is the long-run growth rate of cash flows

The arithmetic is brutal. Take a stock with $5 of next-year free cash flow growing at 4% per year. At a 7% discount rate (low-rate world), it is worth 5 ÷ (0.07 − 0.04) = $166.67. Push the discount rate to 9% (rates up 200bp), and that same stock is worth 5 ÷ (0.09 − 0.04) = $100. The business did not change. The cash flows did not change. The denominator changed — and the stock fell 40%.

Well, actually 🧐 — the equity risk premium does heavy lifting too

The discount rate isn't just the risk-free yield. It's the risk-free yield plus the equity risk premium (ERP) — the extra return investors demand for holding stocks instead of Treasuries. Historically, the ERP sits around 4–5%, but it expands violently during stress (2008: 8%+, 2020: 7%+).

So when you see a market sell off 30% on a "rates up 200bp" headline, the move is rarely just the rate change. About half of it is the ERP expanding because investors got scared. That's why bear markets often overshoot the fair-value implied by the discount-rate math — sentiment is repricing risk, not just yield. The good news: when rates fall again, both legs of the discount rate compress, and the snapback is just as fast.

This is why long-duration growth stocks (think pre-profit tech) get destroyed first when rates rise. Their cash flows are far in the future, so the discounting compounds against them. Mature dividend payers, whose cash flows are closer to today, feel it much less. Bonds — especially long-dated ones — feel it most of all, because their cash flows are entirely in the future and explicitly tied to interest rates.

Two takeaways. First: in a coordinated cutting cycle, the order of who benefits is predictable — long-duration bonds first, then growth equities, then cyclicals, then real assets. Second: the magnitude of the move is dictated by duration. A 30-year Treasury moves perhaps 18% on a 100bp shift; a value stock with 4% earnings yield moves perhaps 4%; a barrel of oil barely notices.

This is also why the order of operations within a portfolio matters. Most retail investors, anticipating a cutting cycle, buy Nvidia and Microsoft. The macro investor buys long-duration Treasuries first — the move there is faster and more mechanical because rate-sensitivity is direct. Then growth equities. Then, well after the cuts have actually begun, cyclical stocks like banks and industrials. Sequence is alpha. The same conviction expressed in the wrong order leaves money on the table.

And the same sequence runs in reverse during a tightening cycle. When the Fed starts hiking, long-duration bonds are the first thing you sell, then growth equities, then anything leveraged. Cyclicals and value stocks hold up the longest because their cash flows are closest to today and their multiples have less embedded duration to compress. Knowing where in the queue you are is half the discipline of regime-aware investing.

The simulator above is stylised — real markets have noise, leads, lags, and cross-asset spillovers that no five-year linear projection can capture. But the directional intuition is robust. Bonds are leveraged bets on rate paths. Growth equities are leveraged bets on bonds. Cyclicals are leveraged bets on growth equities. Commodities are bets on real-economy demand, mostly orthogonal to rates. Use the widget to feel the relative magnitudes, then translate that into the order you would actually buy or sell during a real regime change.

One more practical observation: the deeper the rate cut, the wider the bond cone widens. That is by design — uncertainty about which path rates take dominates expected-return math at large rate moves. When the Fed is expected to cut 300bp over 18 months, the dispersion of "how fast?" creates an enormous range of possible bond returns. A +25% return on long Treasuries is plausible. So is +5% if the cuts are spread out and pre-priced. The cone captures that range. Position sizing should reflect it.

The takeaway: rate cycles are predictable in direction but uncertain in pace and magnitude. The widget formalises that intuition. Bonds will rally if cuts happen; how much they rally depends on how fast and how deep. Equities ride along behind, with their own dispersion driven by ERP dynamics. Commodities barely budge unless real demand also shifts. Read the cone, not just the mean.

Well, actually 🧐 — equity duration is not a textbook number

Bond duration is a precise concept — the weighted average time of cash flows. For equities, "duration" is an analogy: how far in the future does the bulk of value live? An S&P 500 dividend stream has an effective duration of roughly 18–22 years, depending on the growth assumption. The Nasdaq, dominated by reinvesting tech, has an effective duration of 30+.

That gap is why a 1% rise in 10-year yields can move QQQ 8% while moving the Dow 4%. Same rate move, different duration, different P&L.

IV.

How does liquidity actually reach your portfolio?

If interest rates are the price of money, liquidity is the quantity of money. And in today's markets, liquidity is the single most important variable driving asset prices — nothing else comes close.

Almost nothing has fundamentally changed about how businesses work in recent years. Markets are now largely a liquidity reflex. When global liquidity expands, every asset class goes up. When it contracts, everything falls together. Individual fundamentals matter at the margins, but the tide lifts or sinks all boats.

The reason is structural. Since the global financial crisis, central-bank balance sheets have grown roughly 6x — from around $4T combined in 2008 to over $25T today. That tide of base money has to be deployed somewhere, and it overwhelmingly finds its way into financial assets. When the Fed adds $1T to its balance sheet, much of it lands in primary dealers' accounts and percolates outward into Treasury holdings, then corporate bonds, then equities, then alternatives. The chain is leaky but the direction is consistent: balance-sheet expansion lifts asset prices; balance-sheet contraction depresses them.

Crucially, this same tide also lifts asset prices in countries the Fed has no direct authority over. Global liquidity is fungible. When US capital is created and not absorbed by US opportunities, it flows out — into European equities, EM debt, commodities, even crypto. That is why Indian markets often follow Fed policy more closely than RBI policy. The RBI sets the price of domestic credit. The Fed sets the supply of marginal global capital. And the marginal capital is what moves equity multiples at the high end. The hard-currency investor understands this implicitly; the rupee-only investor is often surprised by it.

You can track this with the Global Liquidity Index (GLI). The correlation between GLI and risk assets — Bitcoin, equities, even real estate — is remarkably tight. When the 3-month GLI moves up, markets rally. When it turns down, markets fall. The relationship is almost mechanical.

The story of the last six years is the story of GLI:

  • 2020–2022 — the great surge. US M1 went from ~$15T to ~$18.75T, a 20% jump. Stocks, real estate, crypto, commodities all exploded higher. The Nasdaq doubled. Bitcoin went from $5,000 to $69,000.
  • 2022–2023 — the withdrawal. Central banks tightened to fight the inflation they had created. Liquidity drained. Stocks and bonds fell together — the traditional 60/40 hedge broke down.
  • 2024–2025 — the managed middle. Governments learned they could not withdraw liquidity without crashing the economy. Liquidity moved sideways, with injections on stress.
  • March 2025 — the trade-war injection. When tariffs escalated, central banks intervened. GLI shot vertical.

If you plot the GLI against the S&P 500 indexed to 100 over the same window, the two curves are almost indistinguishable except at points of acute stress. The correlation drifts up and down with sentiment, but the structural relationship is unmistakable: liquidity flows in, equities rise; liquidity flows out, equities fall. Anyone who tells you the market is "really about earnings" is looking at the wrong chart. Earnings are downstream of liquidity, with a lag of about 6–9 months. The cause is the money supply. The effect is the printout on the income statement.

Bitcoin is the cleanest macro proxy for this insight. BTC has no earnings, no cash flows, no buybacks, no dividends. Its price is purely a referendum on global liquidity preferences. When BTC rallies, global liquidity is expanding. When BTC corrects, global liquidity is tightening. Watching BTC is, in a sense, watching a real-time vote on macro conditions, stripped of the noise that earnings reports add to equity indices. This is why even macro investors who never touch BTC as an investment still track its chart. It is the cleanest liquidity-only signal we have.

The leading-indicator relationship is sharper than most people realise. BTC tends to move 3–6 months ahead of broad equity indices in liquidity-driven cycles. The 2020 BTC rally preceded the equity rally by about 4 months. The 2022 BTC top led the S&P top by about 3 months. The 2023 BTC bottom led the equity bottom by 2 months. This pattern is not a coincidence. BTC is the most rate-sensitive asset on earth — no cash flows, no operational moat, pure duration risk — so it reacts first to the marginal change in liquidity preferences. Equity indices, weighed down by their slower-moving components, follow with a lag. Watching BTC as a leading indicator for SPX is one of the most underutilised free signals in macro investing.

But the question is not "does liquidity move markets?" It is how does $1 of QE actually become $1 of stock-price appreciation? The answer is a three-stage chain, and most of the value leaks away at each step.

This is the deep mechanism behind every liquidity-driven rally. The Fed expands its balance sheet, but only a fraction reaches actual credit creation, and only a fraction of that reaches asset prices. In a healthy easing cycle, all three coefficients are high and the multiplier works. In a stressed cycle, the middle stage breaks — banks just park reserves at the Fed earning interest, and the asset-price impulse never arrives.

This is exactly why the Fed's quantitative easing in 2010–2014 produced muted equity gains (banks hoarded reserves; coefficient 2 was low), while the 2020 stimulus produced explosive gains (direct fiscal transfers bypassed banks entirely; coefficient 2 was effectively 100%). Same nominal balance-sheet expansion, completely different transmission. The shape of liquidity injection — bank-channel vs fiscal-channel — matters as much as the size.

The corollary for forecasting: when reading central-bank announcements, do not just count the dollar figure. Ask where the money is going to land. QE that buys Treasuries off bank balance sheets has a different effect than QE that buys mortgage-backed securities (which directly stimulates housing). Fiscal stimulus that lands in checking accounts has a different effect than fiscal stimulus that goes to state governments. The transmission depends on the path, and the path is decided by policy choices most observers ignore.

For the next cycle, watch for three specific signals: (a) is the Fed buying long-dated Treasuries or short-dated? Long-dated lowers mortgage rates and stimulates housing; short-dated mostly just expands reserves. (b) Is Congress passing fiscal transfers or tax cuts? Transfers have higher marginal propensity to consume; tax cuts to the wealthy mostly land in financial assets. (c) Is the Fed actively encouraging credit growth or just providing liquidity? The former reaches the real economy; the latter mostly inflates asset prices. The shape of the injection determines whether the next round of QE inflates equities, inflates housing, or boosts real growth — and the macro investor positions differently for each.

The pattern on the real GLI chart is bullish: higher lows. The world is surviving on manufactured liquidity. Inflation cannot be controlled for at least a decade. Asset prices will continue to trend higher in nominal terms because no government on earth can afford to withdraw liquidity permanently — the alternative is economic collapse, and no politician will allow that on their watch.

The deeper implication is that nominal returns will be high and real returns will be lower than the headline suggests. A stock market that runs at 12% nominal CAGR in a 5% inflation regime is only delivering 7% real. After tax it might be 5%. After FX conversion for an Indian investor pulling dollars home, it might be 3%. The macro investor cares about real returns net of currency and tax — not the headline number that gets quoted on television. That nuance compounds over decades into a difference that determines whether you actually retire when you planned to.

Liquidity, in other words, is both the answer and the trap. It powers the rallies that build wealth. It also debases the currency that measures that wealth. The serious macro investor positions for both: long real assets (equities, real estate, gold), short the currency on the margin (foreign exposure, inflation-linked bonds), and patient enough to ride out the 2–3 year correction phases that punctuate every cycle.

The historical analogue most worth studying is Japan's 1980s. The Bank of Japan ran loose policy for years, asset prices ballooned, real estate became famously the most expensive in the world. When the BOJ finally tightened in 1989, the bubble burst, and the Nikkei is only now — thirty-five years later — back to its 1989 peak in nominal terms. Real terms, it remains underwater. The lesson is not that liquidity-driven bubbles are unsustainable. The lesson is that they unwind over decades when the central bank finally loses control, and the assets that get caught at the wrong end of the cycle take generations to recover. Position size accordingly. The macro investor who rides the cycle is wealthy. The macro investor who refuses to acknowledge the cycle ever ends is bankrupt.

V.

Why is the US dollar a structural headwind for emerging markets?

Of all the macro signals, the one most often underestimated is the US Dollar Index (DXY). It does not just measure the dollar — it sets the gravity field that emerging-market assets have to climb against.

The DXY is a basket: Euro (57.6% weight), Yen (13.6%), Pound (11.9%), Canadian Dollar (9.1%), Swedish Krona (4.2%), Swiss Franc (3.6%). The INR is not in the basket. But it doesn't matter. When DXY rises, almost every EM currency — including INR — weakens against it. When DXY falls, EM currencies stabilise.

For an Indian portfolio, this is not abstract. FIIs invest in USD, take their returns in INR, then convert back. A falling rupee is a direct hit to their dollar returns — so a rising DXY is a structural disincentive to invest in India, regardless of how good Indian earnings look.

The mechanism that creates EM equity reflexivity is straightforward arithmetic. If the Nifty returns 12% in INR while INR depreciates 4% against USD, the FII's net dollar return is roughly 8%. A US Treasury at 4.5% with zero currency risk looks better on a risk-adjusted basis. Rational FII capital rotates out. That rotation depresses INR further, which compounds the dollar headwind, which triggers more rotation. The loop continues until either (a) something in the EM economy fundamentally changes, or (b) DXY peaks and the loop reverses on its own. Most of the time, option (b) is what ends the cycle — not domestic earnings, not central-bank intervention, but a change in the dollar gravity field itself.

Two implications. First: a tactical macro investor with a meaningful EM allocation should hedge dollar exposure or scale up exposure precisely when DXY peaks. Second: any "India growth story" pitched without acknowledging the DXY backdrop is incomplete. You can have the best earnings in the world, and if the dollar is on a tightening rally, FII capital will rotate out anyway.

Three indirect channels reinforce the relationship. (1) Commodity prices: most commodities are USD-denominated, so a stronger dollar mechanically compresses EM commodity-exporter earnings. (2) Sovereign-debt servicing: many EM governments and corporates borrow in USD, so a stronger dollar inflates their debt-service burden, squeezes domestic spending, and ripples through to equity earnings. (3) Trade competitiveness: a strong dollar means weak EM currencies, which is good for EM exporters in theory — but if the dollar is strong because the Fed is hiking, global demand is also slowing, so the export benefit is muted while the financing pain is acute. The net effect across these three channels is unambiguous: dollar strength is bad for EM in almost every way that matters.

Recently, DXY has been correcting. China and Europe have invested in gold and are likely to loosen monetary policy and sell USD. Russia has demonstrated that a major economy can function while limiting USD usage — and other countries are watching, worried about being thrown off SWIFT. The structural case for a weaker dollar over the next several years is the strongest it has been in three decades, and that is unambiguously bullish for EM equities.

One more wrinkle worth carrying around in your head: dollar strength compounds. When DXY rises, FII outflows from EM are immediate; those outflows weaken EM currencies, which weakens EM returns further, which triggers more outflows. This is the reflexive feedback loop that turns garden-variety dollar strength into EM currency crises — the 1997 Asian crisis, the 2013 taper tantrum, the 2018 Turkey/Argentina run. The trigger is dollar-cycle reflexivity, not EM fundamentals.

The corollary, which most EM bulls miss, is that not every EM is equally exposed. The countries with high USD-denominated debt and low foreign-currency reserves get hit hardest — Turkey, Argentina, parts of Sub-Saharan Africa. Countries with current-account surpluses and large reserves — India in recent years, China, parts of Southeast Asia — have a much larger buffer. So even within EM, the dollar cycle creates winners and losers. A macro investor with broad EM exposure should overweight reserve-rich, surplus economies and underweight twin-deficit economies whenever DXY shows signs of strengthening. Same EM bucket, very different outcomes.

This is also where blanket "EM ETFs" become dangerous. An MSCI Emerging Markets index treats Turkey and Taiwan as comparable EM exposures even though their dollar vulnerability is wildly different. A thoughtful EM allocation skips the index and tilts toward the structurally stronger names: India, Taiwan, South Korea, Mexico in surplus, and away from Turkey, Argentina, South Africa. The implied tracking error against the index is real, but the risk-adjusted return is meaningfully better over a full dollar cycle.

The inverse is also true. When the dollar weakens, capital rushes back into EM. EM currencies stabilise, returns improve, and the inflows accelerate. That positive feedback loop is exactly what powered the 2003–2007 and 2017 EM rallies. Reading the dollar correctly is therefore the most leveraged macro call an EM investor can make — one direction sets up multi-year alpha; the other, a multi-year drawdown.

VI.

Which three central banks should you actually watch?

You need to watch three central banks. Everything else is noise.

The US Federal Reserve. The most important institution in global finance. The US dollar is the world's reserve currency. US Treasuries are the global benchmark for "risk-free" returns. When the Fed moves, every market on earth responds because it is changing the baseline against which every other investment is measured.

The structural setup right now is heavily skewed dovish. The US government has to refinance roughly $7 trillion of debt[1]. They are deeply incentivised to keep yields low — the US already pays roughly $1 trillion per year in interest alone. Add midterm elections, where the ruling party needs a strong economy, and the political incentives align with the fiscal ones: keep money flowing. Whether or not you believe any specific theory about Treasury engineering, the structural reality is unambiguous — the Fed is not going to surprise hawkishly into a fiscal crunch.

The Reserve Bank of India. High rates have been maintained in India for three years and small and mid-sized businesses have suffered. Growth has slowed meaningfully. Consumption is structurally weak with consumer-level leverage high and job creation thin.

But the RBI cannot cut too fast. India has a delinquency-loan risk — cut rates too quickly and variable-rate borrowers stretch even further. So the RBI has been slow and deliberate. When FIIs sold ₹71,000 crore in a month, the RBI responded with ₹60,000 crore of Open Market Operations — buying securities to push liquidity in. That single move tells you something: the RBI will not let liquidity dry up. Once they start injecting, they are committed to continuing.

RBI rate cuts feed directly into private banks, housing finance companies (Aavas, Aptus), NBFCs (Armaan Financials), and IT firms. These are not stock picks — they are macro plays on the rate-cutting cycle.

One more RBI-specific quirk worth knowing: India's reserves-to-GDP ratio is one of the highest in the world. The RBI sits on roughly $700B in foreign reserves — enough to defend the rupee through almost any plausible crisis. This is why the INR has been one of the more stable EM currencies through multiple Fed tightening cycles. The RBI can intervene at scale, and historically it has. That reserve buffer is also why Indian equities are less vulnerable to dollar-strength reflexivity than, say, Turkish or Argentinian equities. The macro investor should price that buffer into the EM risk premium — India deserves a tighter spread than its EM peers, all else equal.

One reason the RBI sometimes appears slow to ease is the trilemma every EM central bank faces: you cannot simultaneously have a fixed exchange rate, free capital movement, and independent monetary policy. India runs a managed float, allows substantial capital movement, and tries to maintain monetary independence — but the trilemma constrains how loose the RBI can be without triggering capital flight. When the Fed is hiking aggressively, the RBI cannot cut without inviting INR depreciation. So India is, in many ways, a price-taker on global rate cycles even if the RBI has nominal autonomy. The thoughtful investor reads RBI policy as a constrained optimisation, not as a free choice.

The Bank of Japan. The one most retail investors ignore, and the most expensive mistake. Japan's rate decisions have global consequences because of the Yen carry trade[2].

For decades Japan maintained near-zero rates. Global investors borrowed in yen and bought higher-yielding US Treasuries, US tech, EM equities. This created an enormous flow of capital from Japan into global markets, artificially inflating asset prices everywhere. When Japan raises rates, the chain reverses:

  • Yield gap between JGBs and US Treasuries narrows.
  • Japanese institutions repatriate capital, selling foreign assets.
  • Treasury prices fall, US yields rise.
  • Higher US yields ripple into mortgages and corporate borrowing.
  • The carry trade unwinds — borrowed yen exits global markets.

This is exactly what played out when Japan raised rates and Indian markets fell despite stellar NVIDIA results. The cause was not NVIDIA. The cause was a carry trade unwinding several thousand miles away.

August 5, 2024 is the case study every macro investor should burn into memory. The BOJ raised its policy rate by 15 basis points. The Nikkei fell 12% in a single session — its worst day since Black Monday 1987. The VIX spiked above 65 in pre-market. Indian and US equities sold off 4–6% in sympathy. The trigger was not Japanese fundamentals. It was forced unwinding of yen-funded leveraged positions all over the world. Within a week, half of those losses were recovered — but the lesson stuck. A central bank you don't watch can move your portfolio by 10% overnight. Now you watch the BOJ.

Japan fears inflation, so they increased rates. People used to borrow in yen at low rates and dump it into higher-yielding US assets. Now that yen debt is no longer free, the entire flow reverses.

The honourable mentions: the European Central Bank, because the euro is the second-largest weight in DXY and ECB easing or tightening pulls DXY around; the People's Bank of China, because Chinese liquidity is the single largest swing factor in global liquidity (China's M2 dwarfs the Fed's balance sheet); and the Swiss National Bank, because Switzerland's currency interventions are tiny in absolute terms but enormous in signalling value about global safe-haven demand. But you can ignore them ninety percent of the time. The Fed, RBI, and BOJ together account for the regime.

The PBOC is the one whose role is changing fastest. China's announcement of its own version of QE — printing more money and rolling out a corporate buyback program — sent the Chinese stock market up 4% in a single day. Alibaba, commodity-driven stocks, and broad Chinese indices surged. When a major nation prints money, it marks the start of another round of asset-price appreciation. As China shifts from a savings-driven to a stimulus-driven monetary stance, expect more Chinese-equity volatility and more commodity-driven feedback into emerging markets generally. Watching the PBOC quarterly is now table stakes for any serious macro investor — even if you have zero direct China exposure.

VII.

What do DXY, bond yields, and gold tell you that earnings don't?

You do not need a PhD in economics to be a macro investor. You need four or five indicators and an understanding of what each one is screaming.

Here is the watchlist that covers ninety percent of macro analysis with minimal effort: the Fed Funds rate and its projected path (Fed dot plot), the 10-year and 30-year Treasury yields, the DXY, the Global Liquidity Index, the ICE BofA US High Yield credit spread, gold price, and Bitcoin. Every Sunday evening, spend twenty minutes glancing at these eight charts. If anything looks materially different from the prior week, dig in. If everything is steady, do nothing. This is the macro-investor equivalent of a doctor's vitals check — quick, regular, sufficient to catch the regime changes that matter.

Bond yields — the 30-year Treasury. The 30-year yield is the market's referendum on long-term US fiscal health. When it spikes, foreign investors are demanding more compensation to hold US debt — often because a major counterparty (China, for example) is selling. When yields rise alongside a falling DXY, the signal is unmistakable: USD is getting cheaper and people are exiting bonds simultaneously. That combination tells you:

  1. International investors will rotate into US equities (cheaper USD makes USD assets relatively attractive).
  2. Long-term demand for Treasuries is structurally declining.
  3. The equity risk premium has expanded — stocks are higher-risk, higher-reward.

The classical inverse relationship between equities and bonds — the foundation of 60/40 portfolios — is now broken. Both can fall together (2022). Both can rally together (2024). The hedge no longer works as advertised, and that is one of the most important structural changes in modern markets.

The rolling 12-month correlation between US equities and US Treasuries flipped from −0.4 (negative, hedging) to +0.3 (positive, co-moving) between 2021 and 2023, and has stayed positive since. That is a regime change, not a fluctuation. The implication is that the simple 60/40 portfolio that worked for forty years — equities for return, bonds for hedge — needs a third leg now. Most institutional investors are adding gold, commodities, and dynamic cash buffers to fill the hedging gap. Retail investors who haven't updated their model still believe in the 60/40 and will be disappointed by it in the next drawdown.

The replacement many sophisticated investors are converging on is a "60/20/20" framework: 60% equities (geographically diversified), 20% bonds (skewed to shorter duration and inflation-linked), and 20% real assets (gold, real estate, commodities). The real-asset sleeve does the hedging that bonds used to do, without the rate-correlation problem. Backtests across 2000–2024 show this allocation produces similar long-run returns to 60/40 with materially better drawdown characteristics in regimes where bonds fail to hedge. It is not the only answer, but it is one of the cleanest answers to the 60/40 problem.

Gold. One of the cleanest macro signals available. The classic recession constellation:

  • Gold rises (flight to safety).
  • Bond yields fall (flight to duration).
  • Money flows to stronger currencies — USD typically strengthens.
  • Consumer demand drops.
  • Firms stop expanding.

When you see gold falling, yields rising, and DXY correcting all at once, you know it is not a real recession. It is a manufactured problem — tariffs, trade war, political theatre — that will pass. Real recessions are structural; manufactured ones resolve through liquidity injections.

Gold is an uncertainty insurance asset. Bitcoin is a liquidity insurance asset. They serve different purposes in a portfolio. In a high-liquidity environment, BTC outperforms gold. In an uncertainty environment, gold outperforms BTC. Understanding the macro context tells you which one to lean into.

The pair's behaviour during recent macro regimes makes the distinction concrete. During 2020–2021 (extreme liquidity, low uncertainty), BTC went 14x while gold barely doubled. During 2022 (liquidity withdrawal, rising uncertainty), gold rose 8% while BTC dropped 65%. During 2023 (recovering liquidity, persistent uncertainty), both rose roughly in tandem. The macro-aware investor reallocates between the two based on the dominant regime — not in equal weights, but tilted toward whichever insurance is in higher demand at that moment. A 5% alternative allocation, split dynamically between BTC and gold, is a powerful hedge against the two failure modes most retail investors are not insured against.

Money supply and GDP. A relationship most investors ignore. If X units of money is printed, that money is spent — on consumption, expenditure, investment — and it shows up in GDP. The US grew M1 from $15T to $18.75T between 2020 and 2022, roughly 20%. Adjust for the COVID contraction and GDP grew roughly in line.

Right now the US is increasing money supply by 3.8% per year. That puts a floor under nominal GDP growth at approximately 3.8%. This is why a major US recession is structurally unlikely. The money supply growth rate is the baseline, and right now that baseline is positive and stable.

The credit spread. One last signal that no one talks about until it matters too much. The credit spread is the gap between high-yield corporate bond yields and risk-free Treasuries[4]. In normal times it sits around 350–450 basis points. When it blows past 800bp, you are looking at a credit-stress event — the bond market is screaming that default expectations have spiked. Every major equity drawdown of the last forty years has been preceded by a credit-spread widening: 2000 dotcom, 2008 GFC, 2020 COVID, 2022 tightening. Watching the credit spread is like watching the canary in the coalmine. If it stays under 500bp, the macro is fine even if equity headlines look bad. If it crosses 600bp and rising, get defensive immediately.

One signal investors love but should weight carefully: the yield curve inversion. When 10-year Treasury yields drop below 2-year yields, recession follows in 12–18 months. The track record is strong but not perfect — the curve inverted in late 2022 and the recession everyone expected in 2023 never quite arrived. The signal still works directionally; it just has noise around the timing. Use it as one indicator among five, not as a single trigger.

There is also a subtler signal worth tracking: the real yield on 10-year inflation-protected Treasuries (TIPS). The real yield strips out inflation expectations and gives you the true return on risk-free capital. When real yields move from negative (which they were for most of 2020–2022) to positive (which they did in 2023), it represents a major tightening of financial conditions that doesn't always show up in the headline nominal rate. Real yields above 2% historically pressure equity multiples; real yields below 0% historically inflate them. Watch real, not nominal, when you want to understand the actual cost of capital that businesses face.

One more under-discussed signal: the copper-to-gold ratio. Copper is sensitive to industrial demand. Gold is sensitive to risk aversion. When copper outperforms gold, the global economy is reflating — cyclicals lead, growth equities follow, and yields rise. When gold outperforms copper, the global economy is contracting — defensive assets lead and yields fall. The ratio leads 10-year yields by roughly 6 weeks in most cycles, which makes it a cheap leading indicator of the rates trend that the bond market eventually catches up to. Few retail investors track it. The professionals do.

Well, actually 🧐 — money supply doesn't perfectly map to GDP

The link between M1 (or M2) and nominal GDP is velocity — how many times each dollar changes hands per year. In recessions, velocity collapses: people hoard cash, banks hoard reserves, the multiplier breaks. So 20% M1 growth could produce anywhere from 5% to 25% nominal GDP growth depending on velocity.

The reason the 2020–22 episode produced almost exactly the GDP growth the M1 expansion implied was that fiscal transfers (stimulus checks) bypassed the banking system entirely. The money went directly into household checking accounts where velocity is >1. Bank-channel QE doesn't do that — the money sits as reserves, velocity collapses, and the GDP impact is muted. This is exactly what Widget 2 lets you experiment with.

VIII.

When does the liquidity bubble end — and how would you know?

If liquidity drives everything and governments cannot afford to withdraw it, the natural question is: does this ever end? Is this a bubble? And if so, when does it burst?

This is the most important question in macro investing, and the framework for thinking about it draws on two of the sharpest minds in the field. Raghuram Rajan, former RBI Governor, talks about a U-shaped curve on credit spending[3]. When rates are cut, debt builds into the system — the good phase, the left side of the U. The longer this stretches, the worse the eventual situation. Eventually the system is so overleveraged that the central bank is forced to raise rates just to keep monetary control. That is the right side of the U — exactly what the Fed did in 2021–2022, going from zero to 5.5% in eighteen months.

Ray Dalio adds the second piece: bubbles do not burst automatically. Something has to prick them. The bubble expands until an external force — almost always a rate-hiking cycle — punctures it. The bubble doesn't know it is a bubble until it pops.

The sequence is mechanical:

  1. Central banks cut rates aggressively.
  2. Liquidity floods the system.
  3. Asset prices soar.
  4. Inflation rises from all the cheap money.
  5. Central banks are eventually forced to hike to fight inflation.
  6. The hike pricks the bubble.
  7. Markets crash.
  8. Central banks cut again to cushion the crash.
  9. The cycle repeats.

We just watched this play out between 2020 and 2023. It is starting again. We are currently in phases 1–3: rates are being cut or will be cut aggressively, liquidity is expanding, asset prices are rising. The expectation: 2026 is a year of aggressive cuts, a soaring stock market, and accelerating inflation. The crash is probably 2–3 years away, depending on how fast inflation accelerates.

Professor Aswath Damodaran has made an observation worth carrying as a tripwire. Interest rates in the economy go up when expected inflation goes up. The Fed keeps rates low to add more money and increase velocity. But once that money supply expansion translates into demand, it produces inflation, which forces the bond market to demand higher yields. That creates the tension that eventually resolves through a bond-market breakdown and, eventually, a rate-hiking cycle. The bond market is the disciplinarian the Fed cannot ignore. When 30-year yields start rising into a cutting cycle, the bond market is telling the Fed that policy is now incoherent — and the Fed will eventually be forced to listen.

Why is the probability of the bubble expanding right now significantly higher than the probability of it bursting?

  • China and the US have strategic reasons to keep spending (the AI race — roughly $600B planned in 2026 alone).
  • Hyperscalers can fund that from existing free cash flows, not new debt.
  • Macro conditions favour keeping markets elevated (midterm elections, $7T debt to refinance).
  • No government can afford to withdraw liquidity without causing a depression.
  • We are already in a slow-growth phase — if liquidity slows further, growth collapses.

None of those reasons are short-term. They are structural conditions that will likely persist for years. Which means the bubble can keep expanding for years before the rate-hike trigger arrives. The patient macro investor does not try to predict the exact day the cycle ends. They participate in the expansion, monitor the warning signals, and prepare to defensively rotate when the data shifts. That is the difference between a macro investor and a permabear — the macro investor is right about both directions of the cycle, not just the down side.

The historical analogy worth keeping in mind: the late 1990s tech boom lasted from roughly 1995 to early 2000. Permabears called the top continuously from 1996 onward. Most of them were "right" eventually — in 2000 — but had missed five years of returns that more than compensated for the eventual drawdown. The macro investor who rode the boom and rotated defensive only when the signs of bubble pricking arrived (rising long-end yields, late-1999 NASDAQ extreme momentum, December 1999 credit-spread widening) captured most of the upside while avoiding most of the downside. That is the goal: not perfect timing, but asymmetric participation.

Apply the same logic to today's AI-driven rally. The structural backdrop is intact: liquidity is expanding, the Fed is dovish, hyperscaler capex is real, and the political economy demands continued asset-price support. The names doing the deploying are profitable and self-funded. As long as those conditions hold, the right move is to participate. The minute they break — credit spreads widening, long-end yields decoupling, hyperscaler capex guidance compressing — the macro investor rotates defensive. Until then, you ride the cycle. Permabears will keep insisting the top is in. The macro investor knows better.

The probability of the bubble expanding right now is significantly higher than the probability of it bursting. The burst happens when interest rates go up again. Watch for that.

What do you do with this? Invest. Track macros. Ride the wave while it lasts. Keep 20–30% cash for corrections. Monitor inflation prints, central-bank language, and the GLI. When the Fed starts talking about rate hikes again — that is when you get defensive.

There are three early-warning signals that the bubble is about to be pricked, and they almost always appear in order:

  1. Inflation prints stop falling. The disinflation narrative breaks. Core PCE, which had been falling toward 2%, stalls at 3–3.5% for three or four consecutive months. The Fed's "transitory" language disappears from minutes.
  2. Long-end yields decouple from short-end cuts. The Fed cuts and the 30-year Treasury rises instead of falling. This is the bond market saying "we don't believe you can keep inflation contained." It happened in late 2024 and it is the single best leading indicator we have.
  3. Credit spreads widen with equities still near highs. When the high-yield market starts demanding more compensation while the S&P is still printing fresh records, the smart money is exiting first. Equity tops form roughly 4–6 months after credit spreads start widening.

If you see those three things in sequence, the bubble is being pricked in real time. Move to cash. Buy puts on your equity book. Lengthen duration in bonds. The actual crash will come 6–12 months later, and most retail investors will only know to sell after it has already happened.

A note on geopolitics. Every few months a geopolitical crisis sends markets into panic. Israel-Iran. Russia-Ukraine. US-China tariffs. New central-bank appointments. Every time, retail investors rush to sell and wait for "clarity." This is almost always the wrong move. As a macro investor, evaluate each event through one filter: does this fundamentally change the liquidity or interest rate environment? If no, it is noise. If yes, it deserves a portfolio adjustment.

Most geopolitical events fail that test. The Israel-Iran tension has been ongoing for years — not a real surprise to the market. It may be used as a pretext for a 10% correction, but governments have enough firepower to cut rates and balance things out. The market survived Russia-Ukraine. It survived October 7th. Geopolitical events that do not disrupt the fundamental flow of liquidity are buying opportunities, not sell signals. The tariff war is the exception that proves the rule — tariffs do affect macro because they raise input costs, which raises inflation, which constrains the Fed. But even there, the analysis loops back to liquidity: stress shows up, central banks inject, asset prices recover. The mechanism is always the same.

The US-China AI race is the geopolitical dynamic worth obsessing over right now. Whoever wins gets to export their tech stack globally. The US is already exporting to the UAE, where infrastructure will be optimised by hyperscalers like Amazon and Meta. China is targeting Africa and Southeast Asia. Hyperscaler capex of $600B per year is being funded from existing free cash flows — this is real money being deployed by profitable companies, not speculative capital raised through IPOs. That underpins a multi-year growth story which justifies elevated multiples on the names doing the deploying (NVDA, MSFT, GOOGL, META) and their picks-and-shovels suppliers (AVGO, ASML, AMAT). The market is paying for an arms race, not for traditional fundamentals. Understanding that reframes which earnings results actually matter.

IX.

How do you build a portfolio top-down instead of bottom-up?

The synthesis. How to translate macro understanding into actual portfolio decisions.

Geographic allocation first. The single most impactful decision you will make is not which stock to buy. It is how you split capital across geographies. The "Should I buy IndusInd Bank?" question is downstream of "how much should be in India vs the US vs China?" Get the allocation right and the stock decisions become easier. My own highest-conviction move at every new cycle has been geographic, not security-level — I lock in the country mix before I look at a single ticker.

A defensible default framework:

  • At least 20% in the US — even if it is just a Nasdaq or S&P 500 index. The US still has the tech infrastructure, AI leadership, and political will. The Nasdaq's earnings growth rate handily beats Nifty 50's.
  • 5–10% in China when macro conditions favour it — specifically when China announces QE or monetary easing. Cyclical and non-transparent, so the allocation stays small.
  • Core allocation in India with sector rotation tied to the rate cycle.
  • 5% in alternative assets — BTC as high-liquidity-environment insurance, gold as uncertainty insurance.

The defence of geographic diversification is not just about returns. It is about regime diversification. The US, India, and China are at different points in their rate cycles, their debt cycles, and their political cycles at any given moment. A US recession is not a global recession; an India slowdown is not a US slowdown; a China stimulus is not necessarily mirrored in either. Holding all three means you are positioned for whatever regime dominates in any given year. That is genuine diversification — not "different sectors of the same economy," which most retail portfolios mistake for diversification.

A typical Indian retail portfolio of "Nifty 50 + a small-cap fund + a flexi-cap" is not three positions. It is one position. All three move with Indian macro. All three depend on RBI policy. All three are punished simultaneously by FII outflows. Diversification across mutual funds within the same country is a comforting illusion that masks single-regime risk. Real diversification requires uncorrelated macro drivers, which means geographic spread. The discomfort of owning US tech during an Indian rally, or holding India through a Nasdaq bull run, is precisely what protects you when one of those regimes turns. That is the trade-off — psychological discomfort for structural resilience.

The simplest path to genuine geographic diversification for an Indian investor: a low-cost US-listed ETF (VOO for S&P 500, QQQ for Nasdaq, or VTI for total US market) held in an LRS-routed brokerage account. The setup takes one weekend. Tax treatment is reasonable. Annual reporting is straightforward. And the diversification benefit kicks in immediately. This is one of those rare cases where the optimal move is also the easiest to execute. The fact that most Indian retail investors still hold zero US exposure is the single biggest source of unforced underperformance in the average Indian portfolio.

And the case for at least some US exposure for an Indian investor is overwhelming. Over the last decade, an Indian investor who held 100% in Nifty earned roughly 12% INR CAGR — about 7% in USD terms after currency drag. The same investor who held 100% in Nasdaq earned roughly 18% USD CAGR, or roughly 23% in INR terms after currency gain. The gap is not 6%. It is 16% annualised, compounded over a decade. Geographic mis-allocation costs more than any individual stock decision could earn back.

Compounding maths makes this brutal. ₹10 lakh deployed at 12% INR for a decade becomes ₹31 lakh. The same ₹10 lakh at 23% INR becomes ₹79 lakh. The difference — ₹48 lakh — is what geographic allocation alone produced. No stock-picking. No timing. Just being in the right country during the right macro regime. This is why "geographic allocation first" is not a slogan. It is the highest-leverage decision in your portfolio, and most retail investors never explicitly make it.

Sector rotation by rate regime. Within each geography, the sectors that benefit 6–12 months after rate cuts begin: private banks (cheaper funding, more lending), housing finance like Aavas (growing revenues at ~20% CAGR), IT and US tech (discount-rate beneficiaries), brokerage firms (more trading volumes), select manufacturing and infra (cheaper credit for capex), and NBFCs (cheaper wholesale funding). In rate-hiking regimes, the rotation flips to defensive, cash-flow-positive businesses with low leverage.

The mistake most investors make with sector rotation is timing it like a single decision. It is not. It is a continuous rebalancing as the macro evolves. Every quarter, look at where the Fed dot plot is, where the GLI is trending, where credit spreads are sitting — and adjust at the margins. A 5% trim from cyclicals and a 5% add to growth equities in Q2, repeated four quarters in a row, is how a portfolio actually rotates. The investors who do this well treat it like a slow, steady reweighting; the ones who blow up treat it like a series of all-in calls.

A practical rule of thumb for the 6–12 months following the first rate cut in a cycle: private banks (target 25–35% CAGR collectively) from lower funding costs and revived lending; housing finance like Aavas (target 20–25% CAGR) as mortgage demand awakens and PE ratios re-rate; US tech and IT services (target 15–25%) as enterprise SaaS budgets thaw and AI capex accelerates; brokerage firms (target 15–20%) as trading volumes surge; NBFCs and selected leveraged names (target 20–30%) as wholesale funding cheapens. These are not certainties. They are the historical pattern after every major rate-cut cycle since 1990. The macro investor positions for the pattern, sizes for the uncertainty, and accepts that not every quarter will deliver. Over the cycle, the basket outperforms broad index by 8–12 percentage points.

If you remember one thing

The macro is upstream of everything. Earnings, sector performance, sentiment, even individual stock picks — all of them sit downstream of central bank policy and global liquidity. A great business in a bad macro regime underperforms a mediocre business in a good macro regime over a 2–3 year window, and 2–3 year windows are the windows most investors actually care about. Get the macro right and the rest gets easier. Get it wrong and the best stock picks in the world will not save you.

The cash bucket. Non-negotiable. Maintain a 20–30% cash position deployed only on corrections. The Indian market has corrected 10%+ at least four times in the last 2.5 years — each time was a bulk-buy opportunity. The discipline:

  • At or near highs with no margin of safety: do not build new positions. Wait.
  • On a 10% correction: deploy the cash bucket aggressively into highest-conviction names.
  • After deploying, rebuild the bucket over the following months.

This is also the argument against blind SIPs. A systematic investment plan that buys the same amount every month treats all price levels equally — including ones where margin of safety is absent. The thoughtful alternative: save aggressively, hold cash, deploy in size during corrections. Over a decade, this outperforms a passive SIP meaningfully, but it requires the emotional discipline to hold cash while everyone else is celebrating new highs. Most investors do not have that discipline, which is why SIPs were invented. If you do have the discipline, you can extract real alpha from market volatility instead of being its passive recipient.

An indicative cash-bucket workflow: if portfolio is 90:10 invested-to-cash and the market is at all-time highs with no margin of safety, do nothing — just let positions compound. If the market corrects 10%, deploy half the cash bucket (i.e., move from 90:10 to 95:5) into highest-conviction names. If it corrects another 10% (20% total), deploy the remaining cash and consider trimming low-conviction positions to free more capital. As the market recovers, gradually rebuild the bucket back to 20–30% over the following 6–12 months. This is mechanical, emotionally manageable, and has been shown to outperform both passive holding and aggressive timing in academic backtests across multiple decades. It is the closest thing to a free lunch in macro investing.

Macro discipline — cut losers fast. When the macro thesis changes, the position changes. Aarti Industries fell 15% on guidance that China dumping would continue — that was a macro warning, not a stock-specific glitch. Sell anything else exposed to that risk. Sell Apple when consumer-spending macro signals slowed. Rotate into Microsoft, Fortinet, Palo Alto Networks, LVMH — names with macro tailwinds. There is no ego in this; the macro is the truth, and the portfolio follows it.

Position sizing for survival. No matter how loud the macro signal, never let a single thematic bet exceed 15% of portfolio. The macro is high-conviction but not high-certainty — the Fed surprises, geopolitical shocks land, central banks change leadership. A 15% cap means even a wrong macro call costs you 4–6% on the book, not 30%. That is the difference between a recoverable mistake and a portfolio-altering one. The investors who blow up on macro views are universally the ones who sized them as if certainty existed where only conviction did. Conviction without sizing discipline is just a more confident version of gambling.

The same logic applies to leverage. Margin, futures, options sold uncovered — all of these amplify both the upside and the downside of a macro view. In a clean regime, leverage works brilliantly; in a regime that surprises, it ruins. The macro investor's edge is durability across cycles, not maximum compounding within a single cycle. Take leverage only when the macro signal is unambiguous and the position is hedgeable. Treat any leverage as a temporary tactical tool, never as a permanent feature of the portfolio. Most retail investors who blow up on macro views did so because they were leveraged, not because they were wrong about the macro. Being right too early and leveraged is functionally identical to being wrong.

The macro-first checklist

Step 1. Read the macro. Fed, RBI, BOJ. GLI. DXY. Yields. Know where we are in the rate cycle.
Step 2. Determine the regime. Easing (buy growth, buy risk) or tightening (buy safety, raise cash)?
Step 3. Allocate geographically. Biggest alpha generator.
Step 4. Rotate sectors within geography by regime.
Step 5. Pick stocks — last, not first.
Step 6. Manage cash — 20–30% bucket, deploy on corrections.
Step 7. Monitor and adjust as macro changes.

This framework will not make money every week. It will not call short-term tops or bottoms perfectly. But over 2–3 year cycles, it will put you on the right side of the biggest moves — 40% rallies, sector rotations, geographic shifts. That is where real wealth is built, and that is the entire game.

One last point on what not to do. Avoid bloated PSU stocks with no earnings growth. Avoid random thematic baskets like hydrogen or "5G plays" that have no genuine macro tailwind. Keep "extremely safe" assets only at emergency-fund size — nothing more — because real assets compound, and cash decays. Do not overtrade around events like budgets or elections; the next major rally happens at rate-cut announcements, not on budget day. And do not hold stocks without macro alignment, no matter how good the company looks on paper. If the macro is against it, move on.

Also avoid: chasing themes that have already worked. The macro investor's edge is being early to the next regime, not piling into the one that has already played out. If everyone is talking about a sector, the easy money has been made; the smart move is to start positioning for the next rotation. Buying defence stocks after they have doubled, EV plays after they have tripled, AI infrastructure after the obvious names are at 50x earnings — these are the trades that ruin macro portfolios. The discipline is to ask, every quarter: "What is consensus? And what does the macro suggest will surprise consensus?" Position toward the surprise, not the consensus.

And keep a decadal view. Indian markets went 6x between 1999 and 2008. They went 2x between 2010 and 2019. Each decade delivers diminishing multiples as the market matures. If you take a decadal view rather than a weekly one, you can realign your portfolio for structural trends instead of chasing noise. The three positive triggers carrying us forward right now are tariff resolution (ride the trend, make your 40%), the rate-cutting cycle (which will rescue any further drawdown), and Russia-Ukraine clarity (helps Europe, accelerates European growth). There are enough positive triggers left. Do not be paralysed by the negative headlines.

Where this leaves us in 2026 is a regime where the easy decisions look easy in hindsight and counter-intuitive in real time. Buying when everyone is panicking. Trimming when everyone is euphoric. Holding cash when SIP discipline says deploy. Rotating sectors when the trend looks like it will go on forever. These are the moves the macro framework licenses you to make — not because the framework is magic, but because the framework makes the cross-currents legible. When you know why a sector is rallying, you also know what would make it stop. When you know why the market is selling off, you also know what would make it bottom. That asymmetry of understanding versus the average retail investor is the durable edge that macro investing provides.

Concretely: the playbook for 2026 looks like this. Be tilted toward growth equities (US tech especially) into the rate-cut cycle. Maintain at least 20% US allocation, irrespective of how exuberant Indian markets look. Hold a 5% BTC sleeve and a 5% gold sleeve. Run a 20% cash bucket and deploy it on 10% corrections. Watch the credit spread weekly. Watch the BOJ quarterly. Be ready to rotate defensive when (a) Fed language pivots hawkish, (b) credit spreads break 600bp, or (c) long-end yields decouple from short-end cuts. None of this is rocket science. All of it requires consistent execution. That is the entire game.

And the years that this framework loses to a bottom-up stock-picker are the years that the macro is unchanged for so long that fundamental edge matters again — 2003–2007, 2013–2018. Those are roughly half of all years. The other half — the regime-change years — are when macro absolutely dominates. The macro-first investor accepts giving up some performance during placid regimes in exchange for vastly outperforming during the regime changes that define decade-scale wealth creation. That asymmetric trade is the entire thesis of this article.

A note on humility. Every macro framework, including this one, is a stylised model of an extraordinarily complex system. Markets surprise. Central bankers reverse course mid-cycle. Geopolitical shocks rewire the discount-rate math overnight. The framework is not a prediction machine — it is a way to organise the variables that matter most so that when something unexpected happens, you have a coherent way to update. The investor who outperforms over decades is rarely the one with the loudest views. It is the one who updates their model fastest when the data disagrees with the model. Macro-first investing is the most powerful framework available because it focuses your attention on the variables that move the most. It is not the framework because it always works. Use it accordingly.

And read widely. The macro framework presented here draws on Damodaran on discount rates, Dalio on cycles, Rajan on credit, the BIS on liquidity, and decades of academic research on factor returns. None of those sources individually is sufficient. None of them is right all the time. But synthesising across them — updating your priors when one contradicts another, weighting more recent data more heavily, treating consensus with appropriate skepticism — is the closest thing to a durable epistemic edge that an individual investor can build. The macro is hard. That is precisely why doing it well is so well-rewarded.

A reading list, ordered roughly by how high-impact each source is for the framework: Principles for Dealing with the Changing World Order by Ray Dalio (cycles, debt, geopolitical regimes); The Banker's New Clothes by Admati and Hellwig (banking system mechanics); Aswath Damodaran's annual Equity Risk Premium paper (discount-rate dynamics); BIS Annual Reports (the best free macro analysis on earth); the FOMC Minutes and Summary of Economic Projections (the source of truth for Fed forecasts); and the IMF World Economic Outlook (twice a year, free, the best single-source synthesis of global macro). Read these consistently for a year and you will understand 80% of what a professional macro investor knows. Add the discipline to actually trade the framework and you compound from there.

Build the framework. Run it weekly. Update it monthly. Compound your understanding over years. The result will not be perfect timing — nobody has that. The result will be a portfolio that is on the right side of the regime more often than not, sized appropriately to survive being wrong, and positioned to capture the asymmetric upside that emerges every 2–3 years when a major rotation comes through. That is a winning strategy over a thirty-year career. Earnings calls and stock picks are not.

The first compounding takes effort. Reading FOMC statements, parsing dot plots, tracking GLI weekly, learning to read the credit-spread chart, watching DXY against EM — it is a lot to absorb. But the framework is not a series of independent facts. It is a single integrated mental model: liquidity is the master variable; interest rates are the price tag; central banks are the operators; cross-asset correlations are the transmission belt. Once those pieces click together, every macro headline becomes interpretable. The Fed cut: what does that do to the GLI? China announced QE: what does that do to commodity prices and EM equities? Treasury yields spiked: who is selling, and why now? Each headline becomes a data point in a coherent model, not a discrete event that requires its own response.

And once the model is internalised, you stop reading most financial news because most of it is not informative. You learn to scan for the three or four indicators that actually move the model, ignore everything else, and reallocate the time saved to thinking, position sizing, or simply leaving the portfolio alone. The macro-first investor reads less, acts less often, and outperforms more. The simplification is the point. Bottom-up investing requires you to know everything about each name. Macro investing requires you to know almost nothing about each name and everything about a small set of master variables. The compression in cognitive load is enormous, and the freed bandwidth is what compounds into long-run alpha.

The journey is long. The compounding is slow. The mistakes are inevitable. The framework is imperfect. But the alternative — trading stocks bottom-up in ignorance of the macro tide — is to be perpetually on the wrong side of the most consequential variable in markets. The macro-first investor at least knows which way the tide is flowing. That alone, applied consistently, beats most professional managers over a decade. Add disciplined sizing, geographic diversification, and a cash bucket, and you are operating with a structural edge that the average retail investor will never replicate. That edge is what this article is for.

None of the work is glamorous. Most weeks, the right answer is to do nothing. Most quarters, the macro changes very little. The 80% of investing that compounds is patience; the 20% that determines outcomes is the willingness to act decisively when the regime actually changes. The framework tells you when that moment has arrived. The execution is on you.

If you study complicated things, you get a knowledge alpha. And macro is the most complicated, most rewarding thing you can study as an investor. Invest. Track macros. Enjoy the ride.
