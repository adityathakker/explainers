I.

I. This Is Not a Bubble. This Is a Survival Race.

Every few years, the investing world gets a new scare word. In 2000 it was “irrational exuberance.” In 2008 it was “subprime.” In 2022 it was “inflation.” And now, in 2025-2026, the word of the moment is “AI bubble.”

You hear it everywhere. Financial commentators on television running segments asking whether we are in a repeat of the dotcom era. WhatsApp groups full of people who bought small-cap Indian stocks at the peak now smugly warning that “AI is the next bubble.” Armchair analysts confusing a 20% drawdown on a mega-cap stock with systemic collapse.

Let me be blunt with you: these people cannot differentiate between drawdowns and bubbles. And that confusion is going to cost them the single largest wealth creation opportunity of their investing lifetimes.

There is no AI bubble. This is a race for survival. When people and countries get desperate, there is no end to spending.

Here is what is actually happening. The United States and China are locked in the most consequential technology supremacy race since the Space Race of the 1960s. The firms at the center of this race – Meta, Google, Amazon, Microsoft, NVIDIA on the American side; Huawei, DeepSeek, and a constellation of state-backed companies on the Chinese side – are not spending hundreds of billions of dollars because some venture capitalist told them it was trendy. They are spending because the alternative is strategic obsolescence. The country that wins AI gets to export its tech stack to the rest of the world. The country that loses becomes a technology colony.

This is not speculation. This is geopolitics. And geopolitics does not care about your PE ratio anxiety.

In this article, I am going to walk you through exactly why the AI supercycle is fundamentally different from every previous technology bubble, how the US-China race creates a spending floor that will not collapse, which companies are positioned to win, and how you – as a retail investor – should structure your portfolio to capture what I believe is the defining investment theme of this decade.

I have roughly 80% of my entire stock portfolio in AI-related positions. I have close to $300,000 USD riding on Meta alone. I am not writing this from the sidelines offering opinions. I am backing every word with real money. Let me show you the logic behind it.

II.

II. Why AI Capex Is Fundamentally Different from Dotcom

The lazy comparison – and it is lazy – goes like this: companies are spending enormous amounts of money on a new technology, stock prices are elevated, therefore it must be a bubble. This is the kind of surface-level thinking that loses people money.

Let me walk you through the structural differences, because they matter enormously.

In the dotcom era, companies were burning venture capital and IPO proceeds. Pets.com, Webvan, eToys – these were pre-revenue or barely-revenue companies that raised money from public markets and lit it on fire trying to acquire customers. When the money ran out, the companies died. The spending was funded by hope, not by cash flow.

In the AI era, the spending is coming from the largest free cash flow machines in human history. When Meta announces $100 billion in AI capital expenditure for 2026[1], that money is not coming from a Series D round or a speculative IPO. It is coming from a business that generates record operating profits, record revenues, and has $40 billion-plus in annual share buybacks running simultaneously. When Amazon spends aggressively on AWS infrastructure, it is backed by a $120 billion-plus cloud business compounding at 20% or more annually.

Capex sustainability = (FCF before capex) ÷ (planned capex) a ratio > 1 means the spending is self-funded; < 1 means it requires external capital

Most of this spending comes from their existing free cash flows. So it is not as if this money is coming from unreasonable capital being raised the way it happens for IPOs.

This distinction is not subtle. It is the entire ballgame. Dotcom companies ran out of money and died. Hyperscalers are generating more cash than they can return to shareholders, and they are choosing to reinvest that cash into AI infrastructure because they believe – based on real revenue data, real customer demand, and real competitive pressure – that this is the highest-return use of capital available to them.

The second critical difference is competition-driven necessity. In the dotcom era, most of the spending was optional and speculative. Nobody forced Kozmo.com to offer free one-hour delivery of Pringles. In the AI era, the spending is driven by competitive survival. Every hyperscaler is spending like crazy because if it does not, then other hyperscalers will eat into their market share. Amazon seemingly overspending might not make the market happy, but it is required for its next layer of growth.

This creates what I call a spending floor. Even if one company wanted to slow down its AI capex, it cannot – because its competitors will not. Microsoft cannot stop spending because Amazon and Google will take Azure’s cloud customers. Meta cannot stop spending because if it falls behind in AI-driven advertising optimization, its $140 billion ad revenue base erodes. NVIDIA cannot stop innovating because Google is building TPUs and Amazon is building Trainium chips.

Well, actually 🧐 — what could make this thesis wrong

The cleanest bear case isn't "AI is a bubble." It's a more specific worry: that revenue per dollar of capex falls faster than expected. Hyperscalers are spending against the assumption that AI inference + training compute demand grows at ~50% CAGR for the next 3-5 years. If that growth halves — because model performance plateaus, or because efficiency gains (DeepSeek, mixture-of-experts, distillation) make the same outputs cost 10x less — then the same capex generates much less revenue, ROIC compresses, and the spending floor cracks.

The right hedge against this isn't to skip AI exposure. It's to overweight the firms whose AI revenue is most diversified across use cases (Microsoft, Google) and underweight pure-play infrastructure plays whose entire thesis depends on training-compute demand staying at this growth rate (some smaller GPU-heavy challengers).

Look at the chart above. Dotcom spending followed a classic boom-bust pattern: a rapid ramp fueled by external capital, followed by a violent collapse when that capital dried up. Hyperscaler AI spending follows a steady, accelerating curve funded by internal cash generation. The spending trajectory is steeper, yes – but the funding source is fundamentally more durable.

The third difference is that AI is already generating real revenue. This is not a “build it and they will come” situation. AWS is a $120 billion-plus business growing at 24%. Google Cloud crossed $40 billion. Meta’s AI-driven ad targeting improvements are directly responsible for its record revenue quarters. These are not projections or TAM slides from a pitch deck. These are audited financial statements showing real money flowing through real AI products.

The dotcom bubble burst because the revenues never materialized. The AI supercycle has revenues growing faster than the capex. That is the opposite of a bubble. That is a business cycle.

III.

III. The US-China Race: Why the Spending Cannot Stop

To understand why AI investment is not going to slow down, you have to stop thinking like a stock analyst and start thinking like a geopolitical strategist. Here is the framework.

The United States’ GDP growth is positive. If you take AI out, it becomes negative. Read that again. The American economy, stripped of its AI-driven growth, is actually contracting. AI is not a nice-to-have sector for the US. It is the only thing keeping the economic engine running.

Now extend this logic:

  1. If AI fails, the US economy struggles fundamentally.
  2. If the US economy struggles, it falls behind in manufacturing and loses the technology race to China.
  3. If China wins AI, Chinese firms become the tech stack of the world. Instead of Meta, Google, and Amazon, the world starts using Huawei, DeepSeek, and ByteDance everywhere.
  4. With tech and manufacturing supremacy, China’s growth rate dramatically exceeds the US growth rate. It overtakes the US faster.
  5. Since China is a net exporter, this creates an inflow of capital into China, creates real wealth for Chinese citizens, and allows China to expand its global influence.
  6. The world leader becomes number two. China uses its soft and hard influence to scale faster. The world moves from holding US debt to holding Chinese debt.

Whoever owns tech supremacy owns the next 20 years at least. Tech supremacy right now equals AI.

This is why the US government is participating in circular financing. The government invested in Intel. NVIDIA invests in OpenAI, and OpenAI places orders with NVIDIA[2]. Microsoft does the same with Anthropic. You might look at this and think it is a casino. In a way, it is. But it is a casino that the US government cannot afford to shut down, because the alternative is strategic defeat.

Well, actually 🧐 — circular financing has historical precedent, and it ended badly

In 1999, Cisco, Lucent, and Nortel were vendor-financing customers (CLECs and dotcoms) who turned around and bought equipment from them. Cisco extended billions in vendor loans; when the customers went bust, Cisco took massive writedowns and the stock fell 86%. The pattern looked identical to today's NVIDIA→OpenAI→NVIDIA loop on the surface.

The structural difference: 1999's circular financing was funded by debt that the equipment vendors raised at increasingly punitive rates as the cycle stretched. Today's loop is funded by hyperscaler free cash flow that is ~10x the size of all 1999 telecom-vendor FCF combined. The loop can sustain a 50%+ revenue drop before any participant runs out of cash. That doesn't mean it survives a 90% drop — but the cushion is real.

On the other side, China is pouring state resources into its own AI ecosystem at a scale that makes even American spending look measured. Two major governments – the US and China – are pumping unprecedented money into privately held technology players. Never in recent history has this happened at this scale simultaneously.

And here is the critical investor insight: when countries get desperate, there is no end to spending. This is not a corporate decision that can be reversed in a quarterly earnings call. This is a national security imperative backed by the full weight of two superpowers. The spending floor is set by geopolitics, not by quarterly FCF guidance.

The practical implication for you as an investor is straightforward. The firms at the center of this race – Meta, Google, Amazon, Microsoft, NVIDIA – are not just companies. They are instruments of national power. They are, to put it plainly, too big to fail in a way that goes beyond the financial definition. The US government has a vested interest in their success that transcends normal market dynamics.

Consider the US-India trade deal framework announced recently. Among the key provisions: both sides aim to ramp up trade in technology, with emphasis on GPUs and data center components, alongside deepened joint tech collaboration. India has pledged to procure $500 billion in US goods over five years, with technology as a core category. This is not just bilateral trade – this is the US exporting its AI tech stack to allied nations, exactly the way the supercycle thesis predicts.

The US is already exporting its tech stack to the UAE, which is building data center capacity that will be optimized by players like Amazon or Meta. If Google’s Waymo starts taxi fleets there, they will use the same data center capacities. China is doing something similar in Africa and Southeast Asia. This is how tech stacks are being built and exported. The country that controls the stack controls the economic relationships.

Firms like Meta, Google, Amazon, NVIDIA, DeepSeek are simply tools to win this race. The sooner you get this as an investor, the better it is for you.

IV. The AI Value Chain: Understanding What You Are Actually Buying

One of the biggest mistakes I see retail investors make with AI is treating it as a monolithic theme. “I want to buy AI stocks” is about as useful as saying “I want to buy food stocks” – it tells you nothing about where in the value chain you are positioned, what your risk exposure is, and what kind of returns you can expect.

Here is how the AI stack actually works, layer by layer:

Layer 1: Compute Hardware (Chips). This is NVIDIA, AMD, TSMC, and ASML. These companies build the fundamental processing power that makes AI possible. NVIDIA’s GPUs are general-purpose parallel processors with extremely wide applicability – AI tensor cores are part of a broader GPU architecture that also handles graphics and non-AI numerical workloads. NVIDIA’s core moat is the CUDA platform plus an extensive stack of SDKs (cuDNN, TensorRT, etc.) that most AI frameworks and research code are optimized for, making GPUs the path of least resistance for engineers. This creates powerful network effects: skills, libraries, and third-party tools concentrate on NVIDIA first, raising switching costs. ASML’s lithographic machines are required by everyone – no other firms can copy these. This layer has the highest margins (NVIDIA’s operating margin has jumped from the mid-teens to above 60% by 2025) but also faces emerging competition.

Layer 2: AI-Powered Cloud Infrastructure. This is Meta, Google, Amazon, and Microsoft building next-generation data centers. These are what some call AI factories – facilities that can handle requirements like instant processing for autonomous vehicles, real-time advertising optimization, and large language model inference. Meta alone is building a $10 billion-plus data center in Louisiana (over 4 million square feet, powered entirely by renewable energy), a $1.5 billion facility in El Paso, Texas, and secured $27 billion from Blue Owl Capital to finance expansion. Zuckerberg has announced that Meta is going to build Meta Compute, which is likely to evolve into one of the largest AI compute platforms globally over this decade.

Layer 3: Application Layer. This is where AI meets specific use cases – Waymo for self-driving cars, ISRG (Intuitive Surgical) for robotic surgeries, Duolingo for AI-powered education, and others. The data on Waymo now conclusively proves that crash rates of autonomous vehicles are lower than human drivers. Earlier a driver could drive a car for 12 hours. Now the cars go around 24 hours a day, 7 days a week – that is a massive productivity unlock via AI. This is happening and will only accelerate.

Layer 4: Optimization and Data Layer. Companies like Snowflake and IREN that optimize data center operations, manage data pipelines, and improve the efficiency of AI workloads.

Layer 5: Security Layer. Firms like Fortinet, CrowdStrike, and Palo Alto Networks that protect the entire AI stack from cyber threats. As AI infrastructure becomes more critical, the security layer becomes more valuable.

Layer 6: Services and Deployment. This is where Indian IT companies like Infosys and TCS find their role – deploying and integrating AI solutions for enterprises. Infosys recently partnered with Anthropic; TCS partnered with AMD. US players will need Indian IT for deployment of AI. It is a role, but it is a supporting role, not a leading one. There are zero core AI firms in India. Someone needs to tell you this straight.

For your portfolio, the highest-conviction positions should be in Layers 1 and 2 – the companies that are both building and deploying AI infrastructure, that have massive distribution moats, and whose spending is backed by enormous free cash flows. This is where Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, and Google sit. They own the research moat, the distribution moat, and increasingly the product moat.

A good firm in 2025 has three things: Research moat, Distribution moat, and Product moat. A great firm controls all three levers.

Meta has a great product right now (Instagram, WhatsApp, Facebook – 40% of the world uses Meta’s product every single day). It is spending $600 billion over the next five years on research. And it already has distribution moat. Therefore, by this framework, Meta qualifies as a great firm for the next five years. Use this same framework for any stock you evaluate.

V. The Numbers That Matter: Revenue, ROIC, and the PE Re-Rating Thesis

Let me get specific with numbers, because investing without numbers is just storytelling.

The blended earnings growth of the top hyperscalers is extraordinary. If you take a basket of Meta, Amazon, Microsoft, and Netflix and blend their earnings growth:

  • 1-Year Earnings CAGR (2024-2025): approximately 25-30%, driven by Amazon’s 33%+ EPS growth and Meta’s efficiency gains
  • 3-Year Earnings CAGR (2022-2025): approximately 20-25%, with Netflix stabilizing subscriber growth and Microsoft’s Azure margins expanding
  • 5-Year Earnings CAGR (2020-2025): approximately 30-35%, led by Amazon’s net income compounding at 42%
  • 10-Year Earnings CAGR (2015-2025): approximately 35-40%, reflecting long-term compounding of cloud and advertising dominance

If you had invested $100,000 USD equally across these four stocks, the value today would be $561,000 USD. That is a CAGR of 19% in USD terms, or close to 23-24% in INR terms. And this is buying without any sophisticated timing – just equal-weight allocation across quality.

For comparison, Indian large caps typically compound earnings at 14% in INR terms, which translates to just 9-10% in USD terms. On any CAGR measurement – 5-year, 7-year, 10-year, 20-year – Nasdaq Composite beats Nifty 50. Yes, you need to adjust for USD for fair comparison. These are facts that zero mutual fund guys will tell you, because it would hurt their SIP flow.

Now, there is a more nuanced point here that most people miss. Markets reward different types of revenue very differently. If a consumer goods company earns $1 in revenue, the market might give it a 10x multiple. But if that same dollar of revenue comes from AI, the market can give it a 30-40x multiple. This is not irrational – AI revenue is higher margin, higher growth, and more scalable than traditional revenue.

This is the PE re-rating thesis for companies like Meta. Currently, 98% of Meta’s revenue comes from advertising. The market prices it as an advertising company. But Meta is spending $100 billion to diversify into AI infrastructure, AI compute (Meta Compute), and hardware. If even 30-40% of Meta’s revenues are eventually classified as AI revenue, the stock gets repriced at a dramatically higher multiple on those dollars. This is exactly what happened with Google – the market re-rated it from a “search company” to an “AI company,” and the stock went up 107%.

Meta’s specific numbers are compelling:

  • Last 3-year EPS CAGR: 20.7% per year
  • Last 5-year EPS CAGR: 30.6% per year
  • Last 10-year EPS CAGR: 36.2% per year
  • Revenues at all-time highs
  • Operating profits at all-time highs
  • Capex guidance of $70-72 billion in 2025, largely for AI infrastructure and data centers
  • Share buybacks exceeding $40 billion over the last 12 months – one of the most aggressive repurchasers in the S&P 500
  • Current market cap: $1.6 trillion, with a conservative path to $3-3.5 trillion within 3 years

In India, we keep looking for 20% CAGR opportunities in INR terms. But when a monopoly stock gives 25% CAGR in INR terms with a global distribution moat, we are too scared to invest. The irony would be funny if it were not so expensive.

Amazon’s numbers tell a similar story. Q4 2025 delivered 14% year-over-year revenue growth to $213.39 billion, beating estimates. AWS led at 24% growth ($35.6 billion), its strongest in years. Advertising hit 22-24% growth. Operating margins improved meaningfully, with AWS income up to $12.5 billion from $10.6 billion. Analysts forecast EPS growth near 28% into 2026 as AI investments yield returns.

Operating margins are the key metric to track. Amazon’s operating margin has moved from low single digits in 2016 to about 11% by 2025. NVIDIA’s operating margin has jumped from the mid-teens to above 60%. These expanding margins tell you that the AI spending is not value-destructive – it is building businesses that convert a higher and higher percentage of revenue into profit.

Stock price follows earnings. If these firms are compounding earnings at 18-25% CAGR, and AI is adding an acceleration layer on top, the stock prices will follow. The only question is timing. And if you have a 2-3 year horizon, the timing question becomes largely irrelevant.

VI.

VI. The "AI Spending Won't Be Recovered" Panic -- And Why It Is Wrong

Right now, the single biggest fear in the market is that hyperscalers are overspending on AI and will never recover their capex. This is what is driving the 20-30% drawdowns on stocks like Meta, Microsoft, and Amazon. Tech stocks are correcting because the top five firms are dumping record cash into AI capex.

Let me address this head-on, because this is where the opportunity lies.

First, understand what heavy capex does to near-term financials. When a company spends aggressively on infrastructure, it temporarily depresses free cash flow. Lower free cash flow limits buybacks, dividends, and debt reduction, which frustrates value investors. It heightens vulnerability to rising rates or economic slowdowns, amplifying volatility. This is real. The market reaction is not irrational – it is responding to genuinely weaker near-term cash flow metrics.

But growth investors evaluate this completely differently. They prioritize metrics like revenue growth, TAM expansion, and unit economics over immediate cash generation, accepting temporary FCF dips as the cost of building long-term competitive advantage.

Here is the critical question: do you trust market analysts, or do you trust the management of these top firms, who have been leaders in this space and know where tech is heading?

Three out of four hyperscalers are still founder-led or founder-heavy. Mark Zuckerberg at Meta, the institutional DNA of Jeff Bezos at Amazon, Satya Nadella’s complete transformation of Microsoft, Sundar Pichai at Google. There is a lot at stake for them personally. These firms waited at least a decade to dump money onto something they think is viable. It is no accident that all of them – at the same exact time – decided to go all-in on data centers and infrastructure.

These companies have explicitly said the risk of underspending scares them far more than overspending. And when you look at the data, you understand why:

  • AWS is a $120 billion-plus machine compounding at 20%+. This is the first layer where AI directly generates revenue. AI boosts AWS via inference as a core computing layer, enabling agents that automate tasks beyond summarization. Developers cut coding time 50%+ with Amazon Q. Custom chips reduce costs 30-50% versus GPUs.
  • Advertising is the second layer. Meta and Amazon are already seeing AI-driven improvements in ad targeting that directly boost revenue per impression. Meta’s record revenue quarters are not despite AI spending – they are because of it.
  • Operating expense reduction is the third layer. As AI tools automate tasks across these companies’ own operations – coding, customer support, content moderation, logistics optimization – operating margins expand even as top-line spending increases. Anthropic recently released 11 new plug-ins for its Claude agent, automating tasks like data analysis, presentation creation, and even some sales functions. This is the kind of productivity gain that flows directly to the bottom line of every company deploying these tools.

The comparison I keep coming back to is this: Meta building AI data centers today is like Ford Motors putting the first industrial factories in the US for auto manufacturing in the early 1900s. Did it look ridiculous back then? Yes. Because people continued to believe that cars would never replace horses. Flash forward a century, and we know who was right.

Now people are calling such spending on AI ridiculous. For you as investors, you have a call to make: do you see this spending as necessary, or do you think this is just waste of money? I am backing the thesis that it is necessary. This is not a short-term play, but a clear diversification of revenues and a gearing up for the next decade of growth by investing in critical infrastructure needed to support entire industries: autonomous vehicles, robotic surgeries, drone deliveries, personalized advertising.

The current AI push is not a Meta-specific play or an Amazon-specific play. This is being done by almost all top tech firms in the US simultaneously. This is very different from Meta’s Metaverse play, which was a company-specific bet. There are higher odds for success when the entire industry is moving in the same direction. The Metaverse was one company’s vision. AI is the entire tech industry’s bet – and two governments’ survival strategy.

People saying AI bubble run a huge risk in terms of missing the upside. The cost of missing the upside is much higher than the downside risk.

VII. How to Actually Build an AI Portfolio

Now let me get practical. Knowing the thesis is useless if you do not know how to execute it. Here is how I think about building an AI-first portfolio, and what I am doing with my own money.

The core allocation: 50-80% of your stock portfolio should be AI-themed. I know this sounds aggressive. It is aggressive. But honestly, in life you have to take concentrated risks when it makes sense. This is one of those once-in-a-generation stories. If you simply build an 80% AI-themed portfolio, sit tight and do nothing, you might be sitting on your generational wealth opportunity.

At minimum, your portfolio should be at least 25% AI. Bill Ackman recently put $2 billion on Meta stock. If one of the sharpest allocators in the world is making a concentrated bet, it should at least prompt you to examine the thesis seriously.

The “no-brainer” tier: Meta, Amazon, Microsoft. If you do not know much about investing, simply buy Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft. These have close to zero chance of getting disrupted by AI – because they are the ones doing the disrupting. And all three trade below their long-term PE ratios, so you are still not buying expensive. These are monopoly businesses with 20% CAGR growth profiles at decent valuations.

Here is the specific sizing I use:

  • Meta: 8-10% of portfolio. My average buying price is around $590-595. I have roughly $300,000 USD in the stock. My analysis says it should get to $1,000 within 2-3 years. The firm currently sits at $1.6 trillion market cap. Even conservatively, with the resources it is pouring in, it should go to $3-3.5 trillion in the next 3 years. That is 100% upside. Every dip is a massive buy.
  • Amazon: 8-10% of portfolio. Record revenue growth, AWS at 24% growth, advertising scaling. Limit to 8-10% of portfolio, max it out, and wait.
  • Microsoft: 5-12% of portfolio. Start with 5% and keep room to take this up to 10-12% over time. Azure’s margin expansion story is intact.

The high-conviction tier: NVIDIA, Google. These require more active management because they have higher volatility. NVIDIA’s operating margin above 60% is extraordinary, but it faces emerging competition from Google TPUs and Amazon’s custom chips. My fair price for NVIDIA from DCF analysis is around $210. Buying dips is the strategy, with a maximum allocation of 10%. Build positions at the mean of the Bollinger Band (around 5% allocation), add more at the lower band or 200DMA (up to 7-7.5%), and max out at 10% only at extreme oversold levels.

Google is interesting because the market has already started re-rating it as an AI company. Our community bought Google, and the stock is up 107% on our portfolio. My biggest regret is keeping Google to just 5% of my portfolio. I should have backed it with more money.

The satellite tier: higher risk, higher reward. Stocks like Nebius (AI infrastructure, down 43% from peak, $20 billion market cap with management guiding $7-9 billion in revenues, 5-year pre-orders from Microsoft and Meta), IONQ (quantum computing hardware, collaborations with Amazon, Microsoft, and Google Cloud), Duolingo (AI-powered education, pivoting to become a casual learning platform for math, chess, and more), and CrowdStrike (AI security). These get 1-5% allocations each. They can get wiped out or deliver 10x. Size them accordingly.

The hedge tier: Indian IT. For every 1 unit of Indian IT in your portfolio, you should have 3-4 units of hyperscalers in the US. Indian IT is a turnaround and valuation play, not a growth play. If AI disrupts IT, your AI stocks compensate for the loss. If AI does not move the way you expect, then IT moves back. It is a natural internal hedge within a tech-heavy portfolio. Max out Indian IT positions at 15-20% of your Indian portfolio, and stay put.

Think about it. If Software/IT gets disrupted, who benefits? AI. But what if the thesis on AI is wrong? You are still buying software at a discount. This is an internal hedge.

How to actually buy – the aggregation system. You do not dump 100% of your allocation into a stock on day one. Here is the system:

  1. Buy 30-40% of your intended position at the first support level
  2. If it hits another support, add another 20-30%
  3. If it falls further, add another 20-30%
  4. Then you stop. Fix a percentage exposure to the theme and you are done
  5. Do not make investing an ego issue. Diversify, manage positions, book profits if an asset moves up

This might take months to play out. You are following a system, not chasing dopamine. The key technical levels to watch: Bollinger Band mean (build to 50% of position), 200DMA or lower Bollinger Band (build to 75%), and strong historic support (max out the position).

VIII. Managing Drawdowns: The Hardest Part of the AI Trade

Let me be honest about something. If you cannot handle a 20-30% drawdown on a stock, this strategy is not for you. Period.

Here is what the drawdowns have looked like in recent months:

  • Meta: -30% from top
  • Microsoft: -30% from top
  • Amazon: -20-25% from top
  • NVIDIA: -15% from top
  • Palantir, SoFi: around -30%
  • Bitcoin: -40-45%
  • Silver: -40%

These are not fringe companies. These are the largest, most profitable businesses on the planet. And they are down 20-30% from their highs. If that terrifies you, if you are the person posting panic messages because NVIDIA fell 2-3% the day after you bought it, then stock investing is genuinely not right for you. Sorry to be blunt, but someone has to tell you straight.

The Meta case study is the best teacher here. In February 2021, Meta had significant issues. Revenues were declining, valuations were super high, ad spend from clients was declining because interest rates were rising, and there was a broad tech correction. Meta dropped from a stock price of almost $400 to $80. An 80% drawdown. Catastrophic.

Now, the funny part: if you did nothing – absolutely nothing – held the stock at $400, went through the entire correction, and held it until 2025, the stock went to $800. That is a 19% CAGR through what felt like an apocalyptic drawdown. If you bought the dip along the way, added options, traded actively, your CAGR would have been at least 25-30%. When you see golden stocks fall, buy – do not hesitate. And be okay with the correction.

The critical point about the current drawdown: the 2025-2026 correction does not even have shrinking revenues or financial stress. In fact, the firms are set to expand capacity. The businesses are stronger than they have ever been. The drawdown is entirely about the market’s short-term anxiety over capex spending – the same spending that will generate the next decade of growth.

Look at that chart. The stock price went through a rollercoaster, but revenue barely dipped and then accelerated dramatically. The disconnect between stock price and business fundamentals during drawdowns is where the money is made. Every single time Meta’s stock fell 20% or more while revenues were growing, it was a buying opportunity that produced 50%+ returns within 18 months.

There are three strategies for managing drawdowns:

1. Cash buffer strategy. Keep 15-20% of your portfolio in cash. If you have 100 units in the market, keep 15-20 aside. This gives you dry powder to buy dips without having to sell existing positions at unfavorable prices. For the Indian market, keep roughly 85% invested and 15% cash. For the US market, given the stronger growth trajectory, stay 90%+ invested.

2. Options hedging. This is more advanced, but enormously powerful. For example, buying QQQ put options at an exercise price 10% below current market price with a long-dated expiry creates a floor on your losses. If Nasdaq corrects by 50%, your maximum loss might be capped at 14% from the peak. If you are already sitting on 30%+ profits, your worst case becomes 20% net profits even in a catastrophic scenario. This is what serious portfolio management looks like.

3. Aggressive aggregation on dips. When you are looking at a high-quality growth asset in a recovery pattern, doubling down as it falls can dramatically improve your average cost basis. This works exceptionally well on growth assets. It is a bad strategy in a sideways market – so you need to have conviction about the long-term trajectory.
