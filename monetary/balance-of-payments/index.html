Balance of Payments Is an Identity

A trade deficit IS a capital surplus — by accounting identity. When the US imports $1 trillion more than it exports, foreigners must be net buyers of $1 trillion of US assets. There is no choice in the matter; only the exchange rate, interest rate, and panic level at which the rebalancing clears.

I.

Why does “balance of payments must balance” sound circular?

Here is a sentence that gets passed around every cable-news panel and every G7 communiqué: “The US trade deficit is unsustainable.” Right now, in FY2026, that deficit is running at roughly $1.0 trillion a year — about 3.5% of GDP — and the cumulative tab, the US net international investment position, has hit negative $24 trillion. That number gets thrown around like a dripping debt clock.

I want to convince you the sentence is meaningless, or at least: it doesn’t mean what people think it means.

Here is the one thing this article proves: the current account and the capital account sum to zero. Mechanically. By accounting identity. A trade deficit IS a capital surplus — same arrow, two names. If the US imports more than it exports, foreigners must be net buyers of US assets to the exact same dollar. There is no choice in the matter; only the price — the exchange rate, the interest rate, the panic level — at which the rebalancing happens.

That sounds circular because it is circular, in the way that “debits equal credits” is circular on every balance sheet ever drawn. It’s not a theory. It’s double-entry bookkeeping applied to a country instead of a company. And once you internalise it, half the macro commentary you read — “the dollar is strong because foreigners are funding us,” “China props up Treasuries,” “the petrodollar recycles surpluses” — collapses into a single sentence, viewed from two sides.

By the end you’ll be able to explain, without notes, why the US “trade deficit” and “foreigners own $9.5T of Treasuries”[1] are the same fact, observed twice. And why — in the article after this — the 1974 Saudi oil-for-Treasuries handshake wasn’t a clever political coup so much as the inevitable accounting that had to happen once the US started importing oil and Saudi started running surpluses.

Last article (Triffin’s Dilemma) we established that being the world’s reserve currency requires running a current account deficit — the world needs dollars, you have to ship them out somehow. This article proves the other half: the moment you ship them out, you’ve also imported the offsetting claim. The two are the same act.

II.

What is the current account?

The current account is the running tally of every real-economy flow in and out of a country in a given year. Four buckets, and you can fit them on a Post-it:

CA = (Exports − Imports) + Net income + Net transfers Goods & services trade balance, plus income on foreign assets net of payments to foreigners, plus remittances and aid

For the US in FY2026, the goods trade balance alone is about negative $1.2 trillion. Services run a small surplus (about +$280B, mostly software, finance, IP licensing, tourism). Net investment income is roughly flat — surprising given the US is net debtor, and we’ll come back to that in a sidenote. Net transfers (remittances out, foreign aid out) sit around −$160B. Add the four and you get the headline: CA ≈ −$1.0T, the famous “trade deficit” even though strictly speaking it’s the broader current account that’s in the red.

Two things to notice. First, this is a flow, not a stock. It’s the year’s drip into the bathtub, not the level of water in the tub. Second, every line is a real-economy transaction: a Boeing 787 leaving Seattle for Air India is +$300M of exports; a Toyota Camry arriving in Long Beach is −$28K of imports; Apple paying a German engineer is −$200K of services. There are no financial assets in this account. No bonds, no equities, no FX reserves. Just goods, services, wages, dividends, transfers.

Well, actually 🧐 — the “exorbitant privilege” explains why US net income is flat despite being a $24T net debtor

Standard accounting says: if you owe foreigners $24T and they have parked it in US Treasuries earning ~4%, you should be sending them ~$1T a year in interest. The US sends roughly nothing on net. Why?

Because the assets US investors hold abroad — direct investment in European factories, equity stakes in Asian tech, FDI in Latin American resources — earn far more than the bonds foreigners hold here. Average return on US-owned foreign assets has been ~6–7% over decades; foreigners earn ~3–4% on US assets. The gap is what Giscard d’Estaing called France’s “privilège exorbitant” in 1965.

This is a real economic gift — about $200–300B a year in net income that the standard “debtor nation” framing completely misses. It also means the US can keep running deficits longer than the arithmetic of a normal debtor would allow. We will come back to this in the reserve-currency article.

So far, so accounting. Now the other half of the ledger.

III.

What is the capital account?

The capital account — technically the IMF now calls it the “financial account,” with the “capital account” reserved for a tiny sub-bucket of one-off transfers like debt forgiveness, but every working trader still says “capital account” and so will I — is the mirror image. It tracks every financial-asset flow.

When a Japanese pension fund buys a US Treasury, that’s a capital inflow to the US: a foreign claim on a US asset. When BlackRock buys a French bond, that’s a capital outflow: a US claim on a foreign asset. Net them across the year and you get the financial account balance, KA.

KA = Foreign purchases of domestic assets − Domestic purchases of foreign assets Positive KA = capital flowing in (foreigners are net buyers of your stuff); negative = capital flowing out

For the US in FY2026, KA is positive: net foreign purchases of US assets ran about +$1.0 trillion. Foreigners bought $9.5T of Treasuries (cumulative, current stock), plus equities, plus corporate bonds, plus direct investment in US real estate and factories. US investors bought a lot of foreign assets too — but they bought less than foreigners bought of us. The net tilted in by about $1T.

Now look at the two numbers we just produced:

Current account: −$1.0T (we sent the world $1T more in goods/services than they sent us)
Capital account: +$1.0T (the world sent us $1T more in financial assets than we sent them)

That equality is not a coincidence. Not a happy accident of 2026. Not a thing that “usually happens because foreigners are confident in America.” It is a tautology, true every year, in every country, in every regime, for reasons you can derive from the definition of a transaction.

IV.

Why must the two sum to zero?

Strip the question to one transaction. A US importer wants a $30,000 Toyota. The Toyota gets loaded on a ship in Yokohama and arrives in Long Beach. The US importer hands over $30,000 to Toyota.

Pause there. Where does Toyota’s $30,000 go?

It does not vaporise. It does not return to a hole in Toyota City. There are exactly three things Toyota can do with those dollars:

  1. Buy something American. A Boeing 787, soybeans, a Manhattan apartment, a US Treasury, a share of Microsoft, an oil-rig stake in Texas. Anything denominated in dollars and located in the US.
  2. Hold them as US-dollar deposits (in a US-bank account, or via a JPY-USD swap with a counterparty who ends up holding them). That deposit is itself a claim on the US banking system — a US asset.
  3. Sell them to someone else for yen. But then that someone now holds the $30,000 and faces the same three choices. Pass the parcel; the parcel doesn’t disappear.

Every branch ends the same way: the $30,000 ends up held as, or invested in, a US asset. The dollars literally cannot leave the US financial system — they only change owners inside it. (They’re US-bank-system liabilities; they live on the Fed’s ledgers and member-bank balance sheets. They have no passport.)

So the Camry transaction produces, by construction:

  • One Camry crosses the Pacific eastbound ⇒ current account: −$30K (import).
  • One claim-on-the-US (Treasury, deposit, equity, whatever Toyota chose) crosses the Pacific westbound, on paper ⇒ capital account: +$30K (foreign-owned US asset).

Sum: zero. Always. Every single transaction. Add up every transaction in the year and the country-level CA + KA = 0.

In real-world data the two columns don’t quite match, because national statistical agencies are not omniscient — that residual is called the “statistical discrepancy” and it’s a sidenote you should read.

Well, actually 🧐 — the “statistical discrepancy” line is usually billions

Look at any country’s actual BoP release and you’ll see a third line: “net errors and omissions” or “statistical discrepancy.” In US data it’s typically ±$50–200B in a year — not nothing. China’s has hit −$300B and triggered serious capital-flight investigations.

The identity still holds in theory. The discrepancy exists because customs agents miss containers, tax-avoidance schemes route invoices through third countries, and capital flows through Cayman shell companies are imputed rather than observed. The IMF estimates the global current account should sum to zero (every country’s deficit is some other country’s surplus) — it doesn’t, by about $400B a year. That’s a measurement issue, not an economics issue.

The point: the identity is true by construction. The data is approximate. Don’t confuse the two.

So if you can’t break the identity, what does “the dollar is overvalued” or “China is propping us up” even mean? It means something about the price at which the identity clears — which we’ll see in a minute. First, who actually shows up.

V.

Who funds the US’s $1T annual trade deficit?

“Funds” is a slightly loaded word — it suggests the deficit is a hole that requires filling, when actually it’s the same fact as “net foreigners bought $1T of US stuff this year.” But fine, let’s look at who is on the other side of that arrow.

The CA and KA columns are wildly different in size and sign across them, but in every single case they sum to roughly zero. Saudi’s 2024–25 numbers are the surprise — explained below.

The headline reading: US runs a ~$1T current-account deficit, matched by ~$1T of net foreign asset purchases. The biggest single buyers are Japan ($1.24T cumulative UST holdings, +$113B YoY), the UK ($897B, much of it basis-trade hedge funds, not real money), Cayman ($443B reported, likely closer to $2T true), and the Euro Area aggregate ($2.0T). China + Hong Kong is at $962B and has been a net seller for two years running (−$96B YoY).

Notice the composition shift. The classic 2000s picture was “Chinese central bank recycles trade surplus into Treasuries” — an official-sector flow. The 2026 picture is different: NY Fed custody of foreign-CB Treasury holdings has dropped to $2.70T, the lowest since 2012, with an $82B drop in just five weeks[2]. The official sector is leaving. Private leveraged buyers — Cayman hedge funds doing the cash-futures basis trade — are stepping in. The identity still holds; the who behind it has rotated.

Well, actually 🧐 — Saudi Arabia ran current-account DEFICITS in 2024–25

The mental model says: oil producer with $80 Brent should be running a fat current-account surplus, recycling petrodollars into Treasuries. The 2024–25 data says no — the Kingdom ran current-account deficits in both years, partly because Saudi’s fiscal breakeven oil price is now over $90/bbl. MBS’s domestic Vision 2030 spending — Neom, the Murabba cube, World Cup infrastructure — has pushed government spending to a level the oil price doesn’t cover.

Brad Setser at CFR puts it bluntly: “This is fun to believe, but not really based in reality... not when their balance of payments breakeven is over $90 a barrel.”[3] The GCC sovereigns are net dollar-bond issuers, not net UST buyers. Their dollar exposure runs through PIF and ADIA equity stakes, not central-bank Treasury holdings.

This matters for the next two articles in this series. The petrodollar-recycling story has a real mechanism; it just isn’t the simple “Saudi buys UST” loop people imagine. The capital flow runs through equity, real estate, and PE — and it’s much smaller than the popular telling.

Two reminders before we move on. First: the identity is a residual, not a target. Nobody at the BEA computes a deficit then phones Tokyo to ask for matching capital. The matching happens because every individual transaction is double-entry. Second: the identity says nothing about the price at which it clears. That’s where the interesting macro lives — and it’s what blows up in a crisis.

VI.

What happens when foreigners refuse to fund?

This is the question that should make you nervous about US debt — not the level, but the repricing. The identity holds in every state of the world. But the exchange rate, interest rate, and import volume required to make it hold can move violently.

Imagine a small open economy — call it Thailand, July 1997. It’s been running a current-account deficit of about 8% of GDP, financed by foreign banks lending in dollars to Thai banks and corporates. The capital account is healthily in surplus. Identity satisfied.

Then the foreign banks decide they’ve had enough. Loans don’t roll. Capital tries to leave — KA flips from +8% to −5% essentially overnight. The identity now requires CA to flip from −8% to +5%. By accounting, the country must run a 13 percentage-point swing in its current account, in months.

It cannot do this by exporting 13% more rice. It does it by collapsing the currency until imports become unaffordable. The Thai baht fell 56% in six months. Imports cratered. The trade balance ripped to surplus by force. Indonesia and Korea followed the same arithmetic in the same year.

This is what people mean when they talk about a “currency crisis” or a “sudden stop.” It’s the BoP identity asserting itself the only way it can when capital walks: the currency takes the hit until the trade balance moves enough to close the gap.

And it doesn’t only happen to emerging markets. UK 2022, the Truss mini-budget: a fiscal package the gilt market hated. Foreign holders of sterling started to leave. The pound dropped 8% against the dollar in a week, gilt yields spiked 150bp at the long end in two days, and the Bank of England had to intervene to stop pension funds from being margin-called into oblivion[4]. Same identity, same forced rebalancing — just on a faster timescale in a G7 economy with an open capital account and a leveraged pension system.

The point worth burning in: the US runs a 3.5%-of-GDP current account deficit because it is currently a sufficiently attractive destination for foreign capital that the price-clearing dollar-yield combination lets that flow happen. Change “currently” and the dollar moves, yields move, or import volume moves. It will balance one way or another — the question is just how much breakage along the way.

VII.

Why the petrodollar IS the capital-account side of oil imports

Now we can land the punch the next article is built on.

Through most of the 1970s, the US ran a structural current-account deficit driven heavily by oil imports. Oil arrived from the Gulf. Dollars left the US for the Gulf. By the identity we just established, those dollars had nowhere to go except back into US assets. They could not vanish. They could not become yen except by transferring the holding-the-bag problem to whoever bought them. They had to land somewhere in the US financial system.

The 1974 Kissinger–Simon arrangement with Saudi Arabia did not create the recycling. The accounting identity created the recycling. What the arrangement did was choose the channel: Saudi would recycle via off-market US Treasury purchases (and accept US military protection in return), rather than via, say, equity in Citibank or real estate in London[5].

Same arrow, two names: oil current-account outflow = petrodollar capital-account inflow. The dollar payment for the barrel and the foreign-owned Treasury are literally the same money, observed at two stages of its lifecycle.

So when people say things like “Saudi could stop pricing oil in dollars and crash the Treasury market,” they’re misreading the identity. As long as the US imports more than it exports — from Saudi Arabia or anyone else — the matching capital inflow must exist. The only question is the channel. If Saudi stops buying Treasuries, the dollars they receive for oil still have to be held by someone; they go to Cayman, or to UK basis-trade hedge funds, or to Japanese pension funds via FX swaps, or to PIF’s equity book in Manhattan. The aggregate flow doesn’t move; the composition does.

What can happen — what is happening — is that the official sector’s share of holding US assets is collapsing while the leveraged-private sector’s share is rising. NY Fed custody at the lowest since 2012; Cayman at probably $2T and 8–50x leveraged. Same identity, more fragile holders.

You came in thinking “trade deficit” and “Treasury holdings” were two separate problems. Leave knowing they are the same problem viewed from opposite sides of the same arrow. The next article (The Petrodollar Origin) takes that identity and lays the 1974 deal on top of it — what was choice, what was bookkeeping, and which is which is the only question that matters.

CA + KA ≡ 0. Always. The current account is the goods view; the capital account is the assets view; they describe the same set of transactions, signed opposite. The exchange rate, the interest rate, and the panic level are the three prices at which the identity clears — never whether it clears.

That’s the napkin. Every macro story you read about “foreigners funding America” or “the dollar weaponised” or “de-dollarisation” is, underneath, a story about which prices move and how violently when the composition of who holds the matching capital changes. Never about whether the matching capital exists. It always exists. The double-entry guarantees it.
