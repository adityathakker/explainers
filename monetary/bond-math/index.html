I.

Why does “yields up” matter so much?

Last week the Japanese 30-year government bond yield hit an all-time record — not since-2010, not since-the-financial-crisis, all-time[1]. The 10-year US Treasury is back above 4.4% after a brief dip. The 30-year is at levels last seen in 2007. Every developed-market long-end yield is breaking out at the same time.

That sounds like a small move. It isn't. A holder of a long-dated bond bought at issuance can lose more on the price than they've earned in coupons across two decades. At scale, the same arithmetic eats the US federal budget: the Treasury is on track to spend about $1 trillion on interest this year — roughly 19% of all federal revenue[2]. Every additional 100 basis points the curve reprices higher costs another $3.2 trillion over the CBO's ten-year window.

The entire monetary series is about why long-end yields are breaking everywhere at once — petrodollar erosion, foreign-CB demand collapsing into leveraged hedge-fund basis trades, term premium repricing, the works. None of it lands if you can't first feel why a 1% move on a long bond is a portfolio-ending event.

So this article proves one thing: a 1% yield rise on a 30-year bond destroys about 17% of its price — and about 20% for the low-coupon bonds issued during QE. On a 2-year bond, the same move costs about 2%. Duration is the only number that matters at the long end, and the math fits on a napkin.

Once you have the napkin, every other article in this series is arithmetic you can do in your head.

II.

What is a bond, really?

Strip away the jargon. A bond is a contract to receive a fixed schedule of cash payments on specified dates. That's it.

A 30-year US Treasury with a 4% coupon and $100 face value commits the US government to send you $2 every six months for thirty years, and then a final $100 at maturity. Sixty $2 coupons plus one $100 principal repayment. Those numbers do not change for the life of the bond, no matter what happens to interest rates, the Fed, the war, the election, or the price the bond trades at on a Tuesday afternoon.

The bond's price is what someone is willing to pay today for the right to that schedule. And the right answer to "what is it worth today" is: add up all the future cash flows, but discount each one for how far away it is. A $100 payment thirty years from now is not worth $100 today; it is worth less, because between now and then you could have earned interest on something else.

Price = ∑t=1..N   CFt ÷ (1 + y)t CFt is the cash flow at time t; y is the discount rate per period; N is the number of periods

That's the entire pricing formula. The cash flows on the left are contractual. The discount rate y on the right is market. When you read a headline saying "the 30-year yield rose 10 basis points," that's the market changing the y in this formula. The contractual cash flows didn't move a cent. The present value of those cash flows changed because the discount applied to them changed.

This is the most important sentence in this article: the yield is the discount rate that makes the present value of the future cash flows equal to the current price. Price and yield are two ways of writing the same number. Quoting one implies the other.

Well, actually 🧐 — "the" yield isn't unique

The "yield to maturity" assumes you reinvest every coupon at the same yield, which never happens in practice. A bond also has a "current yield" (coupon ÷ price), a "yield to call" if it's callable, a "real yield" (after inflation), and a slew of others. When traders say "the yield" without qualification, they mean yield-to-maturity, and they accept the reinvestment-assumption fiction because it gives one clean number per bond.

The bigger point is unchanged: any reasonable yield definition is the discount rate that, when applied to the contractual cash flows, reproduces today's price. The maths is the same; only the assumption set differs.

Two facts: cash flows fixed, discount rate floating. Memorise those and the rest of the article is just looking at consequences.

III.

Why are price and yield inverse?

If y goes up, every term in the sum CFt ÷ (1+y)t gets smaller. The denominator grew; the fraction shrank. The price — the sum of those fractions — falls.

If y goes down, every denominator shrinks, every fraction grows, and the price rises. There is no decision-making here, no “the market thinks” involved. It is arithmetic. Price and yield are mechanically inverse.

The interesting question is not that they are inverse — it's by how much. A 1% rise in yield doesn't shave 1% off the price. It can shave 2%. Or 20%. The magnitude depends on the bond's maturity, its coupon, and the level of yields. Well, actually 🧐 — real-world 30-years lose more than this widget shows

The widget uses a 4% coupon for both bonds because that puts them at par and makes the visual cleanly interpretable. But most outstanding US 30-year Treasuries were issued during 2020–2021, when coupons were as low as 1.25%. A 1.25%-coupon 30-year repriced from a 4% yield to a 5% yield falls from about $52 to about $42 — a 20% loss, not 15%.

Lower coupons mean more of the bond’s value is concentrated in the distant principal repayment, which pushes duration up — closer to 21–22 years for a 2020-vintage long bond, versus the 17 years our widget shows. That is why the actual mark-to-market on the Fed’s and Japanese banks’ long-duration bond books has been so brutal: a low coupon multiplies the rate sensitivity.

This is the asymmetry that runs the rest of the article. Now we need a name for it.

IV.

What is duration, really?

Duration has two definitions that sound different and turn out to be (almost) the same number.

The first is Macaulay duration: the weighted average time to the bond's cash flows, where each weight is the present value of that cash flow as a share of the total. A 30-year zero-coupon bond has a Macaulay duration of exactly 30 years — the one and only cash flow lands at year 30. A 30-year coupon bond has a Macaulay duration somewhat less than 30, because some of the cash arrives earlier as coupons.

The second is Modified duration: the percentage change in price for a 1-unit change in yield. This is the one that matters for trading:

dP ÷ P  ≈  − Dmod · dy Dmod is modified duration in years; dy is the change in yield as a decimal (1% = 0.01)

Read that formula in English: "a one-percent rise in yield causes roughly a D-percent fall in price." A bond with a modified duration of 17 loses about 17% when yields rise 1%. A bond with a modified duration of 2 loses about 2%. Same formula, vastly different consequences, depending on the duration number.

The two definitions are linked by: Dmod = DMacaulay ÷ (1 + y). For the yields we care about, the difference is small. When somebody says "duration" without qualification, they mean modified.

So duration is the bond's price-sensitivity to yield, with units of years, and it doubles as a rough indicator of how far in the future the bond's cash flows sit. A 30-year coupon Treasury at 4% has a modified duration of about 17 years. A 2-year coupon Treasury at 4% has a modified duration of about 1.9 years. Multiply by a 1% yield move and you get the price impact: ~17% on the long, ~1.9% on the short. That is the answer to Widget 1.

Geometrically, duration is the slope of the price-yield curve at the current yield, scaled by price. Move the yield a little; the price moves by that slope, times the yield change. V.

Why does a 30-year get destroyed when a 2-year barely moves?

The mechanical answer is hiding in the discount formula. Re-examine the present value of a single cash flow:

PV(CFt) = CFt ÷ (1 + y)t t is the number of years until the cash flow arrives

If yield rises by 1 percentage point — from 4% to 5% — the denominator for a cash flow at year t grows by a factor of (1.05/1.04)t. For t = 1, that's a factor of 1.0096 — the PV shrinks by less than 1%. For t = 30, the factor is 1.328 — the PV of that distant cash flow shrinks by about 25%.

The repricing compounds with time. Every additional year between now and the cash flow gets its own multiplicative discount-rate hit. A 30-year bond has all its principal sitting thirty years out, fully exposed to that compounded hit. A 2-year bond has its principal arriving in 24 months — the same yield move barely touches it because there's almost nothing to compound over.

This is why the long end of the curve does the political work in any bond-market crisis. When commentators say “the market is forcing a fiscal reckoning,” they mean: the 30-year is dropping faster than the Treasury can swap out its short-term bills. Bessent's recent fixation on the 10-year specifically — he has said publicly he is “focused on the 10-year Treasury” — is the political acknowledgment that the long end is the pressure point[3].

Well, actually 🧐 — the US Treasury duration is shorter than you think

The average maturity of US federal debt is around 6 years — much shorter than the 30-year bond's 17-year duration suggests. The Treasury funds itself heavily in bills (under 1 year) and 2-to-7-year notes, partly because they're easier to sell and partly because their duration is shorter so they show less price volatility on the Fed's balance sheet.

But this is a double-edged sword. Short funding means low duration risk on each bond, but it means the Treasury is constantly rolling over — if yields stay high for years, every refinancing happens at the new higher rate. That's how the 1% ⇒ $3.2T figure compounds: not because every existing bond is hit, but because the Treasury keeps issuing into the higher curve.

VI.

When does the duration model break?

Duration is a linear approximation. It is the first derivative of the price-yield curve at the current yield. Linear approximations are very good in a neighbourhood and progressively worse as you move away from the anchor point. Bond price-yield curves are not lines; they are convex — they curve upward as yield falls and the price rises, and curve more gently as yield rises and the price falls.

The practical consequence is that duration overestimates the price loss when yields rise and underestimates the price gain when yields fall. Both errors favour the bondholder. Convexity is a free lunch.

For ordinary moves of 25–50 basis points, duration is plenty. For a sustained regime change of 200–300 basis points — the kind of move long bonds have actually made since the 2020 lows — convexity matters enough that traders quote it as a second number alongside duration. The full second-order Taylor expansion is:

dP ÷ P  ≈  − Dmod · dy  +  ½ · C · (dy)2 C is convexity; the second term is always positive, hence the bondholder’s edge

For the rest of this series — and most macro discussion in 2026 — duration alone is the right tool. The price-y arithmetic is dominated by the first-order term. Convexity is the rounding error you only care about at extremes.

VII.

What does this cost the US Treasury?

Time to land the napkin.

The US has about $39 trillion in federal debt outstanding, of which roughly $28 trillion is held by the public (the rest is intra-government accounting). The CBO publishes a clean sensitivity number: a sustained 100 basis point rise across the curve raises debt service by roughly $3.2 trillion over the ten-year projection window[2]. That is not a duration calculation on the existing stock; it is the compounded cost of issuing into a higher curve as the Treasury rolls and adds new debt.

The current ACM model estimate of the 10-year term premium is roughly +0.68% as of April 2026 — the first sustained positive reading since 2023, and roughly 80 basis points above the deeply negative levels of the QE era[4]. That one factor alone — just the rebuilding of term premium — has shifted the long end about as far as the CBO's +100 bp sensitivity scenario contemplates. The 10-year cost of that re-rating, on the CBO's arithmetic, is on the order of $2.5 trillion in net present value.

This is why every Treasury Secretary speech now mentions the long end. It is why Yellen herself raised fiscal dominance at the AEA in January. It is why Bessent's $20 billion swap line to the UAE in April reads as financial-plumbing diplomacy, not foreign policy. The bond-market arithmetic has become the operating constraint on US fiscal policy.

You came in to understand why a TV chyron about “the 30-year hitting 5%” matters. You leave with the napkin:

Yield up by Δy. Price down by roughly D × Δy. Multiply by debt outstanding for the dollar cost. Multiply by ten years for the Treasury's roll-and-pay arithmetic.

That's it. That's the whole napkin. Now we can go look at why the curve is moving — reserve-currency mechanics, balance-of-payments identities, the 1974 deal, the recycling loop, the basis trade replacing foreign central banks, the term-premium repricing — and at every step in the next eight articles, you'll be able to translate “the 30-year did X” into “the Treasury owes Y more.”

That translation is the whole game.
