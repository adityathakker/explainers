I.

The Axiom: Why Insurance Exists

Before we touch a formula, we need to understand one idea. It is the foundation everything else rests on. Get this right and the rest is arithmetic.

Individual risk is catastrophic. Pooled risk is manageable.

A farmer owns one cow worth ₹80,000. If that cow dies, the farmer loses ₹80,000 — possibly their entire livelihood. The probability of any single cow dying in a year is about 3.5%. Small probability, devastating consequence. This is the structure of risk that creates demand for insurance.

Now imagine 1,000 farmers, each with one cow worth ₹80,000. If each farmer puts ₹2,800 into a common pool, the pool collects ₹28,00,000 (₹28 lakh). On average, 35 cows will die. The pool pays out 35 × ₹80,000 = ₹28,00,000. The pool breaks even.

Each farmer traded a small certain loss (₹2,800 premium) for protection against a large uncertain loss (₹80,000). That is the entire concept. That is insurance.

The fundamental axiom

Insurance works because the cost of a rare, devastating event, when spread across many people, becomes a small, affordable payment for each person. The insurer is the mechanism that makes pooling possible.

II.

The Law of Large Numbers

The risk pool works because of a mathematical law that is the bedrock of all insurance: the law of large numbers[1].

Here is the intuition. Flip a coin 10 times. You might get 7 heads and 3 tails — that is 70% heads, far from the “true” 50%. But flip it 10,000 times and you will get very close to 50%. The more trials you run, the closer the observed average gets to the true average.

limn→∞ (X1 + X2 + … + Xn) / n = E[X] strong LLN: the sample mean converges to the true mean almost surely

For insurance this means: the more policies you write, the more predictable your total claims become. A pool of 50 cows might see 0 deaths or 5 deaths — huge variance. A pool of 50,000 cows will see deaths very close to the expected 3.5%.

This is why insurers want scale. A larger book of business isn’t just more revenue — it is more predictable revenue. The variance shrinks. This is the mathematical reason insurance companies exist: they pool enough risk that the outcome becomes nearly deterministic.

Well, actually 🧐 — the LLN is not the same as the Central Limit Theorem

The LLN says the average converges to the true rate. The CLT says the distribution of the average, after rescaling, converges to a normal distribution. Insurance pricing uses both.

The LLN justifies why you can charge a single premium for a pool. The CLT justifies how much capital you need to hold against the residual uncertainty — because even a large pool's loss ratio fluctuates, and the fluctuation is roughly normal with standard deviation σ / √n. The capital you reserve is essentially a multiple of that fluctuation. Section VI's risk loading is the CLT showing up on the balance sheet.

Key insight

Insurance is not a bet that bad things won’t happen. It is the mathematical certainty that, in a large enough pool, the average outcome is predictable even though any individual outcome is not.

III.

Frequency × Severity = Pure Premium

Now we arrive at the first real formula. This is the foundation of all pricing.

Pure Premium = Frequency × Severity

Frequency is how often the insured event happens. For cattle: what percentage die per year?

Severity is how much it costs when it does happen. For cattle: the payout amount (typically the full insured value).

The pure premium (also called the burning cost) is the expected claims cost per policy. It tells you: if you charged exactly this amount and nothing else, you would break even on claims over the long run.

This is the minimum the insurer needs to collect per policy to cover expected claims. It does not include any expenses, profit, or safety margin. Charging only this would mean breaking even on average — and going bankrupt half the time.

The pure premium is deceptively simple. The hard part is getting the frequency right. Where does 3.5% come from? Is it 3.5% for all cattle everywhere? Obviously not. A young, vaccinated Holstein in Tamil Nadu has a different mortality rate than an old, unvaccinated indigenous breed in Rajasthan. This is where rating factors come in.

IV.

Rating Factors: Not Everyone Pays the Same

Actuaries do not charge everyone the same rate. They segment risk. Different risk profiles get different prices. Each characteristic that affects the probability or cost of a claim is called a rating factor.

For livestock insurance, the key rating factors are:

  • Species — cattle (~3-5%), goat (~8-12%), poultry (~15-25%)
  • Breed — indigenous breeds are hardier; crossbred/exotic have higher mortality
  • Age — very young and very old animals die more (the “bathtub curve”)
  • Geography — flood-prone, drought-prone, and disease-endemic regions differ
  • Vaccination — vaccinated animals have measurably lower mortality
  • Herd size — larger herds correlate with better husbandry practices

Each factor has a relativity — a multiplier applied to the base rate:

Adjusted Rate = Base Rate × Factor1 × Factor2 × Factor3 × …

Notice how quickly the premium compounds. An exotic breed (1.4x) in a flood-prone area (1.3x) that is old (1.5x) doesn’t pay 3.5% — it pays close to 10%. The insurer that doesn’t segment risk this way will attract all the high-risk animals (because they’re getting a bargain) and repel all the low-risk ones. This is adverse selection and it destroys insurance companies.

The art of insurance pricing is not calculating the average. It is knowing who is above and who is below the average, and charging accordingly.

V.

The Expense Loading

The pure premium covers claims. But an insurer has costs beyond claims. Someone has to sell the policy, process the paperwork, investigate claims, and keep the lights on. These are expenses, and they get loaded on top of the pure premium.

The four main expense categories:

Expense What it covers Typical %
Acquisition Agent commissions, distribution, marketing 15–25%
Administration Policy issuance, IT systems, compliance, staff 8–15%
Claims management Field investigators, vet network, adjusters 5–10%
Reinsurance Cession to reinsurers (e.g. GIC Re mandatory 4%) 2–5%

The expense loading formula flips the intuition. You don’t add a percentage to the pure premium — you divide by the complement:

Expense-Loaded Premium = Pure Premium ÷ (1 − Expense Ratio)

Why division instead of addition? Because expenses are expressed as a percentage of the gross premium, not of the pure premium. If expenses are 35% of gross premium, then claims must be covered by the remaining 65%.

This is why insurance feels expensive. A 3.5% mortality rate becomes a 5.5–7% premium rate once expenses are loaded. For low-value products like livestock insurance, the expense ratio is especially punishing — it costs almost the same to process a ₹5,000 premium as a ₹50,000 premium.

I confess the division-not-addition formula above tripped me up the first time I worked through it — it is non-obvious until you see that expenses are a percentage of gross premium, not of pure premium. Once that clicks, the entire shape of the industry's unit economics clicks with it: every basis point shaved off the expense ratio is leveraged, every distribution cost is amplified, and every small-ticket policy is a fight against the denominator.

VI.

Profit & Risk Loading

The expense-loaded premium covers expected claims plus expenses. But the insurer still needs two more things:

Profit Margin

The insurer is a business. Shareholders expect a return on the capital they have locked up (remember: IRDAI requires ₹100 crore minimum, plus 1.5x solvency). A typical profit target is 3–8% of premium. In competitive, subsidized lines like livestock, it is often closer to 0–3%.

Risk Loading

This is the critical concept most people miss. The pure premium is the average expected claims cost. But averages are dangerous. In any given year, actual claims could be much higher than average.

The risk loading is a buffer for uncertainty. The thinner your data, the wider your confidence interval, the larger this buffer needs to be.

Risk-Loaded Premium = Pure Premium × (1 + Risk Load %)

Two common approaches actuaries use:

  • Standard deviation method: Add k × standard deviation of losses. More volatile risk = bigger loading.
  • Cost of capital method: How much return must the capital backing this risk earn? Higher capital requirement = bigger loading.

VII.

The Full Premium Buildup

Now we put it all together. Every insurance premium is built from these layers, stacked bottom-up:

Gross Premium = (Pure Premium × (1 + Risk Load)) ÷ (1 − Expense Ratio − Profit Margin)

VIII.

Running a Book: The Loss Ratio Simulator

The loss ratio is the single most important metric in insurance. It is the ratio of claims paid to premiums collected:

Loss Ratio = Claims Paid ÷ Premiums Collected × 100%
  • < 60%: Excellent. The insurer has plenty of room for expenses and profit.
  • 60–80%: Healthy. Standard for well-priced books.
  • 80–100%: Marginal. Little room for expenses. May be losing money.
  • > 100%: The insurer is paying out more in claims than it collected in premiums. Unsustainable.

The combined ratio adds expenses: Combined Ratio = Loss Ratio + Expense Ratio. Above 100% means the underwriting operation is losing money (though the insurer might still profit from investment income on the float).

IX.

The Credibility Problem

Every calculation above assumed we know the true mortality rate. But in practice, we don’t. We estimate it from data. And the quality of that estimate depends on how much data we have.

Credibility theory is the actuarial framework for dealing with this. It answers: how much should I trust my own data versus the industry average?

Credibility-Weighted Rate = Z × (Own Experience) + (1 − Z) × (Industry Rate)

Z is the credibility factor, between 0 and 1. It increases with more data:

  • Z = 0: You have no data. Use the industry rate entirely.
  • Z = 0.5: Blend equally between your own experience and the industry.
  • Z = 1: You have so much data that your own experience is fully credible.

A common formula for Z is: Z = n / (n + k), where n is your number of claims and k is a constant (often 1,082 for Poisson-distributed claims, known as the Bühlmann credibility)[2].

Well, actually 🧐 — where the 1,082 comes from

Bühlmann's credibility constant k comes from the ratio of two variances: the expected process variance (within-risk noise) over the variance of hypothetical means (between-risk variation). The 1,082 you see in property-casualty textbooks corresponds to a 90% credibility threshold at 1,082 expected claims, under the standard Poisson assumption.

The deeper story is that Bühlmann credibility is a special case of empirical Bayes — you're computing the posterior mean given a prior (the industry rate) and a likelihood (your own experience). Z is just the weight of the data versus the prior. Bayesian and frequentist actuaries arrive at the same formula by different paths.

With only 200 policies, your credibility is low. Your observed 5.2% mortality is pulled back toward the industry 3.5%. As you grow to thousands of policies, your own experience dominates.

This is why new insurers can’t just “set their own rates.” They don’t have enough data. They start with industry rates and gradually adjust as their own claims experience accumulates. It takes years to build full credibility.

Why data is the moat

In livestock insurance, the industry data itself is thin — no centralized national mortality database exists. A tech provider that accumulates animal health and mortality data across thousands of policies has a structural pricing advantage over incumbents flying blind. This is where technology creates real value in insurance.

X.

Why Livestock Insurance Is Especially Hard

Everything you’ve learned above applies to all insurance. But livestock insurance in India faces a set of challenges that make each step harder:

Challenge Impact on Pricing
Adverse selection Farmers insure sick or old animals. Observed mortality >> true population mortality.
Moral hazard Less incentive to care for an insured animal. Some intentional deaths.
No centralized data Can’t build credible mortality tables. Low Z everywhere.
Regional variation Disease, climate, husbandry vary enormously. Need hyper-local rates.
Breed diversity 50+ recognized cattle breeds with different mortality profiles.
Fraud Duplicate claims, phantom animals inflate apparent loss ratios to 130%+.
Small sums insured High expense ratio: processing a ₹5,000 policy costs as much as ₹50,000.

This is exactly why technology matters here. Each tech intervention maps directly to a pricing improvement:

  • Muzzle biometrics (Nandi AI, ID-Scan) → eliminate duplicate/phantom claims → lower fraud load
  • IoT health monitoring (BrainWired, PsiBorg) → continuous underwriting data → better rating factors, lower adverse selection
  • Digital enrollment → lower acquisition cost → lower expense ratio
  • Automated claims triage → lower claims management cost + faster settlement
  • Data accumulation → higher credibility (Z) → more accurate pricing → lower risk loading

Every basis point of improvement in these areas flows directly to a lower premium or a better loss ratio. That is the business case for being a tech provider in this space: you are not just distributing insurance, you are making it actuarially viable.

The bottom line

Insurance pricing is a stack: expected losses + uncertainty buffer + expenses + profit. Each layer can be compressed by better data, better technology, and better processes. The company that compresses this stack most efficiently wins — either as an insurer with better margins, or as a tech provider that makes the insurer’s product viable in the first place.

What you can now teach

The actuarial premium buildup framework. Every insurance premium is built in four stackable layers: pure premium (frequency × severity), risk loading (buffer for variance), expense loading (÷ complement, not minus), and profit margin. Every layer can be measured, traced to a cause, and compressed by better data or technology.
