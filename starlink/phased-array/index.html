Phased Arrays

A flat antenna with no moving parts points its beam anywhere in the sky by adjusting the phase of signals at each element — electronically steered in microseconds, the only way to track a satellite moving at 27,000 km/h.

I.

Why can't a normal dish track a fast LEO satellite?

A Starlink satellite at 550 km altitude moves across the sky at about 7.6 km/s. From the ground, that translates to an angular velocity of roughly 0.7° per second when it passes near zenith, and more at the horizon. A single satellite is in view of any given terminal for 4 to 7 minutes. During that pass, the terminal has to keep its beam locked on the satellite to within a fraction of a degree, then hand off to the next satellite cleanly. Then the next. All day. Forever.

A traditional parabolic dish points by physically rotating. A residential DirecTV dish weighs a few kilograms; a VSAT dish weighs tens. To track Starlink, a mechanical mount would need to slew at 0.7°/s smoothly, settle in milliseconds during handoff, and survive thousands of full-sky sweeps per day for a decade. The motors, gearboxes, and bearings would be expensive, audible, and the first thing to fail. None of this is hypothetical — the Iridium gateways from the 1990s used motor-driven tracking dishes, and they were the most expensive single component on the ground.

So Starlink does something different. The "Dishy McFlatface" terminal on your roof has no moving parts for beam steering. (The motorised tilt at install is just to orient the flat panel toward the right patch of sky; once positioned, it never moves during tracking.) The beam itself is steered electronically — pointed by computation, not by motion. The trick is a 1905-vintage idea called a phased array: many small antenna elements, each radiating the same signal but with a deliberately offset phase, so that their combined wavefronts add up constructively in one specific direction and cancel everywhere else.

This article works through the math from first principles. Two antennas first. Then N. Then how a phase delay relates to a steering angle. Then the trap (grating lobes) and the cost (beam squint). At the end, you should be able to look at a Starlink terminal and say exactly what each of its ~1,280 elements is doing at any moment — and what it would cost, in dB and in degrees, to point somewhere else.

II.

What does "phase" mean for a radio wave?

A monochromatic radio wave at frequency f is a sinusoid: E(t) = A·cos(2πft + φ). The phase φ is just an offset along the time axis — it tells you where in its cycle the wave is at t = 0. Two waves with the same frequency but different phases are the same shape, slid sideways.

For Starlink's Ku-band downlink at roughly 12 GHz, the period of one cycle is 1/f = 83 picoseconds. A full 2π phase shift corresponds to delaying the signal by exactly 83 ps. A π/2 (90°) shift is 21 ps. These are absurdly short intervals — about the time it takes light to travel 6 mm. That fact is the entire reason phased arrays work: the physical spacing between antenna elements (a few millimetres at 12 GHz) is comparable to the wavelength, so small differences in path length translate directly to controllable phase differences.

The wavelength is the conversion factor. Light covers one wavelength in one period:

λ = c / f c = speed of light, f = frequency. At 12 GHz, λ ≈ 25 mm; at 30 GHz, λ ≈ 10 mm.

So a 25 mm path difference between two signals at 12 GHz is exactly one full wavelength — they arrive in phase. A 12.5 mm path difference is half a wavelength — they arrive exactly out of phase, and a coherent receiver sees them cancel. This is the entire phenomenon. Phased arrays are not exotic; they are bookkeeping for path-length differences measured in millimetres.

Two more things to fix in mind before we start adding signals. First, the wavenumber: k = 2π/λ. It's just a units-of-radians-per-metre conversion that shows up everywhere. Multiply a distance by k and you get the phase shift over that distance. Second, complex notation: a wave cos(2πft + φ) is the real part of ej(2πft + φ). Engineers carry the ejφ term and add them; the time-dependent ej2πft factors out cleanly because every element runs at the same frequency.

III.

Why does adding two phased signals make a beam?

Put two identical antennas a distance d apart along a line. Each radiates the same sinusoid. A receiver far away at angle θ from the array's broadside sees the two signals arriving with a path-length difference of d·sin(θ). That extra path adds a phase shift of k·d·sin(θ) = (2πd/λ)·sin(θ) to the signal from the farther antenna.

If we also impose a deliberate transmit phase shift φ on the second element, the total phase difference at the receiver is:

ψ = k·d·sin(θ) + φ geometric path-length phase + electronic phase shift we apply at the element

Two equal-amplitude sinusoids with a phase difference ψ sum to |2·cos(ψ/2)| in amplitude. So the field strength radiated by the two-element array, as a function of angle, is:

|E(θ)| ∝ |2·cos(ψ/2)| = 2·|cos[ ½(k·d·sinθ + φ) ]| "array factor" for a 2-element uniform array, normalised. Doubles when in phase, vanishes when 180° apart.

Where ψ = 0 (signals in phase), the amplitude is 2 — constructive interference, the beam. Where ψ = ±π (signals exactly out of phase), the amplitude is 0 — a perfect null. Two antennas, by themselves, sketch a pattern of alternating beams and nulls across the sky.

Now the key move. If we set φ = −k·d·sin(θ0) for some chosen angle θ0, then at θ = θ0 the geometric phase exactly cancels the electronic phase: ψ = 0, and we get full constructive interference in that direction. We have steered the beam to θ0 by changing one number: the electronic phase shift φ. No motors, no motion. Play with the widget.

IV.

How does an N-element array sharpen the beam?

Generalise. Put N identical antennas in a row, uniformly spaced by d. Apply phase shifts φn = n·φ (linear progression: element 0 gets 0, element 1 gets φ, element 2 gets 2φ, etc.). The total field at angle θ is the sum of N complex exponentials:

E(θ) = Σn=0..N−1 ej·n·(k·d·sinθ + φ) = Σn=0..N−1 ej·n·ψ where ψ = k·d·sinθ + φ. This is a geometric series in ejψ.

Geometric series sum: closed form. Take the magnitude and you get the array factor for a uniform linear array:

|AF(θ)| = | sin(Nψ/2) / sin(ψ/2) | ψ = (2πd/λ)·sinθ + φ. Peak value = N when ψ = 0. Nulls when Nψ/2 = mπ for integer m ≠ 0 mod N.

This is the central formula of array theory. Three things drop out of it:

  • Peak gain at ψ = 0 is N. Twice the elements, twice the field amplitude, four times the radiated power per unit solid angle. Coherent combining is the whole point.
  • Beamwidth shrinks as 1/N. The angular distance from peak to first null is approximately 2/(Nd/λ) radians for a broadside beam — in degrees, roughly 102/(Nd/λ)°. A 32-element array with d = λ/2 has a beamwidth around 6°. Starlink's 1,280-element square array, if treated as roughly 36 elements per side, has a beamwidth around 3°.
  • Side lobes appear at every secondary peak of |sin(Nψ/2)|. The first side lobe of a uniform array sits 13.3 dB below the main beam — meaning ~5% of peak power leaks into the wrong direction. That's the price of using uniform amplitudes; tapering the amplitudes across the array trades main-beam gain for lower side lobes (Taylor, Chebyshev, Hamming windows — the same DSP toolbox as filter design).

Well, actually 🧐 — the element pattern matters

The array factor above assumes ideal isotropic radiators — antennas that emit equally in every direction. Real antenna elements have their own radiation pattern ge(θ): a microstrip patch element, for instance, has a roughly cosine-shaped pattern that peaks at broadside and falls off toward the array's horizon. The total pattern is the product:

G(θ) = ge(θ) × |AF(θ)|2 "pattern multiplication" theorem — valid only for identical, identically-oriented elements

Practically: even a perfectly steered array factor at θ = 60° gets attenuated by the element pattern, which can drop another 3 to 6 dB at that angle. The "you can steer to 60°" claim has a footnote: the gain falls off, and at some angle the link budget breaks. We'll quantify this in section VII.

V.

How do you steer the beam without moving the antenna?

The steering rule is the headline equation of phased-array theory. Set the per-element phase to cancel the geometric path-difference phase at the desired angle θ0. The peak of |AF| occurs at ψ = 0, i.e., when:

k·d·sin(θ0) + φ = 0   ⇔   φ = −k·d·sin(θ0) phase shift per element to steer the main beam to angle θ0 from broadside

Or, written for the full linear array with elements indexed n = 0, 1, ..., N−1:

φn = −n·k·d·sin(θ0) = −n·(2πd/λ)·sin(θ0) linear phase progression. Each element is phase-delayed by an integer multiple of the base shift.

That's it. Plug in θ0 = 0° (boresight, straight ahead): φn = 0 for every element, all signals in phase, beam straight ahead. Plug in θ0 = 30°: each element n needs to delay its signal by n·π·sin(30°) = n·π/2 radians (assuming d = λ/2). Element 0 gets 0, element 1 gets π/2, element 2 gets π, element 3 gets 3π/2, and so on, wrapping modulo 2π.

A concrete example, all numbers worked. Starlink uses d ≈ λ/2 = 12.5 mm at 12 GHz. To steer to θ0 = 45°:

  • Required phase per element: φ = −π·sin(45°) = −2.22 rad = −127°
  • Equivalent path-length offset: φ·λ/(2π) = −8.8 mm
  • Equivalent time delay: −8.8 mm / c = −29.4 picoseconds

Each successive element transmits 29 picoseconds earlier than the previous one. That's the entire steering action: a programmable time delay (or, equivalently, a phase shift) on each element. Implemented in silicon, it's a few extra gates per channel. Implemented in 1965 with vacuum tubes and ferrite phase shifters, it was the entire AN/FPS-85 SLBM warning radar — the size of a building, costing $440 million in 1969 dollars.

Well, actually 🧐 — "phase delay" vs "true time delay"

The steering rule above uses a phase shift, which is frequency-dependent. If you transmit a wideband signal (say 250 MHz of bandwidth around a 12 GHz carrier), the steering angle drifts slightly across the band — a phenomenon called beam squint with frequency. For narrow-band Starlink channels this is small enough to ignore. For very wideband radars (chirp pulses, multi-GHz signals), a true time delay (TTD) is needed, which steers identically at every frequency. TTDs are implemented as switched lengths of transmission line, not phase shifters, and are bulkier — a real cost in radar design.

The remarkable thing is the steering speed. A microwave phase shifter in modern silicon switches in nanoseconds. A whole beam can be repointed across the sky in less time than a router takes to look up an IP address. This is why a single Starlink terminal can multiplex across several visible satellites at once and seamlessly hand off — the beam can jump 30° in a microsecond, sample a new satellite, jump back. No mechanical system can compete.

VI.

What is a grating lobe, and why does element spacing matter?

The array factor |sin(Nψ/2)/sin(ψ/2)| peaks not only at ψ = 0 but at every ψ = 2πm for integer m. That's the periodicity of the sine in the denominator. We want only the ψ = 0 peak (the main beam) to fall in real angles −90° ≤ θ ≤ +90°. The other peaks — called grating lobes — should fall in the "invisible region" where |sinθ| > 1.

Work it out. The next peak occurs at ψ = ±2π, which means:

(2πd/λ)·sin(θg) + φ = ±2π
sin(θg) = ±λ/d − φ/(2πd/λ)   ⇔   sin(θg) = sin(θ0) ± λ/d where θ0 is the desired steer angle (using φ = −k·d·sinθ0)

For a grating lobe to not exist (no second peak in visible space), we need |sinθ0 ± λ/d| > 1 for both signs. This collapses to the grating-lobe condition:

d / λ ≤ 1 / (1 + |sinθmax|) where θmax is the maximum steering angle the array must support

Check the corners. If you only ever point at broadside (θmax = 0), then d/λ ≤ 1 — spacing up to one full wavelength is safe. If you need to steer to θmax = 60°, then d/λ ≤ 1/(1 + 0.866) = 0.536 — about half a wavelength. The classical "rule of thumb" of d = λ/2 is the conservative limit that allows steering all the way to θmax = 90° with no grating lobes ever appearing. This is the antenna world's Nyquist criterion: it's the same logic as sampling a signal — you must spatially sample fast enough (small enough spacing) to avoid aliasing.

Why does this matter? A grating lobe is a full-power beam pointed in the wrong direction. If you're trying to talk to one satellite, a grating lobe means you're also blasting another patch of sky with the same signal — wasted power, plus interference into whoever lives there. For receive arrays, it's even worse: a grating lobe is an additional sensitivity peak, so noise and interference from an unintended direction get amplified along with the wanted signal.

VII.

What is the price you pay for steering off-boresight?

The array factor at the steered angle θ0 has peak amplitude N regardless of θ0 — the formula seems to promise free steering. It doesn't. Three physical effects bite the gain as you point further from broadside.

1. Projected aperture. An array of physical aperture A, viewed at angle θ0 from broadside, presents a projected aperture of A·cos(θ0). Antenna directivity scales with aperture; so directivity falls by a factor of cosθ0 just from geometry. At θ0 = 60°, you lose 3 dB — half your gain — before any other effect kicks in.

G(θ0) / G(0) = cos(θ0)   →   loss(dB) = −10·log10(cosθ0) aperture-projection loss. 30°: −0.6 dB; 45°: −1.5 dB; 60°: −3.0 dB; 75°: −5.9 dB

2. Element pattern. As the sidenote in section IV noted, each element has its own radiation pattern, typically with a cosq(θ) shape for some q between 1 and 1.5. Multiply that into the array factor and you lose another 1 to 3 dB at 60°. The element pattern is the floor: even with infinite array factor, you cannot exceed what one element radiates in that direction.

3. Beam broadening. The half-power beamwidth grows as 1/cosθ0. At 60° the beam is twice as wide as at broadside — meaning less spatial selectivity, more interference from neighbouring sky, and lower tolerance to pointing error.

Stack the three. From boresight to 60° off-boresight, a typical Starlink-class array loses around 6 dB of total gain (3 from aperture, 2-3 from element pattern, plus some from amplitude tapering effects). 6 dB is a factor of 4 in power. The link budget needs that margin built in — or the system has to refuse satellites below a certain elevation.

Well, actually 🧐 — why the Starlink dish has a slight install tilt

A 2D phased array on a flat panel mounted exactly horizontally has its boresight pointing straight up — great for satellites at zenith, terrible for satellites approaching the horizon. If the satellite is at 30° elevation (60° from boresight), you've already paid 6 dB of gain loss before any rain or pointing error.

Hence the small motorised tilt at install. The Gen2 Dishy auto-orients its panel to point at the centre of the most useful patch of sky for your latitude, biased slightly toward the equator (where the satellite passes are more frequent at favourable elevations). This isn't beam-steering — it's a one-time mechanical optimisation of the boresight, so the array spends most of its time steering ±30° rather than ±60°. It's the cheap mid-tier fix between "fully mechanical" and "no movement at all," and it buys back 3 dB of usable link margin for the cost of one stepper motor that runs once.

This is why the θmax = 60° limit shows up in every commercial phased-array spec. Beyond that, the combined gain loss makes the link uneconomic to close. Mid-tier optimisations (the install tilt, hexagonal lattices, amplitude tapering) buy you a couple of dB; beyond that you need a fundamentally different antenna or a closer satellite.

VIII.

How does Starlink's terminal actually do this in silicon?

The math is the same as it was in 1958. What changed is the implementation cost.

Each element in the Starlink array needs four things behind it: (1) an RF amplifier for transmit/receive, (2) a phase shifter, (3) a digital control line to set the phase, (4) DC power. In a 1965 radar, each of those was a discrete vacuum-tube or ferrite component bolted to a chassis. For a 1,280-element array, the bill of materials would have been astronomical — the entire AN/FPS-85 had only 5,184 elements and cost $440 million to build.

The Starlink terminal achieves this with beamforming integrated circuits (BFICs). A single BFIC drives 4 to 16 elements: each channel contains an RF front-end (LNA on receive, PA on transmit), a 6-bit digital phase shifter, and a 6-bit digital attenuator (for amplitude tapering). Modern BFICs come from companies like Anokiwave, Renesas, and (rumoured for Starlink's v2) custom SpaceX silicon. Each BFIC is a few square millimetres of die, costs under $10 in volume, and dissipates around 1 W per channel.

The phase shifter is the heart of the chip. There are two main architectures:

  • Passive vector-modulator: combine an in-phase (I) signal and a quadrature (Q) signal with variable weights. By choosing I, Q amplitudes you synthesise any phase 0 to 360° with arbitrary resolution. Low loss, but requires an active mixer-style topology with linearity constraints.
  • Switched-line / loaded-line: switch in pieces of transmission line of specific length, giving discrete phase steps (typically 5.625° per LSB for a 6-bit shifter). Glassy in implementation, simple to characterise, but quantises phase.

A 6-bit phase shifter resolves 2π/64 = 5.625°. At 12 GHz, that corresponds to ~1.3 ps of resolution — about the time light takes to travel 0.4 mm. The quantisation introduces small pointing errors and slightly elevated near-in side lobes, but the practical effect is below 0.5 dB.

The whole beamforming computation — figure out φn for each of 1,280 elements, send the bits to the BFICs — runs on a microcontroller on the terminal's main board. The math is trivial: φn,m = −(2πd/λ)·(n·sinθ0·cosφ0 + m·sinθ0·sinφ0) for element at lattice position (n, m), beam steered to (θ0, φ0) in spherical coords. The terminal can recompute every element's phase a thousand times per second; the bottleneck is the satellite handoff scheduler, not the trigonometry.

Well, actually 🧐 — the 2D array is just the 1D array twice

Everything in sections III through VI was a 1D linear array. A 2D rectangular array of M×N elements has an array factor that is the product of the 1D factors in each axis:

AF2D(θ, φ) = AFx(θ, φ) × AFy(θ, φ) "separable" array. Beam shape is a pencil; steering is independent in each axis.

For the Starlink hexagonal lattice, it's slightly different (the factor depends on the lattice vectors), but conceptually the same: the array sums coherently across both dimensions, the beam is a 2D pencil pointing at (θ0, φ0), and steering is just a 2D version of the 1D rule. Everything in this article generalises cleanly; we just stuck to 1D to keep the equations one-line.

The economics that made this possible track Moore's Law plus mmWave silicon plus 5G investment. A 2010 phased array was a $50,000 defence-industry curio. A 2024 phased array sits on a roof in rural Wyoming serving Netflix at 200 Mbps for $349. The math didn't change. The silicon got cheap.

IX.

The full beam-steering synthesis

Put every piece on one page. Here is the full operational logic of a phased-array terminal:

  1. Wavelength: λ = c/f ≈ 25 mm at the 12 GHz Ku-band downlink. k = 2π/λ.
  2. Layout: N elements per axis, spacing d ≈ λ/2 (so grating lobes never reach the visible region). Total elements: N², around 1,280 for Starlink.
  3. Steering rule: to point at (θ0, φ0), set element (n, m) to phase φn,m = −k·d·(n·sinθ0·cosφ0 + m·sinθ0·sinφ0).
  4. Beamwidth: roughly λ/(N·d) radians; for a 60 cm aperture at 12 GHz, ~3°.
  5. Side lobes: first sidelobe of a uniform array is 13.3 dB down. Tapering reduces it but costs gain.
  6. Grating-lobe constraint: d/λ ≤ 1/(1 + |sinθmax|). Starlink picks d ≈ λ/2 and accepts |θmax| ≤ 60°.
  7. Scan loss: pointing at θ0 costs −10·log10(cosθ0) dB from aperture projection, plus 1–3 dB from element pattern, plus broadening of the beam.
  8. Update rate: phase shifters switch in ns. The terminal can re-steer the beam thousands of times per second. Satellite handoffs (every ~4 minutes) are imperceptible.

That's the entire stack from Maxwell's equations to a roof in Wyoming. The phased-array idea is sixty-five years old. The reason it took until the 2020s to put one on a residential rooftop is not that someone discovered a new equation — it's that the implementation finally became cheaper than a single rotating dish.

The one thing

A flat antenna with no moving parts steers its beam anywhere in the sky by setting one phase shift per element, computed from the simple rule φn = −n·k·d·sinθ0. Wave interference does the rest: signals add up where you want them, cancel everywhere else, all in less time than your CPU takes to handle an interrupt. Mechanical pointing is dead for fast satellites; phase arithmetic is the future, and the future runs on silicon you can buy for ten dollars per channel.
