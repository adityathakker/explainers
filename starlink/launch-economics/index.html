Launch Economics

Falcon 9 reuse didn't just cut the cost of rockets — it cut the cost of every satellite constellation that depends on launch. At $20,000/kg-to-LEO, no constellation pencils. At $1,500/kg, Starlink ships.

I.

Why is launch so expensive — derived from the rocket equation?

Here's the claim worth absorbing before anything else: Falcon 9 reuse changed the satellite-cost equation, not just the rocket-cost equation. At $20,000/kg-to-LEO — the cost in the Shuttle era — no satellite constellation of any meaningful size pencils. At $1,500/kg, which is roughly where Falcon 9 reused sits in 2025, a 4,400-satellite Starlink shell becomes a budgetable line item. At $200/kg, the target Starship eventually wants to hit, putting things in orbit becomes banal — the cost dimension flips from "the dominant constraint" to "a rounding error." Starlink does not exist because someone had a clever business plan. It exists because the dollars-per-kilogram fell.

To see why launch is hard at all, you have to start with the only equation that matters: the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation, derived by Konstantin Tsiolkovsky in 1903. It tells you the velocity change a rocket can achieve as a function of how much propellant it carries and how fast that propellant leaves the nozzle.

Δv = ve · ln(m0 / mf) Δv = velocity change (m/s); ve = effective exhaust velocity (m/s); m0 = initial mass (full); mf = final mass (empty, payload + structure)

The right-hand side has a logarithm in it, and that log is the entire reason rockets are hard. To go faster you need more propellant. But more propellant means more mass to accelerate. Which needs more propellant. The exponential blows up. To reach low Earth orbit you need roughly Δv ≈ 9.4 km/s — not the 7.8 km/s orbital velocity, but that plus gravity losses (about 1.5 km/s) and drag losses (about 100 m/s).

For a kerosene/LOX first stage like Falcon 9, the exhaust velocity is roughly ve ≈ 3,050–3,420 m/s depending on whether you're at sea level (Isp ~311 s) or vacuum (Isp ~348 s). Plug 9,400 / 3,200 into the rocket equation:

m0 / mf = eΔv / ve = e9400/3200 ≈ e2.94 ≈ 18.9× mass ratio: full rocket is 18.9× the mass of the empty rocket plus payload

The empty mass is everything — engines, tanks, fairing, plumbing, avionics, recovery hardware, plus the payload itself. To put 1 kg into LEO with a single-stage rocket, you'd need roughly 19 kg of fully-fuelled rocket. Of that 19 kg, only about 6% can be empty structure plus payload — the rest is propellant. And the structure has to be strong enough to hold all the propellant, withstand 3-G acceleration, and survive Max-Q aerodynamic pressure. The reason real rockets are multi-stage (Falcon 9 has two, Starship has two) is that staging lets you throw away dry mass mid-flight, easing the geometry. But the underlying tyranny is the same.

This is why every kilogram of payload to orbit has historically cost thousands of dollars. The vehicle itself is mostly propellant tanks; the part you care about — the satellite — is a tiny fraction of liftoff mass. A Falcon 9 lifts about 549 tonnes at liftoff and delivers about 22.8 tonnes to LEO. That's a payload-mass fraction of 4.2%. The other 95.8% is propellant, tankage, and engines that have to be paid for somehow.

Well, actually 🧐 — the rocket equation hides a lot

The Tsiolkovsky equation ignores gravity losses (the propellant you burn just holding the rocket up while it accelerates), drag losses, and finite burn time. It also assumes constant ve, which is false — specific impulse changes with altitude as ambient pressure drops. For ballpark sizing, engineers use the delta-V budget: 9.4 km/s to LEO is the canonical number for low-Earth ascent, but the real burn is split across two stages with different ve values. Falcon 9 first stage burns ~7 km/s with sea-level Merlins, then the second stage adds ~2.4 km/s with a vacuum Merlin (Isp ~348 s, ve ~3,420 m/s). The rocket equation is correct; it just doesn't capture how engineers actually divide the work.

II.

What did expendable launch actually cost — and why?

For sixty years — from Sputnik in 1957 until Falcon 9 first re-flew a recovered booster in 2017 — rockets were expendable. You built a Saturn V, you flew it once, you watched the most expensive piece of hardware ever manufactured by humans burn up in the atmosphere or sink into the ocean. There were exceptions (Space Shuttle, sort of) but the rule held.

Expendable launch costs are dominated by manufacturing. You amortise the entire vehicle — engines, tankage, avionics, fairing — over a single flight. The numbers, normalised to dollars-per-kilogram-to-LEO in inflation-adjusted 2024 dollars:

Two things jump out. First, the Shuttle was supposed to be the breakthrough — partially reusable, weekly cadence, $100/kg target. It came in at $65,000/kg, the most expensive launcher in the table. The orbiter was technically reusable, but refurbishment between flights involved tearing down and re-certifying 24,000 thermal tiles, three RS-25 engines, and the boosters retrieved from the ocean. Refurbishment cost more per flight than building a fresh expendable vehicle would have. The Shuttle is the cautionary tale that convinced an entire generation of engineers that reuse was a trap.

Second, even the original Falcon 9 in expendable mode — before recovery — was 5–6× cheaper than the contemporary expendables. SpaceX did this by building everything in-house (no aerospace cost-plus contractors), using kerosene instead of solid boosters or hydrogen, and standardising on one engine (Merlin) used in both stages. The expendable Falcon 9 was already an outlier. Reuse made the gap historically enormous.

Well, actually 🧐 — why expendable was the default for sixty years

The orthodox view, drilled into aerospace engineers from the 1960s through the early 2010s, was that refurbishment cost more than building new. Two pieces of evidence kept this view dominant: the Shuttle (refurbishment ran into the hundreds of millions per flight) and the unspoken fact that the U.S. military, which dominated launch demand, didn't actually want low costs — they wanted reliability and on-time delivery, and cost-plus contractors had no incentive to drive costs down. SpaceX's contribution was not inventing reuse (the concept dates to the 1960s); it was proving that booster refurbishment cost can be lower than booster manufacturing cost, by an order of magnitude, if you design the booster around reuse from day one rather than bolting it on. The retrofit-vs-clean-sheet distinction is the whole game.

III.

How does reuse change the calculus?

If you plot dollars-per-kilogram-to-LEO over time on a log scale, the picture from 1957 through 2015 looks like a noisy band between $10,000 and $100,000/kg. Mostly flat, with the Shuttle as an upward outlier and Soyuz as the steady cheapest option. Then 2017 happens. The first Falcon 9 booster re-flight knocks the dot to $1,500/kg. Falcon Heavy reused, in some configurations, comes in below $1,000/kg. The Starship target is $200/kg. The chart drops two orders of magnitude in less than a decade.

This is the chart that explains Starlink. Above $5,000/kg, no broadband mega-constellation closes its business case. Below it, the constellation cost becomes a tractable line item (single-digit billions for launch). Starlink crossed the threshold at exactly the moment Falcon 9 reuse matured. The question isn't whether Starlink is a clever idea; it's whether anyone could have built it before this cost line was crossed. They couldn't — Teledesic and Iridium and Globalstar all tried in the 1990s, all at $20,000+/kg launch costs, and all imploded.

The dashed line at $5,000/kg is roughly the threshold below which mega-constellations become economically possible — the Falcon 9 reuse data point in 2017 is the first vehicle to cross it.

The 1,000× range of vehicles on this chart is real. Saturn V was, in inflation-adjusted terms, surprisingly cheap per kilogram — about $5,500/kg — because it was huge (140 tonnes to LEO) and the per-kg-amortisation is good when the numerator gets divided by a big denominator. Saturn V's problem wasn't cost-per-kg; it was that it cost $1.5 billion to fly (in 2024 dollars), nobody had 140 tonnes worth of payload to lift, and the per-launch cost was untouchable.

The Shuttle inverted the geometry. Smaller per-flight payload (~24 tonnes) and gigantic refurbishment cost gave the highest $/kg in the entire table. Reuse, badly executed, is more expensive than expendable. The Shuttle's biggest contribution to launch economics may have been convincing everyone that reuse couldn't work — an assumption that held until Falcon 9 disproved it.

IV.

What is the marginal cost of a Falcon 9 launch?

Marginal cost is the right number to think about, not list price. SpaceX charges customers around $67 million for a Falcon 9 launch, but that's the price, not the cost. For internal Starlink missions — which is roughly two-thirds of all Falcon 9 flights in 2024 — SpaceX pays itself the marginal cost, not the list price. And the marginal cost is the relevant number for asking whether Starlink can launch its own constellation profitably.

The marginal cost of one additional Falcon 9 flight, given a fleet of existing boosters, decomposes into four pieces:

  • Refurbishment of the recovered booster: ~$250k. Inspection, replacement of consumables (composite overwrap pressure vessels, soot-stained components), engine checkout. The booster itself is amortised across its flight count.
  • New expendable second stage: ~$10 million. The second stage is thrown away on every flight; this is the largest single line item by far.
  • Propellant: ~$500k. RP-1 (kerosene) and liquid oxygen for both stages. Cheap.
  • Operations: ~$3 million. Range fees, recovery ships, pad crews, mission control, integration labour.

Add them up: about $14–15 million marginal cost per Falcon 9 launch. Divide by ~22 tonnes of LEO payload (reusable mode) and you get roughly $680/kg. SpaceX charges customers $3,000/kg-equivalent for that capacity. The margin is what funds Starship development and the broader business.

The amortisation arithmetic is worth doing yourself. If a Falcon 9 booster costs C dollars to manufacture, the refurbishment costs r dollars per flight, the second stage costs S dollars per flight (expendable), and the booster flies n times before retirement, then the per-flight cost of that booster is:

cost per flight = (C / n) + r + S + ops
$/kg = [(C / n) + r + S + ops] / payloadkg marginal cost converges to (r + S + ops) / payload as n grows large

With C = $30M, r = $0.25M, S = $10M, ops = $3M, and 22 tonnes payload, here's how the per-kg cost falls with reuse count:

Well, actually 🧐 — Falcon 9 booster B1062 flew 23 times

The first reused booster, B1021, flew twice (2016 launch, 2017 re-flight). SpaceX initially projected 10 reuses per booster as a stretch goal. By 2024, Booster B1058 flew 19 times before being lost. Booster B1062 has flown 23 times and is still active. Refurbishment cost per flight is now believed to be in the $250k–$500k range — far below the initial estimate of $1 million. The "reuse won't pay off" orthodoxy missed by a factor of 4–10 in both directions: more flights per booster, and cheaper refurbishment per flight. The exponential reuse-economics curve in the calculator above is not a SpaceX projection; it is the empirical fleet behaviour.

V.

How much could Starship change this number?

Falcon 9 reuse drops $/kg from $20,000 (Shuttle era) to about $1,500 (current commercial price) to about $700 (internal marginal). Starship is designed to drop it another order of magnitude. The trick is that Starship reuses both stages, eliminating the $10M expendable second-stage line item that bottlenecks Falcon 9's marginal cost.

Starship's design parameters, where they're public:

  • Payload to LEO: 100–150 tonnes (Block 1), 150–200 tonnes target (Block 2–3). For comparison, Falcon 9 carries 22 tonnes; Falcon Heavy carries 64 tonnes expendable.
  • Reuse target: both stages, with first-stage rapid reuse (turnaround hours, not weeks). Second-stage (ship) reuse is the harder problem because of thermal protection on re-entry — Starship uses a stainless-steel hull plus replaceable tile shielding, designed for tens to hundreds of flights.
  • Propellant: methane/liquid oxygen (methalox). Cheaper than kerosene, cleaner burn, easier to recondense for on-orbit refilling.
  • Target marginal cost: SpaceX's stated long-run goal is ~$10M per fully-reusable flight, or roughly $100–200/kg at 100–150 tonnes payload. Current actual cost is still in the $50–100M range during the test campaign.

The headline difference is payload size. A Starlink V2 satellite weighs about 1,250 kg. A Falcon 9 launch can carry roughly 22 satellites (mass-limited, not volume-limited — the actual current launches carry 21–24). At 100 Falcon 9 launches per year (the 2024 cadence), that's roughly 2,200 satellites per year assuming all flights went to Starlink (in reality ~60% do). At Starship's target payload, one flight could carry 120 satellites — 15× the per-flight density. At 100 Starship flights per year, that's 12,000 satellites per year — enough to build an entire Starlink shell in 12 months.

VI.

Why doesn't reuse work as well for higher orbits?

One subtle point worth landing: Falcon 9 reuse helps LEO disproportionately. It helps GTO (geostationary transfer orbit) less. It barely helps escape trajectories at all. The reason comes from the rocket equation again — specifically the mass-ratio math.

The delta-V required to reach various destinations:

  • LEO: ~9.4 km/s
  • GTO: ~11.7 km/s (LEO + transfer burn)
  • GEO (after circularisation): ~13.2 km/s
  • Earth escape (TLI to Moon): ~12.4 km/s from Earth surface
  • Mars transfer: ~13.5–14 km/s depending on launch window

Plug these into the rocket equation with ve ~ 3,200 m/s:

mass ratio = eΔv / ve
LEO: e9400/3200 ≈ 18.9×
GTO: e11700/3200 ≈ 39.0×
GEO: e13200/3200 ≈ 60.4× payload mass drops with the inverse of mass ratio for fixed liftoff mass

Now here's the key. For Falcon 9 to recover its first stage, the booster has to reserve propellant for the boostback burn (turn around), re-entry burn (slow down through the atmosphere), and landing burn (touchdown). That reserved propellant reduces what's available for the actual mission. Recovery costs roughly 30% of the booster's total delta-V.

For a LEO mission, where total delta-V is 9.4 km/s, the booster does roughly the first 7 km/s of that. Recovery costs maybe 2 km/s of booster delta-V — manageable, because the booster has thrust to spare at separation. Falcon 9's reusable-mode LEO payload (17.4 tonnes) is about 76% of its expendable-mode LEO payload (22.8 tonnes). You give up 24% of payload for full first-stage recovery.

For GTO, the booster has to fly faster at separation to feed the second stage's longer burn. Now recovery propellant eats further into a higher-energy trajectory. Falcon 9's reusable GTO payload is about 5.5 tonnes; expendable GTO is 8.3 tonnes. The recovery penalty is 33%, not 24%. The boost-back becomes infeasible — Falcon 9 GTO missions have to land downrange on a drone ship, and some GTO missions go expendable to get the payload margin.

For direct GEO insertion, the penalty is worse. For Mars or escape, the booster either has to be expendable or you need orbital refilling to recover the energy. This is why Starship's architecture includes in-orbit refilling: a Starship sent to LEO with no payload tops up a payload Starship's propellant in orbit, effectively resetting the rocket equation for the second leg.

Well, actually 🧐 — the recovery profile differs by mission

Falcon 9 has three recovery modes. Return-to-launch-site (RTLS): the booster does a boostback burn, returns to the launch site, lands at LZ-1/LZ-2 a few km away. Used for low-energy LEO missions where there's propellant to spare. Costs ~30% of usable booster delta-V. Drone-ship downrange (ASDS): the booster doesn't turn around; it flies a normal ballistic arc and lands on a barge 600–900 km downrange. Costs ~20% of delta-V. Used for higher-energy LEO and GTO. Expendable: no recovery hardware, no landing burn, full payload. Reserved for missions where even ASDS recovery costs too much. The fraction of Falcon 9 missions going expendable has dropped from ~15% in 2017–2019 to under 5% by 2024, as Block 5 boosters improved their margin.

VII.

How many satellites can SpaceX launch per week today?

The actual 2024 numbers are worth pinning down, because they're moving fast and the public discourse lags by 12–24 months. SpaceX flew 134 Falcon 9 missions in 2024, up from 96 in 2023 and 61 in 2022. That's 2.6 launches per week on average, with a peak of 3 in some weeks. Roughly 60% of those flights were Starlink missions, ~25% commercial customers, ~10% NASA/military, and a handful expendable for high-energy trajectories.

Of the ~80 Starlink missions in 2024, each launched 21–24 satellites (Starlink V2 Mini, ~800 kg each). That's roughly 1,800–1,900 satellites delivered to orbit in 2024. The total Starlink constellation grew from ~5,000 to ~7,000 satellites over the year. The rest is accounted for by deorbits (Starlink satellites are designed to deorbit themselves at end of life, ~5-year design lifetime).

The constraint, oddly, is not booster availability. SpaceX has roughly 12–15 active boosters in the fleet at any time, each flying every 2–4 weeks. The constraint is the second stage, which is built fresh for every flight. Second-stage production has been scaled aggressively — the McGregor, Texas facility builds about 2 per week — but that's also the practical cadence ceiling. At one launch per work day, you'd need ~5 second stages per week, and the factory isn't there yet.

The other constraint is range scheduling. The Eastern Range (Cape Canaveral) and the Western Range (Vandenberg) have shared resources — tracking radars, downrange recovery infrastructure, restricted airspace coordination. SpaceX accounted for ~80% of all U.S. orbital launches in 2024, and the ranges are now operating closer to their physical limits than they have in any previous era.

VIII.

What does cheap launch enable — beyond Starlink itself?

This is the question that matters strategically. If you think the only consequence of Falcon 9 reuse is "Starlink exists," you've understood about a quarter of the picture. The other 75% is the second-order effects of putting mass into orbit at $1,500/kg falling toward $200/kg.

What was infeasible at $20,000/kg that becomes feasible at $200/kg:

  • Megaconstellations: Starlink, Kuiper (Amazon, ~3,200 planned), OneWeb v2, the Chinese Guowang (~13,000 planned) and Qianfan (~14,000 planned). Total LEO satellite count projected to exceed 50,000 by 2030, up from ~9,000 in 2024. The launch market they require — ~10,000 tonnes to LEO per year — existed nowhere on Earth in 2015.
  • Earth observation at high revisit cadence: Planet Labs, BlackSky, Iceye and similar operate hundreds of small satellites that image the entire Earth multiple times per day. Impossible at expendable launch prices because the per-satellite economics demanded multi-year missions on $100M satellites; achievable at $1,500/kg because you can fly $1M satellites that last 3 years.
  • On-orbit refilling: tankers carrying methalox to LEO for Mars-bound or lunar Starships. Refilling architectures need 5–10 tanker flights per Mars mission. At $1B/launch that's a non-starter; at $10M/launch that's two months of operations cost.
  • Space-based solar power: 1 GW of orbital solar requires ~10,000 tonnes of hardware to GEO. At $20k/kg that's $200B in launch alone. At $200/kg it's $2B — comparable to a terrestrial 1-GW nuclear plant.
  • Orbital servicing and debris removal: services that go pick up, refuel, or deorbit existing satellites. The economics work only when launch is cheap enough that the service vehicle itself doesn't dominate cost.
  • Lunar and Mars logistics: cargo missions to the Moon's surface at scale. Required for any sustainable settlement; impossible at Apollo-era launch costs (~$150,000/kg-to-lunar-surface in 2024 dollars).

There's a useful analogy to airline economics. The cost of moving one passenger one mile in a Boeing 747 is roughly $0.10 — cheap enough that intercontinental travel became routine for a middle-class family. The cost per passenger-mile in a 1950s DC-7 was about $0.50 in 2024 dollars — expensive enough that flying across an ocean was a once-in-a-lifetime expense. The 5× drop, achieved over thirty years, didn't just make 747s cheaper to operate — it created the entire modern tourism industry, the global shipping economy, and the airline-route network. The Falcon 9 cost drop is structurally similar, just compressed into a decade and operating in a different domain. We are at the equivalent moment of "the jumbo jet just entered service" — the platform exists; the industries that will be built on top of it are still being imagined.

Well, actually 🧐 — Starship has to fly hundreds of times per year to amortise development

SpaceX has spent roughly $10B on Starship development through 2025, with another $5–10B forecast before the system reaches steady-state operations. To amortise even half of that against flights, Starship needs to fly hundreds of times. At the Block 1 target of $10M marginal cost per flight and 100–150 tonnes payload, that's $5–10B in revenue—but only if there's demand for that many flights, at those prices. The chicken-and-egg problem: Starlink itself supplies ~50% of that demand by being SpaceX's own customer. The rest has to come from new industries that don't yet exist. This is why SpaceX has been so aggressive about staking out new use cases (lunar Starship for NASA Artemis, point-to-point Earth transport, on-orbit refilling) — they need to seed demand for the platform they're building. The same flywheel that made airliners cheap (cheaper planes drove demand, which drove more flights, which made planes cheaper) is what Starship is betting on.

IX.

The full economics synthesis

Pull everything together:

The same satellite, the same constellation design, the same physics — just by changing one column (dollars per kilogram), the whole picture flips. At $20,000/kg, no broadband constellation closes. At $1,500/kg, Starlink ships, OneWeb ships at lower density, Kuiper ships eventually. At $200/kg, the constellation is so cheap that the satellite hardware becomes the dominant cost — and Starlink V2 satellites cost roughly $1M each, so a full shell is more like $4–5B in satellites and $700M in launch. Cost composition flips.

None of this is about SpaceX being clever. SpaceX is clever, but Falcon 9 reuse is engineering, not magic — the booster design choices that made it possible (kerosene/LOX, throttleable Merlins, grid fins, propulsive landing) are now well-understood. They will be replicated. Blue Origin's New Glenn is broadly similar in approach. China's Long March 8 and Long March 9 reusable variants are in development. The cost line will keep dropping because the underlying physics permits it, not because one company has a moat.

The thing to absorb is that the entire space economy is downstream of $/kg-to-LEO. Latency, link budget, terminal cost, constellation size, replacement cadence — everything we discussed in Why LEO, Not GEO — all of it derives from the assumption that you can put 22 tonnes in orbit for $15M. Take that assumption away, and Starlink is impossible. Granted that assumption, and Starlink is not just possible but inevitable. Someone was going to build it.

My read on these numbers, after staring at them for a while: the Starship cost target is still speculative, but the Falcon 9 marginal numbers are already in the published data. Reuse changed the math more than a decade before anyone built a constellation that needed the math to be changed.

What you can now teach

Falcon 9 reuse didn't just change rocket costs — it changed the entire satellite-constellation cost equation. At $20,000/kg-to-LEO no constellation pencils; at $1,500/kg Starlink ships; at $200/kg orbital infrastructure becomes banal. Starlink exists because dollars-per-kilogram fell, and every other space business that emerges in the next decade will exist for the same reason.
