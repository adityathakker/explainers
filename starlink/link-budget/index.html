I.

Why do RF engineers count in decibels?

An RF link starts at the transmitter with maybe 20 watts of power and ends at the receiver with around 0.000 000 000 000 1 watts — 10−13 W. That's 14 orders of magnitude of decay. Nobody can multiply 14-zero numbers in their head, so radio engineers do something elegant: they take the logarithm. Once everything is a logarithm, gains and losses don't multiply — they add. The entire link from transmitter to receiver becomes a single column of additions and subtractions on the back of an envelope.

That column is the link budget. It is the most reused tool in radio engineering. Every system — Starlink, your phone, your WiFi router, GPS, deep-space probes — passes the same audit. Take the transmitter's output power. Add the gains. Subtract the losses. Compare the answer to what the receiver needs. If you're above the threshold, the link works. If you're below it, no amount of clever modulation will save you. That's the entire framework.

The decibel itself is just a ratio in log scale. The bel[1] is log10 of a power ratio; the decibel is one tenth of a bel. So gain in dB = 10·log10(Pout/Pin). A 10× gain is +10 dB. A 100× loss is −20 dB. A 4,000× loss (the GEO vs LEO path-loss penalty from the last explainer) is −36 dB. Once you've internalised that map, everything in the link becomes a small integer.

dB = 10 · log10(Pout / Pin)
dBm = 10 · log10(P / 1 mW)
dBi = gain relative to an isotropic (omnidirectional) antenna dBm is absolute power referenced to 1 milliwatt; dBi is relative antenna gain

Three suffixes carry meaning. dB is dimensionless — a ratio. dBm is absolute power referenced to 1 mW (so 0 dBm = 1 mW, 30 dBm = 1 W). dBi is antenna gain relative to a perfectly omnidirectional emitter. If you don't keep the suffixes straight, your link budget is just numerology. Watch the units. Always.

RatiodBMental model

That last row is worth pausing on. A 20-watt transmitter on a Starlink satellite radiates into space and the dish on your roof catches a tenth-trillionth of a milliwatt. The system works because every dB of gain on either end — antenna gain, receiver sensitivity, coding efficiency — pays compound interest. Engineers get religion about dB because they have to.

II.

What does the link budget actually compute?

The framework, in one line:

Preceived = Ptransmit + Gtx + Grx − Lpath − Latmosphere − Lrain − Lmisc all terms in dB or dBm; everything you give the receiver, minus everything spacetime took from you

Read it left to right. You start with whatever power the transmitter pumps into its antenna. The transmit antenna doesn't radiate evenly — it focuses energy into a narrow cone, so we add Gtx, the gain in the desired direction. The signal then travels through space, where spreading loss — the inverse-square law — subtracts Lpath. Add the atmosphere (water vapour, oxygen, rain) and miscellaneous losses (pointing error, polarization mismatch, feeder cables). At the far end, the receive antenna catches a tiny fraction and concentrates it via Grx. What's left is Preceived, in dBm.

That number alone isn't enough. The receiver also has to beat its own noise floor by a comfortable margin — the signal-to-noise ratio. So the second half of the budget computes the noise floor and the required SNR, then asks whether Preceived − N ≥ SNRrequired.

Well, actually 🧐 — Friis equation, decibel form

The continuous-form version of the link equation is named after Harald Friis (Bell Labs, 1946)[2]:

Pr = Pt · Gt · Gr · (λ / 4πd)2 all terms are linear (watts, dimensionless gain), λ is wavelength, d is distance

Take 10·log10 of both sides and you get the decibel version we used above — identical equation, friendlier audience. The (λ/4πd)2 term is sometimes called the spreading factor; λ2/(4π) is the effective aperture of an isotropic antenna. So Friis says: take the transmit power, focus it (Gt), spread it over a sphere of area 4πd², catch what hits the receive aperture (the geometric area times Gr). Physics is geometry; geometry is just trigonometry with extra steps.

III.

What is free-space path loss?

Imagine a perfectly omnidirectional transmitter, radiating Pt watts isotropically into space. At distance d, that power has spread uniformly over the surface of a sphere of area 4πd2. So the power density at the receiver is:

S = Pt / (4πd2)   [W/m²] power per unit area on the sphere of radius d — the inverse-square law

The receive antenna captures whatever flux falls inside its effective aperture Ae. For any antenna, the relationship between gain and aperture is:

Ae = Gr · λ2 / (4π) where λ = c/f is wavelength — bigger antennas have more gain at lower frequencies

Multiply the power density by the effective aperture and you get the received power:

Pr = S · Ae = Pt · Gr · λ2 / (4πd)2 isotropic transmit, gain-Gr receive — with Gt too, multiply by it

Pull out the path-loss term — the part that's pure geometry, not antenna design:

FSPL = (4πd / λ)2 = (4πd·f / c)2
FSPL (dB) = 20 log10(d) + 20 log10(f) + 20 log10(4π/c)
FSPL (dB) ≈ 20 log10(dkm) + 20 log10(fMHz) + 32.45 the +32.45 absorbs unit conversions from m to km and Hz to MHz

Two things to notice. First, path loss grows as the square of distance — doubling distance costs you 6 dB (4× the signal goes away). Second, it grows as the square of frequency for a fixed antenna gain — doubling frequency also costs you 6 dB. This is the dominant term in any space link. For Starlink at 12 GHz and 550 km, it's 169 dB — a factor of 1016.9. The 20-W transmitter delivers picowatts to the dish.

Well, actually 🧐 — why are uplink and downlink on different frequencies?

Starlink's downlink (satellite→dish) is Ku-band, around 11–12 GHz. The uplink (dish→satellite) is Ka-band, around 27–30 GHz. Why the asymmetry?

Three reasons. (1) Spectrum allocation: ITU divided the bands long ago and the satellite community got specific slices for each direction. (2) Power asymmetry: the satellite has a small solar array and tight thermal limits, so its downlink runs at the lower-loss frequency to maximise efficiency. The dish on the ground has effectively unlimited power and a bigger thermal envelope, so it can absorb the extra Ka-band path loss on the way up. (3) Rain asymmetry: rain attenuation grows steeply with frequency, so the more critical direction (downlink, since it carries Netflix back to you) gets the more robust frequency. The Ka-band uplink degrades first when it rains; the Ku-band downlink holds longer.

IV.

How does antenna gain rescue the link?

FSPL is brutal. The only thing strong enough to fight it is antenna gain. A directional antenna doesn't create energy — it just redirects what the transmitter sent, focusing it into a narrower beam. Same total power; concentrated in a smaller cone. From the receiver's perspective, signal in the beam is amplified by the gain; signal outside is suppressed.

For a parabolic dish or a uniformly illuminated aperture of area A, the peak gain is:

G = η · (4πA) / λ2
G (dBi) = 10 log10(G) η is aperture efficiency (typically 0.55–0.70 for a real dish, 0.4–0.55 for phased arrays)

Three corollaries fall out. Bigger antennas mean more gain — doubling the aperture diameter quadruples the gain (+6 dB). Higher frequencies mean more gain for the same physical size, because gain scales with A/λ2. Beamwidth narrows as gain grows — a 30 dBi antenna has roughly a 6° half-power beamwidth; a 50 dBi antenna has roughly 0.6°.

Add transmit gain and receive gain into a single “antenna budget” and the picture sharpens. Starlink contributes ~30 dBi (satellite) + ~33 dBi (dish) = +63 dB of pure antenna concentration. That's a factor of two million — without it, the 20-W transmitter would be lost in noise long before reaching your dish. The dish is doing as much work as the rocket.

The product of transmit power and transmit antenna gain has its own name and its own units:

EIRP = Pt · Gt (in linear)
EIRP (dBW) = 10 log10(Pt [W]) + Gt [dBi] Effective Isotropic Radiated Power — what an omnidirectional source would need to transmit to match the directional one in the beam

EIRP is the single number that matters most for whether your signal punches through. The FCC and ITU regulate it, satellite operators publish it, and link budgets always factor it. A Starlink satellite's per-beam EIRP is around +73 dBm (or +43 dBW). A 5-watt cell tower at 15 dBi gain is +52 dBm. A deep-space probe with 100 W and 50 dBi is +100 dBm. Different scales; same calculation.

V.

What does rain do to the link?

Water absorbs and scatters microwaves. Below ~5 GHz, raindrops are much smaller than the wavelength and the attenuation is negligible. Above ~10 GHz, the wavelength shrinks into the raindrop regime (1–3 mm) and the loss climbs steeply. Above 20 GHz, a heavy thunderstorm can outright kill a satellite link. The technical name for this is hydrometeor attenuation; engineers just call it rain fade.

The standard model is ITU-R P.838, an empirical power law calibrated against decades of measurement[3]:

A = k · Rα [dB/km] A = specific attenuation, R = rain rate in mm/hr, k and α depend on frequency and polarization

The coefficients k and α are tabulated in the ITU recommendation. Here are the headline values for vertical polarization (Starlink uses circular, which is roughly an average of horizontal and vertical):

The total path attenuation depends on how much rain the signal actually travels through. For a satellite link, that's the slant path through the “rain layer” — the troposphere up to the freezing level, typically the first 3–5 km of altitude. At elevation angle ε:

Ls = (hrain − hstation) / sin(ε)
Atotal = A · Ls [dB] hrain ≈ 4 km, hstation ≈ 0.1 km, ε = elevation angle of satellite

This is why elevation angle matters so much. A satellite at zenith (ε = 90°) travels through 4 km of rain. A satellite at ε = 25° (Starlink's minimum) travels through 4/sin(25°) ≈ 9.5 km of rain. Low-elevation links eat more rain — over double the attenuation for the same storm.

Well, actually 🧐 — adaptive coding and modulation

Modern satellite links don't just sit at one bitrate and pray it rains less than the budget assumes. They use Adaptive Coding and Modulation (ACM): when the SNR drops, the system automatically downshifts to a more robust (and slower) modulation. Starlink uses the DVB-S2X standard, which has a ladder of about 30 modes from QPSK with rate-1/4 coding (works at SNR ≈ −2 dB) up to 256-APSK with rate-9/10 coding (needs SNR ≈ +18 dB).

In practice this means your Starlink stays connected through light to moderate rain but the throughput drops — you don't notice when the latency-sensitive game keeps working, but you do notice when 200 Mbps becomes 25 Mbps in a thunderstorm. The ACM ladder is what turns “link broken” into “link degraded.” Without it, the system would have to design for the worst-case rainstorm at every moment, leaving most clear-sky bandwidth on the table.

VI.

What does the receiver actually need?

You can't compare received power to zero. You compare it to noise. Every receiver has a noise floor — the random thermal jiggling of electrons in its front-end electronics, plus the cosmic and atmospheric noise that leaks in through the antenna. Signals below the noise floor are undetectable; signals just above it are recoverable only with heavy error-correction.

The thermal noise floor is set by physics, not by engineering. From Johnson–Nyquist[4]:

N = k · T · B
N (dBm) = −174 + 10 log10(B [Hz]) + NF k = Boltzmann's constant, T ≈ 290 K (“room temperature”), B = bandwidth, NF = receiver noise figure in dB

The constant −174 dBm/Hz is the thermal noise floor at room temperature, per hertz of bandwidth. Wider channels collect more noise — a 1 MHz channel has −114 dBm of noise, a 250 MHz Starlink channel has about −90 dBm before the noise figure penalty. The noise figure NF is how much your receiver's own electronics add on top — a good low-noise amplifier (LNA) hits 1.5–2.5 dB; a cheap one might add 6 dB.

The signal-to-noise ratio is the headline. In dB:

SNR = Preceived − N − Limpl Limpl is implementation loss — the gap between theoretical and real-world demodulator performance, typically 1–2 dB

Different modulations need different SNR. The map below is the Shannon-Hartley horizon — it's how much SNR each scheme needs to hit a 10−6 bit error rate with realistic forward error correction:

ModulationBits/symbolRequired SNRUse case

Each step up the modulation ladder costs about 3 dB but buys you ~25% more bits per second. ACM picks the highest rung that fits the current SNR. In clear sky, Starlink runs at 32-APSK or 64-QAM; in heavy rain, it slides down to QPSK and accepts the throughput hit.

VII.

How much margin should you design for?

A link that just barely closes in nominal conditions doesn't close at all in the real world. Antennas point slightly off-axis. Rain happens. Components age and drift. Sun-noise events temporarily blind the receiver. The link budget reserves a few extra dB on top of the minimum — the link margin — to absorb these.

M = SNRavailable − SNRrequired positive margin means the link works; negative means it doesn't

How big should M be? Depends on the application. The industry conventions:

SystemTypical marginWhy
Deep-space probe (Voyager, JWST)3 dBEvery dB costs a kg of antenna; design tight, eat outages
GEO broadcast TV3–5 dBRain outages tolerated for minutes/year
Starlink (consumer broadband)6–10 dBSurvives moderate rain via ACM
Cellular handset8–15 dBMultipath fading, building penetration, body absorption
Mission-critical (aviation, military)15–25 dBHas to keep working in the worst minute of the worst year

Margin is bought with antenna area, transmit power, or modulation conservatism. Each is expensive in its own way. Engineers spend their careers arguing about which knob to turn. The honest answer is usually “all of them, slightly,” because every dB of margin is one less complaint when the system inevitably encounters its worst-case operating environment.

The other reason margin matters: availability. RF systems quote “link availability” as the percent of time the link closes. 99% availability sounds high but is 3.65 days of outage per year — broken. 99.9% is 8.8 hours. 99.99% is 53 minutes. The link budget has to close not at the mean rain rate but at the rain rate exceeded only X% of the year, for your target X. The ITU publishes rain rate exceedance tables by climate zone (ITU-R P.837) and you pick your design point off them.

VIII.

Notice the symmetry. GEO compensates for 40 extra dB of path loss with ~30 dB of extra antenna gain (big dish on the ground) and ~7 dB of extra transmit power. It's tight; HughesNet's link margin is structurally lower than Starlink's, which is why GEO broadband caps out at lower bitrates with higher outage. The 5G mmWave story is the opposite extreme — the transmitter is close enough (300 m) that FSPL is trivial, so the budget is dominated by handheld-antenna constraints and walking-through-walls fading, not space-link physics.

Same framework. Different numbers. Every radio engineer alive reads these tables the same way.

Once you can fill them in, you can debug any link, predict any outage, and call out any vendor whose published specs don't close on paper.

If I had to pick one number to tattoo on my wrist, it would be FSPL at 12 GHz and 550 km: 169 dB. Everything in the link budget is a subtraction from that baseline. Internalize that single number and most of satellite engineering is just bookkeeping around it.

What you can now teach

The link budget is the engineer's universal RF ledger: transmit power and antenna gain (+) on the left, path loss / atmosphere / rain / pointing / feeder / implementation (−) in the middle, receive antenna gain (+) at the end, and a noise-floor + required-SNR threshold the bottom line has to clear. Six standard losses, four standard gains, one threshold — every radio link you'll ever debug fits into that framework, and Starlink's 12 GHz downlink closes with about 2.5 dB of margin in moderate rain.

  1. The decibel was originally one tenth of a bel, named after Alexander Graham Bell by Bell Labs engineers in the 1920s. The bel itself proved too coarse a unit for most applications, so the decibel (one tenth) is the practical version. Both the unit and the convention of logarithmic engineering trace to telephony — long-distance phone-line attenuation was the original problem the bel was invented to describe. ↩
  2. The Friis transmission equation (Harald T. Friis, “A Note on a Simple Transmission Formula,” Proc. IRE, May 1946) is the canonical reference. It assumes perfectly matched, polarization-aligned, far-field antennas in free space — conditions that real terrestrial links violate, hence the additional loss line items in any real budget. ↩
  3. ITU-R P.838 is the current recommendation (latest revision P.838-3, 2005) for specific attenuation of microwave signals by rain. The coefficients are derived from drop-size-distribution measurements integrated against the Mie scattering solution for water spheres. P.839 gives the rain layer height by latitude; P.837 gives rain rate exceedance statistics by climate zone. The trio is what every satellite link designer cites. ↩
  4. The Johnson–Nyquist noise floor formula N = kTB was derived independently by John Johnson (Bell Labs, 1928, experimental) and Harry Nyquist (Bell Labs, 1928, theoretical). It is the absolute floor — you can lower T (cryogenically cooled LNAs in deep-space ground stations operate at 15 K, ~13 dB below room-temperature) but you cannot go below it without violating the second law of thermodynamics. ↩
