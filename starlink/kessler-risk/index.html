I.

What is Kessler Syndrome, and why does the count matter?

In 1978, a NASA scientist named Donald Kessler wrote a paper called "Collision Frequency of Artificial Satellites: The Creation of a Debris Belt"[1]. The argument was simple and bleak: above some critical density of objects in low Earth orbit, collisions between satellites produce so many fragments that those fragments themselves cause further collisions, faster than natural atmospheric drag can clean them up. The orbit runs away. A region of space becomes permanently unusable for decades to centuries.

The mechanism is not exotic. It is the same logic as a chain reaction in a fissile pile, except the neutrons are aluminum shards traveling at 15 km/s. The trigger is the satellite count. With N satellites in a shell, the number of pairs that could collide is N(N−1)/2. Double N, you quadruple the pairs. With 7,000 Starlinks in 2025 the pair count is already ~24.5 million. At 12,000 (the licensed Gen 1 final) it is 72 million. At 42,000 (FCC paperwork) it is 882 million.

This article is the cascade math, end to end. I will show you the pairwise rate formula, the N² growth, what a fragment cloud looks like, how a Monte Carlo simulation can settle into either a controlled regime or runaway, and — most importantly — why Starlink's altitude choice (550 km, not 1,200 km) is the single biggest variable that buys time back. The headline you should leave with: at 550 km natural drag deorbits debris in ~5 years; at 1,200 km it takes 1,000+ years. The altitude is the safety valve.

Three widgets along the way: a pairwise-rate plot you can drag from 1 to 50,000 satellites, a debris-cascade Monte Carlo, and a Mean-Time-To-Cascade calculator by altitude. The math is shown. Where the simple model breaks, I flag it. By the end you should be able to tell whether a given constellation design at a given altitude is self-cleaning or a debris trap.

II.

How do you compute the collision probability between two satellites?

Start with two objects. Each has some cross-sectional area σ (think of it as the silhouette the other object sees). They share a volume V at some closing relative velocity vrel. The expected collision rate, taken from kinetic theory — the same math as gas molecules in a box — is:

dC/dt = σ · vrel · n · Nothers per satellite per second; n = N/V is number density; σ is combined cross-section; vrel is relative velocity

Plug in numbers for a typical Starlink-vs-Starlink encounter in the 550 km shell:

  • σ (combined cross-section): a Starlink v1.5 is roughly a 2.8 × 1.4 m bus plus a 2.8 × 8 m solar panel. Combined cross-section for a pair (looking at a random orientation) is roughly σ ≈ 10 m² = 10×10⁻⁶ km².
  • vrel (relative velocity): two objects in 550 km LEO each move at ~7.6 km/s. The closing speed depends on the angle. Co-planar same-direction: tiny. Co-planar opposing: 15.2 km/s. Cross-inclination at 53°: ~12.2 km/s. Take vrel ≈ 15 km/s as the conservative non-coplanar number.
  • V (shell volume): a thin shell at 550 km altitude with 50 km vertical thickness has volume V ≈ 4πR²h ≈ 2.55×10¹⁰ km³.

So for a single satellite, the rate of being hit by any one specific other satellite is:

dC/dt (one pair) = 10×10⁻⁶ km² · 15 km/s · (1 / 2.55×10¹⁰ km³)
≈ 5.9×10⁻¹⁵ per second ≈ 1.9×10⁻⁷ per year (one pair)

Per pair per year, that is a probability of about 1 in 5 million. Sounds reassuring — until you remember that pairs scale quadratically.

Well, actually 🧐 — the kinetic-theory formula assumes uniform mixing

The dC/dt = σ·v·n formula treats the shell as a well-stirred gas. Real LEO is not. Satellites in the Starlink constellation fly in Walker-Delta patterns with carefully phased planes — pairs in the same plane never collide because they're locked to a relative orbit; pairs in different planes have predictable, repeating conjunction geometry. SpaceX's collision avoidance ledger is built on this: the conjunctions are deterministic and you can autonomously thrust hours in advance.

The kinetic-theory rate is therefore an upper bound for a well-managed operational constellation. It becomes the actual rate once a satellite goes dead, loses attitude control, or sheds an uncatalogued fragment. The cascade math is really about the uncatalogued and uncontrolled population, not the active fleet. That's what makes ASAT tests and dead-satellite collisions so much more dangerous than they look.

III.

Why does N(N−1)/2 explode?

Pick any two satellites out of N. The number of distinct unordered pairs is the binomial coefficient:

P(N) = N · (N−1) / 2 ≈ N² / 2 number of distinct unordered pairs in N objects; the −1 is negligible for large N

That ≈ N² is the entire story. The whole-shell collision rate is the per-pair rate times the number of pairs:

dCshell/dt = σ · vrel · (N²/2) / V Kessler's central formula — quadratic in N, inversely proportional to shell volume

IV.

What does an orbital debris cloud actually look like?

A satellite collision is not two objects becoming one. It is two intact spacecraft becoming a cloud of fragments — some traceable, most not. The standard reference event is the 2009 Iridium 33 / Cosmos 2251 collision[2]. A defunct Russian military comsat (Cosmos, dead since 1995) hit an operational Iridium voice satellite at 11.7 km/s over Siberia. Closing energy: about 50 GJ — the same as detonating 12 tonnes of TNT, except all of it converts into fragment kinetic energy rather than blast.

The result was catalogued by US Strategic Command. The two satellites produced:

  • ~1,800 trackable fragments larger than ~10 cm (the radar floor). About 1,000 are still in orbit a decade later.
  • An estimated >100,000 untrackable fragments in the 1-mm-to-10-cm range — each one still lethal to a satellite at 15 km/s impact velocity.
  • A vertical debris spread of ~1,400 km by year one (debris that gains kinetic energy moves to higher orbits; debris that loses it falls).
  • Trackable fragments dispersed across ~50 km/s spread in semi-major axis equivalents — meaning the cloud doesn't stay in the original shell.

The energy distribution of fragment sizes follows a known power law (NASA's standard breakup model[3]):

N(>L) = 0.1 · M0.75 · L−1.71 N(>L) = number of fragments larger than length L (m), M = total impact mass (kg)

For a Starlink-vs-Starlink collision (combined mass ~2,400 kg in the 800-kg v1.5 era), this model predicts roughly 28,000 fragments larger than 1 cm, of which ~1,500 are large enough to track. Every one of those fragments now has its own collision probability with every intact satellite in the shell. This is the feedback term.

Each fragment is moving at orbital velocity — for a 1-cm aluminum chip at 15 km/s, the impact energy is about 1.1 kJ, equivalent to a small handgun round. A 10-cm fragment is the energy of a stick of dynamite. There is no realistic shielding against either at orbital velocity. Whipple shields work for sub-cm micrometeoroids; against catalogued fragments, the answer is to dodge.

Well, actually 🧐 — the 2007 Chinese ASAT test is worse

In January 2007 China destroyed its own retired weather satellite, Fengyun-1C, with a kinetic ASAT interceptor at 865 km altitude. The collision produced >3,400 catalogued fragments and an estimated 150,000+ smaller pieces. Because the altitude is higher than Starlink's shell, those fragments have natural deorbit times of decades to centuries. As of 2024, roughly 2,700 of the trackable Fengyun fragments are still in orbit. They will mostly still be there in 2050.

The 2007 ASAT test alone increased the total catalogued LEO debris population by roughly 25% overnight, and that population's contribution to long-term collision risk in the 800-1,000 km band is now the dominant term in cascade-risk models. The Iridium/Cosmos collision in 2009 was at 789 km altitude — close enough that the two debris fields interact in long-term simulations. They are the two events that converted "Kessler Syndrome" from theory into operational risk language.

V.

How fast can a cascade multiply?

The cascade is a feedback loop. Each collision produces fragments. Each fragment has its own probability of hitting an intact satellite. Each new hit produces more fragments. The differential equation that captures this:

dF/dt = α · F · N − F / τ F = fragment count, N = intact satellites, α = per-fragment hit rate, τ = mean deorbit time

Two terms. The first (αFN) is fragment generation: it grows with both the number of debris pieces and the number of remaining intact satellites that can be hit. The second (F/τ) is fragment removal: atmospheric drag eventually pulls each piece down. Whether the cascade runs away depends on which term wins.

Set dF/dt = 0 and solve for the critical satellite density:

Ncritical = 1 / (α · τ) below this satellite count, the shell self-cleans; above it, debris compounds

The critical N is the threshold Kessler identified in 1978. Below it, you are fine: every collision is rare enough that drag scrubs the debris before the next one happens. Above it, the system is supercritical: a single triggering collision (an ASAT test, a dead satellite, a Chinese rocket body) feeds itself faster than drag can keep up. The runaway is the cascade.

VI.

Can active avoidance keep up?

The cascade math above assumes ballistic objects with no agency. Real Starlinks are not ballistic. Each one has an onboard Hall-effect thruster running on argon (originally krypton)[4], autonomous conjunction-screening software, and a downlinked decision log to ground control. When the US Space Force's 18th Space Defense Squadron publishes a conjunction warning — a forecast that two tracked objects will pass within some miss-distance — the satellite decides whether to dodge.

Starlink's reported numbers (per SpaceX semi-annual reports to the FCC):

The per-satellite maneuver rate has climbed from 3 to 17 per year, mostly because the conjunction-screening threshold has been tightened and because the shell is more crowded. The total number is staggering: roughly 50,000 deliberate orbital nudges in a single six-month window. None of this scales linearly. If you double the population, you quadruple the conjunctions to screen.

And here is the load-bearing limit. Avoidance requires three things:

  • The object must be tracked. Below ~10 cm, current ground-based radar misses it. The 100,000+ sub-10-cm fragments from the 2007 ASAT test are invisible to the system. You cannot dodge what you cannot see.
  • The satellite must be alive. A dead Starlink — lost attitude control, propellant depleted, software failure — cannot maneuver. About 0.5% of Starlinks have failed in orbit; with 7,000 active that is ~35 dead birds drifting at any time.
  • The time to react must exceed the warning latency. Conjunction warnings typically come in 24-72 hours ahead. A 10-second thrust at 0.02 m/s² produces a track change of meters per minute — just enough to clear a kilometre miss-distance if you start a day ahead. With less notice, you cannot move enough mass quickly enough.

Active avoidance is a powerful mitigation against the catalogued population. It is essentially useless against the uncatalogued cloud generated by a cascade. That asymmetry is the policy concern.

Well, actually 🧐 — the active fleet is not the problem

Read the cascade-risk literature carefully and you'll see the operational satellites are usually not the dominant term in long-term simulations. The dominant term is the population of dead rocket bodies and abandoned satellites already in the 800-1,000 km band — objects that were left there because nobody required deorbiting in the 1970s-1990s. The risk of a runaway cascade is largely a function of past sins, not current operations.

This is partly why Starlink at 550 km is in a different conversation than, say, the Soviet-era satellite graveyard at 850 km. Starlink's design choice is "we'll burn up naturally in 5 years and we steer in the meantime." The Soviet satellite from 1985 made no such commitment. The risk-management argument for new LEO constellations is not "we don't add risk" — it's "we add it at altitudes that self-clean." That's the section coming up.

VII.

What is the deorbit time at 550 km vs 1,200 km?

Atmospheric drag is the only natural cleanup mechanism in LEO. It is not a small effect at low altitudes — it is the dominant orbital perturbation. The atmospheric density at altitude h is approximately:

ρ(h) ≈ ρ0 · exp[ −(h − h0) / H(h) ] exponential atmosphere; H is the scale height, which itself grows with altitude

The scale height H is small near the ground (~7 km) and grows with altitude as the atmosphere thins (~50 km at 550 km, ~80 km at 800 km). What that means concretely: each scale height of altitude cuts the air density by ~1/e ≈ 37%, but the scale height itself grows, so altitude becomes exponentially "safer" in terms of drag.

Plugging this into the drag-induced orbital decay equation (a standard result; the derivation involves the satellite's ballistic coefficient and the integral of drag work along the orbit), you get the deorbit time as roughly:

τ(h) ≈ τ0 · exp[(h − h0) / Heff] crude exponential; in practice you solve the drag ODE numerically

Concrete values (these come from operational deorbit tracking and the NASA Orbital Debris Program Office models):

AltitudeDeorbit time (typical ballistic coefficient)Implication
400 km~1 yearISS regime; needs constant boosting
550 km~5 yearsStarlink shell; self-cleaning
700 km~30 yearsMarginal; debris persists
800 km~100 yearsIridium/Cosmos collision altitude
1,000 km~400 yearsSoviet graveyard belt
1,200 km~1,000+ yearsOneWeb shell; debris trap
2,000 km~10,000+ yearsEffectively permanent

The difference between 550 km and 1,200 km is two and a half orders of magnitude in deorbit time. At 550 km, any fragment from a collision is gone within a decade. At 1,200 km, it is a permanent feature of the orbit on every relevant timescale.

This is the cleanest variable in the whole cascade-risk calculation. The differential equation dF/dt = αFN − F/τ has the τ in the denominator of the removal term. Make τ 200× bigger and the removal term collapses to nearly zero. The cascade is no longer self-limiting.

VIII.

Why does Starlink's altitude choice actually help here?

Combine the two effects. Pairwise rate scales with N²/V (Section III). Deorbit time scales with exp(h/Heff) (Section VII). The Mean Time To Cascade — the expected wait for a debris-creating collision conditional on the constellation never being scrubbed — is approximately:

MTTC(h, N) ≈ 1 / [σ · vrel · (N²/2) / V(h)] but the runaway condition depends additionally on whether MTTC < τ(h)

If MTTC < τ, debris compounds faster than it decays — supercritical. If MTTC > τ, the shell self-cleans — subcritical. Altitude doesn't change MTTC much; it changes τ enormously. A factor-2 altitude increase (550→1100) maybe doubles MTTC. The same altitude increase multiplies τ by ~200×. The deorbit-time term wins, by orders of magnitude.

Starlink's choice of 550 km is not a coincidence and not optimised purely for latency. The orbital lifetime at 550 km is short enough that even if every Starlink in the shell broke up tomorrow, the resulting debris would mostly be gone within a decade or two. The shell is self-cleaning by design. Any chunk that drifts above the operational band drops back down. Any failed satellite reenters within 5 years even with no avoidance maneuver. The altitude is the regulatory backstop, baked into physics.

Well, actually 🧐 — active debris removal is a chicken-and-egg problem

If altitude is the safety valve, why not just deorbit the higher stuff? Active debris removal (ADR) is technically possible: tethers, harpoons, robotic grapplers, drag-augmentation balloons. ClearSpace-1 (ESA), Astroscale's ELSA-d (Japan), and several DARPA-funded prototypes have demonstrated rendezvous and grapple with cooperative targets in LEO.

The problem is the economics and the cooperation. Removing one piece of debris costs roughly $5-20 million on current vehicle prices. The most-dangerous catalogued objects are dead Russian and Chinese satellites; their owners have no incentive to pay, and no other country has legal authority to remove them under the 1972 Liability Convention. To remove a hundred high-priority objects from the 800-1,000 km band would cost $1-2 billion and require diplomatic agreements that don't exist. Meanwhile, every new launch into LEO without active deorbit makes the problem worse. The cleanest answer remains: don't go above 600 km if you don't have to.

The headline number you should leave with is the ratio. At Starlink's altitude:

MTTC(550 km, 12,000 sats) >> τ(550 km) ≈ 5 yr collision rate is low enough that drag scrubs faster than debris generates

At OneWeb's altitude (1,200 km), the same constellation size would put MTTC and τ on the wrong side of crossover, even though OneWeb itself is at a fraction of the size. The altitude choice swings the safety margin by 2-3 orders of magnitude. Starlink at 550 km is closer to Iridium's self-cleaning regime than to the 800-km Cosmos graveyard.

None of this means Starlink is risk-free. It means the structural risk — the long-tail cascade scenario — lives at higher altitudes than the operational band. The acute risk for an individual satellite is being hit by a Chinese ASAT fragment from 2007 that has drifted down into the band. The cascade risk for the constellation, conditional on Starlink doing what it says it will do (deorbit failed satellites, share conjunction data, keep the krypton thrusters fed), is bounded by the atmosphere itself.

What you can now teach

Pairwise collision count grows as N²/2 — quadratically in constellation size — while atmospheric drag deorbit time grows exponentially with altitude. The result: Starlink's 550 km choice is the single biggest knob in cascade risk. 12,000 satellites at 550 km is roughly self-cleaning in 5 years; the same 12,000 at 1,200 km would be a thousand-year debris trap, and the cascade math says they would not stay 12,000 for long.
