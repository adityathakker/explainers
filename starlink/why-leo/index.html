I.

Why is "just use GEO" the wrong answer?

The engineering brief, stripped down: cover the planet with broadband internet from space. The first sensible answer is geostationary orbit. Park three satellites at 35,786 km on the equator, 120° apart. The geometry is famous — one of Arthur C. Clarke's better-known calculations from 1945. The orbital period matches Earth's rotation, the satellites appear motionless from the ground, dish antennas can be screwed to a roof and never moved. HughesNet and Viasat do exactly this. The infrastructure is well understood and the satellite count is small.

Starlink does the opposite. As of 2025 there are roughly 7,000 active Starlink satellites in low Earth orbit at altitudes between 540 and 570 km. The constellation is still being built; the licensed final figure is around 12,000, with FCC paperwork for up to 42,000. Each satellite weighs about 800 kg and lasts roughly five years before deorbiting. Compared to the three-satellite GEO solution, this looks insane.

It isn't. The decision falls out of physics — specifically the interaction between four variables that GEO loses on, individually and in combination: latency, link budget, capacity, and per-bit launch cost. None of these are negotiable. They are properties of the speed of light, the inverse-square law, and the rocket equation. Starlink is not a clever business plan that happens to use LEO; it is the first satellite system whose design accepts what the physics has been saying for thirty years.

This article walks through each constraint and shows how it forces the answer. There are three widgets along the way. The math is shown. The trade-offs are made explicit. At the end, you should be able to tell a friend exactly why GEO loses, and why 4,000 satellites is not a stretch goal but the floor.

II.

What does altitude do to latency?

The speed of light in vacuum is exactly 299,792,458 m/s. A radio signal cannot travel faster. Every meter of separation between the user and the satellite is one-third of a nanosecond of round-trip time, and you cannot buy your way out of it. This is the first thing GEO loses on.

One-way latency, ground to satellite to ground (Earth station back to ground via the same satellite), is:

t1-way = 2 × h / c h = orbital altitude above ground; c = speed of light; ignores ground-network hops

Round-trip time (RTT), which is what a TCP handshake or a voice call actually feels, is twice that. Run the numbers for the canonical orbits:

OrbitAltitudeOne-wayRTTUse case
LEO (Starlink)550 km3.7 ms7 msGaming, voice, real-time
LEO (deeper)1,200 km8 ms16 msBroadband, marginal for voice
MEO (GPS)20,200 km135 ms270 msNavigation; useless for interactive
GEO (HughesNet)35,786 km239 ms478 msStreaming; broken for VoIP/gaming

For context: a typical cross-continent fibre link runs around 50–80 ms RTT. A web page that needs three sequential round-trips (DNS, TLS, HTTP) opens in roughly 250 ms over fibre. The same page over GEO needs three 480-ms round-trips — one and a half seconds of pure waiting before the first byte even arrives. Voice over IP requires sub-200 ms RTT to feel natural; humans start hearing the round-trip as awkward pauses above 300 ms. GEO is over double the limit.

Well, actually 🧐 — the satellite is rarely directly overhead

The bars above assume the satellite is at zenith (straight up). In practice the user terminal sees the satellite at some elevation angle ε above the horizon, and the actual line-of-sight distance — the slant range — is longer than the orbital altitude. Law of cosines applied to the Earth-centre / ground-station / satellite triangle gives:

d = −R·sin(ε) + √( R²·sin²(ε) + 2Rh + h² ) d = slant range, R = Earth radius (6,378 km), h = orbital altitude, ε = elevation angle at ground station

For LEO at h = 550 km, run the numbers across canonical elevations:

  • ε = 90° (zenith): d = 550 km. Slant = altitude. The optimistic case in the chart above.
  • ε = 25° (Starlink's minimum): d ≈ 1,124 km. Slant is 2.0× the altitude.
  • ε = 10°: d ≈ 1,815 km. Slant is 3.3× the altitude.
  • ε = 0° (true horizon): d ≈ 2,705 km. Slant is 4.9× the altitude.

That is why Starlink's user terminals are configured to refuse satellites below ~25° elevation. Below that, slant range balloons, latency grows with it, and the dish has to push through several extra atmospheres of slant-path air. The 25° floor is not arbitrary — it is the elevation at which slant range stays inside 2× altitude, holding round-trip latency under ~15 ms even at the geometric edge of coverage.

The latency gap is not just "GEO is slower." For interactive use, GEO is broken. You can stream Netflix over GEO because Netflix is one-way and pre-buffered — the protocol hides the round-trip. You cannot Zoom over GEO. You cannot play any real-time game. You cannot do TLS in any reasonable time. Any product that needs to be interactive is structurally excluded from the GEO satellite market, no matter how cheap the bandwidth gets.

III.

What does altitude do to coverage?

If GEO loses on latency, surely it wins on coverage? It does — for a single satellite. The math is geometric. A satellite at altitude h can serve every point on the Earth's surface from which its elevation angle is above some minimum εmin (the user terminal won't talk to satellites near the horizon because of atmospheric absorption, multipath, and obstruction). The satellite's footprint is the spherical cap of Earth's surface that satisfies this.

Walking the geometry: let R = Earth radius (6,378 km), h = altitude, and εmin = minimum elevation angle. The angular radius of the footprint (measured from Earth's centre) is:

θ = acos[ R · cos(εmin) / (R + h) ] − εmin law of sines applied to the ground-station / satellite / Earth-centre triangle

And the footprint area, as a fraction of Earth's total surface, is:

Afootprint / AEarth = (1 − cosθ) / 2 surface area of a spherical cap formula

Plug in the canonical orbits with a typical εmin = 25°:

OrbitAltitudeFootprint radiusFootprint area% of Earth
Starlink LEO550 km~940 km~2.8 M km²0.55%
Deep LEO1,200 km~1,700 km~8.7 M km²1.7%
MEO (GPS)20,200 km~6,800 km~110 M km²21.5%
GEO35,786 km~7,500 km~144 M km²28.2%

A GEO satellite is genuinely majestic per-bird: one satellite illuminates over a quarter of the Earth's surface. A LEO satellite illuminates about half a percent. The ratio is roughly 50×.

Well, actually 🧐 — coverage isn't the same as capacity

One GEO satellite covers 30× the area of one LEO satellite. That is a per-satellite coverage advantage, not a per-user capacity advantage. Every user in that gigantic footprint shares the satellite's total downlink — tens of Gbps split across millions of potential customers, even if only a fraction are online at any moment.

This is the real reason GEO broadband providers like HughesNet are capped at 25 Mbps with data caps and high jitter. Their satellites are oversold by physics. Starlink can sell each customer 100–200 Mbps with no cap because each satellite is shared by far fewer simultaneous users (tens of thousands per satellite, vs millions for a GEO bird). Coverage is necessary but not sufficient; the metric that actually matters is bandwidth-per-user, and on that metric LEO with thousands of satellites trounces GEO with three.

IV.

How many satellites do you need for global coverage?

If one LEO satellite covers 0.55% of Earth's surface, the naive answer is 1 / 0.0055 ≈ 182 satellites for full coverage. The actual answer is higher, for three reasons. First, the satellites have to be distributed in orbits — you can't just place them on a sphere. Second, you need redundancy: any point on Earth should see multiple satellites at acceptable elevation, because (a) handoffs need an overlap window, and (b) one satellite is the bottleneck that determines the local capacity. Third, the polar caps and equatorial regions get hit differently by different inclinations, and a single inclination cannot cover both well.

The standard tool for laying out a constellation is the Walker constellation. A Walker constellation is specified by three numbers: total satellites T, number of orbital planes P, and a phase parameter F. Satellites are distributed evenly across planes; planes are distributed evenly in longitude. The relationship between coverage and satellite count is roughly:

T ≈ (4π / θ2) × k θ is the footprint half-angle in radians; k ≈ 1.5–2 is the redundancy factor for n-fold coverage

This is the “sphere-packing” estimate. Real constellations need to handle the inclination problem (orbits don't go straight up-and-down, so polar coverage requires polar orbits), the equator problem (most users are at low latitudes, so most capacity should be there), and the gap problem (you don't want any 30-minute window where a region has no satellites overhead). Walking through real designs:

Notice the OneWeb data point. At 1,200 km, you need roughly 650 satellites to cover the globe with capacity comparable to terrestrial broadband. At 550 km, the math says about 4,400 (Starlink's original v1 design) for the same coverage with higher capacity per area. The altitude trade is real: lower means more satellites but higher capacity-per-unit-area and lower latency.

V.

What does altitude do to launch energy?

Now we get to why the answer until ~2018 was “GEO is the only choice that pencils.” Putting a satellite at LEO is cheap per-kg, but you need a lot of satellites. Putting a satellite at GEO is expensive per-kg, but you need very few. The crossover depended on how much it cost to actually fly a rocket.

The delta-V (velocity change) required to reach an orbit is governed by the rocket equation:

Δv = ve · ln(m0 / mf) ve = exhaust velocity, m0 = initial mass, mf = final mass after burn. Tsiolkovsky 1903.

For an Earth-launched rocket using kerosene/LOX (Falcon 9 first stage):

  • Earth surface → LEO: Δv ≈ 9.4 km/s (after gravity losses and drag)
  • Earth surface → GTO: Δv ≈ 11.7 km/s (geostationary transfer orbit)
  • GTO → GEO circularisation: another ≈ 1.5 km/s (often done by the satellite itself)

That's a 2.3 km/s extra delta-V for GEO. Plugged into the rocket equation, with a typical specific impulse of 311 s (kerosene/LOX) giving ve = 3,050 m/s:

m0 / mf = eΔv / ve
LEO: e9400/3050 = e3.08 ≈ 21.8× mass ratio
GEO: e11400/3050 = e3.74 ≈ 42.0× mass ratio

What does this mean practically? On a Falcon 9, the LEO payload is about 22,800 kg. The GTO payload is 8,300 kg. The GEO payload (after circularisation) is around 4,500 kg, only if the second stage gets restarted multiple times. Same rocket, same propellant, same engineer team — the GEO payload is about a fifth of the LEO payload. Per kilogram, GEO is roughly 5× more expensive to deliver than LEO.

So the per-satellite launch cost ratio is about 5:1 GEO:LEO. But the constellation-size ratio is roughly 1:1,500 GEO:LEO. Total launch cost ratio for the whole constellation: GEO is cheaper by a factor of 5/1500 ≈ 1/300. GEO wins on launch cost by two orders of magnitude.

Well, actually 🧐 — reuse is the missing variable

Those launch-cost numbers assume the rocket is thrown away after each flight, which was true for every operational vehicle except Space Shuttle (and Shuttle was its own catastrophe). Until SpaceX flew a recovered Falcon 9 first stage in 2017, "launch cost" meant the per-mission cost of a one-time-use vehicle.

The Falcon 9 first stage now flies on average 15–25 times before retirement. The marginal cost of an additional flight is the refurbishment plus the second stage (which is still expendable). This collapses the per-kg-to-LEO cost from roughly $20,000 (Shuttle era) to roughly $1,500 (Falcon 9 reused, current commercial). On Starship, expected eventual cost is $100–200/kg. The economics that GEO won on for fifty years assumed nobody could fly cheaply enough to make 4,000-satellite constellations affordable. Starlink is the first proof that assumption is no longer true.

That was the answer until 2018. Reuse changes the constants but not the structure. We come back to that in Section VIII.

VI.

What does altitude do to the RF link budget?

Latency and coverage and launch cost are the headline three. The fourth, and arguably the most underappreciated, is the radio link itself. Radio signals spread out as they propagate. The energy that hits a receiver shrinks with the square of the distance — the inverse-square law. This loss is called free-space path loss (FSPL), and the equation is:

FSPL (dB) = 20 log10(d) + 20 log10(f) + 20 log10(4π/c)
≈ 20 log10(dkm) + 20 log10(fMHz) + 32.45 d = distance, f = frequency, c = speed of light. dB form for the engineering convenience.

At Starlink's main Ku-band downlink frequency (around 12 GHz), the path loss is:

OrbitDistanceFSPL @ 12 GHzExtra vs LEO
LEO (Starlink)550 km169 dBbaseline
Deep LEO1,200 km176 dB+7 dB
MEO (GPS)20,200 km200 dB+31 dB
GEO35,786 km205 dB+36 dB

36 dB. Decibels are logarithmic. 10 dB is 10×, 20 dB is 100×, 30 dB is 1,000×. The GEO signal is roughly 4,000× weaker than the equivalent LEO signal, for the same transmit power and frequency. To close that link, you need to either crank up the transmit power 4,000× (impossible on a satellite with finite solar panels), or increase antenna gain by 36 dB on the receiver (a much bigger dish), or accept 4,000× lower bitrate.

This is why HughesNet's typical residential dish is 0.74 m across (74 cm), and the bitrate is capped at 25 Mbps. Starlink's user terminal antenna is also small — about 60 cm — but the link only has to close to 550 km instead of 35,786 km, and so it can sustain 100–200 Mbps. The terminal antenna size, transmit power, and bitrate are all downstream of the altitude choice.

Well, actually 🧐 — FSPL is not the only loss

Free-space path loss is the dominant term but there are others. Atmospheric absorption matters most at higher frequencies (above 20 GHz, rain attenuation becomes significant; above 50 GHz, oxygen and water vapour bands dominate). Polarization mismatch can cost a few dB. Pointing errors at the user terminal cost more if the antenna pattern is narrow. Multipath fading near the ground (urban, foliage) costs another 2–5 dB in Rayleigh-distributed deep fades.

For Starlink specifically, the Ku-band downlink (12 GHz) sees ~1 dB rain attenuation in moderate rain, and the Ka-band uplink (30 GHz) sees 5–10 dB in heavy rain. The link budget is designed with margin to absorb these — typically 6–10 dB above the minimum threshold — which is why your Starlink dish keeps working in light rain but degrades in a downpour. VII.

What does altitude do to the user terminal?

The radio-link math above has a hidden corollary: closing a GEO link requires a larger antenna or higher transmit power on the user side. For broadcast TV (DirecTV, Dish Network), this is fine — a 60-cm fixed dish bolted to the side of a house is cheap and the link is one-way. For broadband at high data rates, the antenna has to be much larger, more expensive, and mechanically steered with high precision.

Compare the user-terminal economics:

ServiceAntenna sizeAntenna typeHardware cost
HughesNet (GEO broadband)~74 cmFixed parabolic dish, manually aimed~$300 + $150 install
VSAT (enterprise GEO)1.2–3.8 mLarger dish, professionally installed$2,000–$20,000+
Iridium (LEO voice)handheldOmnidirectional whip~$1,000 (low data rate)
Starlink (LEO broadband)~60 cmPhased-array, self-tracking~$349 (subsidised from ~$3,000 BOM)

The Starlink user terminal is the genuinely novel piece of consumer hardware. It is a flat panel with about a thousand individually controlled antenna elements. The beam steers electronically: there is no motor, no moving parts. The terminal sits on a roof, points itself once at install, and then continuously tracks Starlink satellites as they pass overhead at 27,000 km/h. Each satellite is in view for 4–7 minutes; the terminal hands off seamlessly. A mechanically steered dish couldn't keep up — phased arrays are the only way.

This is why a LEO broadband constellation was impossible until phased-array terminals became cheap. The 1990s LEO attempts — Teledesic, ICO, Iridium's broadband cousin — assumed handsets and dishes that didn't exist yet. The economics imploded. Starlink's user terminal is roughly 80% of the consumer cost; SpaceX subsidises it heavily ($349 retail, ~$3,000 BOM in early production, now closer to $1,200) because the marginal revenue from monthly service is what pays for the terminal over time.

VIII.

Why didn't anyone do this in 1998?

Two things had to be true simultaneously for a LEO broadband constellation to make business sense: launch had to be cheap enough to put thousands of satellites in orbit, and user terminals had to be cheap enough to put one in every customer's home. Until roughly 2018, neither was true.

The first attempt was Teledesic (1990s, Bill Gates and Craig McCaw). The plan was 840 LEO satellites delivering broadband. The launch cost was the killer — at $30,000/kg-to-LEO on the Shuttle era, the constellation would have cost over $9 billion just to launch, not counting satellites. Teledesic burned through ~$1 billion and never launched a single operational satellite.

Iridium did launch (1998), but for voice not broadband. Its 66 satellites work, but the user handset is the size of a brick and the data rate is 2.4 kbps. The bankruptcy in 1999 is famous; the constellation kept operating because the satellites were already up there and the bankruptcy auction sold the system for $25 million, vastly less than the $5 billion construction cost.

OneWeb (2014→) is the most direct contemporary competitor. It targets 1,200 km altitude with ~650 satellites. The deeper altitude means fewer satellites (lower launch cost) but higher latency and per-area capacity than Starlink. OneWeb has had to be rescued from bankruptcy once and is now a fraction of Starlink's size. The 550-km vs 1,200-km altitude trade is a real engineering choice, and the answer depends on what specifically you're optimising; Starlink chose latency + capacity, OneWeb chose constellation size.

The two variables that flipped:

  • Launch cost: Falcon 9 reuse drops per-kg-to-LEO from ~$10,000 (mid-2010s expendable) to ~$1,500 (current). Starship aims for $100–200/kg. Below $5,000/kg, a 4,000-satellite constellation costs roughly $5–10 billion to launch — large but feasible. Above $20,000/kg, it costs $100 billion+ — infeasible.
  • Phased-array terminals: the silicon to drive a thousand-element antenna at consumer prices did not exist until ~2018. RF front-end chips, beamforming ICs, and the digital signal processing all rode the same Moore's Law curve as smartphones. A 2010 phased array was a $50,000 lab piece; a 2024 phased array is a $349 consumer good. Without that drop, the user-terminal cost would have made the entire system uneconomic.

Starlink isn't a new idea. It's an old idea that finally fell within reach. I think that's the real story — not a clever business plan, but a decades-old engineering brief that quietly cleared its two cost thresholds within twelve months of each other around 2018.

IX.

The actual decision matrix

Stack the trade-offs side by side. For each metric, write down which orbit wins and by roughly how much:

MetricGEO wins byLEO wins byWhy
Round-trip latency—~70×Speed of light, fundamental
Path loss (RF)—~4,000×Inverse-square law, fundamental
Per-bird coverage~50×—Geometry of altitude
Per-area capacity—~50×Spectrum reuse via spatial diversity
Launch cost per satellite~5×—Rocket equation

GEO wins on per-satellite coverage and total launch cost. LEO wins on latency, link budget, per-area capacity, and broadband user-terminal economics. The winners are not symmetric: GEO's advantages are easy to monetise only if your customers don't care about latency or per-user bandwidth. LEO's advantages are exactly what consumer internet needs.

What you can now teach

The LEO vs GEO decision is forced by physics, not marketing. Light travels at a finite speed (480-ms RTT to GEO breaks interactive use), radio signals decay with the square of distance (4,000× weaker at GEO than LEO), and consumer broadband needs both. The "obvious" 3-satellite GEO solution fails because the only thing GEO is genuinely good at is downstream broadcast to non-interactive customers. Once launch reuse drops the constellation cost below ~$10 billion and phased-array terminals drop below ~$1,000, LEO becomes not just the right answer but the only answer for satellite broadband.
